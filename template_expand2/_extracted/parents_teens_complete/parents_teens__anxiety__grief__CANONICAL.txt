## GRIEF / RECOGNITION v01 — CHILDHOOD ENDING — MID — THROAT TIGHTENING
---
Linda finds her daughter's stuffed rabbit in the closet. The one she slept with until age eleven. It's been in there for three years now. Her throat tightens holding it. The girl who needed this rabbit is gone. There's a teenager in her room now—someone who rolls her eyes at stuffed animals. The rabbit is soft. The girl is somewhere in the past.

================================================================================
## GRIEF / RECOGNITION v02 — LAST TIME — LATE — HOLLOWNESS
---
David realizes he'll never carry his son again. Hasn't for years. There was a last time—must have been when he was eight or nine—and neither of them knew it was the last. The hollowness opens in his chest. All the carrying is over. His son is taller than him now. The boy who fit on his shoulders exists only in photos.

================================================================================
## GRIEF / RECOGNITION v03 — PHASE ENDED — EARLY — HEAVINESS
---
Karen's kitchen is quiet on Saturday mornings now. Jake sleeps until noon. No more cartoons at 7am. No more pancake requests. The heaviness arrives with her coffee. The early-morning boy is gone. The teenage version stays in his room. Same person. Different phase. The Saturday mornings she once wished were quieter are too quiet now.

================================================================================
## GRIEF / RECOGNITION v04 — ROOM CHANGED — MID — CHEST ACHE
---
Michael stands in his daughter's doorway. The posters changed—boy bands replaced by abstract art. Toys became books became makeup. He aches looking at it. The room where he read bedtime stories is the same room. The girl who asked for one more chapter isn't. The ache is for a version of her that aged out while he was watching.

================================================================================
## GRIEF / RECOGNITION v05 — DOESN'T NEED HER — LATE — EYES STINGING
---
Susan's son handled the college applications himself. Didn't need her to review them. Didn't ask for input. Her eyes sting with the independence. She wanted him to be capable. He's capable. She's standing outside a process she used to be inside of. He's launching. She's watching the launch from further away than she expected.

## GRIEF / MECHANISM_PROOF v01 — WAVE PATTERN — MID
---
The waves come when Linda expects them and when she doesn't. Friday night—daughter doesn't want to watch their show anymore. Wave. Tuesday—finds the old lunch box in the garage. Wave. Random Wednesday—nothing triggers it, just the missing arrives. The pattern is: fine, fine, wave, fine, wave. The phases end. The waves don't.

================================================================================
## GRIEF / MECHANISM_PROOF v02 — ORDINARY TRIGGERS — EARLY
---
David finds them everywhere. The hook where the backpack used to hang—wave. The passenger seat his son sits in instead of the car seat—wave. The pediatrician bill that's not a pediatrician anymore—wave. The childhood is everywhere in absence. The ordinary things hold what's gone. The waves come through the most mundane doors.

================================================================================
## GRIEF / MECHANISM_PROOF v03 — FORGETTING THEN REMEMBERING — LATE
---
Karen almost makes two boxes of mac and cheese. Jake used to eat a whole box. Then she remembers: he's not that hungry anymore. Hasn't been for years. The forgetting is a tiny gift—for a second, the hungry little boy exists. The remembering is fresh. She makes one box. The teenager takes three bites. The forgetting happens again next month.

================================================================================
## GRIEF / MECHANISM_PROOF v04 — SAME LOSS DIFFERENT DOORS — MID
---
Michael finds the daughter-loss in different places. Monday—she doesn't want help with homework. Tuesday—she picked out her own clothes (when did that start?). Thursday—she said "don't worry about it" instead of telling him what's wrong. Same loss. Different entry points. The little girl who needed him opened each door. The teenager closed them.

## GRIEF / MECHANISM_PROOF v05 — WAVES DON'T ASK PERMISSION — EARLY
---
Susan is in a work meeting when the wave arrives. Her son's first-grade drawing is on her desk—the one he made of their family. He drew himself holding her hand. The wave doesn't care that she's presenting. Her eyes sting in front of colleagues. He drew himself at her side. He's across the city now, not needing her hand.

## GRIEF / TURNING_POINT v01 — WAVE DOESN'T DESTROY — MID
---
Linda finds the rabbit and braces for devastation. The wave comes—the girl who needed it, gone. But she doesn't drown. She holds the rabbit. Cries a little. Puts it back. The grief is huge. She's still standing. She walks downstairs and her teenager asks what's for dinner. The wave passed. The day continues. Both things.

================================================================================
## GRIEF / TURNING_POINT v02 — MEMORY WITH WARMTH — LATE
---
David is hit by the carrying-loss. Then a different memory surfaces: his son at four, arms around his neck, laughing as David pretended to drop him. The laughter is in the memory. Real laughter. The grief is still there. But the memory brought his son—not just the loss of him. The boy existed. Made that sound. The wave passes. Something warm stays.

## GRIEF / TURNING_POINT v03 — SOMEONE ELSE TOO — EARLY
---
Karen mentions the Saturday morning thing to her friend. "I miss the early years sometimes." Her friend's eyes fill. "I found his baby shoes last week." Other parents carry this too. The heaviness isn't Karen's alone. It's what happens when children grow. The loss is shared. It doesn't diminish. But it has company now.

================================================================================
## GRIEF / TURNING_POINT v04 — STILL HERE DIFFERENTLY — MID
---
Michael aches at the changed room. Then his daughter comes in, shows him something on her phone—a meme, something he doesn't fully understand. But she wanted to share it. With him. The little girl who asked for bedtime stories is the same person who shares memes now. Different phase. Same daughter. She's still here. Just newer.

## GRIEF / TURNING_POINT v05 — LOSS AND GAIN — LATE
---
Susan's eyes sting at the independence. He didn't need her. Then she notices: he called last week to ask about her day. Not for help. Just to talk. The boy who needed everything is the young man who chooses to call. Different kind of need. Different kind of presence. The stinging doesn't stop. But there's something else too.

## GRIEF / EMBODIMENT v01 — KEEPS THE THING — MID
---
Linda doesn't move the rabbit. It stays in the closet. She visits it sometimes—not often, not morbidly. Just acknowledges it. The girl who loved it existed. The stuffed animal is evidence. She doesn't need to look at it daily. Doesn't need to pretend the phase isn't over. Just keeps it where it is. Memory, housed.

================================================================================
## GRIEF / EMBODIMENT v02 — SAYS IT QUIETLY — LATE
---
David is driving with his son. Somehow it comes out: "I miss carrying you sometimes." His son looks over, half-confused, half-something else. "That's weird, Dad." Then, quieter: "Thanks for all that, though." The acknowledgment is enough. The boy knows he was carried. The man doesn't need to be carried. David said it. Both things are true now.

## GRIEF / EMBODIMENT v03 — MAKES NEW SATURDAY — EARLY
---
Karen can't have cartoon Saturdays back. But she can have something. She asks Jake if he wants to get lunch—just the two of them. He shrugs. "Sure." Not enthusiastic. But he comes. They eat tacos. He shows her a video. It's not 7am pancakes. It's noon tacos with a teenager. Different phase. Still Saturday.

================================================================================
## GRIEF / EMBODIMENT v04 — ENTERS THE NEW ROOM — MID
---
Michael stops lingering in the doorway. Enters his daughter's changed room. Sits on the bed with the new comforter. Asks about the poster—who's the artist? She explains. He doesn't understand all of it, but he's in the room. The old room is gone. This room is here. He can be in the room that exists instead of mourning the one that doesn't.

================================================================================
## GRIEF / EMBODIMENT v05 — LETS HIM LAUNCH — LATE
---
Susan reads the college acceptance. Her son wrote the essays himself. Chose the school himself. Her eyes sting. She doesn't insert herself. Doesn't claim any credit. Just says, "I'm proud of you." He smiles—really smiles. The launching is his. The watching is hers. She can sting and be proud. Both things. The wave and the launch.

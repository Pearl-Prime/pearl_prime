## SHAME / RECOGNITION v01 — YELLED AT TEEN — MID — FACE HEAT
---
Linda yelled. The door slammed and she yelled through it—something she swore she'd never say. Her face heats in the silence after. Her voice is still echoing in her head. That wasn't who she wanted to be. Her daughter is crying behind the door. Linda is standing in the hallway, face burning, knowing she just became the parent she promised herself she wouldn't become.

## SHAME / RECOGNITION v02 — OTHER PARENTS SAW — LATE — CHEST DROP
---
David's son argued with him in the parking lot. Loudly. Other parents watched. Now David is in the car, chest dropped through the floor. They saw. They saw him fail to handle it. His son is sulking in the passenger seat. The argument is over but the witnesses exist. Other families drive away, taking their judgment with them.

## SHAME / RECOGNITION v03 — LOST PATIENCE PUBLICLY — EARLY — SHOULDERS CURLING
---
Karen snapped at Jake in the grocery store. "I said no, stop asking." Her voice carried. A woman in the next aisle looked over. Karen's shoulders curl around the moment. Jake is thirteen—old enough to be embarrassed, young enough that she shouldn't have snapped. He walks ahead of her now, distance deliberate. Her shoulders stay curled past the checkout.

## SHAME / RECOGNITION v04 — FORGOT SOMETHING IMPORTANT — MID — STOMACH SINKING
---
The recital was yesterday. Michael forgot. His daughter performed and he wasn't there. His stomach sinks through her polite "it's fine, Dad." It's not fine. The empty seat where he should have been. She took a bow and looked for him. The sinking continues through dinner, through the evening, through the night. He forgot. She'll remember.

## SHAME / RECOGNITION v05 — SAID THE WRONG THING — LATE — THROAT TIGHT
---
Susan was trying to help. Her son was upset about a breakup. She said "you'll meet someone better"—watched his face close. Wrong thing. Her throat tightens around the well-meaning words that made it worse. He went to his room. She's standing in the kitchen with a tight throat and the knowledge that she made his pain bigger instead of smaller.

## SHAME / MECHANISM_PROOF v01 — REPLAY LOOP — MID
---
It's midnight. Linda is replaying the yelling. The words she used. Her daughter's face. The door slam. She should have stayed calm. She's the adult. The replay runs again. Same words. Same face. Same burning feeling. Two days later, the replay still runs at unexpected moments. Driving. Showering. The yelling lives in a loop now.

## SHAME / MECHANISM_PROOF v02 — MAGNIFICATION — EARLY
---
David's parking lot argument was thirty seconds. In his head, it's been thirty minutes. He can see every face that watched. Every raised eyebrow. Every judgment. The actual moment keeps growing. By evening, half the school saw. By morning, everyone knows he can't control his kid. The argument was small. The shame expanded it.

## SHAME / MECHANISM_PROOF v03 — SAME MOMENT DIFFERENT DAYS — LATE
---
Karen is in a meeting Wednesday when the grocery store snap returns. Jake's face. The woman watching. Her too-loud voice. Thursday it's there while cooking dinner. Friday it interrupts sleep. The moment happened Sunday. It's still happening. Every day finds a new time to deliver the same curl in her shoulders. The snap echoes through the week.

## SHAME / MECHANISM_PROOF v04 — SMALL THING EXPANDS — MID
---
One missed recital. Michael has been present for hundreds of moments. Drove to practices. Sat through games. Helped with homework every week. The one miss is filling the frame. It's blocking the hundreds. His stomach sinks for the one thing, not the hundreds. The small failure has grown larger than the large presence. The math doesn't help.

## SHAME / MECHANISM_PROOF v05 — SELF AS PROBLEM — EARLY
---
Susan can see the pattern now. Wrong thing at the breakup. Wrong thing last month during the test stress. Wrong approach to the friend drama. She's the common denominator. The throat tightens at the accumulation. Three moments become evidence of fundamental failure. She's not just having bad moments. She's a bad-moment parent. The throat knows it.

## SHAME / TURNING_POINT v01 — NAMED INTERNALLY — MID
---
Walking the dog. Linda is mid-replay when she notices: this is shame. Not the yelling—the feeling about the yelling. The endless loop. The heat that returns. She's not processing the yelling anymore. She's marinating in shame about it. Her daughter forgave her the next morning. The shame didn't get the memo. The loop is still running.

## SHAME / TURNING_POINT v02 — THEY DON'T REMEMBER — LATE
---
David braces for the parking lot thing to come up. It's been two weeks. His son mentions needing a ride. Normal request. No reference to the argument. No lasting damage visible. The thing David has carried for fourteen days apparently doesn't exist for his son. The witnesses—whoever they were—are living their lives. David's the only one holding it.

## SHAME / TURNING_POINT v03 — OTHER PARENTS TOO — EARLY
---
Lunch with a friend. Karen mentions the grocery store snap, half-expecting judgment. Her friend laughs—not at her, with her. "I told mine to walk home last week. In front of the entire soccer team." Other parents snap too. Karen's curled shoulders ease slightly. The woman in the next aisle probably has her own moments. Probably doesn't remember Karen's.

## SHAME / TURNING_POINT v04 — APOLOGY LANDED — MID
---
Michael apologized for the missed recital. Meant it. His daughter said, "It's okay, Dad. I know you wanted to be there." The words land in the pit where his stomach sank. She forgave him. Actually forgave him. The sinking hasn't fully stopped. But she's hugging him now. The forgiveness is real. His shame hasn't accepted it yet.

================================================================================
## SHAME / TURNING_POINT v05 — ONE MOMENT, NOT ALL MOMENTS — LATE
---
Susan catches herself mid-thought. "I always say the wrong thing." But that's not true. Last week she said the right thing during the college stress. Last month she knew exactly what he needed to hear after the game. One wrong thing at the breakup. One. Her throat loosens slightly. One isn't always. One is just one.

## SHAME / EMBODIMENT v01 — DOESN'T REPLAY — MID
---
The yelling wants to replay. Linda feels it starting—the words, the face, the door. She redirects. Not suppressing. Just choosing something else to do with her attention. The replay offered itself. She declined. It'll offer again later. She'll decline again. The yelling happened. She apologized. The loop doesn't need another run tonight.

## SHAME / EMBODIMENT v02 — STAYS IN PUBLIC — LATE
---
School event. The parking lot parents might be here. David's chest wants to close. He goes anyway. Sees a few familiar faces. Nods. Nobody mentions the argument. Nobody's eyes hold judgment. He stays for the whole event. The chest tightness was there. He was there too. Both things. The event was fine.

## SHAME / EMBODIMENT v03 — TELLS SOMEONE — EARLY
---
Karen tells her sister about the grocery store snap. The whole thing—the voice, the woman, the curl that's been living in her shoulders. Saying it out loud makes it smaller. Her sister shares one of her own. The telling doesn't erase the moment. It puts it in proportion. Snaps happen. Parents recover. Kids recover. The curl loosens.

================================================================================
## SHAME / EMBODIMENT v04 — SHOWS UP NEXT TIME — MID
---
Another recital. Michael is there early. Front row. His daughter sees him when she walks out. Smiles. He's here this time. Doesn't make up for the miss—nothing will—but he's here now. The sinking hasn't fully stopped. He's in the seat anyway. Watching anyway. Being present doesn't fix the absence. It just starts new.

## SHAME / EMBODIMENT v05 — TRIES AGAIN — LATE
---
Susan's son is upset about something else now. Different problem. She pauses before speaking. Doesn't try to fix it. Just asks what he needs. He talks for twenty minutes. She mostly listens. When she does speak, his face doesn't close. The throat is relaxed. This time she got it closer to right. One time doesn't erase the other. But this time is its own time.

Because you're not trying to heal a narrative.

#### Variant 1

Here's why talking about trauma isn't always healing:

When you talk about a traumatic experience, your body reactivates.

It goes back into the state it was in when the thing happened.

Heart rate up. Stress hormones released. Muscles engaged.

That's not just memory.

**That's re-experiencing.**

And if there's no container — no grounding, no resource, no way to bring yourself back to safety — you just stay activated.

You come out of the conversation more dysregulated than you went in.

That's called re-traumatization.

And it happens way more often than people realize.

---

This isn't to say you should never talk about what happened.

But talking should come after resourcing.

After you've built the ground to stand on.

After your body has some capacity to hold what comes up.

**The order matters.**

Most therapy skips to the talking too fast.

Most TikTok trauma content skips resourcing entirely.

And that's why so many people stay stuck.

Not because they're not trying.

Because they're trying in the wrong order.

---

#### Variant 1

Let me explain trauma in somatic terms.

Peter Levine — the founder of Somatic Experiencing — spent decades studying how animals recover from threat.

Here's what he found:

Animals complete their survival responses.

They run. They fight. They freeze. Then they shake it off. Literally.

And once the shaking is done, they're back to baseline.

No PTSD. No chronic activation. No trauma stored in the body.

Humans have the same capacity.

But we were taught to suppress it.

"Stop shaking."
"Calm down."
"Pull yourself together."

**And so the survival energy stays stuck.**

That's what trauma is.

Not the event.

The uncompleted response to the event.

---

The body is waiting to finish what it started.

To complete the run that never happened.

To express the fight that was never safe to express.

To let the freeze thaw out.

But instead of completing, we analyze.

We talk about what happened.

We try to understand it.

And the body just keeps holding.

**The fix isn't more understanding. It's somatic completion.**

That's a process that happens in the body — not in the story.

---

**IMPORTANT SAFETY NOTE:**

Deep trauma processing should be done with professional support.

The exercises in this chapter are **resourcing tools** — not processing tools.

They build the ground you need before doing deeper work.

If you're dealing with significant trauma, work with a trained somatic therapist.

This book can support that work. It can't replace it.

#### Variant 1

**Building the Container**

Before any trauma work, build the felt sense of safety.

*"We're not going to go into your trauma. We're going to build the ground you'll need to stand on when you're ready to. This is resourcing. It's the step everyone skips."*

---

**5 Senses Grounding**

This practice brings you back to the present moment.

It interrupts activation by anchoring you in what's real and here.

---

Look around wherever you are.

**Name 5 things you can see.**

Not judging. Just naming.

---

**Name 4 things you can feel physically.**

The chair. Your clothes. The temperature. Your feet on the floor.

---

**Name 3 things you can hear.**

Near sounds. Far sounds. Anything.

---

**Name 2 things you can smell.**

Even if it's just the neutrality of air.

---

**Name 1 thing you can taste.**

Whatever's present right now.

---

**Now take a breath.**

You're here.

In this room.

In this moment.

Whatever happened before — it's not happening now.

This is the ground.

**This is where safety lives.**

#### Variant 1

**Nervous System Regulation — Triangle Breathing**

This is the daily practice that expands your window of tolerance over time.

*"Your window of tolerance is how much activation you can handle before you dysregulate. Small window = small triggers set you off. Big window = you can hold more before losing center. This practice makes the window bigger."*

---

**Triangle Breathing — 4-6 seconds**

We're going to breathe in a triangle pattern.

Inhale — Hold — Exhale.

The extended exhale brings you back to parasympathetic.

The hold builds tolerance.

---

**Inhale through your nose for 4 counts... 2, 3, 4.**

**Hold for 4 counts... 2, 3, 4.**

**Exhale slowly for 6 counts... 2, 3, 4, 5, 6.**

---

**Again.**

**Inhale... 2, 3, 4.**

**Hold... 2, 3, 4.**

**Exhale... 2, 3, 4, 5, 6.**

---

**Continue for a few more rounds.**

[Silence for practice — about 60 seconds]

---

This is the practice you do daily.

Not when you're triggered — when you're fine.

It's training for capacity.

So that when activation comes, you have more room to hold it.

#### Variant 1

**Compassionate Witnessing — Monster Compassion Breath**

This is for meeting difficult emotions through the body without drowning in them.

*"This practice comes from a tradition of compassionate witnessing. You're not going to fix anything. You're going to hold what's there — with your breath as the anchor."*

---

**Monster Compassion Breath**

Sometimes emotions feel like monsters.

Big, dark, overwhelming.

The instinct is to run from them.

This practice is about staying present with them — without being consumed.

---

Find a comfortable position.

Close your eyes.

---

Think of something difficult.

Not the hardest thing — something medium.

Something that carries emotional weight.

---

**Notice where you feel it in your body.**

Chest? Belly? Throat?

Don't analyze. Just locate.

---

**Now breathe with it.**

Inhale... toward the sensation.

Exhale... staying present with it.

---

**Inhale... I see you...**

**Exhale... I'm still here...**

---

You're not fixing it.

You're not making it go away.

**You're just staying.**

---

**Continue for a few more minutes.**

[Silence for practice — about 3 minutes]

---

**Gently let it go.**

Breathe normally.

Notice how you feel.

Something shifted? Even a little?

**That's what compassionate witnessing does. It doesn't solve. It holds.**

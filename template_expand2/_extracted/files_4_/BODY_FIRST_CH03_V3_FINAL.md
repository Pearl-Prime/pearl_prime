"I appreciate you thinking of me, but I'm not available for that."

**They're not a communication skill.**

They're a nervous system state.

And if your body is running a survival pattern that says "danger = making someone uncomfortable," no script in the world is going to save you.

You don't need better words.

**You need a body that can tolerate someone else's disappointment.**

#### Variant 1

Here's why the boundary scripts don't work:

They assume you have access to your voice when you're triggered.

You don't.

When someone puts pressure on you — real or perceived — your nervous system runs a threat assessment.

"Are they going to be upset if I say no?"

"Am I safe if they're unhappy?"

"What's the safest thing I can do right now?"

And if your nervous system learned that other people's unhappiness is dangerous — even when it isn't — it will hijack your response.

Your prefrontal cortex (the part that knows the boundary script) goes offline.

Your survival brain takes over.

And your survival brain only knows one move: **make them comfortable.**

This is called the fawn response.

And it's not talked about nearly as much as fight, flight, or freeze.

But if you're someone who can't say no — someone who absorbs other people's emotions, who puts everyone else first, who loses yourself in relationships — fawn is probably running your life.

**And you can't out-script it. You have to out-regulate it.**

#### Variant 1

Let me explain fawn in somatic terms.

Most people know the three trauma responses: fight, flight, freeze.

But there's a fourth one that gets overlooked: **fawn.**

Fawn is the appeasement response.

It says: "I can't fight this. I can't run. I can't shut down. So I'll make myself whatever they need me to be."

For people who grew up in environments where other people's emotions were unpredictable — or dangerous — fawn was the smart move.

It kept you safe.

It kept the peace.

It made you the "easy" one, the "good" one, the one who didn't cause problems.

But here's the cost:

**You lost access to your own needs.**

Because every time you felt a need, you checked: "But what do they need?"

And your need got pushed down.

Over and over.

Until you couldn't even feel it anymore.

---

The good news?

Your body still knows.

Even if your mind has lost the signal, your body remembers what no feels like.

It's in your gut — the tightness when something's wrong.

It's in your throat — the closing when you want to speak but can't.

It's in your shoulders — the bracing when you're about to say yes to something you don't want.

**The first step isn't learning to say no. It's learning to feel no.**

#### Variant 1

**Finding Your Body's No**

Before you can say no, you need to feel it.

*"I want you to think of the last time you said yes when you meant no. Where did you feel it? Your stomach? Your throat? Your jaw? That sensation IS your boundary. You just haven't been listening to it."*

---

**Three-Part Breath / Dirga**

This practice is about filling your whole body with breath.

Not shallow chest breathing — that's what happens when you're stressed.

Full, three-part breathing that takes up space.

Because people-pleasers are trained to take up less space.

This practice reverses that.

---

Find a comfortable position.

Close your eyes if that feels okay.

---

**Part one: Belly**

Place one hand on your belly.

Inhale slowly.

Send the breath all the way down into your belly.

Feel your hand rise as your belly expands.

Exhale.

Feel your hand fall.

Do this three times.

---

**Part two: Ribs**

Now move your hand to your side ribs.

Inhale.

Fill the belly first — then let the breath expand into your ribs.

Feel them spread sideways.

Exhale. Let everything release.

Three times.

---

**Part three: Chest**

Place your hand on your upper chest.

Inhale.

Fill the belly, expand the ribs, then let the breath rise into your chest.

Your whole torso is full.

You're taking up space.

Exhale slowly. Let it all go.

Three times.

---

**Now — all together.**

Inhale: belly, ribs, chest.

One continuous wave of breath.

Taking up as much space as your body can hold.

Exhale: chest, ribs, belly.

Releasing completely.

---

Do this for the next few breaths.

And as you do, notice:

What does your body feel like when it's taking up space?

This is your body remembering what it's like to not shrink.

**This is the foundation of a boundary.**

#### Variant 1

**Grounding Under Pressure**

This practice is for the moment when someone is asking.

When the fawn response is activated.

When your heart is racing and your body wants to say yes just to make the discomfort stop.

*"The extended exhale is your anchor. It keeps your parasympathetic nervous system online so your thinking brain doesn't get hijacked."*

---

**Extended Exhale 4-8**

The rule is simple: exhale longer than you inhale.

We're going to use a 4-8 pattern.

Inhale for 4 counts.

Exhale for 8 counts.

The extended exhale activates your vagus nerve and tells your body: "We're safe. We don't have to fawn right now."

---

Let's practice.

**Inhale through your nose... 2, 3, 4.**

**Exhale slowly through your mouth... 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8.**

---

**Again.**

**Inhale... 2, 3, 4.**

**Exhale... 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8.**

---

**Once more.**

**Inhale... 2, 3, 4.**

**Exhale... 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8.**

---

Now here's how you use this in real life:

When someone asks you for something and you feel that tightening —

Before you answer —

Take one of these breaths.

Just one.

That one breath can be the difference between an automatic yes and a conscious choice.

**It buys you time. And in that time, your real answer might actually surface.**

#### Variant 1

**Voice as Body — Lion's Breath**

People-pleasers lose their voice.

Literally.

The throat locks. The words get stuck. The volume drops.

This practice opens that back up.

*"We're going to reclaim your throat — not with words, but with sound. This might feel ridiculous. That's okay. It's also going to feel freeing."*

---

**Lion's Breath**

This is a yoga practice that releases tension in the throat, jaw, and face.

It looks ridiculous.

It sounds ridiculous.

But it works.

---

Here's how:

Sit comfortably.

Take a deep breath in through your nose.

Open your mouth as wide as it goes.

Stick out your tongue — fully extend it toward your chin.

Exhale forcefully with a "HA" sound.

As loud as you're comfortable with.

---

Let's do it.

**Inhale deeply through your nose.**

**Open wide — tongue out — exhale: HAAAAA.**

---

**Again.**

**Inhale...**

**Open — tongue out — HAAAAA.**

---

**One more.**

**Inhale...**

**HAAAAA.**

---

How does your throat feel now?

More open? Lighter?

You just released tension that's been holding your voice hostage.

**People-pleasers carry so much in their jaw and throat. This practice starts to let it go.**

The more you do it, the easier it becomes to actually speak.

When your throat isn't locked —

**Three-part breath** — teaches your body to take up space again.

**Extended exhale** — keeps you regulated in the moment of pressure.

**Lion's breath** — unlocks the throat that's been holding your voice hostage.

None of these will make you a boundary-setting master overnight.

But they start to change the foundation.

They train your nervous system to tolerate discomfort.

And when your nervous system can tolerate someone else's disappointment —

**You get your no back.**

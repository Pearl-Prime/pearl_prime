#### Variant 1

There's a sentence you've been saying for years.

You probably don't even know you're saying it.

It shows up when you're about to post something real. When you're drafting a text you actually mean. When you're thinking about going to that thing, or saying that thing, or showing up as the version of yourself that isn't performing.

The sentence is:

*"Once I'm not anxious, then I'll do it."*

Or maybe your version sounds like:

*"Once I stop overthinking, I'll post the thing."*  
*"Once I calm down, I'll text them back."*  
*"Once this passes, I'll say what I actually think."*  
*"Once I feel steady, I'll show up as myself."*

Same sentence. Different words.

And here's the thing—

This sentence sounds responsible. It sounds like self-awareness. It sounds like you're taking care of yourself, waiting until you're ready.

But it's not.

It's a trap.

*"Who you are right now isn't allowed to act."*

The rule keeps running. And you keep waiting.

#### Variant 1

Here's what's happening.

Every time you say that sentence—  
*"Once I'm not anxious, then I'll do the thing"*—

You're training your brain to treat anxiety like a stop sign.

You're saying:  
*"This feeling means danger. This feeling means I'm not ready. This feeling means I can't."*

And your brain listens.

Because that's what brains do. They learn.

So the next time you think about doing something real—

Your brain says:  
*"Wait. You felt anxious last time you tried this. That must mean it's not safe."*

And you feel it again.

That tightness. That static. That pull to wait.

Not because the thing itself is dangerous.

But because you've been teaching your brain that *the feeling* is the problem.

---

And here's the worst part—

The more you wait, the louder it gets.

Because your world gets smaller.

The posts you don't share. The conversations you don't have. The places you don't go. The version of yourself you don't let out.

All of it shrinks.

And your brain notices.

It says:  
*"Okay, so we're not doing that anymore. Got it. That must be dangerous."*

So the next time feels harder.

The next thing feels bigger.

The next version of yourself feels further away.

Not because you're broken.

Not because you're getting worse.

But because you've been living by a sentence that sounds like wisdom—

But works like a cage.

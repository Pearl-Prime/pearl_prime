She's sorting mail when a card falls out—handwritten, from an old friend she hasn't heard from in years. The friend describes a moment with him, decades ago, that she'd forgotten entirely. Reading it, something shifts. Not a flood, but a hairline fracture in the glass. A single detail pierces through: the way he laughed at that dinner, head thrown back, completely unguarded. She holds the card for a long time.

She hadn't known there was a name for this, or a timeline, or that others walked through the same museum of their own lives. She asks a question she's never asked anyone: "Did you think something was wrong with you?" The friend shakes her head. She feels seen for the first time in months.

There's something beneath the glass now, faint and shapeless, knocking. She doesn't know what it is. She lies in the dark and lets it knock. She doesn't have to open the door tonight. She writes him a letter she will never send. The words come slowly, clinical at first—I am still here, the house is quiet, I water your tomatoes. Then something loosens.

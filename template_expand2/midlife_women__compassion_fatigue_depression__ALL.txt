## BATCH: compassion_fatigue / overwhelm (20 atoms)

================================================================================
## OVERWHELM / RECOGNITION v01 — EVERYONE NEEDS — HEAVINESS
---
Karen wakes to needs. Her mother needs a call. Her daughter needs advice. Her husband needs attention. Her boss needs the report. The heaviness arrives before coffee. Everyone needs a piece of her. Her capacity to care is somewhere behind the weight.

================================================================================
## OVERWHELM / RECOGNITION v02 — EMOTIONAL QUEUE — BUZZING
---
Margaret's day is stacked with feelings. Mother's loneliness. Daughter's anxiety. Friend's divorce. The buzzing in her head is the emotional load. She can hear all of it. Can't feel any of it clearly.

================================================================================
## OVERWHELM / RECOGNITION v03 — CAREGIVER PILE — FOG
---
Susan is caregiver to three people now. Mother with mobility issues. Father-in-law with memory loss. Husband with depression. A fog settles. With three, she's spreading thin enough to be nearly absent for each.

================================================================================
## OVERWHELM / RECOGNITION v04 — ASKING STACKS — SLOWED MOVEMENT
---
"Mom, can you...?" "Honey, would you...?" "Karen, I need..." The fourth time in an hour. Linda's movements slow before answering. The giving has been continuous. The tank doesn't refill between requests.

================================================================================
## OVERWHELM / RECOGNITION v05 — HOLDING TOO MUCH — PRESSURE BEHIND EYES
---
Patricia holds her mother's fear about her health. Her daughter's worry about her baby. Her sister's grief about her divorce. Each person's feelings enter her system. The pressure builds behind her eyes. Everyone's weight, no one's release.

================================================================================
## OVERWHELM / MECHANISM_PROOF v01-05
---
v01: The pattern runs all week. Karen finishes with mother—daughter calls. Finishes with daughter—husband needs her. By evening, she's been a resource for everyone. Zero for herself.
v02: Margaret carries conversations with her. Mother's loneliness at lunch. Daughter's anxiety through dinner. By night, she's holding feelings that aren't hers.
v03: Susan's care obligations grow. Last year: one parent. This year: three people. The care doesn't become more efficient. It just divides smaller.
v04: "Just a minute" happens twenty times. Linda counts at 6pm. Twenty minutes times twenty requests is six hours of support. Each minute felt small.
v05: Patricia's emotional absorption compounds. Morning: mother's fear. Afternoon: daughter's worry. Evening: sister's grief. Each feeling layers on the last.

================================================================================
## OVERWHELM / TURNING_POINT v01-05
---
v01: Her daughter handles her own problem. One need lifted. Karen's list is still long. But shorter by one.
v02: Her husband takes the call with mother tonight. Margaret doesn't carry that conversation.
v03: Susan hires help for her father-in-law two days a week. One person gets someone else's attention sometimes.
v04: Linda says, "Can we talk at 3?" The person says okay. The tank gets an hour.
v05: Patricia says, "I can't hold that for you right now." The person finds someone else to talk to.

================================================================================
## OVERWHELM / EMBODIMENT v01-05
---
v01: Four people need things. Karen picks one. Helps her mother. Just that. Others wait.
v02: Margaret schedules gaps between emotional conversations. The feelings will still be heavy. There'll be space.
v03: Susan takes Sunday off from caregiving. Her husband covers. One day without the pile.
v04: Linda closes her door for 30 minutes. No "do you have a minute." The tank gets a rest.
v05: Patricia goes for a walk alone. Fifteen minutes of not holding anyone's feelings.


## BATCH: compassion_fatigue / watcher (20 atoms)

================================================================================
## WATCHER / RECOGNITION v01 — MOTOR AUTOPILOT
---
Karen is listening to her mother's worries before she registers the call started. Her voice makes appropriate sounds. "Mmhmm." "That sounds hard." She's somewhere behind her ears, watching a daughter perform compassion. The call happens. She's technically there.

================================================================================
## WATCHER / RECOGNITION v02 — DELAYED CARING
---
Margaret hears her daughter's news—the anxiety is back. She says supportive things. The caring arrives three seconds later, muted. By then she's already moved to solutions. The emotion and the response are out of sync.

================================================================================
## WATCHER / RECOGNITION v03 — CAREGIVER MONTAGE
---
Susan sees herself in fragments. Hand on mother's shoulder. Face concerned with father-in-law. Voice warm with husband. The in-between is gone. She's a montage of supportive gestures, not a continuous person caring.

================================================================================
## WATCHER / RECOGNITION v04 — GOING THROUGH MOTIONS
---
Linda asks the right questions. "How are you feeling?" "What would help?" Her voice knows the script. She watches herself perform a caring person. They seem helped. She's not inside any of it.

================================================================================
## WATCHER / RECOGNITION v05 — SENSORY MUTING
---
Patricia sits with her crying sister. The tears are visible—theoretically. She's watching them from behind glass. Her hand reaches out. The touch happens. The moment is important. She's receiving it on delay.

================================================================================
## WATCHER / MECHANISM_PROOF v01-05
---
v01: The pattern runs all week. Monday call: autopilot. Tuesday crisis: autopilot. Wednesday check-in: autopilot. Karen is present for all of it. Inside none of it.
v02: Good news: daughter feeling better. Bad news: mother's health declined. Both arrive at the same muted frequency. Margaret is present for both. Touched by neither.
v03: Susan has the caregiver conversations. "I'm here for you." "We'll figure this out." The words come from somewhere trained. The feeling doesn't.
v04: Linda's caring has been distant for weeks. Same conversations. Same supportive words. The connection that used to happen doesn't.
v05: Patricia has sat with three crying people this month. Each time, the right responses emerged. Each time, the tears landed far away.

================================================================================
## WATCHER / TURNING_POINT v01-05
---
v01: Karen is on the call when something shifts. Her mother laughs at something—real, surprised. Karen laughs too, before she can observe it. For three seconds, the glass isn't there.
v02: "Mom." Her daughter says it twice. Then something catches. She's Mom. That's her. She's in this conversation.
v03: Susan hugs her mother and for a moment, feels the frailness. Not as information—as sensation. Present tense caring.
v04: Linda laughs before she can stop it. Something her friend said—actually funny. The laugh comes from her chest.
v05: The sister mentions their grandmother. Something about the specific memory breaks through Patricia's glass. She feels it. Present tense.

================================================================================
## WATCHER / EMBODIMENT v01-05
---
v01: Karen notices the drift starting. Doesn't follow. Keeps attention on her mother's actual words.
v02: Daughter's news. Margaret feels the drift. Doesn't let it. Stays in the feeling, whatever it is.
v03: Susan catches the drift. Comes back. Hand on her mother's arm. Really feeling the skin.
v04: Linda is about to go automatic. Stops. Takes a breath. Asks something real. "What's the hardest part right now?"
v05: Patricia is finishing the conversation. Catches the drift. Stops. Looks at her sister's face. "I really do love you." From here.


## BATCH: compassion_fatigue / grief (20 atoms)

================================================================================
## GRIEF / RECOGNITION v01 — THE CARING THAT'S GONE — THROAT TIGHT
---
Karen remembers how she used to feel in conversations with her mother. Present. Patient. Actually curious about her day. Now she's performing the routine. Her throat tightens thinking about three years ago. That daughter isn't in this kitchen anymore.

================================================================================
## GRIEF / RECOGNITION v02 — PATIENCE THAT LEFT — HOLLOWNESS
---
Margaret used to have patience for her daughter's anxiety. The same reassurance needed twice: no problem. The slow processing: fine. A hollowness opens where the patience used to be. She can see where it was.

================================================================================
## GRIEF / RECOGNITION v03 — JOY GONE — HEAVINESS
---
Susan finds an old photo—she's laughing with her mother, genuinely enjoying the visit. The heaviness fills her chest. That daughter existed. The visits now are duty. She hasn't felt that joy in a year.

================================================================================
## GRIEF / RECOGNITION v04 — PRESENCE MISSING — CHEST ACHE
---
Linda watches herself in family gatherings. Physically present. The actual being-there that used to be natural is missing. Her chest aches for the version of herself who could really be with people.

================================================================================
## GRIEF / RECOGNITION v05 — TENDERNESS ABSENT — EYES STINGING
---
Patricia used to feel tenderness when people she loved struggled. Soft, genuine concern. Now she feels... management. The tenderness is absent. Her eyes sting missing the person who used to care that way.

================================================================================
## GRIEF / MECHANISM_PROOF v01-05
---
v01: The waves come when Karen expects them and when she doesn't. Old voicemail from mother—her own patient voice: expected wave. Random moment feeling irritated at her mother's call: unexpected.
v02: Margaret finds the lost patience in ordinary places. The spot on the couch where she used to sit for the long conversations. The patience isn't there anymore.
v03: Susan finds the missing joy through different doors. Monday: old photo. Tuesday: someone asking "remember when you loved visiting your mom?"
v04: Linda forgets, then remembers. She starts to really engage with someone, then remembers she doesn't do that anymore. The forgetting is brief.
v05: Patricia is fine managing the crisis. Fine being supportive. Then she finds a card she wrote five years ago—so tender—and the wave arrives.

================================================================================
## GRIEF / TURNING_POINT v01-05
---
v01: Karen finds the old voicemail. Braces for collapse. The wave comes—she sees her former patience. But she doesn't drown. She can hold the grief.
v02: Margaret braces for the patience memory. The wave comes—but there's warmth. Those conversations were real. She was really present.
v03: Susan reads the old joy. She felt that. The visits were genuinely good. Just because it's gone doesn't mean it wasn't real.
v04: In one conversation, something flickers. Linda is actually interested in what someone is saying. The presence isn't fully gone.
v05: A family member struggles. For a moment, Patricia feels tender. The caring self isn't dead. Just very far away.

================================================================================
## GRIEF / EMBODIMENT v01-05
---
v01: Karen tells her husband. "I used to care more about these conversations. I don't know where that went." The loss is named.
v02: Margaret doesn't delete the old texts where she was patient. They're proof. That version existed.
v03: Susan looks at the joyful photos sometimes. Not to feel bad. To remember. She was that daughter.
v04: Linda clears one afternoon. Space for presence to maybe return.
v05: The tenderness flickered. Patricia follows it. Stays in the caring moment. Small. But present.

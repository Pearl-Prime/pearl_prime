# Comedy / Gag Manga — Style Bible

> Absorbed from PR #295 branch (`claude/manga-12ep-arc-authoring-egnwqf` @ `4c6e2c3d59`), reworked per operator ruling Q-MPU-02 (2026-07-24): #295 itself is never merged; this lane's PR is the landing path.

Touchstones: *Gintama* (Sorachi Hideaki), *Nichijou* (Arawi Keiichi), *Azumanga Daioh* (Azuma Kiyohiko), *Daily Lives of High School Boys* (Yasunobu Yamauchi), *Cromartie High School* (Nonaka Eiji), *Grand Blue* (Inoue Kenji / Yoshioka Kimitake).

## 1. Market Contract

Readers arrive for pure comedic release — the specific pleasure of a panel timed so well it produces an audible reaction, delivered by a cast whose fixed comedic roles the reader can predict and still be surprised by. Unlike drama-adjacent genres, comedy manga's contract does not require emotional investment as an entry fee: a reader can drop in mid-series and still land the joke, because the engine (a boke/tsukkomi dynamic, an escalating absurd premise, a deadpan ensemble) resets every beat rather than accumulating unresolved stakes. The unspoken promise: nothing here will ask more of you than your attention for the length of the gag. Readers will accept: wild tonal whiplash within a single page, characters who are "flat" by drama standards but precisely calibrated for their comedic function, fourth-wall breaks, and running gags that pay off chapters or volumes later. They will reject: a joke that explains itself, comedic beats that arrive with no setup, and a cast so chaotic that no one anchors the reader's sense of what's actually being reacted to. The genre's contract is: *I will make you laugh on a schedule you can't quite predict, using people you already know.*

## 2. Visual Rules

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Panels per page | Format-dependent — yonkoma (four-panel strip) format runs a strict 4-panel vertical stack, 2–3 strips per page; standard gag-manga grid format runs 6–9 panels per page, denser than drama genres, to deliver rapid beat-to-beat comedic timing |
| Words per page | 30–90 (canonical pacing range) |
| Words per balloon (max) | 12 |
| Dialogue:caption ratio | Dialogue-dominant; narration/caption is minimal — comedy trusts the panel and the exchange, not narrated exposition |
| Silent-panel ratio | 5–20% (canonical range; the beat-pause panel before a punchline is this genre's load-bearing silent panel — sparse but precisely placed) |
| Reaction-shot frequency | Very high — the reaction *is* frequently the joke, not just a response to it |
| Spread frequency | Rare — comedy spends spectacle sparingly; a spread used for a gag reads as a deliberate, self-aware escalation, not a genre default |
| Narration tolerance | Minimal |
| SFX usage | Heavy comedic |
| Background density | Minimal — backgrounds simplify or vanish entirely behind a character mid-gag (the genre's signature "blank void" reaction panel) |
| Screentone | Flat/minimal on standard panels; halftone "shock lines" radiating from a stunned face; solid black silhouette for pratfall/collapse beats; the deliberate art-style break (chibi, super-deformed, or sudden hyper-detailed "serious" rendering) is itself a screentone-adjacent comedic device |
| Line weight | Uniform, clean line for the "default" cast register; a punchline panel may deliberately break line-weight consistency — thicker, cruder, or chibi-simplified lines signal "this panel is the joke," a visual tell readers learn to anticipate |

Cover palette references: Primary-pop registers (saturated single-accent color blocks — orange/black *Gintama*-adjacent, or flat pastel *Nichijou*-adjacent) rather than the moody gradients of drama genres; comedy covers favor a single character mid-expression over an ensemble tableau, signaling "this book is funny" before the title is even read.

SFX vocabulary: ガーン (shock/dismay, often accompanying a visible vertical-line effect), ズコー (comedic pratfall/face-plant collapse — the physical punctuation of a failed straight-man correction), ドン引き (visible recoil/uncomfortable silence from onlookers), パチパチ (blank, uncomprehending blink — beat before a tsukkomi line lands), しーん (crickets; total dead silence, rendered as onomatopoeia rather than a truly blank panel), ビシッ (a sudden dramatic point/pose struck for comedic emphasis, often undercutting itself), ガクッ (sudden physical deflation/slump on a joke landing badly on purpose). Punchline panels frequently pair a minimal-word balloon with a large, standalone SFX character taking up disproportionate panel space — the SFX itself becomes the joke's visual payload.

## 3. Pacing

**Gag-unit structure (page/scene level):**
1. Setup — a boke delivers a claim, plan, or observation that is subtly (or wildly) off; played completely straight
2. Escalation — the flawed premise is taken further, either by the boke doubling down or by a second character taking it seriously
3. Beat pause — one short silent or near-silent panel; the "ma" (間) — the timing gap that lets the reader's own recognition catch up before the punchline lands
4. Punchline / tsukkomi — a straight-man correction, physical reaction (often a ズコー collapse), or a sudden reversal that resolves the gag
5. Reset or callback — the scene either resets to baseline (episodic gag) or plants a beat that a later chapter will pay off as a running gag

**Per-volume arc shape:** Unlike escalating-stakes genres, comedy volumes are episodic-cumulative rather than arc-driven: each chapter is largely self-contained, but running gags, cast dynamics, and a slowly deepening sense of the ensemble's relationships accumulate across the volume without requiring plot resolution. A volume's "shape" is closer to a comedy album's track sequencing than a story arc — vary gag *type* (verbal, physical, structural/meta) chapter to chapter so the reading rhythm doesn't flatten.

Chapter hook family: *premise-drop* — open directly on the absurd claim, plan, or situation with minimal establishing context; comedy earns the right to skip scene-setting because the payoff arrives fast.

Chapter ending convention (canonical page-turn triggers): `absurd_escalation` (the gag's premise is pushed one level further than the reader expected, ending on the escalation itself rather than its resolution), `anti_climax` (a buildup that deliberately resolves as nothing — the anticlimax IS the punchline), `rapid_reversal` (a sudden status/expectation flip in the final panel — the boke turns out to have been right, or the tsukkomi turns out to be the actual fool). A chapter should land on exactly one of these, and different chapters in sequence should not repeat the same trigger back to back.

**Format note:** yonkoma (four-panel strip) format compresses this entire five-beat structure into panel 1 (setup) → panel 2 (escalation) → panel 3 (beat pause, often silent) → panel 4 (punchline), one strip per gag, with no reset/callback beat available inside the strip itself — callbacks in yonkoma-format series operate across strips, not within one.

## 4. Dialogue

**Register:** The boke/tsukkomi (manzai-derived) double act is the genre's core dialogue engine. The *boke* ("fool"/airhead role) states something false, absurd, or off-kilter while playing it completely straight — no self-awareness, no wink to the reader. The *tsukkomi* ("straight man"/retorter role) corrects, redirects, or physically reacts to the boke's statement, and that correction supplies the comedic tempo that makes room for the next joke. Strong ensembles (*Gintama* is the canonical example) rotate who plays which role scene to scene rather than fixing it permanently — a character who is the tsukkomi in one exchange can be the boke in the next, which keeps a large cast from calcifying into single-joke characters.

**Key dialogue patterns:**
1. **The Deadpan Escalation** — a character states an increasingly absurd claim in the same flat register throughout, never modulating tone to signal "this is a joke"; the comedy is entirely in the gap between delivery and content (*Nichijou*'s signature mode).
2. **The Tsukkomi Correction** — a short, sharp interjection ("Oi." / "That's not— wait, why—") that names exactly what's wrong with what was just said or done; length is the joke's enemy here — the shorter the correction, the sharper the beat.
3. **The Running-Gag Callback** — a phrase, prop, or behavior from an earlier chapter recurs verbatim or with a small variation; the payoff is recognition, not novelty, so the callback must be reproduced precisely enough to be recognizable.
4. **The Fourth-Wall Aside** — a character directly acknowledges the manga's own conventions, genre expectations, or (in extreme cases) the reader/publication itself (*Gintama*'s signature device); use sparingly enough that it remains a surprise device rather than a structural crutch.
5. **The Straight-Man Anchor Line** — in ensemble-chaos scenes, one character's single grounded observation ("...are we seriously doing this?") gives the reader a fixed point inside the escalation; without this anchor, large-cast chaos scenes can read as noise rather than comedy.

## 5. Character

**Cast structure:** Comedy manga casts are built around *fixed comedic function* more than psychological arc — each character has a legible "type" (the airhead, the deadpan anchor, the schemer, the delinquent-who's-secretly-soft) that the reader can predict, which is precisely what makes deviation from type land as a joke. This is not a failure of characterization; it is the genre's version of character design, parallel to how a sports manga fixes each teammate's speech register (see `sports_competition.md` §4) but tuned for comedic payoff rather than dramatic distinction.

- **The boke (fool/straight-faced absurdist)** — commits fully and sincerely to whatever flawed premise the gag requires; never plays for the joke internally.
- **The tsukkomi (retorter/straight man)** — the reader's proxy inside the scene; their reactions calibrate how absurd the reader is meant to find what just happened.
- **The floating role-swapper** — in ensemble comedies, at least one character (often the nominal protagonist) must be capable of playing either role depending on scene, preventing the cast from calcifying.
- **The escalator** — a character whose function is specifically to take someone else's bad idea and make it structurally worse (recruiting others, formalizing the absurd plan, raising the stakes of the bit itself).
- **The bystander/normal person** — present in most successful ensembles as an audience surrogate who reacts to the chaos as chaos, preventing the whole cast from reading as uniformly unhinged.

**Antagonist note:** Comedy manga rarely requires a true antagonist; conflict is generated by premise and personality friction rather than opposition. Where a rival or authority figure appears (a teacher, a rival group, a delinquent gang as in *Cromartie High School*), they should be absorbed into the gag ecosystem — playing boke or tsukkomi to the main cast — rather than staged as genuine narrative opposition.

## 6. Failure Modes

1. **The joke explains itself.** A character stating why something is funny, or a caption underlining the punchline, kills the beat. Trust the panel and the timing.
2. **No anchor in ensemble chaos.** Without at least one grounded reaction (the bystander or a reliable tsukkomi), a large-cast gag scene reads as noise instead of comedy.
3. **Escalation without a reset.** A gag that keeps escalating past its natural ceiling without ever landing a punchline or beat-pause exhausts the reader instead of building momentum.
4. **Type-locked cast with zero deviation.** If the boke is never once right and the tsukkomi never once wrong, the cast calcifies into a joke-delivery machine rather than a comedic ensemble — the best gag casts occasionally invert expectation.
5. **Running gags that mutate instead of recur.** A callback only pays off if it is recognizable; over-varying a running gag each time it appears erodes the recognition payoff that makes callbacks work.
6. **Overusing the fourth-wall break.** A device this strong loses its comedic voltage fast; reserve it for genuine surprise moments, not a default punchline tool.
7. **Uniform panel/line-weight register.** If punchline panels are drawn identically to setup panels, the reader loses the visual cue that a beat has landed; the genre depends on a legible shift (chibi, blank background, line-weight break) to mark the joke.
8. **Sincerity deployed without recalibration.** A genuinely emotional beat inside a gag-format series (Gintama's occasional serious arcs are the model) can work, but only if the tonal shift is deliberate and clearly bounded — bleeding sincerity into the gag register without a clear switch confuses the reader about which mode they're in.

## 7. 48-Volume Shape

Comedy's macro-structure is cumulative and cast-driven rather than escalating-stakes, but a 48-volume run still needs shape to sustain a long readership.

**Volumes 1–12 — Formula Establishment.** Core cast and their comedic roles (boke, tsukkomi, escalator, anchor) are fixed and legible by volume 2. Gag-of-the-week structure dominates; the reader learns the cast's rhythms. First running gags planted, not yet paid off. New side characters introduced with immediately legible comedic function.

**Volumes 13–24 — Running-Gag Accumulation.** Callbacks from Arc 1 begin recurring with payoff; the ensemble's dynamics deepen (relationships implied through repeated bit-partnership, never through direct emotional declaration). Cast expands further; role-swapping (a boke playing tsukkomi, and vice versa) becomes more frequent as the reader's familiarity with the cast allows more sophisticated inversion. Optionally, one or two chapters interrupt the gag register with a grounded, lower-comedy beat (the *Gintama* pattern) — sparingly, and clearly bounded.

**Volumes 25–36 — Format Play.** With cast and running gags fully established, the series can afford structural experimentation: meta-humor about its own conventions, art-style breaks used more boldly, parody chapters, and fourth-wall material used more confidently because the reader now has enough history with the cast to catch layered references. This band is where a comedy series differentiates itself from its own early volumes.

**Volumes 37–48 — Legacy Payoff.** Long-dormant running gags from volumes 1–12 resurface and pay off with maximum recognition value. The cast, now deeply familiar, can support a higher ratio of subversion (roles inverting, gags landing precisely *because* the reader expects the old pattern). Many long-running comedy series close with a single genuine emotional or dramatic arc as a capstone (again, the *Gintama* model) — if used, it should be clearly the exception that proves how much affection the gag-format built for the cast, not a genre pivot.

## 8. Panel Scaffolding

Per-panel fields (9):

1. `framing` — ELS / LS / MS / MCU / CU / insert / blank-void-reaction / chibi-insert
2. `beat_role` — one of {setup, escalation, beat_pause, punchline, tsukkomi_correction, callback, anti_climax, fourth_wall_aside, group_reaction}
3. `gag_type` — one of {verbal, physical, structural_meta, running_gag, parody}
4. `character_role_in_gag` — one of {boke, tsukkomi, escalator, bystander_anchor, floating_swap}; drives which character's panel gets the reaction-shot priority (§2 — `very_high` reaction-shot frequency)
5. `callback_flag` — boolean; true if this panel references a prior chapter's gag verbatim or near-verbatim; callback panels should visually echo the framing of their original occurrence where possible
6. `art_style_break` — one of {none, chibi, super_deformed, sudden_hyper_detail, line_weight_crude}; marks the panel as the joke's visual payload; must appear on punchline/tsukkomi_correction beats at meaningfully higher frequency than on setup/escalation beats
7. `sfx` — from comedic vocabulary (ガーン, ズコー, しーん, etc.) or null
8. `dialogue` — ≤12 words per balloon (canonical `words_per_balloon_max`); tsukkomi_correction lines should trend toward the short end (≤6 words) for maximum sharpness
9. `silence_flag` — boolean; true on beat_pause panels specifically (the "ma" timing gap); silent panels should stay within the canonical 5–20% range and cluster immediately before punchline panels rather than spreading evenly through a chapter

Constraint hint: `beat_role=punchline` panels must be immediately preceded by either a `beat_role=beat_pause` panel (`silence_flag=true`) or a `beat_role=escalation` panel — never by another punchline, which flattens comedic rhythm. `callback_flag=true` panels require the referenced original chapter/panel to be identifiable in scripting metadata so the visual echo can be verified downstream.

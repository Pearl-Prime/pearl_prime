# Historical / Period Drama — Manga Style Bible

Touchstones: *Vinland Saga* (Yukimura Makoto), *Vagabond* (Inoue Takehiko), *Bride's Story* (Mori Kaoru), *Cesare* (Souryo Fuyumi), *Innocent* (Sakamoto Ryoma), *The Rose of Versailles* (Ikeda Riyoko), *Drifters* (Hirano Kouta).

## 1. Market Contract

Readers are adults 20–45 (seinen primary; older josei crossover) who show up for **grief across time, legacy and self-worth, courage in context, and the tension between honor and authenticity**. They want the past to feel inevitable and yet alive — a world that has its own gravity. They will reject: dialogue that sounds like a contemporary Twitter thread; history-as-costume (modern psychology in period drag); and resolution that erases the weight of what was lost. Comfort is available only in earned form. The historical genre's fundamental promise is: *this world was real, these stakes were real, and they mattered*.

Viking is a setting flavor inside this genre — not a genre unto itself. *Vinland Saga* is classified as "Historical epic / Viking-era action-drama" (cover_design/09:321). Other first-class settings: Edo, Tang dynasty, Roman Republic/Empire, Heian, Meiji, Cold War. The genre contract holds across all of them.

## 2. Visual Rules

| Metric | Value | Note |
|---|---|---|
| Panels per page | 4–7 | Denser than iyashikei; allows environmental panels within flow |
| Words per page | 50–100 | Heavier than iyashikei; lighter than shojo interior monologue |
| Dialogue : caption ratio | 50 : 50 | Narration carries historical context; dialogue carries character |
| Pages per chapter | 24–32 | Seinen/josei monthly standard |
| Black-fill ratio | 0.20–0.35 | Heavier than iyashikei (0.10–0.20); conveys era weight and shadow |
| Silent panel ratio | 20–35% | Matches seinen baseline |
| Screentone density | Restrained | Used for shadow and atmosphere; never ornamental sparkle |
| Environmental detail | High | Background is as important as character — *Vinland Saga* model |
| Line weight | Varied | Heavy on armor/weapons/architecture; lighter on faces; Inoue brushwork model |
| Spread frequency | 1–2 per volume | Reserved for era-defining moments: a battle's aftermath, a city first seen |

**Cover and typography signal:** Environmental composition with character small-in-frame signals literary ambition and is the correct historical-genre convention — not a rule violation. Low-saturation palette (steel-blue-grey, muted earth-brown, near-black) is the genre's default "serious/adult/literary" signal. Typography: Zen Kaku Gothic New 900 + Cinzel or Barlow Condensed; metallic silver / gunmetal treatment. For Japanese body text in literary register, Shippori Mincho or Source Han Serif JP is preferred over gothic for historical volumes.

## 3. Pacing

**Era-establishing opening (chapters 1–3):** Three to five silent environmental panels before any character appears — landscape, season, a tool or weapon — then locate the protagonist inside the world. The hook is *invitation to an era*, not a question. Ground the reader in sensory/material reality before politics or backstory.

**Political/social conflict layer (chapter 4–volume arc midpoint):** Faction, law, religion, or class enters as the structural antagonist. Escalation is societal-scale before it is personal. Individual conflicts must be shown as expressions of era-forces, not merely character flaw.

**Personal arc (midpoint–volume end):** The protagonist's interior stakes surface in full. The political layer does not resolve; the personal layer shifts the character's *position inside* that layer. Resolution is relational and earned, not systemic.

**Time-as-character:** Every volume must carry a seasonal or calendrical rhythm visible in the panels. Weather, agricultural cycles, festival dates, military campaign seasons. The era breathes. Chapters without a temporal anchor feel like costume drama.

**Per-volume = a season of history.** Each volume is one season of one campaign, one year at a court, one migration leg. Readers can place themselves in the calendar.

## 4. Dialogue and Narration

**Register:** Era-appropriate but readable. Archaic surface — period honorifics, formal address — without impenetrable vocabulary. The Vinland Saga principle: Norse characters speak with authority and gravity, not in unreadable mock-Old-English. Intelligibility is non-negotiable.

**Honorific patterns (Japanese settings):** Court and official contexts use full formal register (**-dono**, **-sama**, **-daimyo**, **-heika**). Military/peer contexts may drop to surname only or functional title. A character using a superior's given name without honorific is a plot beat of transgression, intimacy, or grief.

**Political and philosophical dialogue is primary action.** An argument over whether to break a treaty, an interrogation about loyalty, a debate on the meaning of conquest — these ARE the fight scenes of the historical genre. They must be written with the same structural tension as a sword duel: advance, parry, exposed position, decision.

**Narration ratio is high.** Caption boxes carry historical context, era-specific vocabulary definitions, and the weight of retrospect. A narrator who speaks from temporal distance ("In the forty-third year of the Eiroku era, no one yet knew what that morning would cost them") is genre-correct and expected. Narration boxes should be visually distinct — rectangular, slightly formal, separated from the atmospheric float of internal monologue.

**Internal monologue:** Handled via environmental correlative as the first choice; explicit only when the character's interiority *diverges* from visible action. A general who smiles while his thoughts are "I have just signed my own execution" — the gap is the story.

**Pronoun patterns:** **Watashi** default for formal/adult contexts. **Ore** for rough soldiers and outsiders. **Washi** for elderly authority figures. Military characters may use **jibun** as a disciplined self-effacement. Pronoun shifts across volumes signal status change.

## 5. Character

**Protagonist archetype — three valid entry points:**
1. **Outsider** — enters the historical world from its margins (a foreign slave, a provincial samurai, a translator) and sees it with necessary estrangement. *Thorfinn* model.
2. **Heir** — born into the center of the era's power and must choose what to do with it. *Cesare* model; *Rose of Versailles* model.
3. **Witness** — a figure positioned to observe events they cannot control. Their arc is understanding, not mastery. Best for tragedy-structured stories.

**Supporting cast:** Family unit (living or lost), faction peers (who define social belonging), and at least one historical figure whose documented fate creates dramatic irony for the reader. The reader knows Nobunaga dies; the character does not. That gap is a genre instrument.

**Antagonist:** The societal force is the primary antagonist more often than an individual. Empire, feudalism, religious law, epidemic, famine, or a class system that grinds people into shapes they did not choose. Individual antagonists who embody the era-force are more effective than personal villains who happen to live in period costume.

**Grief across time** is the emotional load that distinguishes historical from adventure. Every major arc should carry at least one grief that cannot be healed because the person or world is simply gone. Resolution is learning to carry it, not removing it.

## 6. Failure Modes

1. **Anachronistic dialogue.** A samurai saying "to be fair..." or a Roman soldier using modern therapeutic vocabulary. Read each line aloud and ask whether it could have been said by someone formed entirely by that era.
2. **Over-authentic obscurity.** Excessive jargon, untranslated titles, and insider historical knowledge that requires footnotes to follow. *Bride's Story* handles textile and material culture with visual specificity, not vocabulary gates.
3. **Westerners-as-villains / Orient-as-backdrop.** Setting Europe or America as the villain of an Asian historical story, or vice versa, without structural complexity reduces history to propaganda. Every faction must have comprehensible reasons.
4. **History as costume.** The characters speak and think like contemporary people who happen to be wearing period clothes. The era must shape their assumptions, their silences, their concept of the self.
5. **Violence as spectacle without aftermath.** Battles must have consequences — bodies, grief, logistical ruin — not just action-movie choreography.
6. **Rushing the political layer.** Introducing the personal arc before the era-force is established leaves the protagonist's stakes weightless.
7. **Resolving the era.** The historical world does not fix itself. The series ends; the world does not. Endings that imply the protagonist's arc "changed history" must be earned across many volumes.

## 7. 48-Volume Shape

Historical / Period Drama is architecturally suited to multi-era sagas. Suggested shapes:

**Three-era saga (recommended for flagship 48-volume build):**
- **Volumes 1–14:** Era One — the founding conflict. Protagonist enters, era-force established, first faction war, personal grief anchor set. Ends on a historical rupture (a defeat, an exile, a coronation that changes everything).
- **Volumes 15–30:** Era Two — the consequences. A generation later, or the same protagonist older. The era-force has transformed but not disappeared. Political complexity deepens. New outsider/heir enters as viewpoint refresh.
- **Volumes 31–44:** Era Three — the reckoning. Convergence of consequences from Eras One and Two. The time-as-character element is now explicit: characters quote the dead, revisit ruins, and must decide what the past means.
- **Volumes 45–48:** Coda. A final witness arc. Small-scale, intimate. The era recedes; ordinary life resumes or does not. The grief is acknowledged and named.

**Alternative: single-protagonist deep-time arc** (Vagabond model) — one character across ~40 volumes, each 10-volume block a different decade of their life. Era-force is constant (civil war aftermath, religious and political destabilization); the character's relationship to it changes.

Per-volume = one season of one campaign. Per 10–15 volumes = one era-arc. The series is capable of carrying a multi-century scope if the narrative torch is passed between characters across era-arcs.

## 8. Panel Scaffolding

Per-panel fields (9):

1. `framing` — ELS / LS / MS / MCU / CU / insert / environmental-establishing
2. `beat_role` — one of {era_hook, political_advance, personal_reveal, environmental_breath, grief_beat, confrontation, aftermath, silent_witness, closure}
3. `era_marker` — visible temporal signal in panel (season indicator, calendar object, era flag/sigil, known historical artifact), or null
4. `costume_detail` — specific period-accurate costume element called out in panel description (material, insignia, damage state), or null
5. `geography_setting` — named or typed location (e.g., "Jutland coast, winter", "Tang capital outer gate", "Edo castle lower corridor"), or null
6. `formality_register` — one of {court_formal, military_peer, intimate, outsider_low, internal_only} — governs dialogue and caption register for this panel
7. `weapon_or_tool` — period-specific object present in frame (sword, plow, printing press, longship oar), or null; must be era-accurate
8. `dialogue` — ≤20 words; era-register enforced; or null
9. `caption` — narration-box text ≤25 words; retrospective or contextual; rectangular formal box; or null

Constraint hints: `era_marker` must appear on ≥1 panel per page-spread; `formality_register` must be consistent within a scene and can only shift at scene boundary; `beat_role=environmental_breath` or `silent_witness` must appear on ≥20% of panels.

## 9. Locale Weighting

| Market | Priority | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **ja_JP** | Primary | Historical genre is core domestic register; Afternoon/Big Comic/Shogakukan literary seinen tradition; serialization home |
| **en_US** | High — seinen import | *Vinland Saga* 2.5M+ US units; established reader base for literary historical manga; Kodansha USA pipeline |
| **zh_TW** | Cultivation/historical crossover | Strong historical fiction culture; cultivation/wuxia adjacency creates pre-warmed genre appetite |
| **zh_CN** | Secondary (gray-zone per D-19) | Historical-fiction genre is one of the dominant domestic categories; AI disclosure required |
| **ko_KR** | Adjacent (rendered + held per D-18) | Sageuk (historical drama) webtoon tradition is native; Korean readers have strong genre literacy for period drama |
| **fr_FR** | Viking-setting specific | French bande dessinée tradition includes Viking historical epic as an established category; *Vinland Saga* specifically indexed to French market via Viking-setting flavor |

Typography locale notes: ja_JP literary register uses Shippori Mincho or Source Han Serif JP. zh_TW: Source Han Serif TC. ko_KR literary: Nanum Myeongjo. fr_FR: Cinzel + Montserrat — Montserrat explicitly listed as "French market versatile" (typography/04:1825).

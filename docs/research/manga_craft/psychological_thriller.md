# Psychological Thriller — Manga Craft Bible

Touchstones: *Death Note* (Ohba/Obata), *Monster* (Urasawa Naoki), *Mob Psycho 100* (ONE), *20th Century Boys* (Urasawa), *Liar Game* (Kaitani Shinobu), *Doubt* (Yoshiki Tonogai).

## 1. Market contract

Readers are seinen and josei adults, 25–45, skewing toward the tech-worker and analyst archetype — people who already live inside their own heads and will recognize over-analysis as self-portrait, not caricature. They are showing up for *the gap between what is perceived and what is real*: paranoia dressed as competence, imposter syndrome dressed as investigation, obsession loops dressed as deduction. They will pay fourteen chapters of rising dread for one panel where the protagonist discovers the threat was partly themselves all along.

They will reject: supernatural entities as the primary threat source (that is psychological *horror*, a different contract); enemies who are simply evil without internal logic; protagonists who are passive recipients of plot; reassuring resolutions that confirm all perceptions were accurate. The genre promise is: **reality is a construction, and yours is already slightly wrong.** Deliver that or lose the reader.

Reference titles from `docs/CJK_CATALOG_PLAN.md` §5: `cogclar_jp_07` through `cogclar_jp_11` live inside this shell — securities analyst, screenwriter, gaslighted salaryman, perfect-memory sufferer, IFS negotiator. The craft rules below govern all five.

**Spread rule:** One spread per 2–3 chapters maximum. Spreads reserved for the *revelation that reframes*: the moment the protagonist (and reader) sees that what they believed was systematically wrong. Never used for action or chase sequences.

## 3. Pacing

**Chapter hook family:** *Assumption planted.* Open with the protagonist's confident internal read of a situation — presented straight, no ironic distance yet. The reader accepts it. The hook is the not-yet-visible gap between that interpretation and what the art slightly suggests. Every chapter opens inside a possibly-wrong mind.

**Chapter ending convention:** *The widened gap.* End each chapter on a panel that adds one piece of information the protagonist's hypothesis cannot explain — or explains incorrectly. The gap between what they conclude and what the reader suspects is the engine. Hard cliffhangers are secondary to perceptual dissonance. Final panel almost always close-up: protagonist's face, the evidence object, or the room slightly altered from how they described it in caption.

**Scene-to-scene transitions:** Hard cuts dominant — match-cuts on similar shapes (a clock face cuts to a spiral doodle; a locked door cuts to a locked jaw). The visual echo is subconscious paranoia grammar. Environmental bleeds (the iyashikei technique) appear only in "false relief" moments — brief scenes where the protagonist wrongly believes the threat is resolved.

**Per-volume arc shape:** Each volume = one hypothesis cycle. Volume opens with the protagonist's theory fully formed; by midpoint a crack appears they try to re-explain; by the final chapter the theory has been revised — but the revision contains its own hidden flaw. No volume resolves cleanly. The reader should finish each volume slightly less certain than they started.

## 4. Dialogue

**Register:** Analytical, precise, internally self-correcting. Characters do not emote; they *assess*. The protagonist's internal monologue reads like a case file being revised in real-time. The gap between their spoken register (controlled, professional, adult — desu/masu in workplace scenes; plain register in internal monologue) and the increasing desperation of their private reasoning is the genre's primary tonal instrument.

**Narration:dialogue ratio:** ~55% narration, 45% dialogue. Internal monologue boxes carry the argument; spoken dialogue is the surface the protagonist tries to maintain. When the ratio inverts — when external speech begins to dominate and internal monologue shrinks — that signals the protagonist is losing their analytical distance. Use this structurally at volume midpoints and series climaxes.

**Unreliable-narrator conventions:**
1. The protagonist's caption CONFIRMS something the panel art CONTRADICTS. The reader sees both. Do not call attention to this; trust the reader.
2. Retrospective captions ("I should have seen it then") placed over scenes where nothing visible was wrong — plants the seed that perception itself failed, not just attention.
3. Other characters' speech transcribed in caption with quotation marks and the protagonist's gloss in brackets: *"You seem tired," she said [meaning: you are showing.]*. The bracketed reading is sometimes correct, often not.
4. The antagonist (or systemic threat, or protagonist's own cognition) never explains its logic. Explanations are supplied entirely by the protagonist — and they are always partly wrong.

**Density per bubble:** 10–30 words typical (longer than shonen). Philosophical provocations are genuine, not rhetorical — characters debate what is knowable, not what is right.

## 5. Character

**Protagonist archetype:** The hyper-analyst — high functional competence, high internal noise. Career identity is load-bearing: the securities analyst whose market paranoia and personal paranoia share the same cognitive loop (`cogclar_jp_07`); the screenwriter who rehearses catastrophic futures (`cogclar_jp_08`). Their professional skill is real. The terror is that their skill and their distortion run on identical machinery. Watashi pronoun register; desu/masu in professional contexts; plain, slightly terse language in internal monologue.

**Supporting cast function:** Two types only. *The anchor* — one relationship (colleague, partner, sibling) that represents the cost of the protagonist's spiral; the anchor's dialogue is always shorter and more direct, which reads as either reassuring or sinister depending on the volume. *The mirror* — a character whose paranoia or clarity is the protagonist's road not taken; placed at volume midpoint to show what the protagonist could become in either direction.

**Antagonist grammar:** The threat is *diffuse and potentially internal*. In Death Note, Light's antagonist is ultimately his own capacity for self-deception. In Monster, Tenma's antagonist is a real person — but the deeper threat is Tenma's certainty that he understands Johan. The psychological-thriller antagonist is never simply wrong or simply right. When a human antagonist exists (the gaslighting company in `cogclar_jp_09`, the IFS inner part in `cogclar_jp_11`), they must have internal logic the reader can follow even while rejecting the conclusion.

## 6. Failure modes

1. **Explaining the unreliable narrator to the reader.** If a caption box says "I realize now I was lying to myself," the unreliability is over — the protagonist has become reliable about their unreliability.
2. **Making the threat supernatural.** Once a ghost or entity enters, the contract shifts to psychological horror. Keep the threat human, systemic, or cognitive.
3. **Protagonist incompetence as the source of distortion.** The genre requires a *skilled* person whose skills are misfiring. An incompetent protagonist is just a mystery story.
4. **Resolving the final mystery cleanly.** A full explanation in the last chapter evacuates all retroactive dread. Leave one facet unexplained — not as a sequel hook, but as honest acknowledgment that perception has limits.
5. **Antagonist revealed as purely evil with no self-model.** The antagonist must believe they are right; their logic must be reconstructible even if monstrous.
6. **Environmental reassurance.** Using warm, soft, iyashikei-adjacent visual grammar to signal "this is a safe chapter" undermines the paranoia gradient. The visual field should remain consistently pressured even in quieter scenes.
7. **Overuse of the protagonist's correct prediction as competence signaling.** Each correct prediction must be immediately followed by a scene where the same logic produces a wrong prediction — otherwise the hyper-analyst becomes a power fantasy, not a portrait.
8. **Missing the cost.** The obsession loop must accrue visible cost (the anchor relationship degrading, physical symptoms, professional exposure) by volume 3 or the reader loses investment.

## 7. 48-volume shape

48 volumes = **three 16-volume investigations**, not one continuous case. Each investigation is structurally complete and can be read standalone. The protagonist is the same across all three but the cognitive architecture shifts:

- **Volumes 1–16 (Investigation One):** Protagonist's analytical system is intact, possibly heroically so. The unreliability is subtle and reader-privileged; the protagonist never fully sees it. Ends with a resolution that is correct on the surface and wrong at depth.
- **Volumes 17–32 (Investigation Two):** The protagonist knows about their distortion from vol. 1's aftermath. They now meta-monitor their own analysis — which creates a second-order distortion (the monitoring itself becomes unreliable). Ends with the protagonist having been more correct than they believed AND more wrong.
- **Volumes 33–48 (Investigation Three):** The question is no longer what is true, but whether the distinction between correct analysis and paranoid analysis can be maintained at all. The antagonist in this arc may share the protagonist's cognitive architecture. Ends ambiguously — the final panel should be unresolvable on whether what the protagonist sees is real.

Within each 16-volume investigation: **4-volume act structure** — vol. 1–4 (theory formation), vol. 5–8 (first crack), vol. 9–12 (false resolution), vol. 13–16 (collapse and reframing). The vol. 9–12 false resolution is where the visual grammar briefly lightens — the "it's over" moment that the reader does not trust.

## 8. Panel scaffolding

Per-panel fields (9):

1. `framing` — ELS / LS / MS / MCU / CU / insert / **distortion-CU** (close-up with slight fisheye or geometric warp, used for unreliable-perception beats)
2. `beat_role` — one of {assumption_plant, evidence_detail, gap_widening, false_relief, anchor_cost, mirror_moment, analytical_spiral, revelation_reframe, ambiguous_close}
3. `narration_certainty` — integer 1–5 (1 = protagonist states fact with full confidence; 5 = protagonist openly doubts their own reading); must trend toward 4–5 by chapter's final panel
4. `dialogue` — ≤25 words or null; spoken register controlled and adult; never expressive in public context
5. `caption` — protagonist internal monologue, ≤35 words; may conflict with panel art (flag with `unreliable_flag`)
6. `unreliable_flag` — boolean; true when caption content and panel visual content point to different conclusions; must appear on ≥15% of panels per chapter
7. `black_density` — float 0.0–1.0; should correlate with `narration_certainty` inversely (more certain = heavier blacks = cognitive constriction)
8. `screentone_type` — {paranoia_vignette, clean_flat, gradient_lightening, none}; antagonist-POV panels use clean_flat only
9. `silence_role` — {threatening, contemplative, none}; threatening silence reserved for scenes where the protagonist waits for a response that recontextualizes

**Generator constraint:** `unreliable_flag=true` on ≥15% of panels; `beat_role=false_relief` no more than 3 times per volume and always followed within 2 panels by `gap_widening`; consecutive `narration_certainty=1` panels ≤3 before a crack must appear.

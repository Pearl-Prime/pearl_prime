# Procedural — Style Bible

> Absorbed from PR #295 branch (`claude/manga-12ep-arc-authoring-egnwqf` @ `4c6e2c3d59`), reworked per operator ruling Q-MPU-02 (2026-07-24): #295 itself is never merged; this lane's PR is the landing path.

Touchstones: *Case Closed / Detective Conan* (Aoyama Gosho — investigative-procedural spine), *Golden Kamuy* (Noda Satoru — survival-procedural hybrid: tanning, hunting, Ainu craft process rendered with documentary specificity), *Silver Spoon* (Arakawa Hiromu — agricultural-technical procedural), *Dr. Stone* (Inagaki Riichirō / Boichi — scientific-procedural, civilization rebuilt one verified step at a time), *MASTER Keaton* (Urasawa Naoki / Hokusei Katsushika / Takashi Nagasaki — insurance-investigator procedural). Genre row: `canonical_genre_list.yaml` id `procedural`, label "Procedural", `pacing_proxy: workplace`. Interaction-grammar quality gate: `authority_team_or_system_pressure`.

## 1. Market Contract

Readers are process-literate adults and older teens (18–40) who get satisfaction from watching a correct method beat a hard problem — the same appetite that drives cooking-technique videos and courtroom-procedural TV. They are not reading for shock twists; they are reading for *legibility earned through work*. They will accept: technical vocabulary used correctly and un-glossed, a case/problem that takes real chapters to close, competence porn, a protagonist who is right because they followed the process, not because they are gifted. They will reject: solutions arrived at by narrative convenience (the culprit confesses for no reason, the cure appears from nowhere), a "genius flash" that skips the legwork, and process explained *at* the reader in an unbroken lecture block. The genre's contract is: *show the method working, in order, and let the reader solve alongside the process* — the pleasure is procedural transparency, not mystery-box withholding. Golden Kamuy's tanning-a-hide sequences and Silver Spoon's cheese-fermentation charts are canonical proof that reader tolerance for real technical detail is *high* when the detail is staged as active problem-solving rather than dumped as reference material.

## 2. Visual Grammar

- **Panels per page:** 5–8. Process sequences (evidence processing, lab work, fieldcraft) favor tight step-panels — 3–4 small panels showing sequential hand-action in place of one summary panel. Reveal panels drop to 1–2 large panels; the compression *into* fewer, bigger panels is what signals "this is the payoff."
- **Words per page:** 55–95, adapted up from workplace's 60–110 range at the low end because procedural leans more on staged visual sequence than dialogue to carry information — a well-drawn process panel needs fewer words than a meeting-room exchange does. Case-briefing and interrogation scenes spike to 100–130. Fieldwork/process-execution scenes drop to 15–35 (let the hands speak).
- **Dialogue-to-caption ratio:** ~60:40. Slightly more dialogue-forward than workplace_drama's 55:45 — procedural information is usually walked through *out loud* (briefing the team, explaining to a junior partner, thinking aloud over evidence) rather than carried in private interior caption, because the process itself needs an in-fiction audience to justify its exposition.
- **Silent-panel ratio:** 15–25%, adapted down slightly from workplace's 20–30% baseline. Procedural silence is concentrated, not ambient: the beat right before a test result, the close-up on a hand lifting evidence, the pause when a theory clicks. Fewer ambient silent panels than workplace drama because process sequences are inherently busy with visual information even when wordless.
- **Reaction-shot frequency:** HIGH around reveals — adapted UP from workplace's medium baseline. Investigative reveals need a visible audience: the team's faces recognizing what a clue means, the suspect's micro-expression shift, the mentor's raised eyebrow. A process-reveal without a reaction panel undersells the payoff the reader was working toward.
- **Black-fill ratio:** 0.15–0.25 standard; spikes to 0.30–0.40 during nighttime fieldwork, autopsy/lab sequences, or high-tension confrontation reveals. Drops to 0.05–0.15 for daylight documentation and report-writing scenes.
- **Screentone use:** Medium-to-heavy on technical surfaces — cross-hatch and mechanical tone on instruments, evidence tables, lab equipment, hides/tools/machinery depending on process type. Diagram-style inset panels (a map, a cutaway, a flowchart) are genre-native and should use flat, unshaded line art to visually separate "diagram register" from "scene register."
- **Line weight:** Precise, technical-illustration-adjacent for tools/instruments/diagrams; looser, more expressive line for character faces during reaction beats. The contrast between ruled-precision object rendering and loose character linework is itself the genre's visual signature — it says "the process is exact; the people are not."
- **Diagram/inset convention:** At least one true diagram panel (map, flowchart, cutaway, evidence board with string) per chapter that deals in active investigation/problem-solving. This is the genre's equivalent of workplace drama's chibi-release — a stylistically discontinuous insert that exists purely to carry process information compactly.

## 3. Pacing

- **Chapter hook family:** *Anomaly-first.* Open on a detail that doesn't fit — a wound that doesn't match the weapon, a crop yield that's wrong, a symptom cluster with no diagnosis, a stone tool where none should be. The hook is a puzzle piece announcing itself as a puzzle piece, not a full scene of institutional pressure (contrast workplace drama's deadline-first hook).
- **Chapter ending convention:** *Process-step landing, not full resolution.* The chapter closes on one confirmed fact, one eliminated hypothesis, or one new question generated by the last answer — never the full case closing mid-arc. A chapter that resolves everything it opened has skipped the genre's core pleasure.
- **Mid-chapter architecture:** First third = anomaly observed and framed as a problem. Middle = method applied — the actual dirty work of testing, questioning, cross-referencing, replicating. Final third = a result that is *partial* — confirms one thing, complicates another. Save full-clarity reveals for arc climaxes, not individual chapters.
- **Per-volume arc shape:** Each volume carries a **case/problem arc** (external: the specific investigation, disease, crop failure, technical build) and a **method-mastery arc** (internal: the protagonist or team getting measurably better at the underlying discipline — faster triage, sharper cross-examination, more reliable technique). Stagger by half a volume, as in workplace drama; the method-mastery arc is what accumulates across the whole 48-volume run.
- **Reveal cadence:** One "aha" reveal every 1.5–2 chapters is the healthy default; more frequent than that and the process reads as arbitrary genius-flashes rather than earned deduction. Reserve full case-closing reveals for the final 1–2 chapters of a volume.
- **Team-briefing beat:** At least one scene per volume where the process itself is explicitly stated aloud to a newcomer or skeptic character — this is the genre's sanctioned exposition valve (see Failure Mode 1) and should be staged as a real character interaction with pushback, not a monologue.

## 4. Dialogue

- **Process-vocabulary register:** Technical terms are used correctly, in context, and are NOT immediately re-explained in plain language by another character unless a newcomer character is present to justify the explanation. Silver Spoon names actual dairy chemistry; Dr. Stone names actual chemical formulas; Golden Kamuy names actual Ainu tools and techniques. Precision is the flex; readers who don't know the term infer it from the visual process shown alongside it.
- **Briefing-speech patterns:** Procedural exposition is legitimate only when it is *directed at an in-fiction listener with a reason to need it* — the rookie, the outside expert, the skeptical superior who must be convinced. Pattern: character states a step → listener pushes back or asks the obvious question → the answer reveals the next layer of the process. This dialogic structure prevents the lecture-block failure mode.
- **Interrogation/challenge micro-scenes:** 2–5 panel exchanges where a claim is tested — a suspect's alibi, a colleague's hypothesis, a technique's real-world result. Short, adversarial, information-dense. The genre's version of workplace drama's ambiguous hallway exchange, but here the ambiguity resolves into a testable claim rather than staying deniable.
- **Deduction-aloud register:** When a protagonist reasons through evidence, render it as clipped declarative fragments tied to specific visual referents ("Water on the left boot only. It rained after he arrived, not before.") — never as an unbroken paragraph of pure logic disconnected from the panel's visual evidence.
- **Mentor/junior register:** The senior practitioner corrects method, not just conclusions — "you tested the wrong variable" carries more genre-authenticity than "you're wrong." Correction is procedural, not moral.

## 5. Character

- **Protagonist type:** A person who is competent through method, not gift — their edge is thoroughness, correct sequencing, or refusal to skip a step, not innate genius (even child-detective archetypes like Conan solve through observation-and-testing, not psychic insight). The protagonist's flaw is frequently a mirror of their strength: the obsessive re-checker who cannot let a loose thread go, the technician who trusts data over people.
- **The authority/institution figure:** A superior, licensing body, editor, or client who represents the standard the protagonist's process must satisfy — budget, deadline, evidentiary bar, professional certification. Per interaction-grammar's `authority_team_or_system_pressure` gate, this pressure must appear on-page regularly, not just as backstory.
- **The team/partner:** Procedural work is rarely solo in credible execution — a partner, junior colleague, or cross-disciplinary specialist who covers a gap in the protagonist's method (the medical examiner to the detective, the chemist to the survivalist, the seasoned farmer to the transfer student). The partner's competence must be visible and specific, not decorative.
- **The skeptic:** A character who doubts the process itself, not just this instance of it — this role forces the genre's exposition to earn its place on the page (see Dialogue §2) and dramatizes the stakes of getting the method wrong.
- **The case/problem itself as antagonist-shaped force:** As with workplace drama's institutional antagonist, procedural's true opposition is often not a villain but the problem's own resistance to being solved — entropy, decay, bureaucracy, an uncooperative body or field or machine. When a specific person embodies obstruction (a culprit, a rival institution, a saboteur), they must have a comprehensible reason the process itself can eventually surface — motive is data too.

## 6. Failure Modes

1. **Process as infodump.** Technical explanation delivered in an unbroken caption or monologue block with no in-fiction listener, no visual demonstration, and no pushback. This is the genre's single most common and most fatal failure — if a reader can skip the paragraph without losing the plot, the paragraph should not exist.
2. **Genius-flash shortcut.** A conclusion arrived at without shown legwork — the detective "just knows," the cure "just works." Breaks the show-the-method contract; readers who came for procedural transparency feel cheated by narrative convenience.
3. **Evidence-free confession.** An antagonist who explains their own crime/error unprompted because the plot needs the reveal, rather than being caught by the method actually shown on-page.
4. **Inconsistent process rules.** A technique, protocol, or system that works differently in different chapters to suit plot needs. Procedural readers track internal consistency closely; broken process-rules read as cheating.
5. **Team as furniture.** Partners and colleagues who exist only to receive exposition and never contribute a genuine procedural insight of their own — flattens the team arc workplace drama and procedural both depend on.
6. **Case-of-the-week with zero accretion.** Individual cases that never build the protagonist's or team's method-mastery arc (§3) read as filler; the 48-volume shape requires visible skill accumulation, not a reset button each chapter.
7. **Diagram-panel absence.** A process-heavy chapter with no diagram/inset panel (§2) forces all technical information into dialogue, overloading word count and triggering Failure Mode 1.

## 7. 48-Volume Shape

The 48-volume pre-plan structures as four **discipline-mastery macro-arcs**, each approximately 12 volumes, mirroring workplace drama's career-stage structure but keyed to procedural competence rather than institutional standing:

- **Arc I — Apprentice Method (Volumes 1–12):** Protagonist learns the discipline's basic vocabulary and process from the ground up, usually attached to a mentor or institution that enforces standards. Cases/problems in this arc are small in scope but methodologically complete — each one teaches one procedural building block. Main question: *can I actually do this correctly, under supervision?* Volume 12 closes with the protagonist completing a full process independently for the first time.
- **Arc II — Independent Casework (Volumes 13–24):** Protagonist runs cases/problems with real autonomy; the authority figure now reviews rather than supervises. Complexity rises — multi-step problems, competing hypotheses, cases that resist first-pass method. Main question: *when the standard method doesn't fit, what do I trust?* Volume 24 closes on a case that is solved through a genuine methodological innovation, not a shortcut.
- **Arc III — System Pressure (Volumes 25–36):** The institution itself becomes contested — budget cuts, a rival methodology, a case with political stakes that pressures the protagonist to cut corners. The team dynamic matures; the protagonist now trains juniors and must defend process, not just execute it. Main question: *can the method survive contact with power that wants a faster answer?*
- **Arc IV — Standard-Setter (Volumes 37–48):** Protagonist's method has become the institution's standard, or a rival institution's challenge forces a definitive test of it. The capstone case is deliberately imperfect in its resolution — factual, incomplete, methodologically sound rather than triumphant. Volume 48 does not close the discipline's larger unsolved questions; it closes on the protagonist's relationship to the process itself — a torch passed, a method written down for the next apprentice.

## 8. Panel Scaffolding

Per-panel fields (9):

1. `framing` — ELS (scene establishing, crime scene/field/lab) / LS / MS / MCU / CU / insert / diagram-inset
2. `beat_role` — `anomaly_hook` | `process_step` | `evidence_reveal` | `hypothesis_test` | `team_briefing` | `reaction_shot` | `deduction_aloud` | `partial_resolution` | `case_closing`
3. `location_type` — `field_site` | `lab_or_workshop` | `briefing_room` | `archive_or_records` | `interrogation_or_interview` | `outside_institution`
4. `process_domain` — string naming the active technical discipline in this panel (forensics, agriculture, chemistry, engineering, medicine, craft-trade, etc.); drives correct technical vocabulary and screentone choice
5. `evidence_or_variable` — what specific fact/object/data point this panel is establishing, testing, or overturning; `null` only on pure reaction or transitional panels
6. `reaction_flag` — boolean; `true` marks a dedicated reaction-shot panel following a reveal; per §2, reveals require at least one adjacent `reaction_flag=true` panel
7. `diagram_flag` — boolean; `true` = flat-line diagram/inset register (map, flowchart, cutaway, evidence board); at least one per process-heavy chapter
8. `dialogue` — ≤20 words in-process; briefing/interrogation panels may run ≤35 words if structured as challenge-and-answer (see Dialogue §2)
9. `silence_flag` — boolean; concentrated, not ambient — true on pre-result tension panels and immediate post-reveal beats; target 15–25% of panels per chapter

Constraint hint: `evidence_reveal` and `case_closing` beat_role panels must have an adjacent `reaction_flag=true` panel within 1–2 panels; `process_step` panels with `dialogue` present must have an in-fiction listener established in the scene (no process_step dialogue directed at empty air/reader).

# Family — Style Bible

Touchstones: *Yotsuba&!* (Azuma Kiyohiko — episodic child-POV household comedy), *Usagi Drop / Bunny Drop* (Unita Yumi — single-guardian caregiving drama; volumes 1–4 are the genre's caregiving-register gold standard, volumes 5–10's romance pivot is the genre's canonical cautionary tale, see §6), *Sweetness & Lightning* (Amagakure Gido — single-parent + food, straddles family and food genres, useful cross-reference for how a second craft-vessel can carry a family arc without becoming a food-genre book), *Kodocha-adjacent* domestic-realism register. Genre row: `canonical_genre_list.yaml` id `family`, label "Family", `pacing_proxy: social_issue`. Interaction-grammar quality gate: `family_pressure`.

## 1. Market Contract

Readers span a wide age band (20s–50s, often parents themselves, plus a substantial childless/pre-parent readership reading for the caregiving fantasy or the memory of being cared for) who are reading for *the specific texture of one household* — not family-in-general, but the particular configuration of people sharing walls, meals, and unspoken debts to each other. They will accept: slow accretion instead of plot, a chapter that is "just" a bath-time negotiation or a grandmother's silence at dinner, real generational friction that doesn't resolve into a group hug, and children who are genuinely strange and specific rather than cute-shaped plot devices. They will reject: a household that functions as generic sitcom furniture, conflict manufactured for an episode and forgotten by the next, a child character who exists only to be adorable at the adult protagonist, and — per Usagi Drop's cautionary history — any narrative move that retroactively reframes a caregiving relationship as a romantic one. The genre's contract is: *this specific family, in this specific configuration, and the small daily cost and gift of being responsible for people you did not choose in the way you chose them* — care rendered as labor and as love simultaneously, never as one without the other.

## 2. Visual Grammar

- **Panels per page:** 4–7. Domestic space is small and physically close; panels favor medium-to-close framing on faces, hands, and household objects (a shared plate, a school bag, a folded futon) over wide establishing shots. Drop to 2–3 for quiet evening/bedtime beats — the loosening grid signals the household settling for the night, echoing workplace drama's decompression logic but applied to domestic rather than after-work space.
- **Words per page:** 40–75, adapted down from social_issue's 60–100 baseline — family's register leans more on gesture, object, and silent domestic action (setting a table, tying a shoe, folding laundry) than on stated dialogue. Multi-generational conflict scenes (a grandmother and adult child disagreeing over the child's upbringing) can spike to 90–110; these should be rare, reserved for real generational-friction beats, not routine.
- **Dialogue-to-caption ratio:** ~65:35. Dialogue-forward relative to workplace drama (55:45) because household life is lived out loud — mealtime, bath time, and bedtime negotiation are inherently verbal — but captions carry the adult caregiver's private exhaustion, doubt, or tenderness that cannot be said in front of a child.
- **Silent-panel ratio:** 30–40%, adapted UP from social_issue's 25–45 baseline toward its higher end and beyond — explicitly wider than social_issue's issue-driven tension allows, because family's core beats are domestic-quiet (a sleeping child's breathing, a shared silence over a meal, a hand on a door not yet opened) rather than issue-confrontation. Note this adaptation: social_issue's silence is often *held tension before a revelation*; family's silence is more often *the register itself* — closer to iyashikei's baseline calm, but returning to a household rather than a landscape.
- **Reaction-shot frequency:** Medium-high, concentrated on the child or youngest-generation character's face — children in this genre must be drawn as active perceivers whose reactions the reader is meant to read as real information (confusion, delight, a question not yet asked), never as passive cute-objects.
- **Black-fill ratio:** 0.10–0.20 standard; a genuinely warm, low-threat baseline closer to iyashikei than to workplace drama. Spikes to 0.25–0.30 only during real generational-conflict or crisis beats (illness, a caregiving crisis, a hard money conversation) — the household's visual "weather" should darken only when the stakes are genuinely domestic-serious.
- **Screentone use:** Soft dot-screen and gradient tones dominate — warm light through a kitchen window, the texture of a worn tatami mat, steam off a bath or a bowl of rice. Household objects (toys, dishware, hand-me-down furniture) rendered with enough specificity to read as *this family's* objects, not generic prop-shop furniture — this specificity is the genre's primary anti-genericness tool (see Failure Mode 2).
- **Line weight:** Soft, rounded contour on children and domestic objects; slightly heavier, more worn-in line on adult and elder characters' faces — hands and faces of older-generation characters should carry visible linework "weight" (wrinkles, worn knuckles) that reads as accumulated care-labor, not just age-marking.
- **Household-configuration establishing shot:** At least one panel per chapter that re-establishes the household's physical configuration — who is in which room, who is missing, the current state of shared space (a spare futon still out, a school bag by the door). This is the genre's equivalent of workplace drama's office-floor establishing shot; the household's *shape* is a character in itself and its state should shift visibly across the story.

## 3. Pacing

- **Chapter hook family:** *Domestic-routine-interrupted.* Open inside an ordinary household routine (making a bento, a morning argument over socks, a grandmother already awake before anyone else) and let one small deviation from the routine be the hook — a missing object, an unusual silence, an unscheduled visitor. The hook is a crack in the routine, not an external crisis announcing itself (contrast procedural's anomaly-first hook, which announces its wrongness immediately; family's crack is more easily missed, more ordinary-looking, until it isn't).
- **Chapter ending convention:** *A held domestic image, not a summary.* Final panel is usually a physical household tableau — a light turned off, a door left slightly open, a shared blanket, a place at the table — that carries the chapter's emotional weight without stating it. Close to iyashikei's reflective-silence ending but returning to the *household*, specifically, rather than to landscape; this is a "landing," per workplace drama's terminology, not a cliffhanger.
- **Mid-chapter architecture:** First third = the routine crack observed by one household member, usually the caregiving adult or the child. Middle = the household attempts its normal coping mechanism and it partially fails — a first conversation that doesn't land, a plan that doesn't account for someone's real need. Final third = a small, concrete adjustment — not a full resolution, a next-time-we'll-try-this beat that the household actually enacts (a new routine, a small admission, a chore reassigned).
- **Per-volume arc shape:** Each volume carries a **household-configuration arc** (external: someone moves in or out, a generation's health changes, a school or work transition reshapes daily logistics) and a **caregiving-relationship arc** (internal: the specific bond between two household members deepening or straining). Stagger by half a volume as in workplace drama; do not let the configuration arc's practical urgency swallow the relationship arc's slower internal work.
- **Generational-marker cadence:** At least one beat per volume that is explicitly cross-generational — a grandparent and grandchild alone together, an adult child seeing a parent's old habit repeat in themselves, an inherited object (recipe, tool, piece of clothing) changing hands. This is the genre's structural proof that the household spans real time, not just a nuclear-family snapshot.
- **No premature time-skip.** Per Failure Mode 1, large time-skips that jump a child character into adulthood mid-series must never be used to manufacture a new relationship register between existing household members — if a time-skip is used at all, it should widen the household's world (new members, new configuration), not re-cast an existing caregiving bond as something else.

## 4. Dialogue

- **Register duality by generation:** Each generational voice in the household must be dialect-distinct — a child's incomplete syntax and literal questions, a working-adult's clipped practical shorthand, an elder's more formal or old-fashioned phrasing and indirect speech. The moment two generations start sounding identical on the page, the household loses its specific texture (Failure Mode 2).
- **Child-speech register:** Rendered with real linguistic specificity — mispronunciations, invented words, non-sequitur questions that are functionally profound (Yotsuba&!'s entire comic engine runs on a child's literal, sideways questions revealing what adults have stopped noticing). Never write child dialogue as miniature-adult dialogue; that is the single fastest way to make a family book feel fake.
- **Caregiving-negotiation micro-scenes:** 2–4 panel exchanges over small physical logistics — who's picking up whom, whether a bath happens now or after dinner, who forgot the permission slip. These are the genre's version of workplace drama's hallway exchange: short, concrete, and carrying more relational information than their surface content states.
- **Unspoken-debt register:** Care work in a household is frequently owed, given, and never verbally accounted for. Dialogue should let characters gesture toward this without naming it directly ("You didn't have to do that" / "I know" is a complete, sufficient exchange) — direct verbal gratitude or resentment monologues should be rare and therefore weighted, not routine.
- **Elder-speech and indirection:** Older-generation characters frequently communicate approval, worry, or correction indirectly — through action (an extra portion served, a blanket added) rather than statement. When an elder character does speak plainly, it should land as a notable event, not a normal register.
- **No lecture-delivery adult wisdom.** As with workplace drama's "preachy career advice" ban, an adult caregiver's dialogue that resolves into stated parenting wisdom or life-lesson delivery is the genre's capital crime. Emotional truth is shown through the caregiving act itself, not narrated as a takeaway.

## 5. Character

- **The caregiving adult:** Not necessarily a biological parent — a guardian, older sibling, grandparent, or found-family figure who has taken on responsibility for a household member younger or more dependent than themselves, often with real ambivalence about having done so. Their competence is uneven and visibly learned on the job; per genre contract, they must never be simply "naturally good at parenting" — the labor and uncertainty must be visible.
- **The child/youngest-generation character:** An active perceiver with a real interior life and a distinct, specific personality — not a cuteness-delivery device. Their questions and behavior should regularly surface things the adult characters have stopped seeing (Yotsuba&!'s structural engine). Per `family_pressure` interaction-grammar gate, this character's needs must generate real on-page pressure on the caregiving adult, not just warmth.
- **The elder/oldest-generation character:** Carries household history and often a different, sometimes friction-generating standard for how care "should" look. Their authority is real but not absolute — the household's generational-marker beats (§3) depend on this character being neither a saintly font of wisdom nor a comic obstacle, but a person shaped by a different era's version of the same caregiving pressures.
- **The peer-household or outside-family figure:** A neighboring family, a school friend's household, or an extended-family branch that offers a comparison point — makes the protagonist household's specific configuration visible by contrast, the way workplace drama's outside friend makes institutional life visible by contrast.
- **The absent or complicating figure:** A parent who has died, left, or is otherwise structurally absent — very common as the household's founding condition (Usagi Drop's absent grandfather/father, Sweetness & Lightning's deceased mother). This figure's absence should continue to exert active pressure on the household (a habit inherited, an object kept, a question a child eventually asks) rather than functioning as pure backstory that's resolved once and never returns.

## 6. Failure Modes

1. **Time-skip relationship recast.** Jumping the timeline to reframe an existing caregiving bond as a romantic one, or otherwise using a time-skip to manufacture a relationship register the household's premise didn't earn. This is Usagi Drop's own canonical failure — the manga's volumes 5–10 aged Rin into a romantic partner for the man who raised her from early childhood, which retroactively poisoned the caregiving story readers had trusted for four volumes and produced the still-current fan shorthand "getting Usagi Dropped" for a story that betrays its own premise this way. Absolute rule for this catalog: a caregiving relationship established in Arc I must remain a caregiving relationship through Volume 48.
2. **Generic sitcom family with no specific texture.** A household distinguishable from any other household only by character names — no specific objects, routines, generational speech patterns, or configuration quirks. If the household could be swapped into any other family story unchanged, it has failed the genre's core contract.
3. **Child as cuteness-delivery device.** A child character whose only narrative function is to be adorable at or for the adult protagonist, with no real interiority, questions, or pressure-generating needs of their own.
4. **Conflict-of-the-week with no accretion.** Household friction manufactured for a single chapter and forgotten by the next, with no lasting effect on the household-configuration or caregiving-relationship arcs (§3).
5. **Uniform generational voice.** Every household member speaking in the same register regardless of age — collapses the genre's central texture-generating device (§4).
6. **Adult-wisdom lecture delivery.** Caregiving insight stated as narrated life-lesson rather than shown through the caregiving act. See Dialogue §6.
7. **Absence used once and dropped.** An absent parent or founding loss introduced as backstory motivation and then never exerting further pressure on the household — flattens what should be a recurring structural presence (Character §5).
8. **Crisis-only register.** A household rendered only through its hard moments (illness, money trouble, loss) with no ordinary-routine chapters between them — family's genre pleasure depends on the ordinary-routine baseline the crises interrupt; without it, crises lose their contrast and weight.

## 7. 48-Volume Shape

The 48-volume pre-plan structures as four **household-configuration macro-arcs**, each approximately 12 volumes, tracking the physical and relational shape of the household across real time rather than a single generation's career stages:

- **Arc I — Formation (Volumes 1–12):** The household's founding configuration is established — a new guardianship, a household reshaped by loss or arrival, or simply the household as it has long existed, now seen closely for the first time. The caregiving adult is visibly uncertain; competence is earned in small, specific increments. Main question: *can this configuration of people actually function as a household?* Volume 12 closes on the first moment the household's new normal feels genuinely stable, without being declared "solved."
- **Arc II — Growth Friction (Volumes 13–24):** The youngest-generation character's needs change as they grow (school, peers, independence-seeking), straining routines the household built in Arc I. Generational-marker beats deepen — an elder's health or role shifts, requiring renegotiation. Main question: *how does this household hold together as its members change at different rates?* Volume 24 closes on a real, unresolved generational disagreement that the household has to keep living with, not solve.
- **Arc III — Wider Household (Volumes 25–36):** The household's boundary is tested by the outside world — school, work, an extended-family conflict, a health crisis, or a new member joining. The caregiving adult now supports others in the household through a hardship, mirroring the care they themselves once received or lacked. Main question: *what does this household owe the people just outside it, and what do outsiders owe this household?*
- **Arc IV — Legacy (Volumes 37–48):** The youngest-generation character approaches genuine independence; the household's configuration shifts again, this time toward the next generation taking on caregiving weight themselves (without collapsing into a romantic-recast of Arc I's bond — see Failure Mode 1). The capstone volume does not resolve the family's story into an ending; it closes on the household's continuation — an object, a routine, or a room passed to whoever is now responsible for it. Volume 48 should land the way a good bloodline saga does: not "the end," but "and it continues, differently now."

## 8. Panel Scaffolding

Per-panel fields (9):

1. `framing` — ELS (household establishing, full room/home exterior) / LS / MS / MCU / CU / insert / object-insert (a shared object in close-up)
2. `beat_role` — `routine_establish` | `routine_crack` | `caregiving_negotiation` | `generational_marker` | `unspoken_debt_beat` | `reaction_shot_child` | `crisis_beat` | `domestic_landing`
3. `household_configuration` — string naming who is currently present/absent in the household at this panel's moment (e.g. `grandmother+parent+child`, `child_alone_waiting`, `parent+new_member`); must be updated whenever the configuration changes within a chapter
4. `generational_marker_flag` — boolean; `true` marks a panel explicitly carrying cross-generational content (an inherited object, a repeated habit, an elder-child exchange); target at least one `true` panel per volume per §3
5. `care_object` — the specific household object or physical care-act anchoring this panel (a folded futon, a bento box, a bath, a hand-me-down toy), or `null`; used to enforce household-specific texture per Failure Mode 2
6. `child_pov_flag` — boolean; `true` marks a panel rendered from or centered on the youngest-generation character's literal perception; used to enforce Character §5's active-perceiver requirement
7. `dialogue` — ≤15 words for child-register dialogue; ≤25 words for adult caregiving-negotiation dialogue; elder-register dialogue may run indirect and shorter, ≤12 words preferred
8. `silence_flag` — boolean; silent panels must appear at a minimum 30% of total panels per chapter, wider than social_issue's proxy baseline per the explicit pacing adaptation in §2; at least one full wordless domestic-tableau panel per chapter mandatory
9. `black_fill_intensity` — `low` (0.10–0.20, standard domestic baseline) | `elevated` (0.25–0.30, crisis_beat panels only)

Constraint hint: `beat_role=caregiving_negotiation` panels must never resolve into stated adult-wisdom dialogue (Failure Mode 6) — the negotiation must resolve through an action or `care_object`, not a monologue. A chapter with zero `generational_marker_flag=true` panels for more than 2 consecutive chapters should be flagged for review against §3's cadence requirement.

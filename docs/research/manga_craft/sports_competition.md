# Sports / Competition Manga — Style Bible

Touchstones: *Slam Dunk* (Inoue Takehiko), *Haikyuu!!* (Furudate Haruichi), *Ace of Diamond* (Terajima Yuji), *Eyeshield 21* (Inagaki Riichiro / Murata Yusuke), *Captain Tsubasa* (Takahashi Yoichi), *Ping Pong* (Matsumoto Taiyou), *Real* (Inoue Takehiko), *Initial D* (Shigeno Shuichi), *Blue Lock* (Kaneshiro Muneyuki).

## 1. Market Contract

Readers are Gen Z and young adults (core 14–28, secondary 28–40 returnees) who arrive for the feeling of *earned progress under pressure* — not fantasy power scaling, but the specific satisfaction of a body trained past its fear threshold delivering when it matters most. They carry ADHD hyperfocus patterns, performance anxiety, imposter syndrome at peak moments, and financial pressure around athletic careers. The genre's unspoken promise: your struggle is real, your body is the instrument, the team makes you larger than yourself. Readers will reject: unearned victories, protagonists who succeed without visible cost, opponents who exist only to lose, and emotional beats that stay inside the character's head without visible physical consequence. The sport must feel like it has weight — rules matter, physics matters, stamina depletes.

## 2. Visual Rules

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Panels per page | 5–8 (wide action panels alternating with tight reaction grids; impact moments break to 1–2 panels) |
| Words per page | 40–80 (drops to 5–15 during match climaxes; rises to 60–80 in training/strategy scenes) |
| Dialogue:caption ratio | 60:40 (dialogue dominant in off-court; narration/play-by-play dominant mid-match) |
| Pages per chapter | 18–22 (weekly shonen standard; 24–28 for climactic match chapters) |
| Black-fill ratio | 0.20–0.30 (higher than iyashikei, lower than horror; blacks used for impact shadows, crowd silhouette, night training) |
| Silent-panel ratio | 15–25% (impact moments, pre-serve tension, the breath before a decisive play) |
| Screentone | Speed lines from point of contact radiating outward; sweat-bead detail on skin during exertion; crowd-blur tone for stadium atmosphere |
| Line weight | Sharp and kinetic — thick contour on bodies in motion, thin precise lines for technical diagrams and equipment; uniform weight would flatten the impact |

Cover palette references: Basketball Orange (`#FF6B00` field, `#1A1A1A` title) for *Slam Dunk*-register; Volleyball Arena orange/black for *Haikyuu!!*-register; Elite Blue (`#0A1A3A` deep navy, `#0040FF` royal accent) for psychological/prestige sports (*Blue Lock*-register).

SFX vocabulary: バスッ (ball impact/swish), キュキュ (shoe squeak on court), ズバン (spike), ドオォ (crowd roar), ビュン (speed of throw), ガッ (collision), ずだだ (running footsteps). Commentary boxes use a distinct rectangular format, sometimes with a microphone-icon source label. Scoreboard captions as graphic elements (score, time, set number).

## 3. Pacing

**Game-arc structure (chapter level):**
1. Pre-game — team state, individual anxiety, opponent scouted; ends on the whistle/serve
2. First-half — establishing the match rhythm, protagonist's flaw exposed early, opponent's technique revealed
3. Halftime crisis — the coach's one quiet instruction that reframes everything; physical cost made visible (blisters, breath, injury)
4. Second-half climax — technique crystallized under pressure; the decisive play earns its spread panel by what was paid for it in training chapters
5. Aftermath — win or loss, the silence breathes before any speech; post-loss silence is the genre's most powerful beat

**Per-volume arc shape:** Each volume = one season arc nested inside one character growth arc. Season arc tracks standings and tournament placement. Character growth arc tracks a specific technical or psychological threshold. The two arcs must converge at the volume's climax.

Chapter hook family: *proximity-to-game* — open on the protagonist's body in a specific physical state (tape on fingers, chalk on hands, the weight of a ball) before the scene establishes where they are. The hook is the body before the mind.

Chapter ending convention: a game chapter ends mid-play or on the instant before a decisive contact, never on the outcome. An off-game chapter ends on a single image of physical practice continuing after dark, or a brief exchange that shifts a relationship one degree.

## 4. Dialogue

**Register:** Three distinct registers must be maintained simultaneously and must not bleed into each other.

- *Coach-speak* — aphoristic, technical, minimal. Coach Ukai (Haikyuu!!) corrects footwork and names emotional avoidance; Coach Anzai (Slam Dunk) speaks rarely and his restraint IS his character arc. The pattern: great sports manga coaches say less than you expect. One quiet observation outweighs ten motivational speeches.
- *Teammate-banter* — shorthand develops over the series. Early chapters: full tactical sentences. Late chapters: single words or glances that show team cohesion through compression. Each teammate has a distinct speech register (captain vs. ace vs. underdog).
- *Interior pre-shot focus* — split-second thought during a crucial play: time "freezes," a paragraph of tactical/emotional interior monologue, then action resumes at full speed. Fear and doubt stated honestly: "My legs won't move." The quiet player's rare words hit hardest.

**Signature technique naming:** Named moves (e.g., "Goujin Fire Shot," "Quick Minus Tempo") must be introduced before they are executed — one chapter of practice failure, then the name earns its panel when it lands in competition. Named technique pages use vertical title treatment with high-weight brushstroke typography.

**Play-by-play caption convention:** Rectangular announcer boxes with light-tinted background, set in a slightly different typeface from interior monologue. Keep to one box per page during high-action sequences; the action must not be buried under commentary. Scoreboard caption blocks update every 3–5 pages during match chapters.

**Key dialogue patterns:**
1. Pre-match declaration — captain, stated simply, no elaboration
2. Tactical call — in-game shorthand adjustment
3. Rival's respect — mid-match acknowledgment of opponent's strength
4. Coach's one line — timeout instruction that reframes everything
5. Post-loss silence — minimal dialogue; let the loss breathe for at least one full page
6. Senpai's farewell — graduating senior's quiet message; maximum emotional impact with minimum words

## 5. Character

**Protagonist:** Rookie with raw potential and a specific visible flaw, OR a comeback player carrying the weight of prior failure (injury, burnout, ejection from a team). Both archetypes require that progress be measured in small, specific, humiliating increments — not montage. Physical cost is visible: blisters, exhaustion, injury shown honestly. The Sakuragi pattern (Slam Dunk): start at zero, train until it is boring and repetitive, show that boredom.

**Supporting cast:**
- Coach — minimal speech, high authority, carries a backstory that explains their restraint
- Teammates — each must have a distinct speech register AND a distinct body language on the court; the quiet player's rare words hit hardest
- Manager/support staff — emotional intelligence role; observes what players cannot see about themselves

**Antagonist:** The rival team, humanized and respected. The opponent's internal monologue must appear at least once per match arc — turning them from obstacles into people. The best rivals become mirrors: they have solved the protagonist's current flaw, which is why beating them means solving it.

## 6. Failure Modes

1. **Protagonist wins everything.** Undefeated heroes are boring and break the genre contract. The most powerful narrative tool in sports manga is loss. *Slam Dunk* and *Haikyuu!!* both include devastating defeats that redefine the series.
2. **Power-up without training.** Sudden mid-match abilities without groundwork drain trust earned over prior chapters.
3. **One-man show in a team sport.** Individual growth must serve collective growth; the team IS the protagonist.
4. **Generic opponents.** Every rival team requires personality, motivation, and at least one sympathetic interior moment.
5. **Training as filler.** If a training scene does not advance character psychology, a relationship, or a specific technical threshold, it is dead weight.
6. **Over-explaining the sport's rules.** Either explain naturally through gameplay or trust the reader. Infodumps kill pacing.
7. **Melodrama in winning streaks.** A run of victories without visible cost reads as false. The body must be seen depleting even during triumph.
8. **Absent team chemistry.** If the only inter-team relationships shown are hierarchical, the team reads as units, not people.

## 7. 48-Volume Shape

**Volume 1–12: Rookie Season.** Protagonist joins team, flaw introduced by chapter 2, first loss by volume 2, first small victory earned by volume 4. Tournament arc begins volume 6. First rival team that mirrors the protagonist's current weakness encountered volumes 8–10. Season ends in defeat that is nevertheless a step forward.

**Volumes 13–28: Middle Seasons.** Training arcs that double as relationship arcs. A second sport position or technique mastered. A teammate's personal crisis that shifts team dynamics. At least one major loss that splinters the team temporarily and forces explicit reunion. Rival from the first arc reappears, now changed.

**Volumes 29–40: Championship Pursuit.** Stakes escalate to regional or national level. The protagonist confronts the psychological threshold — not a physical limit but the fear of succeeding, or the weight of representing others. The coach's backstory revealed at the decisive moment. A teammate's retirement or injury forces the team to become something new.

**Volumes 41–48: Graduation / Retirement Arc.** The senior class faces their last season. The senpai farewell beat carries maximum emotional weight. The protagonist takes the captain or mentor role, reversing the teacher-student dynamic. Final volume: the sport itself appears on the last spread without characters — the court, the ball, the empty net — letting the setting speak for what was left there.

## 8. Panel Scaffolding

Per-panel fields (9):

1. `framing` — ELS / LS / MS / MCU / CU / insert / court-diagram / scoreboard-graphic
2. `beat_role` — one of {pre_game_tension, first_contact, technique_execution, reaction_shot, coach_cut, crowd_beat, interior_focus, aftermath_silence, training_cost}
3. `in_game_flag` — boolean; true = match is active, governs SFX vocabulary and caption type
4. `technique_name` — string or null; if set, triggers vertical title-card treatment on this panel
5. `perspective` — one of {pov_on_ball, athlete_pov, spectator_sideline, coach_bench, aerial_court, opponent_pov}; POV-on-ball panels must use speed-line convergence toward viewer; spectator panels use crowd-blur screentone background
6. `body_state` — one of {fresh, fatigued, peak_exertion, injured, post_collapse, controlled}; governs sweat-bead density, line weight on musculature, breath-visible SFX (ハァ)
7. `sfx` — from sports vocabulary (バスッ, キュキュ, ズバン, etc.) or null; never environmental sfx during in-game panels
8. `dialogue` — ≤12 words during in_game_flag=true; ≤25 words off-game; coach-speak ≤8 words preferred
9. `silence_flag` — boolean; true on impact panels and post-loss panels; consecutive non-silent panels ≤6 during match sequences

Constraint hint: `technique_name` must appear in at least one training panel before it appears in an `in_game_flag=true` panel. `body_state=peak_exertion` must be visible on the protagonist in every match chapter's second half.

## 9. Locale Weighting

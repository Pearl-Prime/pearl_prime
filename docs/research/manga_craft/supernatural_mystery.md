# Supernatural Mystery — Style Bible

Touchstones: *Mushishi* (Urushibara Yuki), *xxxHOLiC* (CLAMP), *Natsume's Book of Friends* (Midorikawa Yuki), *Mononoke* (Yoshitake Kenji adaptation).

## 1. Market Contract

Readers are adults 25–45, skewing josei women and seinen women — the same cohort who drive iyashikei sales but want *narrative structure* underneath the atmosphere. They are showing up for the encounter with the unknowable rendered intimate: a spirit with unfinished business, a boundary between worlds made briefly permeable, a human protagonist who can hear what others cannot. They expect episodic resolution (each encounter has a complete arc) threaded onto an accumulating emotional through-line.

They will reject: jump-scare pacing, body horror, explicit gore, over-explained supernatural systems, and saccharine closure. Bittersweet is the contract — the spirit finds rest, but something is also lost.

The `stillness_press` brand carries somatic healing and sleep-anxiety content inside this shell: the spirit's unfinished business is always legible as a human emotional residue (grief, unspoken regret, generational wound). ## 2. Visual Rules

- **Panels per page:** 4–7. Avg 5. Not the dense grid of seinen (5–9 per `manga_genre_writing_styles_2026_04_04.md:89`) nor the airy spread of iyashikei (avg 2.5 per `iyashikei_minimalism.md:9`). Mid-register; irregular panel heights to let atmosphere breathe.
- **Words per page:** 30–65. Dialogue pages run 45–65; atmospheric transition pages 0–20.
- **Dialogue:caption ratio:** 55:45. Narration carries heavier load than iyashikei (70:30 dialogue) but lighter than shojo. Protagonist's observational captions frame encounters; spirit speech arrives as dialogue.
- **Pages per chapter:** 24–34. Episodic self-contained structure requires full arc space. Monthly magazine register.
- **Black-fill ratio:** 0.20–0.35. Higher than iyashikei (0.10–0.20) to mark spirit-space; lower than horror. Black fills define boundary zones — the space where spirit and human world touch.
- **Silent panel %:** 28–42%. Approaching seinen (20–35%) and iyashikei (35–55%) simultaneously. Silence is the medium through which spirit presence is felt before it is seen.
- **Screentone convention:** "Soft fog/atmosphere" — gradient tones used to build depth-of-field haziness at spirit-encounter moments. Light tones (10–30% density) for mist and water; mid-tones (40–60%) for spirit bodies; heavy tones reserved for liminal spaces (torii threshold, deep forest, still water surface at night). Avoid solid mechanical screentone patterns; prefer organic gradient application.
- **Line weight:** Variable — light for human characters in daylight register; slightly heavier, brushier for spirit forms and liminal environments. The visual weight of a spirit should feel different from the visual weight of a person. Fine-nib precision on architectural detail (shrine wood, paper wards, ritual objects); soft brushwork for spirit anatomy.

## 3. Pacing

**Structure:** Case-of-the-week backbone with a serialized through-line threading 6–12 chapters. Each episode = one spirit encounter, one case resolution. The through-line tracks protagonist's accumulating somatic toll (in `stillness_press` framing: each absorbed memory leaves residue in the body) or deepening relationship with a recurring spirit guide.

**Chapter hook:** Encounter-with-the-supernatural as the opening beat. Unlike iyashikei's weather-first hook, supernatural mystery opens on the *sign of the encounter* — an object out of place, a sound from an empty room, a person who should not be there. The sign precedes the explanation by at least one full scene. Never open with the spirit itself; open with its trace.

**Chapter ending:** Bittersweet release. The spirit's unfinished business is resolved — but resolution is always loss as well as peace. A child spirit finds its mother; the protagonist watches them fade and is left with the empty house. Avoid full comfort (iyashikei baseline restoration); avoid total despair. The correct register is *mono no aware* — the pathos of passing — rather than catharsis or horror's refusal of closure.

**Scene-to-scene transitions:** Via shrine imagery, mist, still water, threshold objects (torii, paper lanterns, genkan doorways). These carry the eye between scenes the way nature-bleed transitions carry iyashikei. A panel of torii receding into fog serves as both scene boundary and tonal marker: we are crossing between registers.

**Pacing rhythm within a chapter:** Sign → investigation → encounter → negotiation/ritual → release → aftermath. The aftermath beat (protagonist alone, post-resolution) is mandatory — it is where the emotional residue is deposited and where readers process. Never skip to the next scene immediately after spirit departure.

## 4. Dialogue

**Register:** Gentle, reflective, precise. Characters speak in full sentences more often than iyashikei characters but with more pauses and trailing registers than seinen. The protagonist's dialogue tends toward careful observation ("This ward feels older than the shrine itself") rather than exclamation or declaration.

**Narration ratio:** Protagonist's first-person captions carry 30–40% of page content. Narration is observational and somatic — sensory impressions, not emotional labeling. "The air near the threshold felt heavier than air should feel" rather than "I was afraid."

**Spirit-speech vs. human-speech distinction:**
- Human characters: standard conversational Japanese register, complete sentences, normal punctuation cadence. Josei-register: measured, adult, no slang.
- Spirit characters: speech rendered with slight formal archaism — not incomprehensible, but slightly off-register, as if the grammar belongs to a prior era. Shorter utterances. Questions rather than declarations. Spirits do not explain; they ask. "You can hear me?" "Why have you come to this room?" Fragment quality: "The box. Under the floor. The third plank."
- Spirit-speech bubbles are visually distinct: soft-edged or ragged-edged bubbles rather than hard-bordered, slightly smaller text, occasionally borderless floating text for spirits still learning to communicate.

**Interior monologue:** Used for somatic sensation at encounter moments — the protagonist's body registers what the mind has not yet named. Always brief (1–2 lines), always sensory. Matches iyashikei's environmental correlative principle: we do not say "I sensed grief here"; we say "my chest contracted, the way it does before snow."

## 5. Character

**Protagonist archetype:** A medium, sensitive, or observer — someone who perceives the spirit world at cost. Not a warrior, not a detective in the procedural sense, though detective structure surrounds them. The cost of perception is the series' engine. In `stillness_press` titles: this cost is explicitly somatic — absorbed memories accumulate in the body. The protagonist is usually female in josei-register series, gender-flexible in seinen-adjacent. Carries containment as a habitual posture — the sensitivity requires management, not performance.

**Spirit/yokai cast:** Each episodic spirit is a character, not a monster. They have history, attachment, unfinished obligation. They appear because something is incomplete, not because they are malevolent. Design them with specificity — a 1920s schoolgirl who cannot remember why she is still in the hallway; an old man who keeps setting the table for someone who moved away forty years ago. Their specific era and context should be researchable from their appearance before the case reveals it.

**Supporting human cast:** Small (3–5 recurring). A grounding human companion who does not perceive spirits and represents the cost of the protagonist's isolation — the relationship she strains by seeing what they cannot. A mentor figure (shrine keeper, antiquarian, elder practitioner) who provides context without over-explaining. One recurring human whose grief created a spirit the protagonist will eventually encounter.

**Spirit guide / familiar:** Optional but common (Natsume's Nyanko-sensei, xxxHOLiC's Mokona). Serves as tonal pressure-valve — gentle irony, deflation of protagonist self-seriousness — and provides exposition that feels natural ("That's a binding-type attachment, not a residual one. Different ritual.").

## 6. Failure Modes

1. **Over-exposing the supernatural rules.** The moment the spirit world has a codified taxonomy with named tiers and predictable behavior, the contemplative quality collapses into shonen power-system dynamics. Mushishi deliberately leaves mushi biology ambiguous. Never provide a complete rulebook.
2. **Sentimental endings.** Bittersweet release is the contract. Spirits who are "saved" cleanly and protagonists who feel purely better afterward betray the form. Something must remain unresolved or newly lost.
3. **Casework without emotional stakes.** Each case must carry a human theme (grief, generational wound, unspoken apology) that resonates with the series' somatic through-line. "Spirit has unfinished business, protagonist helps" without specifying *what* the human cost was is procedural filler.
4. **Protagonists who explain the atmosphere.** If the caption reads "The shrine gave off an aura of sadness," the reader has been robbed. Show the broken offering dish, the overgrown approach path, the single unlit lantern.
5. **Rushing past the aftermath beat.** Post-release scenes are where readers absorb the episode. Cutting immediately to next-case setup removes the emotional resonance that makes the genre therapeutic.
6. **Horror register contamination.** Spirits shown as threatening to the protagonist's physical safety — jump-cut reveals, body-horror visual language, dread-without-context — breaks the contemplative contract. Spirits are sad, not dangerous.
7. **Over-populating the spirit cast.** When every chapter introduces three new spirit types with new rules, the reader cannot maintain the intimacy that makes individual spirit-as-character work. One primary spirit per case; occasional returning spirits only.

## 7. 48-Volume Shape

48 volumes in supernatural mystery = **ritual-of-encounter cycles with a slow through-line accumulation**, not an epic plot arc.

**Volumes 1–12 (Cycle I — Learning to Hear):** Protagonist at early fluency. Encounters are bewildering, incomplete. Resolution comes through listening, not acting. Spirit cases introduce the central themes: grief, unfinished love, generational obligation. Through-line: protagonist accumulating sensory residue; first hint that this has a cost.

**Volumes 13–24 (Cycle II — The Weight of It):** Protagonist at full competence but beginning to feel the somatic toll. Cases become structurally more complex — spirits with multiple parties, ambiguous fault. A serialized arc (4–6 volumes) follows a spirit whose case requires protagonist to revisit her own family history. Through-line resolution: protagonist must perform a ritual of release on herself, not only others.

**Volumes 25–36 (Cycle III — Teaching and Distance):** Protagonist trains or mentors another sensitive. The through-line arc follows the mentor relationship and the tension between transmitting knowledge and preserving mystery. Cases in this cycle trend toward generational trauma — spirits from earlier in the 20th century, longer historical context.

**Volumes 37–48 (Cycle IV — Quiet Return):** Protagonist nearing the edge of how much she can hold. Final arc: a spirit whose unfinished business is the protagonist's own — something from her history she did not know she had left incomplete. Resolution in the final volume is neither triumph nor tragedy but *settling* — the protagonist finds a sustainable relationship with her sensitivity, having learned its limits. The final chapter ends on a threshold: she stands at a torii, the mist is present, and she has a choice about whether to walk through. We do not see her choose.

## 8. Panel Scaffolding

Per-panel fields for deterministic generation (9 fields):

1. `framing` — ELS / LS / MS / MCU / CU / environmental-insert / threshold-shot (character at boundary — doorway, water edge, torii passage)
2. `beat_role` — one of {encounter_sign, investigation, spirit_present, negotiation, ritual_step, release, aftermath, transition_threshold, atmospheric_breath}
3. `spirit_present_flag` — boolean; true requires spirit-register visual treatment (screentone fog layer, soft-edge spirit bubble if dialogue present, heavier line weight on spirit form)
4. `atmosphere_tone` — one of {daylight_human, dusk_liminal, deep_spirit, fog_transition, post_release}; drives screentone density and black-fill level
5. `ritual_step` — null or one of {preparation, invocation, listening, negotiation, binding, release, sealing}; when non-null, visual composition centers on the ritual object or gesture
6. `subject` — what/who is in frame; spirit forms named by descriptor ("child-spirit, 1940s dress, partial opacity") not generic label
7. `dialogue` — ≤15 words; null on atmosphere_breath and transition_threshold panels; spirit dialogue in archaic register
8. `caption` — protagonist voice, ≤18 words, somatic/observational only; null on spirit_present panels where visual carries the moment
9. `silence_flag` — boolean; true on atmosphere_breath, post_release, and transition_threshold beats; silence_flag=true must appear on ≥28% of panels; consecutive non-silent panels ≤5

## 9. Locale Weighting

**ja_JP (primary):** Natural market home. Yokai, shrine imagery, onryō tradition, *mono no aware* — all culturally embedded. Platform: LINE Manga (primary), ComicWalker (free-to-read). Demo: josei women 25–45 and seinen-adjacent male readership. Reference titles have JP sales bases: *Mushishi* 6M+, *Natsume* 17M+, *xxxHOLiC* 14M+.

**en_US (iyashikei-adjacent):** Existing US manga audience that imported *Mushishi*, *Natsume*, and *xxxHOLiC* is an established beachhead. Genre is not mainstream in the US but has a committed niche (Seven Seas, Viz Media have demonstrated licensing appetite). Position as "iyashikei with narrative spine" for US shelf discovery. Platform: manga digest format, manga aisle placement. Cover design should use iyashikei-register atmospheric palette (muted greens, dusk blues, fog whites — IYA-D "Winter Interior" register) with the addition of a single high-contrast threshold or spirit element.

**zh_TW / zh_CN (crossover via shared spirit-tradition aesthetic):** Taiwan and mainland China share deep spirit-world narrative traditions (ghost festivals, ancestral obligations, taoist spirit geography) that map cleanly onto the supernatural mystery register. The emotional content — unfinished obligation, grief crossing generations, the living's debt to the dead — is directly culturally resonant. TW platform: LINE Comics TW. CN platform: Kuaikan / Bilibili Comics with careful topic framing (avoid "ghost" framing in CN descriptions; prefer "memory," "remnant," "unfinished presence"). CN distribution gray-zone per spec D-19; AI disclosure required.

**ko_KR (rendered + held per OQ-5/D-18):** Webtoon-supernatural format adaptation potential strong but distribution held pending Korea AI Act enforcement clarity. Render now; ship when policy clarifies.

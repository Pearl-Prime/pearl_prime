# Workplace Drama / Comedy — Style Bible

Touchstones: *Aggretsuko* (Yeti / Sanrio), *Wotakoi: Love Is Hard for Otaku* (Fujita), *Kakushigoto* (Kouji Kumeta), *Shinya Shokudō* (side-work register). Strategic Shell #9. Brands carried: `career_lift_workplace`, `high_performer_workplace`, `executive_calm_workplace`, `morning_momentum_workplace`, `optimizer_workplace`, `focus_sprint_workplace`.

## 1. Market contract

Readers are millennial professionals 26–40 — late enough in career to feel the weight of institutional systems, early enough to still be asking whether the system is worth inhabiting. They are not reading for escape into fantasy; they are reading for *recognition* — the panel that names the thing they feel Monday morning but cannot say aloud. They will accept: burnout as narrative engine, imposter syndrome rendered with specificity, social anxiety played with deadpan comedy. They will reject: a morality-play ending where the system reforms, a boss who is simply evil, and any career advice delivered as a sermon. The genre's contract is: *I see you, and it is genuinely absurd*. The comedy register (Aggretsuko's death-metal karaoke) is the pressure valve that makes the burnout content bearable; neither element works without the other.

## 2. Visual grammar

- **Panels per page:** 5–7 (office environments reward tight grids; grid formality mirrors institutional constraint). Drop to 3–4 for end-of-day reflective sequences — the loosening of the grid *is* the decompression.
- **Words per page:** 60–110 (josei/seinen blend). Meeting scenes peak at 120+. Commute and corridor scenes drop to 25–40.
- **Dialogue-to-caption ratio:** ~55:45. Corporate dialogue is external performance; the 45% interior caption layer is where the character actually lives. The gap between what is said in the meeting room and what the caption says simultaneously is the genre's primary irony engine.
- **Silent-panel ratio:** 20–30%. Beat silences after a boss's pronouncement. Threatening silences during performance review close-ups. One full wordless panel per chapter minimum — usually the commute home, face reflected in train window.
- **Black-fill ratio:** 0.15–0.25 standard; spikes to 0.35–0.45 during burnout-spiral sequences. Drops to 0.05–0.10 in after-work "recovery" scenes.
- **Comic-release frequency:** One chibi or SD-art panel every 4–6 pages. Aggretsuko's death-metal sequences are the canonical example — the absurdist break *must* occur or the reader accumulates undischarged dread.
- **Screentone use:** Medium density. Gradient tones on office windows (gray fluorescent light feel), halftone fills on suits and cubicle walls. Mood tones shift to soft dot-screen in private/after-work scenes — the visual texture should literally soften when characters leave the building.
- **Line weight:** Slightly heavier contour than iyashikei; slightly lighter than standard seinen. Suits and architecture benefit from ruled-line precision. Character faces use a finer, more expressive line that resists institutional geometry.
- **Character-design uniformity:** Office attire creates visual sameness by design — this is a feature. Protagonist differentiation via small accessories, hair, posture. The moment a character changes out of work clothes, art style should visually relax.

## 3. Pacing

- **Chapter hook family:** *Deadline or meeting-room setup.* Open on a task due in 48 hours, a calendar notification, or a conference-room gathering shot before anyone speaks. The hook is institutional pressure, not personal crisis.
- **Chapter ending convention:** *End-of-day reflective beat.* Final panel is a transition out of the office (train platform, elevator doors closing, convenience store entrance at night) with a caption that recontextualizes the chapter's surface events. Not a cliffhanger — a *landing*.
- **Mid-chapter architecture:** First third = institutional pressure building. Middle = a social encounter that has subtext the protagonist cannot address directly. Final third = private processing — inner monologue, solo commute beat, or after-work scene with the "outside friend."
- **Per-volume arc shape:** Each volume carries two parallel arcs — a **project arc** (external: product launch, reorganization, performance review cycle) and an **interpersonal arc** (internal, slowly building). Stagger by half a volume; do not resolve both simultaneously.
- **Deadline-driven cliffhangers:** Legitimate — Friday-morning project failure, unexpected cc: on email, reorg announcement mid-chapter. One per volume maximum.

## 4. Dialogue

- **Register duality:** Corporate-formal and intimate-private must be visually distinct. Corporate speech uses clean rectangular bubbles, full sentences, polite desu/masu. Private speech uses rounded or irregular bubbles, sentence fragments, and register collapse. The contrast between bubble shapes is itself characterization.
- **Meeting-speech patterns:** Meeting dialogue is performative — characters say what they are *required* to say. Internal captions during meeting scenes carry the actual content. Pattern: character delivers a competent meeting contribution → caption reveals anxiety, doubt, or completely unrelated thought occurring simultaneously. The irony is the content.
- **Hallway/corridor micro-scenes:** 1–3 panel exchanges. Short, ambiguous, deniable. "How's the Nakamura file?" can mean three different things depending on who is asking.
- **After-work register:** When characters leave the building, sentence length contracts, honorifics drop, and ellipses appear. The linguistic decompression mirrors the physical one.
- **Chibi internal-monologue releases:** During meetings or formal encounters, the protagonist's internal state rendered as SD/chibi insert — character imagining screaming, the death-metal sequence, the fantasy of quitting dramatically. These inserts must be stylistically discontinuous from surrounding art. Restrict to 1–2 per chapter.

## 5. Character

- **Protagonist type:** Junior-to-mid-career professional, late 20s to mid-30s. Competent enough that incompetence is not the source of their struggle — the struggle is *the system itself*, or their own internalized version of it. Aggretsuko's Retsuko is the canonical template.
- **The boss:** Mid-level manager, not a villain. Their defining quality is *institutional capture* — they once wanted what the protagonist wants but traded it incrementally, and they are not fully aware of this. The most effective boss characterization shows occasional glimpses of the person they were before the institution finished shaping them.
- **The peer:** Same career stage as the protagonist, different coping strategy. Where the protagonist internalizes, the peer externalizes. The peer is the character who says things the protagonist only thinks.
- **The outside friend:** Does not work at the same company; may not work in the same industry. Provides a frame in which the protagonist's workplace concerns sound genuinely absurd when described aloud. Carries the "what are you actually doing with your life" question.
- **The institutional antagonist:** Not a person — the system itself: the review structure, the headcount freeze, the reorg, the metrics dashboard. When a specific person embodies institutional harm, that character must also have a comprehensible institutional motivation — they are not cruel, they are also caught.

## 6. Failure modes

1. **Corporate cynicism without interiority.** Depicting workplace absurdity without access to what it costs the protagonist emotionally. Satire without interiority is commentary, not story.
2. **Boss-as-villain oversimplification.** The moment the boss becomes a cartoon obstacle rather than a shaped-by-the-same-system person, the genre's central argument collapses.
3. **Preachy career advice.** Internal monologue that resolves into actionable career insight is the genre's capital crime. Emotional truth, not productivity wisdom, is the target register.
4. **Project arc at the expense of interpersonal arc.** Workplace drama that is only about the project becomes a procedural.
5. **Comedy register without consequence.** Chibi releases and absurdist sequences that never reconnect to the weight of the protagonist's actual situation.
6. **Uniform art register.** Failing to visually distinguish between office space and after-work space, between corporate dialogue and private dialogue.
7. **Resolution through the system.** The institution reforms, the bad boss is removed — these endings violate the genre contract. The protagonist changes or finds a way to carry the weight differently; the system does not change.

## 7. 48-volume series shape (career-stage arcs)

The 48-volume pre-plan structures as four **career-stage macro-arcs**, each approximately 12 volumes:

- **Arc I — Junior (Volumes 1–12):** Protagonist is new to the institution, learning its spoken and unspoken rules. Main question: *do I belong here, and do I want to?* Imposter syndrome is loudest. Volume 1 opens with Day One; Volume 12 closes with the first moment the protagonist trains someone else.
- **Arc II — Middle (Volumes 13–24):** Competence established; now the costs are visible. Burnout enters as a structural concern, not an episode. Main question: *what am I paying, and is it worth it?* Volume 24 closes on a threshold decision — not necessarily acted upon yet.
- **Arc III — Leadership (Volumes 25–36):** Protagonist has responsibility for others. The institutional antagonist is now partly *themselves* — they make the decisions they once resented receiving. Main question: *can I be inside the system without becoming it?*
- **Arc IV — Mentor (Volumes 37–48):** The outside-friend function shifts inward — protagonist becomes capable of seeing their own institutional capture with some distance. Main question: *what do I actually want this place to be?* Volume 48 does not resolve the institution; it closes on the protagonist's relationship to their own continuation.

## 8. Panel scaffolding

Per-panel fields (9):

1. `framing` — ELS (establishing shot, office floor) / LS / MS / MCU / CU / insert / chibi-insert
2. `formality_register` — `corporate` | `transitional` | `private` | `after-work` (drives bubble shape, line weight, register of dialogue)
3. `location_type` — `open_plan` | `meeting_room` | `corridor` | `elevator` | `commute` | `outside_office` | `home`
4. `beat_role` — `pressure_build` | `meeting_performance` | `subtext_exchange` | `inner_monologue_reveal` | `comic_release` | `end_of_day_descent` | `after_work_breath` | `cliffhanger_reveal`
5. `character_pose` — `formal_seated` | `standing_presentation` | `corridor_walk` | `slumped` | `commute_standing` | `after-work-relaxed` | `chibi-exasperation`
6. `comic_release_flag` — boolean; `true` = SD/chibi or absurdist-insert panel; must appear at least once every 4–6 pages
7. `caption_layer` — internal monologue text (≤25 words) that contradicts or runs parallel to spoken dialogue; `null` in after-work scenes where register is already private
8. `black_fill_intensity` — `low` (0.05–0.10, after-work recovery) | `standard` (0.15–0.25) | `high` (0.35–0.45, burnout-spiral)
9. `silence_flag` — boolean; silent panels must appear at minimum 20% of total panels per chapter; one full wordless panel per chapter mandatory

Constraint hint: `formality_register=corporate` panels must never have `comic_release_flag=true` unless the comic insert is explicitly framed as a protagonist's private fantasy.

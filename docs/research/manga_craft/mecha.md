# Mecha — Style Bible

Touchstones: *Neon Genesis Evangelion* (Anno/Sadamoto), *Mobile Suit Gundam: The Origin* (Yasuhiko), *Eureka Seven* (Bones), *RahXephon* (BONES/Izubuchi), *Knights of Sidonia* (Nihei Tsutomu), *Patlabor* (Headgear).

Genre Shell #15 — Mega-example tier (spec D-9: Evangelion-tier franchise revenue exemplar, $16B). Cognitive clarity brand persona, 15% allocation. Differs from sci_fi_cyberpunk: mecha is **scale-cinematic** (identity against mass, mortality against machine); cyberpunk is **system-noir** (individual against network). Core thematic registers: depression as mecha (Evangelion), scale-as-mortality, identity vs. machine, the cockpit as last private space.

## 1. Market Contract

Readers (14–35, skewing male in shounen/seinen mecha, mixed in psychological mecha) arrive for two simultaneous experiences: **spectacle** (giant machines colliding, scale that dwarfs human agency) and **interiority** (what it costs a person to be inside the machine). They will tolerate — in fact demand — extended quiet between action sequences. They will reject: mechanical detail that has no human consequence, escalation that never circles back to cost, and protagonists who suffer without psychological specificity. The contract is: *you will show me something impossibly large, and then you will make me feel how small and necessary the person inside it is.* A mecha series that forgets the cockpit interior has broken the contract. A mecha series that forgets the machine has failed a different way.

## 2. Visual Rules

*Source: `manga_dialogue_system/02:656` (pacing metrics); `manga_dialogue_system/01:561–596` (bubble shapes, SFX); `manga_cover_design/01:1643–1651` (scale, palette); `manga_cover_design/04:1789` (typography).*

- **Words per page:** 5–25, typical 12. Battle sequences compress toward 5; cockpit-crisis sequences expand toward 25 on the interior-monologue pages.
- **Dialogue lines per page:** 2–4.
- **Silent panels per chapter:** 5–8. Silence is structural — pre-launch stillness and post-battle aftermath demand wordless panels.
- **Chapter length:** 22–35 pages.
- **Silent-panel ratio:** 20–30%.
- **Dialogue-to-caption ratio:** ~50:50. Cockpit interior monologue carries 60% of emotional weight.
- **Black-fill ratio:** 0.25–0.40. Heavier than most genres — mechanical detail requires dense shadow to read three-dimensionally. Space sequences push toward 0.40 (void black).
- **Screentone:** Minimal. Cross-hatch and machine line preferred over tone. Where tone appears: metal gradient on mecha surfaces, void-grey for space. Organic flower/sparkle tone is category error.
- **Line weight:** Bold mechanical detail on mecha forms; lighter on human faces inside cockpits. The contrast between the two line weights signals the scale gap without an explicit caption.
- **Scale contrast (signature device):** A tiny human figure dwarfed by the mecha is the genre's primary recognition signal at thumbnail size. Compose covers and splash pages with mecha at 60–80% of canvas; human figure — when present — positioned low or at frame edge.
- **Bubble shapes:** Electronic/comms bubbles — sharp-cornered, screen-border style suggesting a monitor. Human cockpit speech — rounded but sharp-edged. Interior existential monologue — large, borderless, floating in abstract or void background. Military briefings — square narration boxes with columnar text.
- **Cover typography:** Zen Kaku Gothic New 900 (Japanese), Cinzel or Barlow Condensed (Latin/display); metallic silver / gunmetal palette; metallic stroke effect.
- **Programmatic fonts:** Audiowide (OFL) for robotic/comms speech; Exo 2 (OFL) for mecha status screens and HUD text overlays.

## 3. Pacing

- **Chapter cadence:** Contemplative → explosive. Open with stillness (hangar, briefing, pilot's face in low light). Build through cockpit prep. Erupt into battle. Collapse back into aftermath. Each chapter is a breath: inhale, hold, exhale.
- **Beat sequence (chapter-level):** mecha-launch → cockpit-crisis → battle → debrief. The debrief is never optional — it is where the cost is named, even obliquely.
- **Chapter hook:** Environmental or mechanical: the sound of hydraulics cycling, the hum of a reactor waking, a callsign transmitted into silence.
- **Chapter ending:** Either a cost made visible (damaged mecha, pilot's hands shaking, a callsign that no longer responds) or a strategic unresolved tension. Mecha does not use romantic-style "almost" cliffhangers; it uses tactical or psychological ones.
- **Spread usage:** 1–2 per volume. Reserved for first-launch (machine seen at full scale), maximum-impact battle beat, or horror-register reveal. Never spent on dialogue.
- **Per-volume arc shape:** Pilot-arc + mech-evolution-arc. Mecha-design changes are structural arc markers, not cosmetic updates.

## 4. Dialogue

*Source: `manga_dialogue_system/01:575–596` (SFX); `manga_dialogue_system/02:238–252` (register, exclamations).*

- **Register:** Military hierarchy enforces formal speech (pilots to commanders, crew to captains). Inside the cockpit during battle: raw, informal, fragmented. Formality collapses under g-force and fear; this collapse is characterization.
- **Pronoun use:** Watashi in command contexts; ore in combat. Shinji Ikari's boku is a deliberate signal — infantile for a child pilot.
- **Comms speech:** Cold, clipped, callsign-first. "Eva-01, status." "Unit Three, break off."
- **Interior monologue:** First-person, not addressed to anyone. Borderless or abstract-background panels. Carries the psychological weight the action sequences cannot.
- **Caption boxes:** Heavy in military planning sequences. Eureka Seven diary/journal captions are genre-standard.
- **Narration-to-dialogue ratio:** ~50:50.

**Core exclamations:**
- 了解！(Ryoukai!) — "Roger!"
- 発進！(Hasshin!) — "Launch!"
- 撃て！(Ute!) — "Fire!"
- 逃げちゃだめだ (Nige-cha dame da) — "I mustn't run away" (Evangelion register; reluctant-pilot archetype)
- 攻撃開始！(Kougeki kaishi!) — "Begin attack!"

## 5. Character

- **Protagonist archetype:** Reluctant teen pilot (Shinji: boku, paralysis, forced into cockpit) or burned-out adult (experienced pilot returning, traumatized, grieving). Both share a core trait: the machine demands more than they believe they can give.
- **Supporting cast:** Mech engineer (attachment to the machine; emotional anchor or exposition bridge), commander (authority figure — may be antagonist-adjacent or deeply compromised), rival pilot (parallel story; carries the version of the protagonist's arc that goes differently). Cast of 4–8.
- **Antagonist grammar:** Rival mecha (the equal-and-opposite machine), the alien/enemy system (unknowable scale), or the machine itself becoming antagonist (Evangelion register: the Eva that acts without the pilot's consent). The most powerful mecha antagonist is the one that was once safe.
- **The mecha as character:** Give the machine narrative personhood — a name used affectionately, a damage history, behaviors that exceed the pilot's commands. The relationship between pilot and mecha is a primary relationship of the series.
- **Emotional arc per volume:** Reluctance → forced action → cost recognized → fractional integration.

## 6. Failure Modes

1. **"Mech porn"** — mechanical detail without character interiority. The machine is the genre's body; it is not the genre's subject.
2. **Meaningless escalation in mech specs** — each arc introduces a more powerful machine without raising emotional stakes correspondingly.
3. **Ignoring physics for spectacle** — internal consistency in how mechas move and take damage builds reader trust.
4. **Cockpit bypass** — staging major battles entirely in exterior wide-shots with no cockpit interiority. The cockpit is the genre contract's delivery mechanism.
5. **The pilot as function** — treating the protagonist as a vehicle for action sequences rather than a psychology inside a machine.
6. **Commander-as-exposition-unit** — military briefings that exist only to explain the plot.
7. **Rival pilot with no arc** — the rival exists to be defeated. Structurally inert.
8. **Emotional resolution before the mecha is resolved** — the arc closes when both the pilot and the machine have changed.

## 7. 48-Volume Shape

- **Origin arc (vols. 1–8):** First encounter with the machine — pilot resists, submits, or is forced. Machine design: baseline. Theme: what is being asked of this person?
- **Trial arc (vols. 9–18):** Pilot begins to understand the machine's full scope — and hidden nature. First major mecha damage or loss. Machine design: scarred. Theme: what does the machine cost?
- **Ascendance arc (vols. 19–32):** Pilot-and-machine reach peak integration — then the system they serve is revealed as compromised. New or transformed machine. Theme: what are they fighting for?
- **Meaning-making arc (vols. 33–48):** The machine may be destroyed, transcended, or fundamentally altered. The pilot must answer: who am I without the cockpit? Theme: identity after the machine.

Each arc turn coincides with a visible mecha-design change so the narrative shift is legible visually.

## 8. Panel Scaffolding

Per-panel fields (8):

1. `scale_marker` — one of {cockpit_interior, exterior_ground, exterior_aerial_wide, space_void, hangar, command_room, debrief_room}
2. `mech_state` — one of {idle, launch, active_combat, damaged, destroyed, post-battle, absent}
3. `framing` — ELS / LS / MS / MCU / CU / insert / environmental-insert; low-angle preferred for mecha-dominant panels
4. `beat_role` — one of {pre_launch_stillness, cockpit_crisis, battle_action, aftermath_cost, debrief_reckoning, interior_monologue, scale_reveal}
5. `comms_overlay_present` — boolean; true = sharp-corner screen-style bubble, Audiowide font
6. `hud_text` — string or null; rendered in Exo 2; max 8 words; tactical/status content only
7. `action_intensity` — integer 0–5 (0 = complete silence; 5 = full-bleed impact panel with SFX at panel-breaking scale)
8. `sfx` — from canonical vocabulary above, or null; scale to `action_intensity`

Constraint hints: `action_intensity=0` (silence) on ≥20% of panels; consecutive `action_intensity ≥ 4` panels ≤ 3 before a mandatory drop to ≤ 2; `scale_marker=cockpit_interior` on ≥25% of battle-sequence panels.

## 9. Locale Weighting

- **ja_JP:** Origin and primary market. Serialization: Shounen/seinen magazines (Monthly Shounen Ace, Afternoon, Young Animal). Formal military speech and SFX vocabulary native.
- **en_US:** Strong Evangelion and Gundam audience. Tolerance for psychological mecha and slow-burn interiority high.
- **zh_TW / zh_CN:** Strong cyberpunk-mecha crossover audience. Scale-cinematic register resonates. zh_CN gray-zone distribution per spec D-19.
- **ko_KR (rendered + held per D-18):** Webtoon-mecha emerging. Vertical scroll favors exterior action — adapt pacing to preserve cockpit sequences.
- **France:** Strong mecha fanbase (Goldorak/Grendizer fandom, BD crossover readership). Classical gravitas of Cinzel typography well-calibrated for French market.

# Action / Battle Manga — Style Bible

Touchstones: *Naruto* (Kishimoto Masashi), *Dragon Ball* (Toriyama Akira), *One Piece* (Oda Eiichiro), *My Hero Academia* (Horikoshi Kohei), *Demon Slayer* (Gotouge Koyoharu), *Jujutsu Kaisen* (Akutami Gege), *Vinland Saga* (Yukimura Makoto).

Genre Shell #10 — Large tier. Carries: courage, resilience, somatic (body as tool not enemy), perseverance, finding strength. Renamed from `shonen` slug: demographic ≠ genre. Vinland Saga is action_battle/seinen; My Hero Academia is action_battle/shonen. Same archetype grammar, different word density.

## 1. Market Contract

Readers arrive carrying a wound of powerlessness, inadequacy, or isolation. The genre's unbreakable promise: *escalation is always available; limits exist to be broken; the body that suffers can become the body that prevails.* Readers will reject: power-ups without cost, mentors who survive too easily, ensemble cast abandoned for solo protagonist worship, and stakes that never land.

This is the highest-volume genre in global manga: Naruto 250M+, One Piece 530M+, Dragon Ball 260M+, Demon Slayer 150M+. The genre shell is the revenue multiplier. The `stoic_edge_battle` brand persona (CJK Catalog Plan §3, Tier 3) lives here: courage + resilience, 6 series target.

## 2. Visual Rules

| Metric | Value | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Panels per page | 5–9 | Higher than shojo/iyashikei; slanted, dynamic borders |
| Words per page | 30–80 | Action pages drop to 5–15; dialogue scenes reach 80 |
| Dialogue:Caption ratio | 70:30 | Heavy dialogue; captions for flashback and tactical inner voice |
| Pages per chapter | 18–22 | Weekly serialization standard (Jump/Magazine) |
| Black-fill ratio | 0.20–0.35 | High contrast; energy auras, shadow work, impact black |
| Silent panel % | 10–20% | Strategic silence at power-up peaks and post-battle breath beats |
| Screentone | Speed-line dense | Radial speed lines from impact point; motion arcs on limbs |
| Line weight | Bold + dynamic | Thick contour on protagonist; thinner environment lines |

Bubble shapes: spiky/jagged for battle cries and power declarations (scale to 12+ points at climax); round/oval default; scream bubbles that bleed off panel at arc-peak moments.

SFX vocabulary (giongo/giseigo/gitaigo): DON (impact/reveal), BAKI (bone crack), GOGOGO (menacing aura approaching), SHU (fast slash), ZUDADA (running at speed), KA (eye opening sharply/flash of light), GOOO (energy aura building). SFX are full visual elements occupying background space, not marginal labels.

Cover at thumbnail scale: warm palette (red/orange/yellow dominant), single hero or ensemble group in dynamic action pose, bold black-stroked title lettering, publisher badge upper-left, radial speed-line background from character center-of-mass.

## 3. Pacing

**Chapter level:** Every chapter closes on a hook. Permitted forms: mid-battle freeze-frame at moment of impact; antagonist revealing new power tier; unexpected ally or betrayal reveal; protagonist reaching a new physical/emotional limit. The last panel is the highest-value real estate.

**Within-chapter rhythm:** breath-then-strike. A high-energy exchange (3–5 pages of dense panel action) is followed by a 1-page breath beat — a wide establishing shot, a silent reaction panel, or a memory flash — before the next escalation.

**Power-up reveal protocol:** Build-up narration + observer reaction shots ("Impossible—!") + single protagonist declaration or wordless yell. The moment of transformation itself is usually silent or reduced to one word. Observer dialogue confirms the power shift for the reader.

**Per-volume shape:** One four-beat cycle per volume — Challenge → Setback → Growth → Triumph. Triumph in volume N is never the series ceiling; it becomes the new baseline for volume N+1.

## 4. Dialogue

**Register:** Declarative, direct, loud. Lines rarely exceed two sentences. Exclamations dominate. Philosophical shouts ("What is strength without something to protect?!") are genre-appropriate and expected. Motivational register is the default mode.

**Six canonical battle dialogue patterns:**
1. The Vow — "I will become [X]!" Stated early; echoed at crisis moments.
2. The Rival Acknowledgement — "You've gotten stronger… but not strong enough."
3. The Mentor's Cryptic Lesson — delivered pre-training; understood only post-mastery.
4. The Mid-Battle Flashback Trigger — "I remember now… what sensei said…"
5. The Nakama Declaration — "I'm not fighting alone anymore!"
6. The Limits-Breaking Cry — single word or name-yell at climax of power-up.

**Battle banter:** Mutual provocation masked as competitive respect. Pattern: "insult → clash → grudging acknowledgement → new challenge." Rivals push each other upward; contempt always has an undercurrent of admiration.

**Rallying speeches:** One per arc, maximum. Delivered when the team has lost hope. Short — five sentences or fewer. Earned through demonstrated sacrifice, not eloquence.

**Signature catchphrases:** Each major character has a verbal tic or recurring declaration that functions as identity signature. Assign in character design.

**Internal monologue during fights:** Three permitted functions only — tactical analysis, emotional revelation, flashback trigger. Do not use for exposition about the opponent's power system.

## 5. Character

**Protagonist archetype:** Underdog with hidden potential. Visibly inadequate in volume 1. Power derives from emotional core (protecting others, refusal to yield) rather than natural talent alone. The protagonist's body is the primary instrument of the series' somatic teaching — suffering made visible, recovery made legible. The reader positions themselves as peer, not observer.

**Supporting cast:** Rival (competition-as-love language; mirrors protagonist's growth), Mentor (cryptic, withholding, will sacrifice themselves in the right arc — but use this once), Team (each member carries a distinct wound and a distinct speech register; ensemble strength is the genre's structural advantage over solo-power-fantasy). Neglecting the ensemble is a primary failure mode.

**Antagonist design:** Each arc villain is more powerful than the last. The final villain of an arc should feel genuinely unbeatable until the protagonist integrates the arc's growth lesson. At deep structure: the villain embodies what the protagonist rejects in themselves. Surface defeat is mandatory and must satisfy fully.

**Demographic flexibility:** Same archetype grammar serves both shonen (younger reader, simpler word register, more exclamation marks, faster escalation) and seinen (older reader, denser internal monologue, higher cost per power-up, moral ambiguity in villain). Adjust word density and philosophical depth per target demographic; do not alter the core archetype grammar.

## 6. Failure Modes

1. **Power-creep escalation without a ceiling.** Endlessly raising power levels until stakes become meaningless. Fix: establish a conceptual ceiling early or pivot to tactical/strategic fight choreography after physical escalation maxes out.
2. **Exposition dumps during battle.** Characters monologuing their own ability mechanics mid-fight destroys tension. Exposition belongs in training arcs or pre-battle scenes.
3. **Repetitive arc formula without variation.** "Meet enemy → lose → train → win" works three times. Fourth repetition without structural variation loses the reader.
4. **Mentor death as default emotional escalation.** Earned once per series. Used twice, formula. Used three times, poisons reader trust.
5. **Talk-no-jutsu resolution.** Protagonist convinces villain to change through speech alone. Effective once as deliberate subversion; stale on any repetition.
6. **Abandoned training logic.** Detailed power systems introduced in early arcs then discarded for raw emotional-surge victories.
7. **Ensemble cast abandoned for protagonist.** Side characters exist only for the protagonist's benefit, with no independent arcs.

## 7. 48-Volume Shape

Four arc families, ~12 volumes each:

**Arc Family 1 — Training Arc (vols 1–12):** Protagonist weakness established. Mentor introduced. Core power system revealed. First real defeat. First growth loop (Ki-Sho-Ten-Ketsu: introduction → grueling training → breakthrough tied to emotional realization → new ability demonstrated in combat). Volume 12 closes with the protagonist recognizably different but clearly not at the ceiling.

**Arc Family 2 — Tournament Arc (vols 13–24):** External competitive structure forces protagonist against peers, not monsters. Rival relationships deepen. Ensemble members face their own version of the protagonist's core wound. The tournament is a mirror, not just a bracket. Volume 24 closes with an outcome that surprises the protagonist's prior self-image.

**Arc Family 3 — Revelation Arc (vols 25–36):** The system the protagonist has trained inside is revealed to be flawed or complicit. The mentor's secret. The cost of the power system to those outside the protagonist's circle. Moral complexity enters without abandoning the genre's fundamental optimism. This is where demographic register diverges most sharply: shonen version resolves via friendship and determination; seinen version allows genuine irresolution.

**Arc Family 4 — Final Confrontation Arc (vols 37–48):** The series villain is the protagonist's shadow at maximum power. All supporting cast arcs converge. The protagonist's body carries every training scar from vols 1–36 — this arc is the somatic integration of everything the series has cost. The final victory must feel genuinely earned through demonstrated sacrifice. Volume 48 closes at the genre's canonical integration beat: the cost is reconciled, not erased.

## 8. Panel Scaffolding

Per-panel fields (9):

1. `framing` — ELS / LS / MS / MCU / CU / ECU (eyes/fist/impact point) / impact-zoom (subject + radial speed lines, background abstracted)
2. `motion_lines` — none / arc (limb trajectory) / radial (impact from point) / parallel (linear speed) / rotational (spin technique) — scale 0–5 indicating density
3. `sfx_type` — impact (DON, BAKI, DOKA) / aura (GOGOGO, GOOO) / movement (SHU, ZUDADA) / reaction (KA, PIKU) / breath (HAA) / null
4. `character_pose` — neutral / attack-launch / impact-receive / guard / power-charge / collapse / rise / flashback-insert
5. `power_state` — baseline / focused / technique-active / power-up / exhausted / limit-break
6. `bubble_shape` — round (dialogue) / spiky-4 (surprise) / spiky-8 (declaration) / spiky-12 (power-peak) / scream-bleed (arc climax) / cloud (internal thought) / null
7. `dialogue` — ≤20 words; fragments and exclamations preferred; null for silent panels
8. `caption` — tactical inner voice or flashback trigger only; ≤18 words; null preferred during high-intensity action sequences
9. `silence_flag` — boolean; true at power-up reveal pages, post-battle breath beats, and mentor-departure moments; must appear on 10–20% of panels per chapter

Generator constraint: `silence_flag=true` minimum 10% of panels per chapter; consecutive non-silent panels maximum 7 before a mandatory breath beat.

## 9. Locale Weighting

| Locale | Weight | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **ja_JP** | Origin market | Weekly serialization (Jump, Sunday, Magazine) or monthly. B&W tankobon. Right-to-left. Effort-reward symmetry is non-negotiable reader contract. Sacrifice moments carry maximum cultural weight. |
| **en_US** | #1 import market | Manga aisle format (5"×7.5" B&W digest). Genre is highest-selling manga category in US retail. Dragon Ball, Naruto, MHA all top en_US charts. No format adaptation needed from ja_JP. |
| **zh_TW** | Strong | Tankobon import + licensed editions. Action/battle strongest genre performance in TW manga market. |
| **zh_CN** | Strong (gray-zone per D-19) | Vertical-scroll tiáomàn adaptation needed for platform performance on Kuaikan/Bilibili. Full color preferred. AI disclosure required. |
| **ko_KR** | Webtoon-action variant (rendered + held per D-18) | Vertical-scroll full-color webtoon format (Naver/Kakao). Korean action webtoon mature genre with own conventions. Episode-ending cliffhanger mandatory. |

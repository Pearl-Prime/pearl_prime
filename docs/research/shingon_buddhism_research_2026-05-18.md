## §0. Executive summary (read first)

Joshin is a Shingon teacher. Shingon — formally 真言宗 (Shingon-shū, "True Word School") and broadly **Mikkyō** (密教, "Esoteric Teaching") — is Japan's principal Vajrayāna (tantric) Buddhist tradition, founded by **Kūkai (空海, 774–835)**, posthumously **Kōbō Daishi (弘法大師)**. Its center is **Mount Kōya (高野山, Kōyasan)** in Wakayama Prefecture.

Shingon is **not Zen**. Both are Mahāyāna, both arose in Japan, but they are doctrinally and stylistically distinct registers:

- **Zen** strips ritual, distrusts conceptual elaboration, points at "direct experience," uses kōan and zazen.
- **Shingon** centers ritual, embraces a populous deity-cosmology, teaches through embodied form (mudrā, mantra, mandala), and treats the entire phenomenal world as the body, speech, and mind of the cosmic Buddha **Mahāvairocana (Dainichi Nyorai)**.

A Shingon teacher does **not** talk about kōan, satori, kenshō, "just sitting," or "the sound of one hand" — those would be Zen drift. A Shingon teacher talks about **the three mysteries (sanmitsu)**, **the syllable A (Ajikan)**, **Dainichi Nyorai**, **the two mandalas**, **Buddhahood in this very body (sokushin-jōbutsu)**, **the goma fire**, and **the unbroken transmission from master to disciple**.

The five most useful Shingon practices for the anxiety-topic application are listed in §6. The most accessible single anchor practice is **Ajikan** — visualization of the syllable A on a moon-disk on a lotus.

## §1. Lineage and history

### §1.1 Founder: Kūkai / Kōbō Daishi

| Date | Event |
|---|---|
| **774** (some sources 774; older 774 standard) | Born in Zentsūji, Sanuki Province (now Kagawa Prefecture on Shikoku) to an aristocratic family. |
| **791** | Begins Confucian classics at the Daigakuryō in Nara. |
| ~795–804 | Abandons orthodox studies for ascetic mountain wandering; composes the *Sangō Shīki* (三教指帰, "Indications of the Goals of the Three Teachings") at age 24, comparing Confucianism, Daoism, and Buddhism and arguing for Buddhism. |
| **804** | Sails for Tang China as a member of a state expedition. |
| **805** | Arrives at the Qinglong Monastery (青龍寺) in Chang'an (modern Xi'an); meets **Huiguo (惠果, 746–805)**, eighth patriarch of esoteric Buddhism, who initiates him through repeated **abhiṣeka (kanjō)** within months — Huiguo reportedly tells Kūkai he had been awaiting him "as if pouring water from one vase into another." |
| **806** | Returns to Japan as the ninth patriarch of esoteric Buddhism, bringing texts, ritual implements, and mandalas. |
| **812** | Conducts the first public initiations (abhiṣeka) at Takao-san. |
| **816** | Receives imperial grant of Mount Kōya from Emperor Saga; founds it as a training mountain. |
| **817** | Composes the *Sokushin-jōbutsu-gi* (即身成佛義, "Attaining Enlightenment in This Very Existence"), the *Shōji-jissō-gi* ("The Meanings of Sound, Sign, and Reality"), and the *Unji-gi* ("The Meanings of the Word Hūṃ"). |
| **823** | Granted the temple **Tō-ji (東寺)** in Kyoto — becomes the urban headquarters of the school. |
| **828** | Founds the Shugei Shuchi-in, an early school open to commoners. |
| **830** | Completes the *Jūjūshinron* (十住心論, "Treatise on the Ten Stages of the Development of Mind"). |
| **835** | Enters his final *samādhi* on Mount Kōya, 21st day of the 3rd lunar month, age 62. |

The Shingon legend — still actively held — is that Kūkai did not die at Kōya. He entered **eternal *samādhi*** (called **nyūjō**, 入定) on the eastern peak of Kōya, and remains there alive in deep meditation, waiting for the future Buddha Maitreya (Miroku Bosatsu). His mausoleum at the Okunoin cemetery is treated as the residence of a living teacher, and food offerings are placed there twice daily by current monks. For a Shingon teacher, **Kōbō Daishi is contemporaneous** — not a historical founder one reads about, but a present teacher one is in relationship with.

### §1.2 Transmission chain: India → China → Japan

The patriarchal lineage Shingon claims:

1. **Mahāvairocana Buddha** (cosmic, not historical) — the source.
2. **Vajrasattva** — receives the teachings on Iron Stūpa in South India.
3. **Nāgārjuna / Nāgabodhi** — opens the stūpa.
4. **Śubhakarasiṃha (善無畏, 637–735)** — brings the *Mahāvairocana Sūtra* to China.
5. **Vajrabodhi (金剛智, 671–741)** — brings the *Vajraśekhara Sūtra* lineage.
6. **Amoghavajra (不空, 705–774)** — major translator and systematizer.
7. **Huiguo (746–805)** — Kūkai's master.
8. **Kūkai (774–835)** — founder in Japan.

This is the **dual-mandala transmission**: the Womb Realm line from Śubhakarasiṃha and the Diamond Realm line from Vajrabodhi/Amoghavajra were synthesized by Huiguo and then transmitted to Kūkai. The two-mandala unity (see §2) is therefore a structural inheritance of the transmission itself, not a later interpretation.

### §1.3 Sub-schools: Kogi vs. Shingi

The principal division is between:

- **Kogi Shingon (古義真言宗, "Old Shingon")** — centered at **Kōyasan** (Kongōbu-ji, Mt. Kōya) and at **Tō-ji** in Kyoto. Doctrinally argues that Mahāvairocana preaches the Dharma directly from the *dharmakāya* (truth-body).
- **Shingi Shingon (新義真言宗, "New / Reformed Shingon")** — founded by the reformer **Kakuban (覚鑁, 1095–1143, posthumously Kōgyō Daishi)** at Mount Negoro. Doctrinally argues that the *dharmakāya* preaches through Mahāvairocana's *saṃbhogakāya* (enjoyment-body) — a subtle point of theology that produced a real institutional split. After Negoro was destroyed by Toyotomi Hideyoshi in 1585, Shingi monks scattered and founded the **Buzan-ha (豊山派)** at Hase-dera (Nara) and the **Chizan-ha (智山派)** at Chishaku-in (Kyoto).

Post-Edo there are roughly **18 Shingon sub-schools** with separate headquarters. The three a contemporary teacher is most likely to be ordained in are **Kōyasan Shingon-shū**, **Tō-ji Shingon-shū** (both Kogi), and **Chizan-ha / Buzan-ha** (Shingi).

**Operator-decision item:** Joshin's specific sub-school is not yet specified (see §8). The default if unstated should be **Kōyasan Shingon-shū** — it is by far the largest and best-known internationally, and is the lineage of the only Western Shingon-ordained teacher with significant English-language profile, Shinzen Young (ordained at Kōyasan, 1970–1973).

### §1.4 Living lineage holders today

Shingon has a much smaller Western footprint than Zen or Tibetan Vajrayāna. The handful of Western-facing teachers:

- **Shinzen Young** (Yōgi Shinzen) — ordained at Kōyasan 1970, returned to U.S. 1974. His later teaching synthesizes Shingon with Theravāda vipassanā and Zen, so he is **not** a pure-register Shingon voice, but his Kōyasan training is the strongest English-language Shingon-formation credential.
- **Hokai Sobol** — Croatian Shingon teacher, scholarly register, writes on the six great elements (rokudai) and Kūkai's philosophy for Western audiences.
- **Eijo Nishimura, Taisen Miyata, Kōshin Ohtani** and other Japanese-born teachers who have led American Kōyasan temples (e.g., Kōyasan Buddhist Temple, Los Angeles; New York Kōyasan Mission).

A useful contemporary register reference for Joshin's voice is **a Kōyasan-trained Japanese teacher who has lived long enough in the West to speak in clear English** but who has not blended in Zen vocabulary. The voice should be **embodied, ritual-literate, slightly more devotional than Zen, and unembarrassed about deity-language**.

## §2. Core doctrine — what makes Shingon distinct

### §2.1 The Three Mysteries (三密, Sanmitsu)

This is **the** signature doctrine. Every Buddha possesses three "mysteries" of activity: body, speech, and mind. These are mysteries because in their fullness they are inconceivable to ordinary cognition. The practitioner emulates and aligns with the three mysteries of Mahāvairocana through:

| Mystery | Buddha-activity | Practitioner-activity |
|---|---|---|
| **身密 (shinmitsu)** — body | The Buddha's cosmic embodiment | **Mudrā** (印, *in*): ritual hand gestures and bodily postures |
| **語密 (gomitsu)** — speech | The Buddha's preaching that pervades the cosmos | **Mantra / shingon** (真言): sacred sound, recited aloud or silently |
| **意密 (imitsu)** — mind | The Buddha's enlightened mind | **Mandala visualization** and **samādhi**: contemplative absorption |

When the three are performed simultaneously and in alignment, the practitioner's body, speech, and mind become indistinguishable from those of Mahāvairocana — and this **is** Buddhahood.

Kūkai writes (paraphrased from *Sokushin-jōbutsu-gi*): "If a practitioner makes a mudrā with the hands, recites a mantra with the mouth, and dwells in samādhi in the mind, then the three mysteries become *kaji* (加持, mutual empowerment), and Buddhahood is realized in this very body."

The phrase **"every word uttered is a mantra, every bodily movement a mudrā"** captures the goal: not that one performs ritual at specific times, but that the entire phenomenal life eventually becomes ritual disclosure.

### §2.2 Sokushin-jōbutsu (即身成仏) — "Buddhahood in this very body"

The doctrine that most sharply differentiates Shingon from earlier (exoteric) Buddhist schools. Where exoteric Mahāyāna held that Buddhahood requires three immeasurable aeons (三大阿僧祇劫) of cultivation, Kūkai argued that the esoteric teachings make Buddhahood available **in this body, in this life**.

"This very body" (即身, *sokushin*) does not mean only the physical flesh — it means *the lived embodied existence of body-and-mind as one whole*. Three readings of *sokushin-jōbutsu* in the tradition:

1. **Intrinsic Buddhahood** — "in itself one's body already is an actualized Buddha" (the body is the *dharmakāya*).
2. **Realized Buddhahood through empowerment** — "with the present body one becomes a Buddha" (through *kaji*, mutual empowerment).
3. **Manifest Buddhahood** — "quickly one's body becomes a Buddha's" (through accelerated esoteric practice).

A Shingon teacher therefore does not point students toward a future event or a far-off attainment. The teacher points toward a recognition that is **already structurally available in the present embodied situation**, accessed through ritual alignment of the three mysteries.

### §2.3 Mahāvairocana (Dainichi Nyorai, 大日如来)

The center of Shingon cosmology. Mahāvairocana ("Great Illuminator") — Japanese **Dainichi Nyorai**, "Great Sun Tathāgata" — is the **cosmic Buddha** whose body is the universe itself. Not a deity who created the universe and stands apart, but the universe-as-Buddha. The historical Buddha Śākyamuni is, in Shingon cosmology, a *nirmāṇakāya* (manifestation-body) of Mahāvairocana.

Mahāvairocana's body is composed of the **six great elements (六大, rokudai)**: earth, water, fire, wind, space, and consciousness. The first four are the four physical states; space (*ākāśa*, 空) is the spaciousness that allows them to interpenetrate; consciousness (*vijñāna*, 識) is the awareness that knows them. The doctrine that all six interpenetrate without obstruction (*rokudai mu-ge*, 六大無碍) means that **mind and matter are not separate** — they are equally the body of Dainichi.

### §2.4 The two mandalas (両界曼荼羅, Ryōkai Mandara)

| Mandala | Japanese | Sanskrit | Aspect |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Womb Realm** | 胎蔵界 (Taizōkai) | Garbhadhātu | **Principle** (*ri*, 理): compassion, the generative matrix, the world as it nurtures awakening |
| **Diamond Realm** | 金剛界 (Kongōkai) | Vajradhātu | **Wisdom** (*chi*, 智): the indestructible adamantine knowledge of the awakened state |

In both mandalas Dainichi sits at the center; they are two views of the same single reality. The Womb is the world flowing **downward** as compassion from the source; the Diamond is the world ascending **upward** as wisdom toward the source. A Shingon priest receives two distinct abhiṣekas, one for each mandala, before being recognized as a full **ācārya** (阿闍梨, *ajari*, "master").

For everyday teaching purposes the symbolism collapses cleanly: **the world is both already a womb of compassion and already a diamond of wisdom** — Joshin's voice can lean on either or both depending on context.

### §2.5 Esotericism, transmission, and kanjō (abhiṣeka)

Shingon is *mikkyō* — secret/esoteric — in a specific sense. The "secrecy" is not gnostic withholding; it is the principle that **the teaching is transmitted from master to disciple, embodied, in person**, and is not fully conveyable in writing. Texts are insufficient; the living transmission completes them.

The transmission ritual is **kanjō (灌頂, abhiṣeka, "sprinkling from the crown")**:

- **Kechien-kanjō (結縁灌頂)** — "bond-establishing initiation," open to lay people; held publicly at Kōyasan twice yearly (spring and autumn). The blindfolded initiate throws a flower onto the mandala; wherever it lands names the deity they will hold as their personal *honzon* (本尊, principal Buddha).
- **Jukai-kanjō / Denbō-kanjō (傳法灌頂)** — full Dharma transmission, conferred by a master on a disciple who has completed the four-fold preliminaries (*shido kegyō*, 四度加行) and is being recognized as an *ajari*.

Kūkai's transmission from Huiguo at Qinglong-si is the prototype, and every Shingon priest's authority traces back through an unbroken chain of these face-to-face transmissions.

## §3. Practices — substantive teacher-bank content

### §3.1 Daily liturgical structure

A representative Shingon daily service (Kōyasan style, lay or temple) — useful as the **rhythmic skeleton** for any Joshin atom that depicts practice:

1. **Veneration** — opening prostrations and "*On sarva-tathāgata-pāda-vandanam*."
2. **Repentance** (懺悔, *sange*).
3. **Three Refuges** (三帰, *sanki*) — taking refuge in Buddha, Dharma, Saṅgha.
4. **Three Jewels affirmation** (三宝, *sanbō*).
5. **Ten Precepts** (十善戒, *jūzenkai*).
6. **Bodhicitta** vow (発菩提心, *hotsu-bodaishin*).
7. **Heart Sutra** (般若心経, *Hannya Shingyō*) — recited fully.
8. **Thirteen Buddha mantras** (十三仏真言, *jūsanbutsu shingon*) — one mantra each for thirteen tutelary Buddhas including Fudō Myōō, Shaka Nyorai, Monju Bosatsu, Fugen Bosatsu, Jizō Bosatsu, Miroku Bosatsu, Yakushi Nyorai, Kannon Bosatsu, Seishi Bosatsu, Amida Nyorai, Ashuku Nyorai, Dainichi Nyorai, and Kokūzō Bosatsu.
9. **Mantra of Light** (光明真言, *Kōmyō Shingon*) — recited typically 7 or 21 times.
10. **Gohōgō (御宝号)** — refuge in Kōbō Daishi: "*Namu Daishi Henjō Kongō*" (南無大師遍照金剛).
11. **Dedication of merit** (回向, *ekō*) — extending the merit to all beings.
12. **Closing aspiration**.

The Heart Sutra is recited in every Shingon service. The Shingon version is the *rufubon* (流布本) — a 262-character text with the **"Bussetsu" (佛說, "the Buddha spoke")** opening that Zen versions omit. This is one of the most reliable surface markers in a text: **a Shingon teacher will introduce the Heart Sutra as "*Bussetsu Maka Hannya Haramita Shingyō*" not as "*Maka Hannya Haramita Shingyō*"**.

### §3.2 Goma (護摩) — the fire ritual

The single most visually distinctive Shingon practice. **Goma** is the Japanese form of Sanskrit *homa*, a ritual of offerings poured into a consecrated fire. The fire is understood as the **purifying wisdom of the Buddha** (the *jñāna* of Fudō Myōō, who is invoked as the central deity of the rite). Inscribed wooden prayer sticks (**gomagi**, 護摩木) carrying written prayers and intentions are fed into the fire and consumed.

In a goma the priest is in full robes at an altar, before a fire-pit, with conch (**horagai**, 法螺貝) and drum (**taiko**) accompaniment. The rite is performed daily at most major Shingon temples. Lay people commission specific goma for: protection, healing, fulfillment of intentions, dispelling obstacles (*sokusai*, 息災), and the dead.

For an anxiety/mental-health teacher context: **the goma is the canonical Shingon image of "burning what no longer serves."** A Joshin atom about releasing intrusive thoughts, ruminative loops, or compulsive worry can quite naturally use the goma as metaphor or as an actual recommended observance.

### §3.3 Mantras — the speech-mystery

Every Shingon practitioner has a set of core mantras. The most commonly cited:

- **Dainichi Nyorai mantra (Womb-Realm form, the short "five-syllable form")**: **"On a-bi-ra-un-ken (Bazara-da-do-ban)"** — Japanese rendering of *Oṃ a vi ra hūṃ khaṃ*. The five syllables represent the five elements (earth-water-fire-wind-space) of Dainichi's body. This is the form for which the user's prompt asked specifically; it is correct.
- **Dainichi Nyorai mantra (Diamond-Realm form)**: "*On bazara-da-do-ban*" (*Oṃ vajra-dhātu vaṃ*).
- **Mantra of Light (光明真言, Kōmyō Shingon)**: **"*On abokya beirōshanō makabodara mani handoma jinbara harabaritaya un*"** — Japanese rendering of *Oṃ amogha vairocana mahāmudrā maṇi-padma jvāla pravartaya hūṃ*. Recited to purify karma; sand empowered with this mantra is sprinkled on graves. It is "one of the most common elements of Shingon services and liturgies… appropriate for practice both by lay and ordained people" (Wikipedia, *Mantra of Light*).
- **Fudō Myōō mantra**: "*Nōmaku samanda bazaradan senda makaroshada sowataya un tarata kanman*" — Japanese rendering of *Namaḥ samanta-vajrāṇām caṇḍa mahāroṣaṇa sphoṭaya hūṃ traṭ hāṃ māṃ*. The mantra of immovable wisdom — used when a practitioner needs to hold steady through difficulty.
- **Gohōgō**: "*Namu Daishi Henjō Kongō*" (南無大師遍照金剛) — "Homage to the Great Teacher who Illuminates All-Pervasively, the Diamond." This is the **refuge phrase for Kōbō Daishi himself** and the single most recognizable Shingon devotional utterance. A Shingon teacher will end a teaching, a pilgrimage stage, or a moment of difficulty with this phrase. Pilgrims on the Shikoku 88 walk speak it constantly.
- **Heart Sutra mantra (ending)**: "*Gyatei gyatei haragyatei harasōgyatei boji sowaka*" — Japanese form of *gate gate pāragate pārasaṃgate bodhi svāhā*. Used at the close of the Heart Sutra.

Pronunciation note: Shingon recites mantras in **Sino-Japanese phonetics**, not in any Sanskritic Indian style. This means the Japanese transliterations above are the canonical *spoken* form, not approximations.

### §3.4 Mudrā — the body-mystery

The two most important mudrās for Joshin-voice teaching:

- **Hokkai-jō-in (法界定印)** — the cosmic *samādhi* mudrā: hands resting in the lap, right palm over left palm, thumbs touching. This is the Dharmakāya mudrā, the body-of-truth mudrā, the form in which Dainichi is depicted in the Womb Realm. **It is the default sitting mudrā for Shingon meditation** including Ajikan.
- **Chi-ken-in (智拳印)** — the "wisdom-fist" mudrā: left hand makes a fist with the index finger extended; right hand wraps around the left index finger. This is Dainichi's mudrā in the Diamond Realm. It is the more dramatic, more recognizable Shingon mudrā in art.

There are also the **five Dhyāni-Buddha mudrās** (for the four directional Buddhas plus Dainichi at center), and elaborate sets used in the four preliminary practices (*shido kegyō*). For everyday teaching purposes, **the hokkai-jō-in is the mudrā Joshin would describe to a layperson learning to sit**.

### §3.5 Ajikan (阿字観) — meditation on the syllable A

The lay-accessible Shingon meditation par excellence — and the practice that should anchor most "how do I actually practice" Joshin atoms.

**Form:**
- Sit in hokkai-jō-in mudrā (hands in lap, thumbs touching).
- Visualize, in front of you (or in your heart center, depending on the form), a **full white moon-disk** (gachirin, 月輪).
- On the moon-disk, visualize a **white lotus flower** (renge, 蓮華), opening.
- On the lotus, visualize the **Siddham letter A (अ / 阿)** — the seed syllable.
- Hold the three together: moon → lotus → A.

**Meaning:**
- The syllable **A** is the source of all sounds in Sanskrit (every consonant carries an implicit A), and metaphysically it is the seed of **non-origination** (本不生, *honpushō*) — the recognition that nothing arises in itself, all phenomena are empty of intrinsic existence.
- The moon-disk represents the **luminous awareness** that knows.
- The lotus represents **awakening rising out of the muddy world** without being stained by it.

Ajikan, susokukan (breath-counting), and gachirin-kan (moon-disk-only) are the **three preliminary meditations** that every Shingon priest learns before beginning the four preliminaries. They are also the **three practices most often taught to lay people** because they require no robes, no implements, no mandala-room, and minimal initiation.

### §3.6 Susokukan (数息観) — breath-counting

The simplest Shingon meditation. Inhale, exhale, count "one." Inhale, exhale, "two." Continue to ten, then start at one. Return when the mind wanders. This is the Japanese name for what early Buddhism calls *ānāpānasmṛti* — mindfulness of breathing. Shingon includes it as a preliminary because the steadied breath is the substrate for mantra and visualization.

Note: Zen also teaches susokukan. The difference is **what follows**: in Shingon, susokukan is **a doorway into Ajikan and mantra practice**, not an end-in-itself. A Shingon teacher will say something like, "First the breath becomes steady; then on that steady breath, the mantra rides; then in the silence between mantras, the A appears." A Zen teacher might say, "Just count the breath. There is nothing else to do." Same exercise, different framing.

### §3.7 Pilgrimage: the Shikoku 88 and Mount Kōya

- **Shikoku 88-temple pilgrimage (四国八十八ヶ所, Shikoku Hachijūhakkasho)** — a 1,200-km circular walking pilgrimage around Kūkai's home island, linking 88 temples associated with him. Pilgrims (**henro**, 遍路) wear white robes and conical sedge hats and chant *Namu Daishi Henjō Kongō*. About 150,000 people do part or all of it each year. The pilgrimage is **explicitly Shingon** and explicitly held to be **walked with Kōbō Daishi as companion** — the hat is inscribed *dōgyō ninin* (同行二人, "two travelers walking together"), meaning the pilgrim and Kōbō Daishi.
- **Mount Kōya (高野山)** — the headquarters, the cemetery (Okunoin) where Kōbō Daishi resides in *samādhi*, the temple-town with ~120 sub-temples, ~50 of which host overnight lodgers (**shukubō**, 宿坊). UNESCO World Heritage since 2004.

These are the two great geographic/embodied anchors of Shingon practice. A Joshin atom can authentically reference *henro*, *shukubō*, *Okunoin*, *dōgyō ninin*, *gohōgō* — all four are everyday Shingon vocabulary.

| 数息観 | susokukan | breath-counting | The preliminary meditation |
| 四度加行 | shido kegyō | the four preliminary practices | Priest training |

### §4.2 Common Sanskrit terms a Shingon teacher uses

*Dharmakāya, vajra, mudrā, mantra, mandala, abhiṣeka, samādhi, bodhicitta, dhāraṇī, ācārya, hūṃ, oṃ, svāhā, prajñāpāramitā, śūnyatā* (Japanese: *kū*, 空).

### §4.3 Zen drift — terms a Shingon teacher would NOT naturally use

These would all be **red flags** in a Joshin atom indicating accidental re-introduction of Zen content:

- **kōan / 公案** — Zen-specific pedagogical paradox-puzzle. A Shingon teacher does not "give a kōan."
- **satori / 悟り** — although Shingon uses *enlightenment* / *awakening* language, *satori* as the canonical term-of-art for sudden insight is Zen.
- **kenshō / 見性** — "seeing one's nature," Zen sudden-awakening term.
- **zazen / 坐禅** — "seated Zen." Shingon meditators sit, but they don't call it zazen — they call it *Ajikan*, *susokukan*, *gachirin-kan*, etc.
- **shikantaza / 只管打坐** — "just sitting," the Sōtō Zen method.
- **mu / 無** — the Zen "Mu" kōan.
- **dokusan / 独参** — Zen private interview.
- **roshi / 老師** — the Zen honorific for a senior teacher. Shingon uses *ajari* (阿闍梨) or *daiajari* (大阿闍梨) or *sōjō* (僧正). For Kūkai specifically: *Daishi* (大師).
- **"beginner's mind" / shoshin / 初心** — Shunryū Suzuki's signature Zen phrase. Not Shingon-native.
- **"just this" / "just sitting" / "nothing special" / "ordinary mind"** — the deliberately reductive Zen register.
- **"the sound of one hand," "what is your original face,"** etc. — Zen kōan-tropes.

### §4.4 Phrases natural to a Shingon teacher

(Use these as model phrasings for Joshin atoms.)

- "*Namu Daishi Henjō Kongō.*"
- "We say *dōgyō ninin* — we walk with Kōbō Daishi."
- "Form the mudrā. Recite the mantra. Hold the image in mind. These three together — the three mysteries — and Dainichi's body becomes your body."
- "The syllable *A* is the seed of non-origination. Before any word, there is *A*. Before any thought, there is *A*."
- "Your body is already the body of Dainichi. The practice does not add anything. It removes what hides this."
- "The goma fire is the wisdom that burns what no longer serves."
- "On the moon-disk, the lotus. On the lotus, the *A*."
- "Buddhahood in this very body — *sokushin-jōbutsu*. Not after death. Not in some future life. Here, in this body, now."

## §5. Voice / register characteristics — how a Shingon teacher talks

A Shingon teacher's voice differs from a Zen teacher's voice in five concrete ways:

| Dimension | Zen voice | Shingon voice |
|---|---|---|
| **Toward ritual** | Suspicious; suggests letting forms drop | Embracing; ritual *is* the path |
| **Toward deity-language** | Avoids; "no Buddha outside the mind" | Comfortable; Dainichi, Fudō, Kannon are spoken of as present |
| **Toward conceptual elaboration** | Cuts it off; "show me, don't tell me" | Welcomes it; cosmology, doctrine, lineage all matter |
| **Toward the body** | Emphasized but minimally (posture, breath) | Centrally embodied — mudrā, chant, fire, sand, smell of incense |
| **Toward the lineage founder** | Often abstracted (Bodhidharma is more legendary than present) | Living and contemporaneous — Kōbō Daishi is *here*, on Kōya, now |

The Shingon register is **warmer, more devotional, more sensory, more cosmological, more elaborated** than the Zen register. It is also **less paradoxical and less iconoclastic** — a Shingon teacher does not slap students, does not say things like "if you meet the Buddha on the road, kill him," does not push toward a sudden break.

What a Shingon teacher **does** push toward: **repetition until alignment is so steady that the three mysteries operate continuously.** It is a path of accumulation, alignment, and habituation, not a path of sudden rupture.

A way to remember it: **Zen is austere; Shingon is dense.** Zen subtracts; Shingon arranges. Zen points at the moon and says "the finger is not the moon"; Shingon paints a moon-disk and tells you to put a lotus on it and a seed-syllable on the lotus and sit in front of it for an hour.

**Example contemporary quote (Hokai Sobol on the six elements):** "Form is mind and mind is form, without obstruction or limitation" (attributed to Kūkai, used by Sobol as the central statement of Shingon's non-dual cosmology). The willingness to **state a metaphysical thesis directly** rather than dance around it is a Shingon trait.

**Example Kūkai quote (closing of Sangō Shīki, age 24):**
> "Eternity, Bliss, the Self, and Purity are the summits on which we ultimately belong. I know the fetters that bind me in the triple world; why should I not give up the thought of serving the court?"

Note the four nouns — Eternity, Bliss, Self, Purity. These are the *four guṇas of nirvāṇa* in Mahāyāna and Kūkai uses them affirmatively. Compare to the early Theravāda and early Zen registers, which emphasize *anicca*, *dukkha*, *anattā* (impermanence, suffering, non-self). Shingon's register is **affirmative** where Zen's is often **negative**. This is one of the cleanest single tells.

## §6. Shingon applied to anxiety / mental-health (the Joshin catalog use-case)

The operator's catalog uses Joshin specifically for the **anxiety topic**. This section is the most operationally important: it is where Pearl_Editor needs explicit doctrinal-and-practice guidance to author atoms that are both genuinely Shingon and genuinely useful for an anxiety-overwhelm-hypervigilance audience.

### §6.1 The Shingon diagnosis of anxiety

Shingon does not have a clinical anxiety category. But its cosmology gives a frame: **anxiety is what happens when the three mysteries are out of alignment.** The body is bracing (mudrā distorted), the speech is looping (mantra replaced by rumination), and the mind is flooded with fragmented images (mandala fragmented). The Shingon **remedy** is therefore not philosophical reframing (as Zen might offer) and not exposure (as CBT might offer) — it is **realignment of the three mysteries**.

This is unusually well-suited to modern anxiety because anxiety is a **body-speech-mind disorder simultaneously**: somatic hyperarousal, mental looping, often verbal/inner-speech overactivity. Shingon's tripartite practice addresses all three channels at once.

### §6.2 The three mysteries applied to anxiety regulation

| Channel | Anxiety pattern | Shingon intervention |
|---|---|---|
| **Body** | Hypertonic, braced, shallow breath, restless | **Mudrā** — specifically *hokkai-jō-in* — places the hands in a deliberate, sustained, symmetric posture. This is a somatic anchor. Combined with seated stillness and breath, it interrupts hyperarousal. |
| **Speech** | Rumination, intrusive thoughts, inner verbal loop | **Mantra recitation** — most accessibly *Kōmyō Shingon* (Mantra of Light) or the short Dainichi mantra (*On a-bi-ra-un-ken*). Replacing ruminative inner speech with rhythmic, repeated, semantically stable sacred sound. This is *not* thought-suppression — it is *replacement of one verbal stream with another*. |
| **Mind** | Fragmented, catastrophizing, image-flooded | **Visualization** — *gachirin-kan* (moon-disk alone) or *Ajikan* (moon-lotus-A). Replacing chaotic imagery with a stable, luminous, unified single image. The single image holds. |

The deep claim: when all three are sustained simultaneously for even five minutes, the parasympathetic shift is reliable, and the practitioner experiences themselves as **already at home in their own body**, not as someone who needs to defend against an unsafe world.

### §6.3 The five most actionable practices for the anxiety application

In order of accessibility — first practice requires nothing; later practices benefit from a teacher.

1. **Gohōgō recitation** — silently or aloud, repeat "*Namu Daishi Henjō Kongō*." Like the Pure Land *nembutsu* in form (repeated short refuge phrase), but Shingon-native. Useful in transit, in waiting rooms, during anxiety spikes. Reframes the present moment as walking-with-Kōbō-Daishi (*dōgyō ninin*) rather than walking-alone.
2. **Susokukan** — breath-counting 1 to 10, repeat. The simplest meditation. Operates on the speech-channel (silent counting interrupts rumination) and the body-channel (the breath naturally deepens). Five minutes a day, twice a day.
3. **Hokkai-jō-in + Kōmyō Shingon** — sit in the cosmic-samādhi mudrā and recite the Mantra of Light. Body + speech together. Three to seven repetitions of the mantra is enough for a beginner; build to 21, then 108.
4. **Gachirin-kan** — moon-disk visualization. Once the breath and mudrā are steady, place a full white moon-disk in front of you (or in the heart center). Hold it for five minutes. This is the **gateway** to Ajikan but stands on its own as a luminous anchor against fragmented imagery.
5. **Ajikan** — the full A-syllable visualization. Moon → lotus → A. With breath, mudrā, and the breath-paced silent mantra "*A*." This is the **complete three-mysteries practice in lay form**: body in mudrā, speech in A, mind on the A-image. Ten to twenty minutes once daily is sufficient.

Practices 1–3 are immediately available to anyone with no transmission and minimal instruction. Practices 4–5 are also available to lay people but ideally with a teacher or written guide (Marcel Votlucka's *Intro to Ajikan Meditation* with an attached Ajikan manual translation is a strong public resource).

### §6.4 The wider Shingon repertoire for distress

For Joshin atoms about specific applications:

- **For overwhelm / "the world is too much"**: *Rokudai mu-ge* — the six elements interpenetrate. The body you are in is already made of the same elements as the mountains. You are not separate from what overwhelms you, so there is nothing to defend against. The frame is **dissolution-into-the-elements**, not exposure-to-the-elements.
- **For hypervigilance / chronic threat-scanning**: **Fudō Myōō**. Fudō is the Immovable. His sword cuts through ignorance; his noose binds wrong views; he stands in flame. A Shingon teacher invokes Fudō as the part of awareness that **does not flinch**. The Fudō mantra (*Nōmaku samanda bazaradan...*) is the most appropriate mantra when steadiness is the specific need.
- **For grief and loss**: the **Mantra of Light**, and pilgrimage to **Okunoin** (literal or imagined) where Kōbō Daishi resides in *samādhi*. The Mantra of Light is canonically used for the dead and for the karma-burdens of the living.
- **For decision paralysis and self-criticism**: the **kechien-kanjō flower-throw**. The image of throwing a blindfolded flower onto the mandala and accepting wherever it lands as one's path is a powerful frame for letting go of perfectionistic control.
- **For ruminative spiritual seeking** ("am I doing this right? am I making progress?"): the doctrine of *sokushin-jōbutsu*. The Buddha you are seeking *is already the body you are in*. Practice does not produce a Buddha; it removes the obscurations that hide one.

### §6.5 Contemporary application precedent

There is no major published Shingon-specific psychotherapy literature in English (unlike Zen — see Suzuki, Watts, contemporary mindfulness research). However:

- **Shinzen Young** has built a contemporary meditation system ("Unified Mindfulness") with explicit anxiety, pain, and stress applications — though his system blends Shingon with vipassanā and Zen. His writings show **Shingon-trained sensibility** (he is comfortable with mantra, embodiment, and ritual) within a modern psychological vocabulary.
- **Hokai Sobol** teaches the six elements and *kaji* (mutual empowerment) to Western audiences with implicit application to fragmentation and Cartesian-dualism distress.
- **The broader Buddhist mental-health literature** (Buddhistdoor, Lion's Roar, Tricycle) consistently finds that practices combining breath, body, and sacred sound show measurable effect on anxiety. Shingon's three-mysteries structure is **precisely** this combination — so the supporting research base, while not Shingon-labeled, applies cleanly.

For a Joshin atom on anxiety, the operative ground is therefore: **anchor in the three mysteries, give a concrete practice (one of the five above), name what is being addressed (body/speech/mind) without medicalizing it, and end with the gohōgō or with the *dōgyō ninin* frame.**

## §7. Compare / contrast table — Shingon vs. Zen vs. Theravāda

For unambiguous migration of old Joshin content from Zen to Shingon, and for general Pearl_Editor discrimination:

| Axis | **Shingon (Joshin's actual tradition)** | **Zen** | **Theravāda** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Yāna** | Vajrayāna (Mahāyāna esoteric) | Mahāyāna | Theravāda |
| **Founder in Japan / first teacher** | Kūkai (空海, 774–835) | Eisai (Rinzai), Dōgen (Sōtō), 12th–13th c. | (Not Japanese; Sri Lankan / SE Asian) |
| **Founder-relationship** | Kōbō Daishi is *living*, in *samādhi* on Kōya | Bodhidharma legendary, historically distant | Buddha is historical, fully passed into parinirvāṇa |

| **Liberation timeline** | *Sokushin-jōbutsu* — this very body, this life | This life through sudden awakening | Multiple lives; full arahantship usually held to be distant |

## §8. Operator-decision items (ambiguities surfaced by this research)

1. **Sub-school for Joshin.** Shingon has ~18 sub-schools. My recommendation, unless the operator specifies otherwise, is **Kōyasan Shingon-shū** (Kogi branch, headquartered at Kongōbu-ji, Mount Kōya). Reasons: (a) largest and most internationally recognized, (b) the only Shingon sub-school with significant English-language teacher presence (Shinzen Young trained here), (c) most common for shukubō stays and so the most recognizable to a Western audience that has read travel writing about Kōya, (d) the Shikoku 88 pilgrimage is principally associated with Kōyasan. **Decision-needed: confirm Kōyasan Shingon-shū as Joshin's lineage, or specify alternative.**

2. **Joshin's specific ordination status.** Is Joshin a *kechien-kanjō* lay practitioner, a *jūshoku* (resident temple priest), a *daiajari* (fully transmitted master with all four-fold preliminaries completed), or an unordained but knowledgeable practitioner? The voice register is significantly different across these levels. **Default recommendation: full ajari (阿闍梨), Kōyasan-lineage.** This is consistent with a teacher persona authoritative enough to give substantive guidance on practice and doctrine.

3. **Joshin's English-vs-Japanese register.** Is Joshin assumed to be Japanese-born teaching in English, Japanese-born teaching through translation, or a non-Japanese ordained at Kōya? My recommendation: **Japanese-born, Kōyasan-trained, fluent English, teaching primarily in English to a Western/American lay audience.** This is the most natural fit for the operator's catalog (which is in en_US). It also justifies the natural code-mixing of Japanese terms into English prose (a Joshin atom can say "the *Kōmyō Shingon*, the Mantra of Light" without it feeling forced).

4. **How explicitly devotional should Joshin's voice be?** Shingon is more devotional than Zen, but the spectrum within Shingon is wide. **Recommendation: warmly devotional but not effusive.** Comfortable saying *Namu Daishi Henjō Kongō*, comfortable referring to Kōbō Daishi as a present companion, comfortable invoking Dainichi and Fudō as real presences — but not pushing the reader to share that devotion. The pose is "I trust this and find it operates; here is how you might try it; you don't have to believe what I believe."

5. **Migration of existing "joshin" Zen atoms.** Two strategies:
   - **Strategy A (purist):** Discard all existing joshin atoms; author new ones from scratch using §3 practices and §4 vocabulary. Highest doctrinal hygiene.
   - **Strategy B (rewrite):** Keep the existing atoms' *psychological observations* (which are often general enough to be tradition-neutral) but rewrite the *practice prescriptions* and *vocabulary* to the Shingon register. Faster, but risks residual Zen tone if not carefully reviewed.
   - **Recommendation:** **Strategy A for any atom that prescribes practice; Strategy B only for atoms that are pure psychological observation with no practice content.** Audit each atom against the Zen-drift wordlist in §4.3 before retaining.

6. **Brand-voice integration.** This research document is the source-of-record for Shingon doctrine. The operator should commission a follow-up **Joshin brand-voice guidelines** atom that combines this research with the publisher's existing voice-and-tone standards. The two documents together form the authoring envelope.

A Note on the Teachings of Adi Da

I was not a direct student of Adi Da. I encountered their work through books, talks, and publicly available teachings. What follows is not an official interpretation of Adi Da's work — it is an application. I have done my best to honor the integrity of the original teachings while translating them into practical guidance for the challenges you may be facing.

Adi Da's understanding of overthinking reshaped the way I see this subject. Their approach — rooted in radical devotion, the Bright, prior freedom, relationship as practice — offers a lens that goes beyond conventional advice. It speaks to something deeper: the patterns beneath the surface, the quiet mechanisms that keep us stuck, and the often-overlooked pathways toward genuine relief.

This book applies Adi Da's teachings to the specific experience of gen alpha students navigating overthinking. It does not replace the teacher's original work. Where I have adapted exercises or frameworks, I have done so with care and transparency. Any simplification is mine, not theirs.

If something in these pages resonates with you, I encourage you to go to the source. Seek out Adi Da's own words — their talks, their writings, their direct teachings. What I offer here is a bridge, not a destination. The real work lives in the original.

You picked up this book because something in you recognized the title. Not intellectually. Deeper than that. Something in your body said: that is what I am carrying.

You are right. And you are not alone in carrying it. Overthinking is one of the most common experiences gen alpha students face — and one of the least understood, because the people who carry it are usually the last to name it.

Chapter 1

Your autonomy is increasing and your parents' hopes are still present. The increase and the presence are sometimes in conflict. The conflict is generating overthinking.

You type the message. Read it back. Delete two words. Re-add them. Add a question mark, remove it, add it again. Wonder if the tone is off. Start over. Twenty minutes later the draft is still sitting there.

Each revision promises clarity, but each one reveals a new angle to second-guess. The message was fine five iterations ago. The overthinking has become its own destination.

That's the draft that never sends. It will come up throughout this book — not because it's the only thing happening, but because it tends to be underneath most of what we're going to look at.

For example, the quickest way to feel the draft that never sends is to watch it take hold in an ordinary moment. Stay with that ordinary moment a second longer. The pattern usually shows itself before the explanation does.

Underneath the feeling is a simple mechanism: you can act inside uncertainty without waiting for it to resolve. When the alarm runs the response, your nervous system treats uncertainty like danger and asks for impossible certainty. That is why ordinary moments feel heavy.

The point is that you can act inside uncertainty without waiting for it to resolve.

Consider the person you find most difficult. What is it about them that tightens you? Now ask a harder question. Is the tightening about who they are? Or is it about who you become in their presence? The mirror does not create what it reflects.

The tradition I draw on sometimes calls what is already here before the scramble the Bright — not a prize in the distance, but the aliveness underneath the grip. I will use that word sparingly. When I do, it means: what stays when you stop forcing your mind to secure what cannot be secured in advance. Most of the time I will speak in plain body-and-attention language instead.

Notice this. The one who has been struggling is also the one who has been maintaining the struggle. Not because you want to suffer. Because the part of you that braces needs activity to survive. Even suffering is activity. The pivot is seeing this without condemning yourself for it.

Now notice what shifted before your mind starts summarizing it.

Practice. Before you send or delete the next message that matters, pause for three full breaths. On the third exhale, choose one action: send, save draft, or delete — any of the three counts. The win is not the choice. The win is that you chose from noticing instead of from panic.

By the end of this arc, you will have a name for what the draft is doing in your body. For now, it is enough to see that the draft and the tightening arrive together.

Chapter 2

The pressure to choose a direction is coming from every direction. Parents, teachers, college websites, TikTok careers. You're overthinking about what you want versus what's expected versus what's profitable.

Your laptop has seventeen tabs. Each tab is a different possible you. You close three. You open four more. Someone knocks. You slam the lid halfway — not because you are doing anything wrong, because the mere presence of another person makes the choosing feel exposed.

The mechanism you met in Chapter 1 is already running: your system is asking for a guarantee it cannot have yet. Indecision is not laziness. It is often grief arriving before the decision does.

Two friends had a falling out. For months they avoided each other. Each waited for the other to apologize. Each was certain the other was wrong.

What neither of them saw was that the conflict was not really about the words that were spoken. It was about two people defending their edges. The argument was the surface. Underneath, each was bracing against the vulnerability of being seen by someone who knew them well.

When they finally spoke, what broke the impasse was not an apology. It was one of them saying: I notice that I am holding something tight and I do not know how to let go of it. The honesty was not about the conflict. It was about the bracing.

Relationship is the mirror that shows you where you are holding. When the mirror shows something uncomfortable, the impulse is to break the mirror. But the mirror is not the problem. The holding is.

Practice. The next time "future" hits as a lump in your throat or a knot under your ribs, name the place out loud if you can — even whispered. You are not solving your life in that moment. You are giving your nervous system a map.

You are still in the first arc — naming how outer pressure meets inner bracing. The pattern is the same whether the pressure sounds like a person or a website.

Chapter 3

Your family has expectations and your culture might have different expectations and your school has different expectations. The conflict is generating overthinking about which one to honor.

Your phone lights up. Three threads. Your cousin wants you at the gathering. Your group chat is planning something that would disappoint your parents if they saw the screenshots. Your school account reminds you about a form. You type three different replies in your head and send none.

The same mechanism: you are being asked to move without a clear "right" move. Your body interprets that as threat. No wonder you freeze.

A man carried anger toward his father for most of his adult life. The anger shaped his relationships, his career, his body. It was the organizing principle of how he held himself.

He tried to release it. He went to therapy. He wrote letters he never sent. He practiced forgiveness meditations. Each attempt was another way of trying to force a clean ending before his body was ready to feel the mess.

What finally shifted was not a technique. It was a moment of seeing. He saw the tightening as it was happening. Not the story about his father. Not the reasons for the anger. The actual physical gesture of bracing. And in that seeing, the bracing became visible rather than invisible. It lost some of its power not because he conquered it but because he stopped being unconscious of it.

The anger did not disappear overnight. But it was no longer running the show from behind the curtain.

Practice. When three expectations pull at once, write one line for each: what does my body do? (Jaw, hands, stomach — keep it physical.) You are not choosing loyalty yet. You are refusing to let the war happen only in your head.

Chapter 4

You're building a sense of self in an environment that's designed to get your attention. The design is making the self-building harder.

You set the math problem in front of you. One problem. The page is quiet. Your phone is not. A notification slides in — not even interesting — and your thumb moves before you decided it would. You read one thread. You answer nothing. You set the phone facedown. You stare at the problem. The numbers have not changed. Something in you still feels like you are losing.

When attention is the product, your nervous system trains on interruption. Overthinking here is not weakness. It is your brain doing what it was rewarded for doing.

A teacher asked her students a question: What are you doing right now?

They answered with activities. Listening. Sitting. Thinking. Breathing.

She said: Look more carefully. Underneath all of that, what are you actually doing?

After a long silence, one student said: I am holding myself together.

Yes, she said. That is what the bracing self does every moment of every day. It holds its shape. The effort is so constant that you mistake it for who you are. But you are not only the holding. You are also the awareness that can notice the holding.

The student said: But if I stop holding, I will fall apart.

She said: You will not fall apart. The one who thinks it will fall apart is the holding itself. What you are cannot fall apart in the way fear claims it can.

Practice. Pick one problem or paragraph. Work on it for ninety seconds with the phone in another room. When the pull to check happens, note it like weather: there it is. Return. Ninety seconds, not an hour. You are training attention, not punishing yourself.

Chapter 5

The schools you could attend have different paths. The different paths are leading to different lives. You're overthinking about which life you want.

You watch a campus tour without sound. Cut to students laughing on the quad. Cut to a library that looks like a cathedral. You switch to another school. Same arc, different colors. Your body does not know how to choose a life from a highlight reel. So it does what it knows: it scans for danger.

A father noticed that every time his teenage daughter pushed back against him, his body tightened. His voice became clipped. His responses became lectures.

He thought he was being a good parent — firm, consistent, authoritative. But what he was actually doing was bracing against the threat of losing control. His daughter was not the problem. His relationship to authority was the bracing.

When he began to notice the tightening before he spoke, something changed. He did not become permissive. He did not abandon his role. But he stopped using his role as armor. The boundary between father and daughter became a place of meeting rather than a fortification.

His daughter noticed the change before he did. She said: You seem different. Like you are actually here instead of performing being here.

Practice. Write two sentences only: What am I afraid choosing will prove about me? What am I afraid not choosing will cost me? Put the pen down. Feel your feet. You are allowed not to know yet.

You have now walked the first arc: naming how overthinking forms when outer pressure meets inner alarm. The next arc turns toward relationship, identity, and performance — the places where other people make the pattern loud.

Chapter 6

Social media made it so everyone can see your life. You're overthinking about what you're showing, who's watching, what they're thinking about what you're showing.

You film a fifteen-second clip. You delete it. You film again with different light. You delete that too. You wonder if your face looks like you mean it. You wonder if meaning it is even the point. The camera is not judging you. The imagined audience is — and the imagined audience is usually a collage of people who are not in the room.

A couple came to a counselor because they could no longer communicate. Each complained that the other did not listen. Each felt unseen.

The counselor asked them to sit facing each other in silence for five minutes. No talking. Just looking.

Within the first minute, both were in tears. Not from sadness. From the shock of actually being seen without the buffer of words and strategies. The bracing self uses communication as a shield. It talks to avoid being exposed. It listens selectively to maintain its position.

In the silence there was nowhere to hide. The bracing became visible to both of them simultaneously. And in that mutual visibility, something relaxed that years of talking had not touched.

The problem was never communication alone. The problem was that both were using communication to avoid the vulnerability that relationship actually requires.

Practice. For twenty-four hours, post nothing new. Stories included. If that feels impossible, shorten the window — but keep the spirit: observe the urge to be seen on your terms, and let it pass once in a while without obeying it.

Chapter 7

Your mental health is being discussed at home, at school, on social media. The discussion is good but it's also making you hyper-aware of your own mental state. The hyper-awareness is generating overthinking.

Dinner is almost over. Someone asks how you are — and they mean it. You feel two things at once: relief that someone sees you, and pressure to report your inner weather correctly. If you say fine, you lie. If you say not fine, you fear a lecture or a diagnosis-by-Google. So you watch yourself trying to pick the safest true sentence. While you watch, you disappear from the room.

Hyper-vigilance about your own state is still vigilance. It still costs your body.

A man spent years building his identity around being calm. He never raised his voice. He never lost his temper. People admired his composure.

But the composure was bracing. It was the self suppressing what it feared — the raw energy underneath the calm surface. He was not peaceful. He was controlled. And control is not the same as freedom.

One day, in a moment of genuine distress, the control broke. He shouted. He wept. He shook with an intensity that terrified him. And then something remarkable happened. After the storm passed, there was a silence deeper than anything his control had ever produced.

The peace that followed the breakdown was different from the peace he had manufactured. It was not held in place by effort. It was what remained when the effort stopped.

Practice. When someone asks how you are, try answering with a feeling word and a body word — for example: anxious, tight chest. You do not owe them your whole story. You owe yourself less performance of okay.

Chapter 8

Your parents have hopes. Your school has expectations. TikTok has a narrative. You're trying to figure out which one is actually yours. The overthinking is you trying to separate your own voice from all the external voices.

You scroll. A clip tells you to hustle. Another tells you to rest. A third tells you to be authentic, which somehow requires buying something. Your thumb keeps moving because each clip promises a final answer. There is no final answer in the feed. There is only the next clip, which is the point.

A woman had a panic attack in a grocery store. Nothing dramatic triggered it. The fluorescent lights, the ordinary aisles, the normal sounds of commerce. And suddenly the floor dropped out from under her sense of self.

In the aftermath she searched for causes. Stress. Diet. Sleep. She found explanations but none of them explained the core of the experience: the self losing its grip on its own continuity.

What she could not see at the time was that the panic was not a moral failure. It was bracing meeting a moment it could not map. The terror was partly the body's honest confusion when the mind insists it should have certainty about everything at once.

The panic did not mean she was broken. It meant her system had been running hot for a long time. Listening to the body after is not weakness. It is repair.

Practice. Name the three loudest voices — not people, narratives. Parent hope. School metric. Trend story. Place a hand where you feel each one. You are allowed to disagree with all three and still be kind.

Chapter 9

You're developing in front of your friends and your friends' families and people on the internet. The development is public. The publicity is making the overthinking worse.

You post something you thought was funny. The likes arrive. So do two comments that feel like tiny judgments. You reread your own joke until it does not sound like you. You consider deleting. You consider explaining. You consider never posting again. Your face is warm. Your stomach dips. The public version of you and the private version of you are arguing in your chest.

A woman was terrified of public speaking. Her therapist suggested exposure therapy. Her coach suggested power poses. Her friends suggested practice.

Every suggestion was a strategy for the bracing self to overcome its fear. And every strategy worked temporarily. She could perform confidence. But the fear returned because the strategies addressed the symptom, not the source.

The source was not only fear of judgment. It was the bracing of a self that believed it needed to be seen in a particular way in order to survive. The audience was a mirror, and she was terrified of what the mirror would show.

What shifted was not courage alone. It was the recognition that the one who was afraid and the one who was trying to overcome the fear were the same tightening. When she stopped trying to fix the fear and simply noticed the tightening as it happened, the fear lost some of its grip. Not because she conquered it. Because she saw through the one who was only ever performing against it.

Practice. After your next post — or after you decide not to post — read comments once, hand on sternum, then close the app for an hour. You are practicing ending the scan, not winning the opinion lottery.

Chapter 10

You said something that made someone upset and you're replaying it trying to understand what went wrong. The overthinking is partly about the incident and partly about whether you're a bad person.

The thread is still there. You reread what you said. You reread what they said. You draft an apology in your head that sounds mature. You draft a defense that sounds fair. You send neither. You wonder which draft would prove you are not a bad person. The question sounds moral. In your body it feels like survival.

A nurse worked night shifts in an emergency room for fifteen years. She was excellent at her job. She saved lives. She also stopped feeling anything.

She did not notice the numbing as it happened. It was gradual. A necessary adaptation. The bracing self learned to close the channel between the body and the heart.

She thought she was being professional. She was being defended. The difference is that a professional can feel everything and still act. A defended person acts without feeling and calls it strength.

When the numbness finally cracked, it was not during a crisis. It was during a quiet moment when a patient held her hand and said thank you. The simplicity of the contact bypassed her defenses. She felt everything she had been holding for fifteen years.

The feeling did not incapacitate her. It restored her. What she had called strength was actually a prison. What she had avoided was the very aliveness that made her work meaningful.

Practice. Before the next message you send in repair, pause for half a breath — literally half an inhale — and feel your feet. Then choose words. Repair is not performance of goodness. It is contact.

Arc two asked you to see how other people and public life turn the volume up on bracing. Arc three moves into shame, decisions, and pressure — the moments when silence and permanence make overthinking feel like a verdict.

Chapter 11

Someone you care about did something that hurt you and you're overthinking about whether to say something, how to say it, whether they meant it.

You pass them in the hallway — or you see the read receipt — and your body does the math before your mind does: if I speak, I risk more hurt. If I stay quiet, I risk eating it. You smile anyway. The smile is work. By lunch your jaw tells you the truth.

When you sit quietly and the mind becomes still, you might notice how much of your day is spent managing other people's comfort at the expense of your own clarity. That is not kindness every time. Sometimes it is fear wearing kindness's clothes.

Practice. Write one sentence you could actually say — not the perfect speech, the true one. Say it or do not. Either way, you have separated the feeling from the fog.

Chapter 12

You're at an age where fitting in matters and being yourself matters and those are sometimes opposing forces. You're overthinking about the balance.

You lay out two outfits. Neither is wrong. Both are you-ish. You imagine version A being read as trying too hard. Version B as not trying enough. You return to the closet. The clock moves. The part of you that braces is trying to pre-approve your own existence before you walk out the door.

Forgiveness, in the sense this book uses it, is not pretending harm did not happen. It is the moment you stop bracing against what happened so completely that your body cannot move. The event does not change. Your relationship to the event can change. That shift lives in the ribs, not only in the mind.

Practice. Tomorrow, wear one choice that is honest even if it is not optimized. Notice who flinches — inside you, not only outside you.

Chapter 13

You did something embarrassing and the thought keeps returning. The mortification is real but amplified by the awareness that it might have been photographed, might be online forever. The permanence is making the overthinking worse.

Maybe someone tagged you. Maybe your face did something you did not know it could do. Maybe the moment is small and the story you tell yourself is huge. Your mind plays the clip on repeat because it believes rehearsal will prevent a future repeat. Your nervous system does not know the difference between the screen in your head and a screen in your hand.

Practice. Feet on the floor. Say one worst-case sentence out loud to an empty room — plain language, no drama words. Let it be boring. Boring shrinks the haunting.

Chapter 14

The college decision is years away but everyone is already thinking about it. You're overthinking about decisions that shouldn't be your responsibility yet.

At the gathering, someone you barely know asks where you are applying. The question is casual. Your body treats it like an exam. You laugh. You deflect. Inside, you are furious that your life is supposed to be a headline at a party.

You are allowed boundaries that sound boring. "I have not decided yet" is a complete sentence. The bracing self will tell you that answer makes you look behind. Sometimes behind is sane.

Practice. Prepare one boundary phrase. Use it once this week. Afterward, notice shoulders and jaw — not to fix them, to witness them.

Chapter 15

You made a choice about something and you're seeing your peers make different choices and you're overthinking about whether your choice was right.

Everyone in the group chat seems to be converging on the same plan. Same class. Same trip. Same aesthetic. You chose differently — or you are choosing slower — and the silence in the chat feels like a grade you cannot read.

Two siblings had not spoken in five years. The original cause of the rift was small — a misunderstanding about an inheritance. But the silence had calcified into an identity. Each sibling had built a version of themselves that did not include the other.

The bracing was not only the conflict. It was the identity that formed around the conflict. To reach out would mean dismantling part of who they had become.

When one of them was diagnosed with a serious illness, the architecture shifted. Not because the illness created urgency. Because it revealed how much energy was being spent maintaining a boundary that served no one.

The phone call that broke the silence was three words long. The three words were not important. What was important was that one person chose to brace less.

Practice. For three days, one line per day: I noticed bracing when ___. Keep it physical and specific. No self-attack at the end of the three days — only pattern.

Arc three showed how shame, permanence, and comparison recruit overthinking as a judge. Arc four widens the lens: the future, the feeds, the world, and the anxiety that moves through families — then integration.

Chapter 16

You're supposed to know what you want to be. Everyone assumes you have an idea. You don't really. The assumption is making the overthinking worse.

The counselor smiles kindly. So — what's the plan? Your mind offers fog. The fog feels like failure. The counselor is not your enemy, but your body does not know that yet. You want to say something impressive. What comes out is honest: I don't know. The honesty costs less than you feared.

A man believed he was not good enough. This belief was not something he thought about consciously. It lived in his body — in the way he entered rooms, in the way he spoke slightly too quickly, in the way he over-prepared for everything.

He tried to overcome the belief through achievement. Degrees, promotions, recognition. Each accomplishment provided a brief respite and then the not-enough feeling returned. The striving was endless because the belief was not a thought. It was bracing.

The bracing said: I am separate and therefore I must prove my worth. No amount of proof can satisfy bracing because bracing does not actually want proof. It wants to keep its shape.

What began to change was not his circumstances. It was his willingness to sit with the feeling of not-enough without trying to fix it. In the sitting, he discovered that the feeling had a texture, a location in the body, a rhythm. And that he was not only the feeling. He was what noticed the feeling.

The exhaustion you feel is not always from doing too much. It is often from holding too tightly.

Practice. Think of someone who irritates you. Notice the specific quality that disturbs you. Ask honestly: where does this same quality live in me? Not as self-attack — as inquiry. Sit five minutes with whatever you find.

Chapter 17

Your identity is still forming and the entire world is watching through screens. The performance is affecting the formation.

In one chat you are careful. In another you are loud. Neither is fake — both are partial. The cognitive cost is not moral. It is muscular. You are switching shapes fast enough that no shape feels like home.

A woman retired after forty years in the same profession. On her first Monday with nowhere to go, she felt a terror she had never experienced during her busiest years.

The terror was not only about purpose. It was the bracing self discovering that it had been using work as its primary grip — the thing it held onto in order to feel real. Without the structure of doing, the self could not locate itself.

She tried to fill the space. Volunteering, hobbies, travel. Each was a new form of striving. Each provided temporary relief and then the emptiness returned.

What finally helped was not filling the space. It was sitting in it. Letting the emptiness be empty. Discovering that underneath the terror of having nothing to do, there was a presence that had been there all along, obscured by decades of productive bracing.

Practice. For one hour, one chat, one tone — your honest middle. If that feels risky, shorten the window. The goal is continuity, not performance of virtue.

Chapter 18

You know about inequality, climate change, social injustice at a level previous generations didn't at your age. The knowledge is making everything feel more urgent and more impossible.

Your feed is evidence and argument in alternating blows. You care. You also feel small. The small feeling collides with the caring and produces a spiral: if I do not fix this, I am complicit; if I cannot fix this, I am helpless. Your thumb keeps moving because stopping feels like abandoning the world.

A couple argued about the same thing for years. The topic changed but the pattern did not. One would pursue, the other would withdraw. The pursuer felt abandoned. The withdrawer felt invaded. Both were convinced the other needed to change.

Neither of them saw that they were not only arguing with each other. They were arguing with their own bracing. The pursuer braced against abandonment by seeking reassurance. The withdrawer braced against engulfment by creating distance. Each person's strategy triggered the other's wound.

The cycle broke when they stopped looking only at each other's behavior and started looking at their own bracing. Not fixing it. Not explaining it. Just seeing it happen in real time.

What you call anxiety is sometimes the self's anticipation of its own overwhelm. It is not only about the future event you are worried about. It is about capacity meeting a world that refuses to be solved in one night.

Practice. Set a timer on the feed. When it ends, end — one deliberate action in the real world after, even tiny: water, walk, wash a dish. Motion tells your nervous system you are not only a pair of eyes.

Chapter 19

You're forming your identity and the identity is being shaped by forces you can't fully control. The awareness of the shaping is making the overthinking about who you actually are more difficult.

Someone says you've changed. They might mean it as neutral. Your body hears accusation. You rehearse a defense of your growth. You rehearse a defense of your loyalty. While you rehearse, you miss the fact that change is not a crime — it is what living does.

Someone your age sat in a room full of people talking about spiritual experiences. Visions. Energy. Days of boundless love.

They had experienced none of these things. They felt inadequate. The bracing self was comparing its inventory to everyone else's and finding itself lacking.

Then a person in the room said something simple: The experience is not the point. The one who is collecting experiences is the point.

They realized they had turned spirituality into another arena for striving and comparison. What is already here — the Bright, in the language of this book's source — is not a trophy experience. It is what is present before, during, and after all experiences, including the experience of feeling behind.

There is a moment just before the old reaction fires where a choice exists. Not a choice to be perfect. A choice to see. Most of the time you miss the moment because bracing is faster than attention. But the gap is always there. Practice widens the gap.

Practice. Five minutes, eyes open, gaze soft. Let the visual field be without organizing it into a story about you. When labeling starts, notice it and return. This is attention without the extra layer.

Chapter 20

Your parents are anxious about your future which makes you anxious about your future which makes them more anxious. The anxiety is amplifying in the system. The overthinking is cyclical.

It is late. The house is quiet except it is not — worry has a sound. You hear a murmur through the wall, or you read a text not meant for you, or you simply feel the field of the family holding its breath about money, school, safety. You want to fix their fear so your body can unclench. You cannot fix their fear. You can name what is yours.

An elderly woman was asked what she had learned in eighty years. She thought for a long time and said: I learned that everything I spent my life building was an attempt to feel safe. And the safety I was looking for was already here before I started building.

She described her life not as a failure but as a long detour. The career, the family, the house, the reputation — each was valuable on its own terms. But underneath each accomplishment was a striving that no accomplishment could satisfy.

She said: I am not sorry for the detour. But I wish someone had told me earlier that the destination was the starting point. You do not arrive at freedom. You recognize that you never left it. Bracing convinced me I was far away from home. But I was always home. I was just too busy striving to notice.

The shift is not from suffering to constant happiness. It is from unconscious bracing to conscious bracing. The bracing may continue. But when it is seen, it is no longer running your life from behind the curtain.

Practice. Ten minutes, natural breath. On each exhale, silently say: I am not solving the whole future in this breath. Notice protest in the mind. Let protest be seen, not obeyed.

Stay with the moment a second longer. The pattern usually shows itself before the explanation does.

Conclusion

You are still here. That means something.

You could have stopped at any chapter. You could have decided this was not for you. But something kept you listening, kept you practicing, kept you noticing. That something is not willpower. It is the part of you that already knew overthinking was a pattern, not a permanent condition.

Trust that part. It is older and wiser than the pattern.

What might be different now, in ordinary days: you might notice the draft earlier. You might feel the jaw before the spiral completes. You might send one message from choice instead of panic. You might leave a scroll session because you recognized the pull. You might say one true sentence at dinner. You might let yourself not know your future for a week without interpreting that as failure. Your parents may still worry. You may still worry. The relationship to the worry can change even when the topics do not.

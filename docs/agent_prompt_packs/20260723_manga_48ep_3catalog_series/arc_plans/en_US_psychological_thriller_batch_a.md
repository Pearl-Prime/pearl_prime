Demographic: seinen | Primary topic: overthinking | Genre: psychological_thriller

**Arc 1 — Obsession Seed (ep_001-012):** Eli Tanaka, a compliance analyst, catches a three-cent discrepancy in a routine client-ledger reconciliation and opens an unofficial working file — a manila folder he isn't supposed to keep — to track it outside the firm's official audit trail. His colleague Dana Ruiz, a records clerk who trusts nothing that isn't timestamped, notices he's staying past building close. The modest cost: he misses his sister's engagement dinner because the numbers "almost line up." The arc turns when Eli finds the same three-cent gap recurring monthly across five years of statements, and can no longer tell whether he's found a fraud or invented one.

**Arc 2 — Spiral (ep_013-024):** Eli builds a private master file at home — years of statements cross-referenced, walls of annotated printouts — and starts pulling internal system access he isn't authorized for to verify his own suspicions. It costs him materially more than arc 1: his manager flags unusual login patterns, Dana starts hedging her answers when he asks her to corroborate his theory, and he stops trusting the firm's own closed audits, re-litigating cases that were settled years ago. The arc turns when the flagged account traces back to one Eli personally signed off on five years earlier — his case file has been building a case against himself.

**Arc 3 — Exposure (ep_025-036):** The firm's internal audit team discovers Eli's shadow file and unauthorized access logs; he's placed on leave pending review, and the three-cent discrepancy escalates into a formal fraud inquiry that names a coworker he'd quietly suspected. The stakes personalize hard — his own methodology is now evidence in someone else's hearing, and Dana is called to testify about what she saw him building. The arc turns when the named coworker's revision-and-recheck habits turn out to mirror Eli's own process exactly, panel for panel — the investigation now looks less like a fraud and more like a reflection.

**Arc 4 — Reckoning (ep_037-048):** Eli is called before a regulatory review board to present the case file itself — now a wall-sized ledger of corrections layered on corrections — in a hearing that could clear the coworker, indict him, or both. This is not a cure: the acceptance layer stays authored_candidate, no claimed resolution. Eli's mastery of the topic deepens not into certainty but into the discipline of stopping — the reckoning is the moment he lets one page of the file stand unrevised in front of the board instead of adding a final correction. Materially bigger than arc 1: what began as a private folder now determines another person's career, and the close is ambiguous — the ledger stays half-shut, the three cents never fully explained.

## executive_calm_workplace
Existing series: `executive_calm_workplace__irene_cho__en_US__burnout__the_calendar_with_no_white` (REUSE_EXISTING — arc plan feeds authoring into this existing series identity, do not propose a new one)
Demographic: seinen | Primary topic: burnout | Genre: psychological_thriller

**Arc 1 — Obsession Seed (ep_001-012):** COO Helen Drake runs her calendar like a security protocol — every fifteen-minute block accounted for, a contingency meeting behind every meeting. The catalyst: a blank slot that has stood untouched for years, the anniversary of her mother's death, gets reflexively double-booked rather than left white. The modest cost is her son's recital, skipped because the protocol required a travel buffer she couldn't override. The arc turns when her chief of staff, Marcus Webb, quietly mentions she hasn't had an unscheduled hour in fourteen months — and she realizes she can't remember the last one either.

**Arc 2 — Spiral (ep_013-024):** Helen adds contingency layers for her contingencies — a shadow calendar synced to her phone that pings if she deviates from the master grid by even five minutes. The cost is materially larger than arc 1: colleagues get routed around her because she reschedules them into 6 a.m. slots to protect the grid, Marcus's own hours start bending permanently to her system, and her son stops asking to be put on the calendar at all, having learned there's no point. The arc turns on a system outage that wipes six hours of her schedule — and Helen, with nothing to execute, cannot function.

**Arc 3 — Exposure (ep_025-036):** An employee she rescheduled at midnight files a formal HR grievance, and the company opens a review of her management style; the calendar becomes an open, embarrassing story inside the org. Stakes personalize further: her now-teenage son tells her flatly, in front of Marcus, that he learned not to need her on a schedule. The arc turns when Helen meets a rising executive who has adopted her exact system and wears it as a badge of competence — and for the first time she sees the machine from outside, in someone who still believes it's armor.

**Arc 4 — Reckoning (ep_037-048):** The company review board convenes to decide whether Helen keeps her role, and mid-proceedings a real family medical emergency arrives — the one event the protocol was built to survive and cannot schedule around. This is not a claimed cure: Helen doesn't dismantle the system in front of the board. The reckoning is smaller and harder — she leaves one hour of one day genuinely unscheduled and lets it happen without a contingency, for the first time since the arc began. Materially bigger than arc 1: the stakes are now her family, her career, and the credibility of a system a peer now depends on — and the close stays ambiguous, the calendar intact except for that single surviving white block.

## high_performer_workplace

Demographic: seinen | Primary topic: burnout | Genre: psychological_thriller

**Arc 1 — Obsession Seed (ep_001-012):** Cole Nguyen keeps every tile on his team's performance dashboard green; one night a single tile flips amber with no traceable cause, and he becomes certain someone above him is quietly re-weighting the metrics to make him look worse. The modest cost: he rebuilds queries until 2 a.m. chasing the amber tile and misses the team happy hour thrown in his own honor. The arc turns when he discovers the amber tile's update schedule matches his own login times exactly — the watcher he's been chasing may be logging in as him.

**Arc 2 — Spiral (ep_013-024):** Cole starts screen-recording his own dashboard sessions and logging every color change, building a private surveillance file on his own work. It costs him more than arc 1: teammates notice him watching them watch the dashboard and go quiet around him, his closest work friend and ops lead Priya Anand starts choosing her words with him carefully, and he formally requests server-level access logs from IT — which flags his own account for review instead. The arc turns when the "watcher" pattern he's been tracking in the login data resolves into a report generated by his own account, viewed from an angle he didn't know existed.

**Arc 3 — Exposure (ep_025-036):** IT security's review of his access requests becomes a visible, whispered-about incident across the floor — was Cole surveilling his own colleagues — and he's pulled into a formal meeting with his director and HR. Stakes personalize: Priya is asked to give a statement about his behavior, straining the one relationship that had stayed steady through the spiral. The arc turns when a new hire, in awe of Cole's old all-green dashboard, asks him to teach her "the trick" — and he watches, from the outside for the first time, exactly what the obsession looks like to someone hungry for it.

**Arc 4 — Reckoning (ep_037-048):** The reckoning is a live all-hands dashboard review where Cole has to present without pre-touching a single number — full exposure, no prep. This is not a claimed cure or a restored reputation: Cole lets one tile stay amber through the entire meeting without explaining it, the acceptance layer staying authored_candidate. His mastery of the topic deepens into tolerating an unresolved metric rather than chasing it. Materially bigger than arc 1: it's no longer his tile at stake but the team's trust in the whole dashboard, and the close is ambiguous — whether anyone was ever really watching him stays unresolved, including to Cole.

## minimal_mind_healing
Existing series: `minimal_mind_healing__ken_arata__en_US__overthinking__one_cup_no_thesis` (REUSE_EXISTING — arc plan feeds authoring into this existing series identity, do not propose a new one)
Demographic: seinen | Primary topic: overthinking | Genre: psychological_thriller

**Arc 1 — Obsession Seed (ep_001-012):** Theo Marsh records a short voice memo to himself every morning over one cup of coffee — meant to be nothing, a habit. One memo, from the morning his mentor Elder gave him an offhand piece of criticism, becomes the memo he replays daily, rewinding to the same eleven seconds. The modest cost: he's late to work re-listening, coffee going cold in his hand. The arc turns when he notices the memo's file timestamp doesn't match his memory of when it happened — he recorded over the original days later, and can no longer be sure what Elder actually said.

**Arc 2 — Spiral (ep_013-024):** Theo starts re-recording the memo from memory each morning, layering "corrected" versions over the old one until his phone holds a folder of a dozen near-identical files. The cost is bigger than arc 1: Elder notices Theo has stopped asking real questions and only replays old ones, and his partner finds him standing at the counter, coffee forgotten, headphones in, not present in the room. He misses submitting real work, convinced the "true" version of the criticism has to be recovered first. The arc turns when he finds the original, unedited file was never deleted — it's been sitting untouched the entire time, one folder over from the copies.

**Arc 3 — Exposure (ep_025-036):** His partner, worried, plays the untouched original for him without asking — the criticism was five ordinary words, nothing like the mythology he built around it. It becomes visible to Elder and the small studio when Theo's missed deadlines get raised openly in a team meeting. Stakes personalize: Theo has to explain, in front of people who respect him, why a years-old offhand comment has been running his mornings. The arc turns when Elder admits, quietly, they don't even remember saying it — the tape outlived the moment for only one of the two people in it.

**Arc 4 — Reckoning (ep_037-048):** Theo makes one more cup of coffee, recorder in hand, and the reckoning is the choice not to press record. This is not a cure — the folder of a dozen versions stays on his phone, unopened but not deleted, and the acceptance layer stays authored_candidate. His mastery of the topic deepens from managing the tape to tolerating its silence. Materially bigger than arc 1: the whole studio, not just Theo, now understands what the ritual cost, and the close is ambiguous — whether he picks the recorder back up tomorrow is left open.

## optimizer_workplace
Existing series: `optimizer_workplace__vera_lin__en_US__overthinking__the_model_that_predicted_him` (REUSE_EXISTING — arc plan feeds authoring into this existing series identity, do not propose a new one)
Demographic: seinen | Primary topic: overthinking | Genre: psychological_thriller

**Arc 1 — Obsession Seed (ep_001-012):** Data scientist Sam Okonkwo discovers that the churn-prediction model he built for the company flagged his own employee ID as high attrition risk three weeks before he'd even considered leaving. He starts pulling the model's other predictions to test them against real colleagues, treating the dashboard like a case file. The modest cost: he skips lunch with a work friend to cross-reference her risk score against her actual mood that day. The arc turns when the model flags his own score climbing again — this time for a reason the training data can't explain.

**Arc 2 — Spiral (ep_013-024):** Sam builds an unauthorized personal fork of the model, feeding it finer-grained data — calendar density, badge-swipe timing, message response lag — on himself and, increasingly, on colleagues he cares about. It gets eerily right: it calls a layoff before HR announces it and a coworker's divorce before it's public. The cost is bigger than arc 1: colleagues start to feel studied, and his friend Priya Chen, the team's data ethics lead, quietly flags that his queries are pulling data outside his access scope. He tips off his own manager to a predicted departure, changing the outcome. The arc turns when Sam realizes his interventions are now inside the training data the model reads — he's no longer predicting the system, he's part of its feedback loop.

**Arc 3 — Exposure (ep_025-036):** A data-access audit surfaces the unauthorized fork, and it becomes a company-wide incident, discussed in an all-hands on data ethics without naming Sam directly, though everyone knows. Stakes personalize: Priya, the one person who understood what he was building, is asked to testify about what she knew and when. The arc turns when a younger analyst, hungry to learn "the trick," asks Sam to teach her the model — and he watches himself from the outside, exactly as certain and exactly as hungry as he was at the start.

**Arc 4 — Reckoning (ep_037-048):** A governance hearing forces Sam to decide whether to hand over the model's full prediction log — potentially incriminating people he predicted about — or destroy it. There is no clean verdict on whether the model was ever really seeing the future or just reading him; the acceptance layer stays authored_candidate, no claimed cure. Sam's reckoning is narrow and specific: he deletes his own personal risk score from the file, one entry out of thousands, and leaves the rest intact for the review board. Materially bigger than arc 1: what began as curiosity about his own resignation now decides other people's careers, and the close is ambiguous — whether the model, now company property under formal review, ever needed him at all.

Both genres escalate materially per the master dispatch, with the explicit
exception that healing escalates seasonally/atmospherically (gentle disruption
→ attunement → baseline one degree deeper, per arc) rather than via stakes —
no villain, no cliffhanger enters a healing arc at any point. Mystery escalates
via the genre's own engine: the gap between perception and reality widens,
the cost of the loop/obsession accrues, and the antagonist stays human,
systemic, or cognitive (never supernatural) across all four arcs.

## stillness_press
Genre: healing | Primary topic: anxiety | CONTINUITY: arc 1 (ep_001-012) already authored and merged — this plan covers arcs 2-4 only (episodes 13-48), continuing Mira Aoki's story per the existing series.

**Arc 2 — Block (ep_013-024):** Winter into early spring. The practice Mira named out loud for the first time at the end of arc 1 (to Devon, over kettle steam) now has to hold under sustained rather than single-moment pressure: a stretch of visibility at work (folded into an ordinary promotion conversation, not a crisis) and a slow-building thread with Kenji from ep_008 becoming a real, recurring relationship rather than one date. The gentle disruption is Mira's mother visiting for the holidays — reopening the father thread from ep_003 without turning it into plot to be solved; the "Open Hand" register (config strategy_03) governs a chapter where Mira receives her mother's grief rather than fixing it. Arc closes on an ordinary spring morning: the same notification mechanic from ep_001/ep_010 fires once more, smaller than ever, folded into a life that has simply gotten a little wider.

**Arc 3 — Block (ep_025-036):** Summer. Devon's echoed gesture (ep_012) recurs and deepens — he brings his own difficult season (a breakup, a family health scare) to Mira without either of them naming what's happening as "teaching." A new "Quiet Crossing" thread (strategy_04) turns inward: an object of Mira's own — her father's, kept in a drawer — surfaces near the anniversary of his death, and the arc's real work is Mira sitting with a grief she has spent two arcs managing rather than facing. No antagonist, no confrontation scene; the jade pendant and kettle motifs carry the emotional weight instead of dialogue. Arc closes at late summer baseline, one degree deeper, the drawer object finally set upright rather than hidden.

**Arc 4 — Block (ep_037-048):** Autumn into the following winter — completing a full seasonal cycle since ep_001's original winter opening. Mira, further along the demographic arc the craft bible calls for (SS7), becomes the calm-observer figure for someone new — not a protégé, just a person at the edge of her life (a new hire, a neighbor) who is quietly carrying what Mira carried in ep_001. The final arc mirrors the series' opening structurally, not thematically: the same kitchen, the same kettle, a notification that fires and settles almost before it's felt. Closing image deliberately echoes ep_001's opening composition at dusk instead of dawn — resonant calm, no claimed cure, the alarm system never "fixed," only lived with more skillfully.

## somatic_wisdom_shojo
Genre: healing | Primary topic: somatic_healing

**Arc 1 (ep_001-012):** Establish Rin, a physical therapist / movement teacher who runs a small studio above a laundromat and reads bodies the way iyashikei protagonists read weather — a held shoulder, a shallow breath, a client's hands that won't quite relax. Opens on the "Seasonal Return" curve (strategy_01): baseline established through the studio's rituals (mats rolled, kettle on, morning stretch), then a gentle disruption — a new client, a former dancer who has stopped trusting her own body after an injury, arrives carrying tension instead of trust. Arc closes with the client's first unforced exhale, not a cure — Rin's own habit of over-explaining the body ("usefulness as armor," per the strategy bank) softens by one degree.

**Arc 2 (ep_013-024):** The "Open Hand" register (strategy_03): a second recurring client, a warehouse worker with chronic pain who has weaponized productivity against his own exhaustion, resists Rin's patience the way the strategy bank's difficult guest resists kindness. In parallel, the dancer client from arc 1 becomes a quiet regular presence, her body slowly re-learning trust through shared, wordless tasks (breath work, simple partnered stretches) rather than instruction. No antagonist; the escalation is entirely in how much of herself Rin is willing to receive from people who arrive defended.

**Arc 3 (ep_025-036):** "The Quiet Crossing" (strategy_04) turns the lens on Rin herself — an old injury of her own, the reason she left performance and became a healer instead, resurfaces near an anniversary. The arc's disruption is internal: a found object (a pair of worn shoes, a program from a show) she has avoided looking at directly. Her own body, usually the instrument she reads in others, becomes the site of the season's gentle work — she has to receive her own grief the way she has spent two arcs teaching others to receive theirs.

**Arc 4 (ep_037-048):** A full seasonal cycle closes. Rin opens the studio's first community class — not because she's "healed" everyone, but because the small rotating cast of clients across the series (the dancer, the warehouse worker, and new faces) have started attuning to each other without her mediating every session. The final arc is demographic, per the craft bible's 48-volume guidance: Rin shifts from solo keeper to a quieter, background presence in a room that now runs partly on its own. Closing image: the studio at dusk, mats rolled by someone else's hands for the first time, Rin watching from the doorway — resonant calm, no antagonist ever introduced across all 48 episodes.

## cognitive_clarity
Genre: mystery | Primary topic: overthinking

**CONTINUITY:** ep_001 ("The Loop Is Not Thinking," `artifacts/manga/chapter_scripts/cognitive_clarity__ahjan__en_US__overthinking__the_loop_is_not_thinking/ep_001.yaml`) is the only authored episode — a complete 5-chapter recognition-to-integration unit, not a stub. Protagonist is an unnamed adult man (referred to only as "he" on the page; called Eli below for planning purposes only — no name is committed to panel text) whose overthinking is externalized as a case-file-like internal loop; established devices: the brain-as-search-engine metaphor, a therapist who teaches "name it, then do something with your hands," a partner who is quietly affected by his absence, and the closing thesis "the loop is not thinking — it is the brain refusing to land." This plan keeps that premise and register, and re-frames its trajectory across 48 episodes using the `psychological_thriller_strategies.yaml` bank (strategy_01 "The Case File That Keeps Revising Itself" governs Eli directly; strategy_05 "The Pattern I Cannot Stop Solving" and strategy_04 "The Protocol Is Running Me Now" govern arcs 2-4) so that "the loop" graduates from a private CBT technique into the full unreliable-narrator engine the mystery genre requires.

**Arc 1 (ep_001-012):** Extends ep_001's single-episode arc (recognition → mechanism → cost → the "name it" move → the week after) across twelve episodes: the loop is shown firing across more domains than the original email (a missed deadline, a misread text from his partner, a false read of a coworker's tone) and, per the genre's craft-trap rule, at least one of Eli's loop-driven guesses turns out to be correct — proving the same machinery that costs him sleep is also professionally real skill. Arc closes on the ep_001 thesis restated under slightly higher stakes: he names the loop out loud in a moment that matters (not alone at a sink) and it holds, imperfectly — an ambiguous-livable close, not a cure.

**Arc 2 (ep_013-024):** Strategy_05 (obsession) takes over: a genuine professional pattern Eli notices — a discrepancy in a colleague's numbers, a project quietly going wrong — pulls him past the healthy use of his skill into full fixation; correct reads and confidently wrong ones start looking identical to him. The anchor relationship (his partner) absorbs visible cost per the genre's mandatory-by-volume-3 rule: missed dinners, a real conversation about being present versus performing presence. A "mirror" character enters — a coworker whose analytical rigidity has calcified into something colder than Eli's — showing the road not yet taken. Arc ends on Eli's hand on the metaphorical pin, choosing not to place it: the thread is left hanging, not solved.

**Arc 3 (ep_025-036):** The false-resolution phase. Eli believes he has mastered the loop — the "Protocol" strategy (strategy_04) takes hold as he builds an increasingly elaborate personal system of checks and rehearsals to manage his own overthinking, and for a while it works almost too well. The turn: the protocol itself becomes the threat, running him rather than serving him, and a genuinely important read (about the colleague's numbers from arc 2, now escalated into a real workplace consequence) is delivered with total false confidence and lands wrong. Cost peaks here — professional exposure, and the partner naming, more directly than before, what the system has quietly cost them both.

**Arc 4 (ep_037-048):** Highest stakes, most diffuse antagonist: the workplace situation reveals itself as systemic rather than personal (pressure, ambiguous accountability, no single villain to blame), and Eli's own cognition becomes indistinguishable from the threat he's tracking — per the genre's antagonist rule, the deepest menace is his own certainty. He does not "solve" the situation cleanly; the arc's climax is the deliberate, repeated choice to leave one thread unexamined and trust his partner's read over his own for once. Final panel stays genre-honest: ambiguous on whether his last read of the situation is accurate — no caption resolves it, no cure is claimed, only a steadier relationship to not-knowing than he had in ep_001.

## adhd_forge_mystery
Genre: mystery | Primary topic: adhd_focus

**Arc 1 (ep_001-012):** Introduce Wren, a young blacksmith working the forge her mentor left her, who authenticates and repairs historical edged weapons for museums and private collectors — reading the grain and temper of old metal the way a detective reads a scene. Her ADHD-coded hyperfocus is established as genuine, hard-won craft (she can lose six hours to a single blade and emerge with an answer no one else could find) and, in the same episodes, a real cost (missed calls, forgotten meals, a burned-down candle of a workshop). A client brings in a "family heirloom" sword that doesn't match its claimed provenance; Wren's hyperfocus on the metal itself, while he waits, becomes the arc's engine. Arc closes with the forgery confirmed — a small, contained mystery solved — and the first clearly named cost: she missed her sister's birthday dinner entirely, mid-focus, and didn't notice until midnight.

**Arc 2 (ep_013-024):** A bigger case arrives: a string of forged "period" weapons tied to a theft ring moving through the regional auction circuit. Wren's hyperfocus, genuinely useful for reading metal, starts misdirecting her investigation — she locks onto one maker's mark and follows it past the point of evidence, missing an obvious lead her apprentice (the anchor relationship) sees immediately. Per the genre's craft-trap rule, one of her metallurgy-based deductions is dead right and the next, built the same way, is confidently wrong. The apprentice's frustration — covering for missed obligations, absorbing the cost of Wren's tunnel vision — becomes visible and named by arc's end.

**Arc 3 (ep_025-036):** False resolution: Wren identifies a suspect — a rival smith she's certain is running the forgery ring — and for several episodes the evidence seems to confirm it, the workshop's rigid, ever-more-elaborate system of checks (steel samples, spectrograph logs, a wall of notes) standing in for the "Protocol" strategy. A mirror character, the rival smith himself, turns out to be innocent but just as consumed by his own single-minded focus on a different obsession — showing Wren a version of herself with none of the guardrails. The real forger is still out there; her confident case collapses publicly, and the cost of the hyperfocus becomes professional, not just personal, for the first time.

**Arc 4 (ep_037-048):** Highest stakes: the forgery ring is revealed to be systemic, not one bad actor — a guild-adjacent authentication network Wren herself has unknowingly supplied blades to for years, meaning some of her own "solved" cases from earlier arcs may be wrong. The antagonist is diffuse and partly her own certainty, per genre convention. The climax is not a triumphant deduction but a deliberate act of restraint: Wren, mid-hyperfocus on the final thread that would name a name, chooses to hand the unfinished lead to her apprentice and step back from the bench — redirecting the hyperfocus rather than being run by it. Final panel stays unresolved on whether the last piece she handed off will confirm or complicate her read; no cure is claimed for the ADHD-coded focus itself, only a first instance of choosing when to wield it.

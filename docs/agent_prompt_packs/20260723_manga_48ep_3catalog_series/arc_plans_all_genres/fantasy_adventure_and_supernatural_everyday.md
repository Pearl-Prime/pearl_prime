## solar_return_isekai
Genre (this exercise): fantasy_adventure | Primary topic: self_worth
Note: reassigned from established genre for full-taxonomy coverage exercise (see genre_assignment_37brands.md)

Portal/cost mechanic: worth-by-output crosses over into worth-by-light. The
protagonist falls asleep after unpaid overtime waiting for the last train and
wakes riding it past its final stop into Aurelia, a world caught in permanent
late-afternoon — the sun sets once per lifetime and has never finished doing
so. Her worth there is tracked literally: a cracked hourglass-lantern she
carries drains every time she spends herself proving her usefulness to
someone, and an empty lantern means she turns translucent and risks
dissolving into the dusk. Genre-native vessel (never named as her teacher):
the Dusk Cartographer, who maps how much light other travelers' routes have
cost them, never lectures.

**Arc 1 (ep_001-012):** She arrives carrying the old-world wound of
self-erasure through overwork; the Cartographer shows her, without
explaining, that the light drains fastest when she spends it to be liked
rather than needed. Turn: the dusk visibly moves for the first time when she
keeps one act of light for herself instead of handing it to a stranger who
demanded it. Renewal is small — she has kept, on purpose, exactly one thing.
**Arc 2 (ep_013-024):** She joins a caravan of dusk-wanderers, each carrying
their own cracked lantern, and discovers her lantern's crack is old — she has
always overspent. Saying no to a companion's plea costs her a real
relationship for the first time. The arc ends with a near-total dissolve
after she saves someone who never needed saving, a consequence that does not
undo itself.
**Arc 3 (ep_025-036):** The world widens to a council of wanderers; she
begins guiding newer arrivals and must tell the difference between real
mentorship and performing selflessness for praise. A rival who spent freely
and dissolved into the dusk stands as a cautionary mirror. The dusk itself
starts threatening to freeze permanently as too many wanderers hollow
themselves trying to fix it.
**Arc 4 (ep_037-048):** She confronts the dusk's source — the world's first
arrivals, who gave everything for others until they became the dusk itself.
Leading a company to give without vanishing risks her own permanent stay if
she misjudges it. Ending is renewal, not mastery: her lantern holds a steady
light for the first time and the sun completes one full cycle, but the years
she spent hollow are not given back, and whether she goes home is left a
live choice, not a triumphant return.

## gentle_growth_healing
Genre (this exercise): fantasy_adventure | Primary topic: self_worth
Note: reassigned from established genre for full-taxonomy coverage exercise (see genre_assignment_37brands.md)

Portal/cost mechanic: worth-by-caretaking crosses over into worth-by-grafting.
The protagonist — a chronic over-giver who measures her worth by what she
fixes for others — leaves a comment on a community-garden app while feeling
worthless and wakes stepping through its QR-code gate into the Tended
Country, where the land's seasons track her own hidden self-neglect in
real time. To mend a dying plot she must graft a living cutting from her own
body — a vine that sprouts at her wrist, a trellis-mark that ages her skin —
and the land flourishes while she visibly wilts. Genre-native vessel (never
named as her teacher): the Seedbearer, a traveling grafter who shows the
practice by doing it, never explaining it. This is a distinct portal, cast,
and cost mechanic from solar_return_isekai's dusk/light/dissolving register.

**Arc 1 (ep_001-012):** She wakes in a patch of the Tended Country dying in
the exact shape of her own hidden burnout. The Seedbearer grafts once in
front of her without comment; her turn is keeping one graft-cutting for
herself instead of giving all of it to the first plot that asks. Renewal is
one leaf tended on purpose, not a bloom.
**Arc 2 (ep_013-024):** She travels between failing garden-holds, giving
grafts from her own body and wilting visibly with each one, until she nearly
empties herself saving a stranger's plot and collapses. She learns a plot can
only truly heal if its own keeper also tends it — her sacrifice alone cannot
carry it. A scar-vine from this arc never regrows; the loss is permanent.
**Arc 3 (ep_025-036):** The world widens to a council of grafted keepers,
including one who over-gives exactly as she used to and is dying of it. She
begins teaching reciprocity by example, not lecture, deepening ties to
recurring garden-holds. A larger threat — the Long Frost, which arrives when
too many keepers hollow themselves — starts spreading toward the whole
country.
**Arc 4 (ep_037-048):** The Long Frost's source is revealed as the country's
founding keepers, who gave everything and became the frost itself. She must
lead a shared tending ritual instead of a single grand sacrifice, risking
becoming part of the frost if the ritual fails. Ending is renewal, not cure —
her own vine regrows a single leaf, the scars stay, and she chooses whether
to keep tending here or carry the practice home.

## spiritual_ground_supernatural
Genre (this exercise): supernatural_everyday | Primary topic: grief
Note: reassigned from established genre for full-taxonomy coverage exercise (see genre_assignment_37brands.md)

Spirit/boundary mechanic ("grounding"): the protagonist can perceive spirits
only through direct contact with certain ground — bare feet on soil, stone,
or a threshold's wood — and every grounding session lets a sliver of the
spirit's grief-residue settle into her own body (cold spots, marks that don't
fade), per the genre's somatic-toll convention. Case-of-week structure with a
serialized through-line. Genre-native vessel (never named as her teacher): an
elder shrine caretaker who provides context without over-explaining.

**Arc 1 (ep_001-012):** Her own recent loss (a grandparent) opens the ability
the first time she takes her shoes off at the family home's threshold. Early
cases are small and personal, building toward one that brushes her own grief.
Each episode ends on the genre's mandatory aftermath beat — release is never
full closure, something stays unresolved.
**Arc 2 (ep_013-024):** Competence grows and so does the somatic toll —
bruises that don't heal, cold that lingers under her skin. A serialized
4–6 episode arc forces her to ground at a site tied to her own family history
and confront a spirit connected to her own dead relative. It ends with her
performing a release ritual on her own grief, not only the spirit's.
**Arc 3 (ep_025-036):** She begins guiding a younger sensitive, showing the
grounding practice rather than preaching it. Cases trend toward generational
trauma — spirits carrying decades-old unfinished business — and she must
weigh protecting her apprentice from the toll against letting her learn it
firsthand.
**Arc 4 (ep_037-048):** The final case's spirit turns out to be tied to her
own unfinished goodbye. The site of final release is the same family-home
threshold from Arc 1. The ending is bittersweet, not curative — she settles
into a sustainable relationship with the sensitivity, keeps a permanent
physical mark from years of grounding, and the last panel holds her at a
threshold without showing her choice to cross it.

## devotion_path_shonen
Genre (this exercise): supernatural_everyday | Primary topic: grief
Note: reassigned from established genre for full-taxonomy coverage exercise (see genre_assignment_37brands.md)

Spirit/boundary mechanic ("devotion path"), distinct from spiritual_ground's
passive listening: the protagonist inherits a family duty to physically
carry the belongings and offerings of the dead to a crossing point — a
bridge, a gate, a shrine threshold — so they can pass on. Modern-reader
catalyst: the deliveries are logged through his late grandfather's old
courier app, repurposed to ping when a grief-item is ready for pickup. Cost:
each carried offering binds a sliver of that spirit's unfinished grief to his
own shadow until the debt is paid — he cannot set it down early, and it grows
physically heavier with every delivery he takes on, per shonen's active,
physical register rather than supernatural_everyday's usual quiet
contemplation. Cast includes a rival delivery-walker who cuts corners and
drops debts early as a cautionary foil.

**Arc 1 (ep_001-012):** He inherits the route after his grandfather's death
and takes his first delivery, expecting it to be simple. The sliver of grief
doesn't fully release on arrival — it stays with him as literal carried
weight, the arc's turn: there is no free crossing, only a debt now owed by
him.
**Arc 2 (ep_013-024):** More deliveries accumulate physical weight — a
heavier shadow, a slower step, marks that don't wash off — while the rival's
shortcuts show what happens when a debt is dropped instead of paid. One
delivery turns out to be tied to his own grandfather's unfinished business,
forcing him to walk a route that revisits his own grief.
**Arc 3 (ep_025-036):** The world widens to a guild of devotion-walkers,
introducing shonen-register training in reading routes and thresholds. He
holds tension between the rival's ethos of speed and glory and his own
slower, real devotion, and stakes rise as a major crossing point begins
failing under the weight of too many walkers cutting corners.
**Arc 4 (ep_037-048):** He leads a mass crossing to relieve the failing
boundary, carrying the heaviest accumulated weight of his career and risking
being permanently bound if he fails. The climactic delivery is his
grandfather's own last unfinished item. The debt is paid and the boundary
holds, but the physical toll of years of carrying stays with him — no
magic cure — and he chooses to keep walking the path as who he is now, not
because the burden was lifted.

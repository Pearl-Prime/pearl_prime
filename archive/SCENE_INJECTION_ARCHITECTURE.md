# PART 1: SCENE SOCKET MAP

## What Is a Scene Socket?

A **scene socket** is a predefined location in the master text where a persona-specific, location-specific lived experience can be injected. The surrounding prose remains frozen and authored. Only the scene changes.

**Socket notation:** `{scene_XX}` where XX is the scene number (01-12)

# PART 2: SCENE AUTHORING GUIDE

## Purpose

This guide ensures content team writes scenes that fit the socket requirements without drifting into generation patterns or therapeutic content.

---

## The Four Laws of Scene Authoring

### LAW 1: Scenes Are Atomic
- A scene is used whole or not at all
- No partial reuse across chapters
- No slicing into components
- No recombination with other scenes

**Violation example:**
```
# WRONG: Reusing part of a scene
scene_01 → full scene in Ch 1
scene_01 (partial) → callback in Ch 11
```

**Correct approach:**
```
# RIGHT: Scene 11 is a NEW scene that ECHOES scene 01
scene_01 → full scene in Ch 1 (post not shared)
scene_11 → NEW scene in Ch 11 (post shared this time)
```

---

### LAW 2: Scenes Are Authored, Not Templated

**NEVER do this:**
```yaml
scene_01:
  text: |
    You're on the {transit_type} heading toward {destination}.
    {peer_count} people {peer_action}.
    Your {body_part} tightens immediately.
```

**ALWAYS do this:**
```yaml
scene_01:
  text: |
    You're on the 38 bus heading toward Aptos High.
    Two girls behind you start laughing.
    Your stomach tightens immediately.
    Is it your hair?
    The mismatched socks?
    Something you missed?
    You stare out the window and feel your body brace for impact.
```

**Why:** Variables inside scenes recreate the generation problem. Scenes must be fully written by a human, once.

---

### LAW 3: Scenes Illustrate, Never Teach

**WRONG — scene introduces a concept:**
```yaml
scene_06:
  text: |
    You feel anxious about sending the text.
    But here's what you don't realize: the anxiety isn't a stop sign.
    It's just information. You can act while it's there.
    So you send the text anyway.
```

**RIGHT — scene shows action only:**
```yaml
scene_06:
  text: |
    11:47pm. Phone glowing.
    The text is typed. Two sentences.
    Your thumb hovers.
    Chest tight. Throat closed.
    You hit send.
    Nothing changes. But it's sent.
```

**Why:** Teaching is the book's job. Scenes show lived experience. If a scene explains something, it's doing the prose's job and will cause drift.

---

### LAW 4: Scene Density Is Capped

**Maximum:**
- 1-2 scenes per chapter
- 12 scenes per book total

**Why:** If every page has a scene, the book becomes exhausting and loses authorial coherence.

---

## Scene Structure Template

Every scene must follow this structure:

```
[CONTEXT LINE]
Where/when this happens. Specific. Physical.

[SENSORY DETAIL]
What the body notices. External stimuli.

[INTERNAL RESPONSE]
Physical sensation. Not interpretation.

[THE SENTENCE (optional)]
The voice of the pattern. Only if socket requires it.

[ACTION/NON-ACTION]
What happens or doesn't happen.
```

---

## Scene Quality Checklist

Before submitting a scene, verify:

| Check | Requirement |
|-------|-------------|
| ☐ | Specific location named (not "a coffee shop" but "the Blue Bottle on Valencia") |
| ☐ | Time of day or temporal anchor present |
| ☐ | At least one physical sensation described |
| ☐ | No therapeutic concepts introduced |
| ☐ | No explanations of why this matters |
| ☐ | Ends with action or non-action (not insight) |
| ☐ | Word count within socket requirement |
| ☐ | No variables or placeholders |
| ☐ | Could be read aloud in 30-60 seconds |

## Persona-Specific Scene Guidelines

### Gen Z (18-27)
**Settings:** Dorm, apartment with roommates, campus, transit, phone, social media, group chats  
**Relationships:** Roommates, classmates, dating apps, friend groups, professors  
**Technology:** Constant — phone always present, notifications, DMs, stories  
**Voice of the sentence:** "What's the point" / "They don't actually care" / "It's not worth the energy"

### Millennial Professional (28-42)
**Settings:** Open office, Slack, Zoom, commute, coworking space, apartment alone  
**Relationships:** Colleagues, manager, partner, old friends (fading), networking contacts  
**Technology:** Work-integrated — calendar, email, professional social media  
**Voice of the sentence:** "Not now" / "When things calm down" / "I don't have the bandwidth"

### Midcareer Executive (35-50)
**Settings:** Conference room, executive floor, airport, hotel, home office, family dinner  
**Relationships:** Direct reports, board, spouse, children (distance), aging parents  
**Technology:** Managed by others — assistant handles calendar, selective attention  
**Voice of the sentence:** "It's not strategic" / "That's not my role anymore" / "I'll circle back when..."

### Creative Professional (25-45)
**Settings:** Studio, coffee shop, gallery, client meeting, portfolio review, creative block  
**Relationships:** Clients, collaborators, audience, inner critic, past work  
**Technology:** Tools of the trade — specific to discipline (camera, DAW, software)  
**Voice of the sentence:** "It's not good enough" / "Who am I to..." / "When I have a better idea..."

### Parent (30-50)
**Settings:** Kitchen, car (pickup/dropoff), school event, pediatrician, bedtime, stolen moments  
**Relationships:** Children, partner, other parents, teachers, own parents  
**Technology:** Fragmented — interrupted constantly, phone as lifeline and leash  
**Voice of the sentence:** "The kids need me" / "When they're older" / "I can't take that time for myself"

### Healthcare Worker (25-55)
**Settings:** Hospital, clinic, breakroom, charting station, commute, decompression at home  
**Relationships:** Patients, colleagues, supervisors, family (understanding vs. not)  
**Technology:** EMR system, pager, secure messaging, compartmentalized personal phone  
**Voice of the sentence:** "I've seen worse" / "They need me more" / "I can't fall apart"

## Scene Rejection Criteria

Reject any scene that:

| Issue | Example |
|-------|---------|
| Uses variables | "You're at {location}..." |
| Introduces concepts | "This is what therapists call avoidance..." |
| Explains the pattern | "The reason you do this is because..." |
| Resolves the feeling | "And then the anxiety finally lifted..." |
| Uses generic locations | "A coffee shop" / "your apartment" |
| Lacks physical sensation | No body mentioned |
| Exceeds word count | 150 words for a 60-word socket |
| Sounds like copy | Marketing tone, inspirational quotes |
| Ends with insight | "That's when I realized..." |

# PART 3: PERSONA SCENE COVERAGE

## How Many Scenes Per Persona?

### Minimum Viable Coverage
**12 scenes** — one per socket, fully authored

This is the baseline. Every persona × topic combination requires 12 unique scenes.

### Why Not Fewer?
Reusing scenes across personas destroys the architecture's purpose. If Gen Z and Millennial share `scene_01`, the personalization is fake.

### Why Not More?
Scene density is capped. More than 12 scenes per book creates exhaustion and dilutes authorial voice.

## Quality Assurance Process

### Scene Submission Workflow

1. **Writer submits scene** with metadata (socket, persona, topic, location)
2. **Content QA checks** against Scene Quality Checklist
3. **Editor verifies** no teaching, no variables, correct word count
4. **Final review** confirms scene fits socket frame (reads naturally with surrounding prose)
5. **Approved scene** enters scene library

### Scene Library Structure

```
/scenes
  /anxiety
    /gen_z
      /santa_cruz
        scene_01.yaml
        scene_02.yaml
        ...
        scene_12.yaml

        scene_01.yaml
        ...
    /millennial_professional
```

## Assembler Integration

The assembler reads:
1. **Master text** (frozen, authored)
2. **Scene library** (persona × topic × location)
3. **Build config** (which persona, topic, location to use)

Output:
```
Master prose + injected scenes = personalized book
```

**No generation. No templates. No drift.**

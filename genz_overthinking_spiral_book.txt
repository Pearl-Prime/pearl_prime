Something that took me time to understand is the difference between being busy and being depleted. They can look identical from the outside — full calendar, constant output, no apparent space. But they are not the same state, and they do not require the same response.

Busy is a condition of volume. There is a lot to do and you are doing it. Depleted is a condition of resource. The supply that the doing draws from has run low. You can be busy without being depleted, if the work is replenishing something as it draws. You can be depleted without being especially busy, if the draw has been steady for long enough and the replenishment has been absent.

The reason the distinction matters is that the solutions are different. More efficiency solves busy. More efficiency does not solve depleted — it often accelerates it, because it increases the draw on the same diminished supply. What solves depleted is replenishment, which usually requires not doing something, or doing something that returns rather than takes. That is a harder prescription to follow in an environment that rewards throughput. But it is the accurate one.

I want to name something about ambition and identity that I think gets underexamined. For people who have organized their sense of self around achievement — around being someone who does well, who works hard, who performs — burnout is not just an energy crisis. It is an identity crisis.

Because the self that is depleted is not a separate self from the self that achieves. They are the same self. And when the capacity to achieve at the usual level diminishes — even temporarily, even for legitimate reasons — it can feel like the self is diminishing. Not just the outputs. The person.

One of the things I got wrong for a long time was treating rest as a reward. I would earn it — finish the project, clear the queue, get to a stopping point — and then allow myself to stop. I have come to think that one of the most important skills — one that is almost never taught — is knowing the difference between a problem that requires more effort and a problem that requires less. Most of the environments that produce high achievers select, heavily, for the first. If something is not working, you try harder. If you are not where you want to be, you do more. The question I find myself coming back to, and that I think is worth sitting with, is: what would it mean to take your own signals seriously. Not to fix them immediately. Not to know what to do with them. Just to take them seriously as information rather than as weakness or inconvenience.

Most of the training — in school, in early work environments, in culture generally — is training in override. You feel tired; you continue. You feel uncertain; you project confidence. Taking your own signals seriously is a different orientation. It means treating the interior experience as data rather than as noise to be managed. It means asking, when something feels off, what that is actually telling you — not to be paralyzed by it, but to let it inform you. This is harder than override, because it does not have the same clear narrative of toughness. But it is more accurate. And accuracy, in the long run, is more sustainable.

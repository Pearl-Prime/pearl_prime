Writing for Robot Narration

SpiritualTech Systems — February 2026

The TTS Problem

Google Play's auto-generated audiobooks use text-to-speech with zero performance control. No vocal warmth. No pacing. No emphasis. No silence. A robot reads your ebook straight, with flat prosody and mechanical rhythm.

This means 100% of the emotional weight must come from how the text is written. The prose itself must create the emotion. Sentence structure must guide the pacing. White space must become breath.

Your V4.5 structure is brilliant (temperature curves, character threads, interrupts, cost chapters). But without TTS-optimized prose, that structure will sound like a Wikipedia article read by Siri.

Core Principle

Write for the ear, not the eye. TTS cannot convey nuance, subtext, or emotional coloring through vocal performance. What survives:

• Short, punchy sentences (TTS handles these cleanly)

• Concrete sensory language (not abstract emotional states)

• Strategic repetition (creates rhythm robots can't kill)

• Paragraph breaks as pause markers (your only pacing tool)

• Dialogue (character voices differentiate okay in TTS)

• Clear imperatives ("Notice X. Feel Y." not "You might notice...")

What dies in TTS:

• Subtle emotional coloring ("a flicker of recognition")

• Long flowing sentences with embedded clauses

• Rhetorical questions (sound preachy)

• Tentative language ("perhaps," "you might," "it's possible")

• Abstract internal states without concrete anchors

Critical Word Count Adjustments for TTS

Your V4.5 reflection ceiling of 350 words was designed for human narration with vocal warmth. In robot TTS, 350 words of teaching = 2+ minutes of monotone lecture. Listeners zone out or skip.

New Reflection Limits for TTS:

Tier S (Deep Books): 220 words maximum, 180 post-impact

Tier A (Extended): 200 words maximum, 160 post-impact

Tier B (Standard/Short): 180 words maximum, 140 post-impact

Tier C (Micro): 120 words maximum

Tier D (Capsules): 80 words maximum

Tier E (Resets): No reflections

RULE: Break teaching into smaller chunks. More white space. More story, less exposition.

Atom-Specific Prose Rules

HOOK Atoms

Purpose: Drop listener into recognition within 15 seconds.

TTS Rules:

• Maximum 12 words per sentence in the opening

• First sentence = pure concrete image, no context

• Second sentence = body state or immediate action

• Third sentence (optional) = single-clause recognition

• No rhetorical questions

• No "Imagine this" or "Have you ever" openers (sound forced in TTS)

• Use present tense for immediacy

❌ WRONG (sounds like lecture in TTS):

"You know that feeling when you're in a meeting and someone asks you a question, and suddenly your mind goes blank, and you can feel everyone's eyes on you, and there's this terrible moment where you realize you have nothing to say?"

Problems: 42-word sentence. Abstract "that feeling." Rhetorical question dies in robot voice.

✅ CORRECT (lands in TTS):

"The question hangs in the air. Your throat tightens. Every face turned toward you, waiting."

Why it works: Three sentences, 11/3/7 words. Concrete images. Present tense. No questions.

SCENE Atoms

Purpose: Ground the listener in a specific moment, body state, or environment.

TTS Rules:

• One sensory detail per sentence

• Concrete nouns + active verbs only

• No "feeling" verbs (felt, sensed, noticed). Use body states.

• Maximum 10 words per sentence for sensory beats

• Paragraph break = scene shift or breath

• Never: "She could feel..." Always: "Her hands shook."

❌ WRONG:

"Sarah sat in the conference room feeling increasingly uncomfortable as the presentation went on, noticing a creeping sense of unease settling in her chest."

Problems: 27 words. "Feeling" and "noticing" = abstract. "Creeping sense of unease" = vague.

✅ CORRECT:

"Sarah's palms sweat against the table. Her breath shallow. The presenter's voice fading to background noise."

Why it works: Three sentences, 7/3/8 words. Body states. Concrete images. No emotional labels.

STORY Atoms

Purpose: Show a character in the engine, paying the cost, or interrupting.

TTS Rules:

• Dialogue differentiates character voice (use liberally)

• Action beats between dialogue, not emotional description

• Maximum 15 words per sentence in emotional moments

• One action per sentence

• White space = silence or beat

• For Tier C (Micro): minimum 130-200 words per story (up from 60-150)

• Never explain what the character feels. Show body state + action.

❌ WRONG:

"Marcus felt overwhelmed by a mixture of shame and defensiveness when his manager criticized the report, experiencing a familiar surge of anxiety that made him want to explain himself but also withdraw."

Problems: 33 words. All internal states. No body. No action. No dialogue.

✅ CORRECT:

"'The data doesn't support this conclusion,' his manager said.

Marcus's jaw tightened. 'I can explain—'

But his throat closed. The words stuck."

Why it works: Dialogue + body state. Short sentences. Concrete actions. White space creates the emotional beat.

Silence Beat Technique:

TTS cannot do literal silence. Write silence as a held moment:

"She sat with it.

Just sat.

Thirty seconds became a minute. The panic didn't leave. But something in her stopped fighting it."

REFLECTION Atoms

Purpose: Teach the mechanism. This is where most TTS audiobooks die.

TTS Rules:

• Teach in 2-3 sentence bursts, then white space

• Use "you" not "we" or "one might" (directness survives TTS)

• No rhetorical questions (sound condescending in robot voice)

• Metaphor = one image, two sentences maximum

• Maximum 12 words per sentence for teaching beats

• Break every 60-80 words with paragraph space

• Never: "Think about how..." Always: "Notice what happens when..."

• Statement-based teaching only. No questions.

❌ WRONG:

"Have you ever wondered why it's so hard to speak up in the moment? The thing is, our nervous system doesn't really care about whether we're in actual danger or just social danger. When we perceive a threat—whether it's a lion or a disapproving look—the amygdala fires, stress hormones flood our system, and suddenly our prefrontal cortex, the part responsible for rational thought and articulate speech, goes offline. It's like trying to write an essay while running from a bear. Your brain has other priorities."

Problems: Rhetorical question opener. 89-word block with no breaks. Mixed metaphors. Casual "the thing is" dies in TTS. Sounds like a TED Talk.

✅ CORRECT:

"Your nervous system doesn't distinguish threats. A lion triggers the same alarm as a critical glance.

When the alarm fires, stress hormones flood your system. Your prefrontal cortex—the part that forms words—shuts down.

You can't think clearly because your brain isn't trying to think. It's trying to survive."

Why it works: No questions. Three chunks. 13/19/15 words. White space = processing time. Direct statements.

EXERCISE Atoms

Purpose: Guide the listener into a practice. This is where TTS actually works well.

TTS Rules:

• Numbered steps or single-line instructions

• Clear imperatives: "Notice. Feel. Stay."

• No "you might" or "perhaps" (kills instruction)

• One instruction per sentence

• Body-based language only (no abstract concepts)

• For timed practices: "Count to four" not "for about four seconds"

❌ WRONG:

"You might want to try noticing your breath, and if it feels comfortable, perhaps place one hand on your chest and see if you can feel it rising and falling. Sometimes it helps to count, maybe four counts in and six counts out, but do whatever feels right for you."

Problems: Tentative language ("might," "perhaps," "maybe"). Too many options. No clear sequence.

✅ CORRECT:

"Place your hand on your chest.

Feel it rise and fall.

Breathe in for four counts. Out for six.

Stay with the rhythm."

Why it works: Direct commands. Clear sequence. No options. Body-focused.

INTEGRATION Atoms

Purpose: Land the listener. This must be SHORT in TTS.

TTS Rules:

• Maximum three sentences

• Concrete body state only

• No "You're okay" or "You've got this" (sounds hollow in robot voice)

• Use "Still here" not "You're safe"

• Present tense grounding

By Integration Mode:

BODY-LANDED: "Feet on floor. Breath steady. Still here."

COST-VISIBLE: "You know the cost now. What it takes. What it protects."

NEXT-KNOWN: "Tomorrow. Same time. You'll do this again."

CHOICE-CLEAR: "You can interrupt. Or you can freeze. The choice exists."

STILL-HERE: "Still breathing. Still feeling. Still here."

Tier-Specific Prose Adaptations

Tier S/A (Deep & Extended Books)

You have hours. Use them wisely.

• Vary sentence length to prevent monotony (but keep emotional beats under 15 words)

• Use paragraph structure as pacing: tight paragraphs for tension, spacious for processing

• Character dialogue can be longer (2-3 sentences per turn)

• Teaching can go deeper, but still chunk every 60-80 words

• Between Parts: use white space more aggressively

Tier B/C (Standard, Short, Micro)

Every sentence carries more weight. Maximum precision.

• Default to 10 words or less per sentence

• Story atoms: 130-200 words minimum for Tier C

• No two SCENE atoms can open with the same syntactic pattern

• Reflections: maximum 120-180 words

• More body, less explanation

Tier D (Capsules)

Surgical precision. One mechanism, one moment, one practice.

• No SCENE in 5-minute capsules (no time to ground—drop straight into story)

• Reflections: 80 words absolute maximum

• Integration: one sentence

• Exercise: 3-4 steps maximum

Tier E (Resets)

Pure regulation. No story. No teaching.

• Maximum 8 words per instruction

• SCENE (2-min only): 60-80 words, body state only

• EXERCISE: guided steps, present tense

• INTEGRATION: one sentence, body-landed

Sentence-Level Techniques That Survive TTS

1. Sentence Fragments for Emphasis

Fragments create rhythm and emphasis that TTS can't kill.

Example: "Not okay. Not even close. But still breathing."

2. Strategic Repetition

Repeat structure or key words to create resonance.

Example: "Same room. Same table. Same panic."

3. Concrete Sensory Anchors

Ground every emotional moment in a body state or sensory detail.

Don't say: "She felt anxious."
Say: "Her chest tightened. Breath shallow."

4. Paragraph Breaks as Breath

Your only pacing tool. Use white space to create processing time.

Tight paragraph = tension:
"He opened his mouth. Nothing came out. His boss waited. The silence stretched."

White space = processing:
"He opened his mouth.

Nothing.

His boss waited."

5. Active Verbs Only

Passive voice and weak verbs die in TTS.

Don't say: "She was feeling overwhelmed by the situation."
Say: "The situation crushed her. She couldn't breathe."

What Never to Do in TTS Prose

1. No Rhetorical Questions in Teaching

Robot voice makes them sound condescending or preachy.

❌ "Why do we freeze?"

✅ "You freeze because the alarm fires."

2. No Abstract Emotional States Without Body Anchors

❌ "She felt a creeping sense of unease."

✅ "Her stomach tightened. Something wasn't right."

3. No Tentative Language in Exercises

❌ "You might want to try placing your hand on your chest."

✅ "Place your hand on your chest."

4. No Long Embedded Clauses

❌ "When you notice the alarm firing, which often happens in moments of perceived social threat, try to remember that your nervous system, having evolved over millions of years to protect you from actual danger, doesn't distinguish between physical and emotional threats."

✅ "The alarm fires. Your nervous system doesn't know the difference. Social threat feels like physical danger."

5. No "You're Okay" Language in Integration

Sounds hollow and dismissive in robot voice.

❌ "You're okay. You're safe. You've got this."

✅ "Still here. Still breathing. Feet on floor."

Testing Your Prose for TTS

Before finalizing any atom, run these checks:

Read Aloud Test:

Read it in a monotone. If it sounds flat or awkward, it will die in TTS. Rewrite.

Sentence Length Audit:

Count words. Emotional beats: 15 max. Teaching: 12 max. Instructions: 10 max. Grounding: 8 max.

Body State Check:

Highlight every emotional moment. Is there a concrete body anchor? If not, add one.

Question Elimination:

Search for "?" — Convert all teaching questions to statements.

White Space Assessment:

Do paragraph breaks create processing time? Or does text run together?

Final Reminder

Your V4.5 structure is world-class. Temperature curves, character threads, interrupt sequences, cost chapters—this is sophisticated emotional architecture.

But structure alone cannot carry emotional weight in TTS. The prose must do the work.

Short sentences. Concrete images. Body states. White space. Strategic repetition. Direct statements.

These are not writing shortcuts. These are precision tools for making emotion survive robot narration.

Write for the ear. The listener cannot rewind to catch a subtle clause. They cannot see paragraph breaks to know when to pause. The robot will not convey warmth or emphasis.

The text itself must breathe.

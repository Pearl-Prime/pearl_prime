# GEN ALPHA STUDENTS - FAMILY CONFLICT SCENES
## Ages 12-16 | Middle School Context | 2026

## SCENE F-01 | The Dinner Table Interrogation
**Setting:** Kitchen, 6:42 PM, Wednesday
**Engine:** Overwhelm

"How was school?"

"Fine."

"What did you do?"

"Stuff."

Riley's dad sighs. "Can you give me more than one word?"

Riley's phone is face-up on the table. Three texts from friends. A notification from the group chat. Her dad's rule: phones at dinner, but you can't touch them.

"We had a test."

"How'd you do?"

"Fine."

"Riley, I'm trying to have a conversation with you."

"I'm answering."

"Barely."

Riley's mom jumps in. "She's tired, Dan."

"I'm asking about her day. That's not unreasonable."

Riley cuts her chicken. Chews. Swallows. Doesn't make eye contact.

She doesn't want to talk about school. Doesn't want to explain the test she probably failed or the friend drama or the teacher who called her out in class.

She wants to eat in silence. Go to her room. Exist without being interrogated.

But her parents want connection. Want conversation. Want access to a version of her that doesn't exist at dinner tables.

"Can I be excused?"

"You barely touched your food."

"I'm not hungry."

Her dad's jaw tightens. "Fine. Go."

Riley goes. Closes her door. Puts in headphones.

Her parents mean well. She knows that.

But sometimes meaning well feels like invasion.

## SCENE F-02 | The Sibling Comparison
**Setting:** Car ride home, 4:17 PM, Tuesday
**Engine:** Shame

Kai's mom picks him up from school. His older sister Mia is in the passenger seat.

"Mia got a 98 on her chemistry test."

"That's cool."

"What'd you get on yours?"

"Haven't gotten it back yet."

Lie. He got a 74. It's in his backpack.

"Mia's applying to summer programs. You should look into that."

"Maybe."

"She got accepted to three already. Very competitive."

Kai nods. Stares out the window.

Mia's perfect. 4.0 GPA. Student government. Debate team captain. Early admission to her dream school.

Kai's… not failing. But he's not Mia.

His parents don't say it out loud. But he hears it anyway.

Every time they mention her achievements. Every time they suggest he "try harder" or "apply himself."

He's the second draft. The one that's good, but not as good.

They pull into the driveway. His mom turns around.

"I'm proud of you, Kai. You know that, right?"

"Yeah."

He doesn't believe her.

Because if she was proud, she wouldn't need to mention Mia first.

## SCENE F-03 | The Divorce Custody Schedule
**Setting:** Dad's apartment, 6:47 PM, Friday
**Engine:** Grief

Maya lives at her mom's Monday through Thursday. Her dad's Friday through Sunday.

She has two bedrooms. Two sets of clothes. Two toothbrushes. Two lives.

Tonight she forgot her charger at her mom's. Her dad doesn't have the right kind.

"Can we swing by and get it?"

"Your mom's not home. She's at yoga."

"I have her key."

Her dad sighs. "Maya, we agreed on boundaries."

Boundaries. That's the word they use now. Like Maya's the border between two countries.

She does homework in silence. Phone dying. Can't charge it.

Tomorrow she's supposed to go to a birthday party. The invite's at her mom's. Her present's at her mom's. Her nice shirt's at her mom's.

She texts her mom: "can you drop off my stuff tomorrow morning"

"Can't, sweetie. Brunch plans. Ask your dad."

Maya doesn't ask her dad. Knows he'll say something about "your mom's job to remember your things."

She'll skip the party. Easier that way.

Her life split in half and everyone acts like it's normal now.

It's not.

## SCENE F-04 | The Parent Mental Health Load
**Setting:** Living room, 9:34 PM, Tuesday
**Engine:** Overwhelm

Zara's mom is crying in the kitchen. Again.

Zara hears it from her room. That specific sound. Her mom trying to be quiet but not quiet enough.

Her dad's out of town. Work trip. Third one this month.

Zara should go downstairs. Should ask if she's okay. Should do… something.

But she's thirteen. She doesn't know what to say. And her mom will just say "I'm fine, honey. Just tired."

She's not fine. She hasn't been fine in months.

Zara texts her older brother at college: "mom's crying again"

"Is she okay?"

"Idk. dad's gone"

"Just give her space. she'll be fine"

Zara locks her phone. Puts in headphones. Turns up music.

She's not a therapist. She's not an adult. She shouldn't have to manage her mom's feelings.

But someone has to. And her dad's not here. And her brother's three hours away.

So Zara goes downstairs. Makes tea. Sits with her mom. Doesn't say anything.

Just sits.

Her mom stops crying. Smiles. "You're so mature for your age."

Zara doesn't want to be mature. She wants to be thirteen.

But someone has to hold this family together.

And apparently tonight it's her.

## SCENE F-05 | The Immigrant Expectations Gap
**Setting:** Kitchen table, 7:52 PM, Sunday
**Engine:** Spiral

Tyler's parents came to the US when they were twenty. Worked three jobs. Put themselves through college.

Tyler's heard the story a thousand times.

Tonight his mom's telling it again. Because Tyler got a B in math.

"We didn't come here for you to get B's."

"It's one grade."

"One grade becomes a pattern. A pattern becomes a future."

His dad nods. "When I was your age—"

Tyler zones out. He's heard this part too.

When his dad was Tyler's age, he was studying by candlelight. Walking miles to school. Translating for his parents.

Tyler's privileged. He knows that. He has a roof, food, wifi, safety.

But his parents' sacrifices live in his chest like debt he can never repay.

Every B is proof he's wasting their struggle.

Every complaint is proof he's ungrateful.

His mom pushes the textbook toward him. "Study harder."

Tyler nods. Takes the book to his room.

He's not allowed to fail. Not allowed to be average.

Because his parents gave up everything so he could succeed.

And success is the only acceptable thank-you note.

## SCENE F-06 | The Phone Surveillance
**Setting:** Bedroom, 8:17 PM, Thursday
**Engine:** Shame

Jordan's dad installed Life360 on his phone. Tracking app. Sees everywhere Jordan goes.

"It's for safety," his dad said.

But it's not just location. It's screen time reports. App usage. Who he texts.

Tonight Jordan's at Dev's house. His dad texts: "why are you still there? Said you'd be home by 8."

It's 8:04.

Jordan texts back: "leaving now"

He's not doing anything bad. Just hanging out. Playing video games.

But every notification feels like supervision.

His friend Maya doesn't have Life360. Neither does Kai.

Jordan asked his dad to remove it. His dad said no. "When you pay for your own phone, you can have privacy."

So Jordan's phone isn't his. It's his dad's. On loan.

He gets in the car. Drives home. Watches his dot move on the map his dad is probably watching.

He's fifteen. Hasn't done anything to lose trust.

But trust isn't the default. Surveillance is.

And Jordan's learning that privacy is a privilege he hasn't earned yet.

Maybe won't ever earn.

Because parents who grew up without tracking can't imagine not using it.

And kids who grew up with it can't imagine freedom.

## SCENE F-07 | The Money Fight Background Noise
**Setting:** Bedroom, 10:42 PM, Tuesday
**Engine:** Watcher

Mira lies in bed. Her parents are fighting downstairs.

Not yelling. Worse. That tense, quiet fighting where every word is sharp.

"We can't afford—"

"I know we can't afford—"

"Then why did you—"

Mira puts in headphones. Plays music. Doesn't help. She can still hear them.

Money fights are the worst. Because they don't resolve. They just… pause.

Her mom wanted to sign her up for dance camp. $800. Her dad said no.

They've been fighting for three days.

Mira doesn't even want dance camp that much. But now it's not about camp. It's about what they can't afford. What they used to afford. What's changed.

Her dad lost his job six months ago. Got a new one. Pays less.

They don't talk about it directly. But Mira hears it. Sees it. Feels it.

Smaller grocery trips. Her mom saying "we have food at home" more often. The conversation about whether they can afford school pictures.

Mira's thirteen. Old enough to understand money stress. Too young to fix it.

The fighting stops. Doors close. Silence.

Mira turns off her music. Stares at the ceiling.

Tomorrow they'll pretend everything's fine.

She'll pretend she didn't hear.

They'll all perform normal until the next money fight.

And Mira will lie here again, headphones in, wondering when financial stress became the soundtrack of her childhood.

# You're Not a Savior - You're a Teacher

## For: Every educator carrying the weight of 30+ lives on their shoulders

You went into teaching to make a difference.

Now you're:
- Counselor
- Social worker
- Nurse
- Parent
- Referee
- Meal provider
- Trauma responder
- Safety officer

**And somewhere in there, you're supposed to actually teach.**

### The Savior Complex Trap

**What teacher training sold you:**  
"You'll change lives! Every child can succeed with the right teacher!"

**What they didn't tell you:**  
You can't save kids from poverty, abuse, hunger, or broken systems. You can only show up and do your best within those constraints.

### The Reality Check

**You are responsible for:**
- Creating a safe classroom environment
- Teaching your curriculum effectively
- Treating students with respect
- Showing up prepared
- Following your legal/ethical obligations
- Being a consistent adult presence

**You are NOT responsible for:**
- Fixing their home life
- Solving systemic poverty
- Healing their trauma (you can support, not fix)
- Making their parents care
- Compensating for broken systems
- Sacrificing your mental health

**There's a difference. Know it.**

### The Framework: BOUNDARIES

**B - Basics first**  
You can't help anyone if you're falling apart

**O - Own what's yours**  
Teaching, yes. Parenting their kids, no.

**U - Understand your limits**  
You're one person, not a miracle worker

**N - No martyrdom**  
Suffering isn't noble, it's unsustainable

**D - Distinguish help from harm**  
Sometimes "helping" enables dysfunction

**A - Accept what you can't change**  
Systems, families, society - not in your control

**R - Recognize impact differently**  
Success isn't saving them all, it's showing up consistently

**I - Invest in yourself**  
Your needs matter too

**E - Exit when necessary**  
Some situations aren't fixable

**S - Separate their crisis from your crisis**  
Their emergency ≠ your emergency (usually)

### What Students Actually Need From You

**Not:**
- You working 70 hours/week
- You spending your own money on supplies
- You sacrificing your family for theirs
- You being available 24/7
- You fixing everything

**But:**
- Consistent presence
- Clear expectations
- Safe environment
- Someone who shows up
- Boundaries they can count on
- A functional adult in their life

**Burned out teachers help no one.**

### The Stories That Break You

You will have students with:
- Abusive homes
- Food insecurity
- Homelessness
- Undiagnosed learning disabilities
- Untreated mental illness
- Neglectful parents
- Trauma you can't imagine

**You cannot save them all.**

**What you can do:**
- Report abuse (legally required)
- Connect families to resources
- Create safe classroom space
- Teach them despite their circumstances
- Be a consistent adult
- Let the system do its job (even when it fails)

**What you cannot do:**
- Take them home
- Fix their parents
- Solve poverty
- Heal their trauma
- Make everything okay

**This will hurt. That's normal.**

### When to Involve Others

**Call CPS/authorities:**
- Suspected abuse or neglect
- Safety concerns
- Legal requirement to report

**Involve counselor/social worker:**
- Mental health concerns
- Family issues affecting school
- Behavioral patterns
- Suspected learning issues

**Involve admin:**
- Serious behavioral issues
- Parent conflicts
- Safety concerns
- When you need backup

**You're not supposed to handle everything alone.**

### The Emotional Toll

**What happens:**
- You go home thinking about students
- You cry about their situations
- You feel helpless
- You question if you're doing enough
- You feel guilty for having boundaries
- You absorb their trauma

**This is compassion fatigue. It's real. It's common.**

### Self-Preservation Strategies

**At school:**
- Take your lunch break (away from classroom)
- Leave by X time (pick a time, stick to it)
- Use your planning period for planning (not other stuff)
- Close your door when you need quiet
- Say no to extra committees

**After school:**
- Have a decompression ritual (drive different route, change clothes, etc.)
- Don't check email after X time
- Talk to someone who isn't a teacher sometimes
- Do something completely unrelated to teaching

**Weekends:**
- At least one full day with zero school work
- See non-teacher friends
- Have a hobby
- Remember who you are outside the classroom

**Summers:**
- Actually rest
- Don't work the whole summer
- Recharge before August panic

### The Parent Problem

**Difficult parents who:**
- Blame you for their kid's behavior
- Demand you fix what they won't address
- Undermine your authority
- Expect you to be available 24/7
- Treat you like the help

**Your response:**
- Professional boundaries
- Document everything
- Loop in admin when needed
- Don't engage in arguments
- Refer to school policies
- "I hear your concern. Here's what's within my scope..."

**You're not their employee. You're a professional.**

### When Students Share Trauma

**What to do:**
- Listen without fixing
- Thank them for trusting you
- Report if required by law
- Connect to counselor
- Don't take it home (easier said than done)

**What not to do:**
- Promise to fix it
- Share your own trauma
- Become their therapist
- Tell other students
- Violate confidentiality (unless legally required)

### The "But What If I'm Their Only..."

**The thought that haunts you:**  
"What if I'm the only stable adult in their life?"

**The reality:**  
That might be true. But you being a stable, boundaried adult is better than being a burned-out, resentful one.

**Sustainable support > unsustainable sacrifice**

### Permission Slips

You're allowed to:
- Leave work at work sometimes
- Say no to extra duties
- Not buy supplies with your own money
- Have a life outside teaching
- Not respond to emails immediately
- Set boundaries with parents
- Take mental health days
- Care about your family as much as students
- Consider leaving teaching if it's destroying you

### Red Flags You're Burning Out

**Check yourself:**
- Dreading going to work
- Crying regularly about school
- Losing sleep over students
- Snapping at loved ones
- Physical symptoms (headaches, stomach issues)
- Drinking more to cope
- Fantasizing about quitting
- Feeling hopeless
- Not caring anymore (emotional shutdown)

**If multiple boxes checked: Get help. Therapy. Admin. Something needs to change.**

### What "Good Teaching" Actually Is

**Not:**
- Saving every child
- Working 80 hours/week
- Never having students fail
- Being available 24/7
- Sacrificing everything

**But:**
- Showing up consistently
- Teaching effectively
- Creating safe environment
- Being fair and respectful
- Growing your skills
- Surviving to teach tomorrow

**Sustainability = success**

### Bottom Line

You're a teacher.

Not a social worker.  
Not a therapist.  
Not a parent.  
Not a savior.

**You're a human doing your job within a broken system.**

Do what you can. Let go of what you can't.

**And remember: You staying healthy helps more kids than you burning out trying to save them all.**

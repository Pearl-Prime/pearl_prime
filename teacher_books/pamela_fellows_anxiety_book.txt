A Note on the Teachings of Pamela Fellows

I was not a direct student of Pamela Fellows. I encountered their work through books, talks, and publicly available teachings. What follows is not an official interpretation of Pamela Fellows's work — it is an application. I have done my best to honor the integrity of the original teachings while translating them into practical guidance for the challenges you may be facing.

Pamela Fellows's understanding of anxiety reshaped the way I see this subject. Their approach — rooted in Western somatic and relational; attachment-informed; nervous system regulation; trauma-sensitive — offers a lens that goes beyond conventional advice. It speaks to something deeper: the patterns beneath the surface, the quiet mechanisms that keep us stuck, and the often-overlooked pathways toward genuine relief.

This book applies Pamela Fellows's teachings to the specific experience of gen z professionals navigating anxiety. It does not replace the teacher's original work. Where I have adapted exercises or frameworks, I have done so with care and transparency. Any simplification is mine, not theirs.

If something in these pages resonates with you, I encourage you to go to the source. Seek out Pamela Fellows's own words — their talks, their writings, their direct teachings. What I offer here is a bridge, not a destination. The real work lives in the original.

Just practices. Short ones. And a way of seeing what is already happening in your body so it stops running the show.

There is an old practice in Western medicine, now being remembered again, called the window of tolerance. It describes a zone where the nervous system can function—not too hyped up, not too shut down. In that zone, learning happens. Healing happens. Everything else happens.

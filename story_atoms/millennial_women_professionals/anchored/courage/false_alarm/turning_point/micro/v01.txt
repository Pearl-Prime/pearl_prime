Alina books fifteen minutes with her manager before the next renewal. She writes the agenda line herself: pricing authority on scope changes. Her hands are cold when she hits send. In the call she says: I want to be the one who states the number when scope shifts, not the one who documents it after the fact. Her manager is quiet for a beat, then says, that makes sense, you've had the number right every time. Alina's shoulders come down half an inch. She has stopped calling it nerves. It has a name now: the exile reflex — her body sounding the banishment alarm over a spreadsheet, because it never learned the difference between a bad quarter and a bad tribe. The alarm was never wrong about the intensity. It was aimed at the wrong century. She writes the number down where the client will see it. On the next call, she says it first.

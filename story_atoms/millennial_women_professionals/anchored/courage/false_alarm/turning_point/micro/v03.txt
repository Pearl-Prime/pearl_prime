Renata sets a rule for herself the Monday after the Friday she stayed quiet: any date that moves gets said out loud within twenty-four hours, to the client, by her, not buried in a draft. The next time a date moves, she opens a blank message and writes one sentence first — the date, the reason, nothing softened ahead of it — before she lets herself add any context at all. Her hands are cold hitting send. She waits for the silence that follows to turn into something bad. It doesn't. The client writes back thanks for the heads-up, we'll adjust on our end. Her shoulders drop. She has a name for the cold hands now: the exile reflex, the same alarm that would fire if she were actually about to be cast out of the group, going off over a rescheduled deliverable. The alarm isn't lying about how it feels. It's lying about what's at stake. She sends the next update in ninety seconds, no draft folder involved.

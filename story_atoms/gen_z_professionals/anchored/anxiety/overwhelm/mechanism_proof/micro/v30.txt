The practice did not live where she thought it lived. Priya assumed it lived in her — willpower, identity, three years of reps compounding into a calmer person. The move falsifies that theory inside two weeks. The mechanism is duller and kinder: habits are loops hung on cues, and every cue was a fixture of the old life. The 1:55 reminder. The microwave timer. The specific hallway between the meeting room and her desk that had become, without her ever deciding it, the place where meetings got closed. The new job did not attack her practices. It simply moved her into a building where none of the hooks were installed, and the loops, having nowhere to hang, dropped. What rushed back was not weakness either — it was the older loop, the vigilance one, which needs no fixtures because it is wired to novelty itself. New names, new acronyms, new ways to fail publicly: the threat system feasts on exactly what the regulation system starves on. She had been carrying the practices like luggage and the vigilance like skin. Understanding this does not rescue her week, and she is too worn down to celebrate it. But it retires the harshest story — that she quit on herself — and replaces it with an engineering problem: new building, no hooks, hooks can be hung.

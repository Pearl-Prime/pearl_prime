The evidence that the climate has changed is boring, which is why she almost cannot see it. Priya keeps no chart, but her calendar keeps one for her: the 1:55 walk, recurring, eighteen months unbroken across two jobs. The post-meeting close-out, invisible now, worn down from three minutes to thirty seconds because the body learned the exit and stopped needing the map. What the mechanism never delivered is the thing she originally ordered — silence. The hum is still there on plenty of afternoons: low vigilance with no object, shoulders a centimeter high, a system that scanned for threat through every open-plan office of her twenties and will not fully believe the war is over. She understood the design only when she stopped grading against zero. Regulation was never the removal of weather. It is the shortening of storms. The spike still comes; it just ends when the trigger does, instead of the old arithmetic — one spike, one lost evening. The rumination still knocks; it gets ninety seconds instead of the night. Averaged across a year, the same nervous system, running the same alarms, now returns her days mostly unspent. The climate changed. The weather kept its schedule. The difference — she can finally say this without flinching — was never the sky. It was the drainage.

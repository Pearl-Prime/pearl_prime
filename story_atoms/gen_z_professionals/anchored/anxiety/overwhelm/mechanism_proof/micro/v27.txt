The forecast starts with one neutral line and ends with a full career obituary. Priya reads we will follow up next week and her mind adds: they are softening the rejection, your references were weak, you should have sent the portfolio sooner. None of that is in the message. Her chest treats the fiction as fact because the body learned that imagining the worst once prevented a surprise layoff three years ago — one accurate forecast, years of false alarms, and the system still calls it a winning record. The old lesson still runs. She checks her bank app. She checks the job board. She checks whether her manager's calendar shows a one-on-one she did not schedule. Each check feeds the spiral a crumb of pseudo-evidence, and each crumb buys another lap. The email has not changed. The catastrophe has grown inside her anyway — a rehearsal so complete it steals an afternoon from a Tuesday that was not yet in danger, leaving her exhausted by a firing that exists nowhere except in her chest. She sees the mechanism only when she compares the screen to the story and finds two different documents: one written by a recruiter, one written by panic.

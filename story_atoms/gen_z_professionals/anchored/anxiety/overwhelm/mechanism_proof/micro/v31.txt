The anatomy of the eight minutes only makes sense on paper, afterward. Priya draws it for herself that weekend: minute one, the surge — adrenaline misfiring during a routine slide, a wave with a fixed chemistry and a fixed arc, built to crest and drain in roughly ninety seconds. Minutes two through eight: the bracing. The instant she fought the pounding — monitored it, catastrophized it, gripped the railings of her own body — she handed the alarm exactly what it reads as confirmation: evidence of ongoing threat. Fighting the wave is the wave. Every clench around the heartbeat was a message that the heartbeat was right to race. The mechanism even explains the strangest part, the part that scared her most — why it ended so suddenly. Nothing rescued her. She simply ran out of bracing when the meeting's attention moved on, and the original surge, ninety seconds old and long finished, was finally allowed to drain. The panic was one event. Her war against the panic was seven. She checks the arithmetic against the chapter and against her own pulse memory and cannot find the flaw — but she also knows that diagrams are cheap in daylight. The real test is the next wave, and whether she can lose that war on purpose.

The cycle has four acts and Priya knows them by heart. Act one: download during a bad week, grant permissions, choose a gentle voice. Act two: three good days, a notification that feels like praise. Act three: a travel week, a late night, a session skipped because she was too wrung out to sit still — the badge turns from green to grey. Act four: silence, then shame, then uninstall or ignore, then act one again under a new icon. None of the apps failed technically. Each one failed the math of her actual life — back-to-back meetings, a body that spikes at 9am and again at 9pm, no room for a twenty-minute ritual marketed as easy. The apps assumed a user with evenings; she has meetings where her evenings should be. The mechanism is not weak will. It is architecture mismatch: a nervous system that needs two-minute bridges attached to transitions she already makes, not a new personality installed on Sunday night. She saw it clearly only once she counted — four apps, four identical arcs, one variable that never changed: the size of the ask. Every program wanted her to become someone first and practice second. The ones that survive ask for two minutes of the person she already is.

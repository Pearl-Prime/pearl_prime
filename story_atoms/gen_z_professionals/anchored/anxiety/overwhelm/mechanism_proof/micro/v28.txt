The loop does not wait for a crisis; it runs on mornings as quiet as this one. Priya wakes, reads three notifications, and her shoulders are already forward before she stands. The word she has for it is normal — everyone checks their phone — but normal does not explain the tightness in her throat before she has spoken a single word today. She scrolls the team channel for problems that might become her problems. She finds none and scrolls again. The second pass is not information. It is confirmation that the scan works, that vigilance equals readiness, that if she stops checking something important will slip through and define her. That is the deal the pattern offers: pay a little dread every morning and nothing will ever surprise you. The phone rewards the loop with novelty every time — a new badge, a new headline, a new nothing. Her body pays in cortisol before breakfast. By 7:05 she is dressed and already spent. The habit is invisible because it looks like responsibility; from the outside it even looks like ambition. The cost is visible only in the mirror — the braced face, the eyes bright with a day that has not started yet — and in the evenings, when the vigilance she could not put down all day refuses to clock out just because she did.

The inherited urgency does not wait for ownership. Priya watches a senior engineer paste a screenshot into the channel and say this is blocking release, and her hands move toward her keyboard before she has read the ticket number. Her pulse answers as if the deadline were hers. She opens the tracker. The ticket is assigned to someone two time zones away who is already typing. She refreshes anyway. The refresh confirms what the first read showed: not her row, not her sprint, not her fire. Her body keeps burning anyway. Her jaw is clenched around a problem she cannot name, because the problem is not in the tracker — it is in the habit of treating every red icon like a personal summons, a habit built at her first job, where missing one alert cost a weekend of cleanup and a month of feeling ashamed in standups. The channel keeps scrolling. Her chest keeps answering. She closes the tab and the activation does not close with it. It follows her to the kitchen and into the four o'clock call, dimming by slow degrees — an alarm that no one assigned to her, and that no one but her can mark resolved.

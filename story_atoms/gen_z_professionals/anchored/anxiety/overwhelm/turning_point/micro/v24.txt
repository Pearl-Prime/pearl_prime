The test is one morning with the phone on airplane mode until she finishes breakfast. Priya expects disaster. What arrives is quieter: toast, tea, the sound of the neighbor's dog, her own voice saying good morning to an empty kitchen like it counts. She checks the phone at 7:22 instead of 6:31. Two messages matter. The rest were weather. Her chest is still not calm — she has a standup at nine — but the spike is smaller, the difference between sprinting and walking to a meeting that has not happened yet. Nothing slipped through. Nobody noticed her forty-five minute absence from a conversation that did not need her. That is the evidence the pattern never let her collect: the world holds together without her surveillance. She repeats the experiment the next day and misses it the third — wakes at 6:29 with her hand already moving, reads everything, arrives at the shower with the old jaw. For a minute she is ashamed, as if one checked phone erased two clean mornings, and then she refuses the shame's math. The two clean mornings still happened. The toast, the dog, her own voice in the kitchen — none of it is erased. The turning point is not perfection. It is proof that the loop is optional on some mornings, and optional is enough to begin unwiring it — one unwatched breakfast at a time.

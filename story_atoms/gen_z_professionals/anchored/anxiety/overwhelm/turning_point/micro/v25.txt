The next wave picks a worse room — the quarterly all-hands, four hundred tiles on the call, her camera on. The hammering starts at the same tempo as before, and the old sequence queues up behind it: fight, monitor, brace. This time Priya does the thing she rehearsed on safe, boring evenings at her desk. She lets her hands be wet. She lets the heart pound without appointing a supervisor over it. Feet flat, one exhale longer than the inhale — it shakes on the way out — and the sentence from the chapter said silently, like a passcode: this is one wave; the bracing is the second. It does not feel like a technique. It feels like agreeing to go under, for the longest forty seconds of her quarter. But the going-under does not come. The crest arrives — awful, familiar — and then, unbelievably, the drain, right on the schedule the diagram promised. Ninety seconds, maybe a hundred. Not eight minutes. She is still shaky when someone asks her a question, and her voice comes out level enough that nobody pauses. Afterward, in the bathroom, she cries briefly — not from panic, from the specific relief of learning the wave has an off-ramp built into it, and that she is allowed to take it. She washes her face slowly, the way you do when there is suddenly time, and the afternoon is still hers when she walks back into it.

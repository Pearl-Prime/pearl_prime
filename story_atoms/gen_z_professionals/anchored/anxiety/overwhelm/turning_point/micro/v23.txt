The interruption is a piece of paper. When the spiral starts its fourth lap — resume, runway, the apartment she will supposedly lose — Priya writes the email's actual words on a sticky note: we will follow up next week. Six words. She reads them out loud in the empty kitchen and hears how little they say. Then she writes underneath them the story her chest has been telling: rejected, unemployable, behind forever. Writing it down feels silly, and she is a little ashamed of how confidently her body authored it. The two documents do not match. They are not even about the same week. The spiral does not allow comparison while it is running; it only allows momentum. She puts the sticky note on the monitor and answers the email with two sentences about her availability. Her heart is still fast when she hits send. The catastrophe tries one more lap around nine o'clock and finds the note still there, six words long, refusing to grow. She sleeps badly, but she sleeps. The turning point was never the recruiter's reply. It was the moment the forecast had to stand next to the evidence and could not fill the note it was written on. In the morning the six words are still on the monitor, curling at one corner, saying only what they said.

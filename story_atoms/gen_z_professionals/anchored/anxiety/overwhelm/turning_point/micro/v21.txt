The boundary is one sentence she practices in the hallway after the bridge call. Not my ticket. Not my fire. She says it quietly, not for the team, for her chest — twice, because the first time her voice does not believe it. Her hands still itch to open the channel. She puts the phone face-down on the desk instead and returns to the task that was on her screen before the caps lock arrived. The document takes a full minute to make sense again — the cursor blinking where she left it, a sentence half-finished by a calmer person from an hour ago. The activation does not vanish. It drops one degree — enough to finish one paragraph before the urge to refresh returns, but when it returns she says the sentence again instead of arguing with it. She does not win the afternoon. She wins four minutes of work that belongs to her queue, not theirs, and then six, and by five o'clock she has lost the thread of the outage entirely and found the end of her own task. Priya marks that as the turning point: not calm, not indifferent, just a single refusal to inherit panic that was never addressed to her name. The cold tea is still at her elbow. She notices it this time — notices, even, that she is thirsty.

The rebuild starts smaller than pride would like. One anchor, not five: the badge reader at the parking-level door, which she touches every morning whether she remembers to or not. Priya pairs it with the only practice that fits inside a swipe — one long exhale, feet on the concrete, before the elevator arrives. The first morning she feels ridiculous. The fourth morning she stops feeling anything about it, which is the point; ceremony is for starting, invisibility is for lasting. Week two she hangs the second hook: the laptop lid, closed at day's end, becomes the new microwave — two minutes, no login, standing in a kitchen that is starting to smell like hers. She skips days. A launch week eats the walk entirely and she is too exhausted on Friday to do anything but the exhale, so she does the exhale. It counts, because counting was never the mechanism; the seam is. By the sixth week the new building has three hooks and her shoulders have relearned the 1:55 drop at a doorway instead of a reminder. Nothing transferred, but everything was rebuildable — that is the sentence she would send back to week two. The regulation was never the furniture. It was knowing how to hang loops on whatever furniture is there.

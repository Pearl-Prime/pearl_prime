The email from recruiting is three sentences long and polite. Thanks for your time. We will be in touch. Priya reads it twice and hears, underneath it, the version nobody wrote: we have decided to move forward with other candidates. She searches her sent folder for typos in her thank-you note. She opens LinkedIn to see whether the hiring manager posted anything that could be read as a signal. Nothing has changed in the thread since 11:07am. Her stomach drops anyway. She calculates how many months of runway she has if the offer disappears — a math problem with no numbers in the email, solved three different ways before six o'clock. She texts a friend a screenshot and asks what do you think this means, then apologizes for asking, then asks again. The message never stopped being polite. The catastrophe is fully authored inside her chest before any reply arrives, complete with a final scene where she has lost the apartment and is explaining the gap on her resume. She knows the words on the screen are not the words her body is answering. But she answers the body's version first, every time — and the evening is spent on a rejection nobody has sent.

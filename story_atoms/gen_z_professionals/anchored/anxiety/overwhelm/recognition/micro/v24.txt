The alarm fires at 6:30 the next morning and she reaches for it before her feet touch the floor. Three Slack badges. A calendar notification for a meeting that is still four hours away. A news headline she never asked to see. She reads all of them lying down, still half in sleep, and feels her heart rate climb as if the day has already started badly. She has not stood up. She has not eaten. She has not spoken to another person. The cortisol is already running a shift no one assigned her. She tells herself she is just catching up, but there is nothing that needs catching up — the badges are a meme thread and a calendar shuffle, and she reads them twice anyway. Her shoulders disagree with the word relax. By the time she walks to the shower her jaw is set and her breath is high in her chest — not because anything happened in the kitchen, but because the phone taught her body that morning equals emergency, one scroll at a time, for three years. She is exhausted by nine and blames the meeting. Priya notices the pattern only after it has completed its first lap — and only because this time she happens to be watching.

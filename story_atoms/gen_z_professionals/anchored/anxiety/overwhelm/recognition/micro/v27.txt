The surge arrives in the middle of a sentence about onboarding metrics. One minute Priya is presenting a slide she knows cold; the next her heart is hammering hard enough to hear over her own voice, her vision tightens at the edges, and a cold certainty floods in that something is medically wrong. She cannot feel her hands, but she keeps talking. The sentence lands. Nobody reacts. Inside, a second emergency is running: what is happening to me — not here, not now. Her chest feels like a fist that someone else is closing. She has read the chapter words — mobilization, false alarm, ninety seconds — and none of them are reachable from inside this. The meeting moves to the next agenda item and she mutes herself and breathes like someone bailing water. Eight minutes later it is over, gone as strangely as it came, leaving her exhausted and embarrassed at a desk where nothing visible happened. She steers around the small conference room for the rest of the afternoon, and that night she catches herself rehearsing excuses for meetings no one has scheduled yet. The panic lasted eight minutes. The fear of the next one moves in like a tenant. That, she will learn, is the part holding the lease.

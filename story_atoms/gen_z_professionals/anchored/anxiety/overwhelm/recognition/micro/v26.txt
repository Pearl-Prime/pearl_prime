The calendar hold did not survive the migration. At the old job it fired at 1:55 every weekday — walk, two blocks, no phone — and her body had learned to loosen at the reminder like a commuter hearing the last stop announced. The new company's calendar imported her meetings and dropped everything else. Priya discovers this on day nine, at 4:40pm, staring at a wall of back-to-back blocks, when it registers that she has not left the building since Monday and her jaw has been set since the morning security briefing. The two-minute timer lives on a microwave in an apartment she no longer cooks in before seven. The post-meeting close-out assumed meetings that ended; here they roll into each other like weather fronts. None of the practices were carried in her head, she can see that now — they were carried in the furniture of a life that no longer exists. She is exhausted in the specific way she had forgotten, the way that predates the practices. But the frightening thought is not the tiredness. It is the question underneath: if three years of regulation could be undone by a change of address, was any of it hers — or was the calm only on loan from a building that kept it when she returned the badge?

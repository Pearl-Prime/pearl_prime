The channel erupts again on a Tuesday in March — red circles, all caps, someone's deploy on fire — and Priya notices the difference before she has finished reading the first message. Her chest tightens: one notch, familiar, almost polite. She reads the thread once instead of four times. Her hand does not move toward the dashboard that was never hers to open. The tightness peaks somewhere around the second screenshot and then, finding nothing to hold onto, starts down the other side while she is still reading. Two years ago this exact alarm cost her an afternoon and left her too exhausted to cook. She remembers that woman clearly and owes her everything — the practices that feel automatic now were hand-cranked then, badly, twice a week, in hallways and parking garages. Nothing dramatic has replaced the old afternoons. That is the strange part: recovery kept none of its promises about fireworks. It just quietly shortened everything — the spike, the replay, the aftermath. The fire in the channel gets contained by the people whose job it is. Her tea is still warm when she finishes it. But she nearly missed the whole milestone, because the absence of a spiral does not send a notification. Nothing happened. It took three years of practice for nothing to happen.

The wellness app sends a streak reminder at 7:04am, into a morning where Priya is already running a deficit. She has not opened the app in eleven days. The badge shows a broken chain icon and a cheerful sentence about getting back on track. Her chest responds as if the notification were a performance review. She deletes the alert without opening the app and feels relief for ninety seconds, then guilt for the rest of the morning — she is ashamed of being ashamed of an app, which somehow doubles the bill. The guilt is not about meditation. It is about another system that treats her body like a project she keeps failing: the gym membership, the journaling app, the water bottle with the time markings down the side, each one purchased in a moment of resolve and dropped in a week that had no room for resolve. She makes coffee and notices the pattern: not the app itself, but the way each abandoned tool becomes proof that she is the kind of person who cannot follow through. She lists the counter-evidence — she has never missed a deadline, never skipped a standup — and the guilt is unmoved, because the guilt was never about consistency. The story is harsh. The data is incomplete. But the story does not read data. It reads streaks.

He worked the extra twelve. Now it is 3 a.m. and Marcus is on his back in the dark, wide awake, running the number the shift was supposed to fix. Time-and-a-half times twelve, minus the tax, against the gap her cut hours left. The math comes out fine. His jaw stays tight anyway. He took the shift to buy the family a floor to stand on, and the shift has taken the one thing the money cannot: the sleep before the next one. He is on shift in five hours. His eyes stay open.

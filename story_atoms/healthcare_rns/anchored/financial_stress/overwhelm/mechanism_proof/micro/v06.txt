The Post-it has a twin now, and a third behind it, all folded into the same pocket, all the same figure copied in a hand that presses harder each shift. Colleen recalculates while a patient's IV runs, while the call light chimes, during the eleven seconds the elevator takes. Her thumbnail has worn a soft crease into the top square. Twenty-six years turning strangers over gently in these beds, and the arithmetic that decides whether she can ever stop doing it fits under one thumb.

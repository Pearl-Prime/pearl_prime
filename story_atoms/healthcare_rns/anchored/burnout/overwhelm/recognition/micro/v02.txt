Junior is nine minutes from home when his hands start compressing. The wheel is at ten-and-two and his palms are pushing down into it, wrist over wrist, on the beat of a song that isn't playing — a hundred and ten a minute, the metronome tempo they drill. Room 4 didn't make it. He signed off two hours ago, ate half a granola bar, clocked out. Now the dashboard is a monitor and the red temp light is a rhythm he's chasing, and he does not tell his hands to stop.

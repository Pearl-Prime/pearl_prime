Three nights running, Rafi lies flat and his pulse keeps the pace of a floor that isn't under him. Every time the radiator ticks his eyes snap to the bedroom door, tracking it like a monitor about to alarm. He runs the shift back — the airway he got on the second try, the one he didn't get at all — auditing each move for the version where he was faster. His jaw aches by morning. He has bitten through the night guard twice this month. He laces his shoes for work.

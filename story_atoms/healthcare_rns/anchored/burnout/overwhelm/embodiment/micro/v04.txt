End of shift, Wes takes the crash-cart checklist to the break room and, on the back, draws a second column. He writes one name in it — the grandchild's, blue crayon from the pediatric drawer because it's what's in his pocket. It is not a resurrection. Forty-three is still forty-three. But he folds the paper into his badge holder instead of pressing his wrist, and when the new grad asks if he's okay, he says the count is just heavier on one side, and leaves it there.

# Interviews Are Theater - Learn Your Lines

## For: Everyone who blanks the second they share their screen

You know how to code.  
You've built things.  
You've solved problems.

But the second an interviewer says "share your screen and implement a binary tree," your brain turns to mush.

**Welcome to tech interviews: where being good at your job and being good at interviews are two completely different skills.**

### The Uncomfortable Truth

Tech interviews don't measure how good you are at the job.

They measure how good you are at:
- Performing under pressure
- Talking while coding
- Solving problems you've practiced
- Playing the game

**And yes, it's a game with rules you can learn.**

### Why Interviews Feel Impossible

**The brain science:**
- Coding alone: Calm, focused, problem-solving mode
- Coding while being watched: Fight-or-flight panic mode

**Your brain on interviews:**
- Can't remember basic syntax
- Forgets how arrays work
- Blank on things you use daily
- Overthinks simple problems

**This is normal. This happens to everyone.**

### The Interview Types (And How to Beat Each)

**1. The Screening Call**
- They're checking you're not lying
- Basic tech questions
- "Tell me about yourself"
- Why this company

**How to win:** Have your story ready. Be enthusiastic. Ask good questions.

---

**2. The Coding Challenge (Take-home)**
- Build something in 2-4 hours
- Usually 8+ hours of actual work
- They say "don't spend too long"
- Everyone spends too long

**How to win:** 
- Make it work first, make it pretty second
- Write a good README
- Add a few tests
- Deploy it somewhere
- Don't goldplate

---

**3. The Live Coding**
- Share screen, solve problem, while talking
- Usually LeetCode-style
- Most stressful interview type
- Determines 90% of outcomes

**How to win:** Practice. Seriously. There's no shortcut.

---

**4. The System Design**
- "Design Twitter"
- Whiteboard architecture
- Talk about trade-offs
- For mid/senior roles

**How to win:** Learn the patterns. Ask clarifying questions. Draw diagrams.

---

**5. The Behavioral**
- "Tell me about a time..."
- STAR method
- Testing cultural fit
- Actually pretty important

**How to win:** Have 5-7 stories ready. Practice telling them.

### The Live Coding Strategy

**Before you write any code:**

1. **Repeat the problem back**  
"So I need to [X], and return [Y], handling [Z] edge cases?"

2. **Ask clarifying questions**  
"Can I assume the input is sorted?"  
"What should I return if it's empty?"

3. **Talk through your approach**  
"I'm thinking I could use a hash map here because..."

4. **Check if they agree before coding**  
"Does that sound reasonable?"

**While coding:**

5. **Think out loud**  
"I'm creating a variable here to track..."  
"Now I'll iterate through the array..."

6. **Name things clearly**  
`result` not `r`  
`currentMax` not `cm`

7. **Write pseudocode first if stuck**  
// loop through array  
// check if current > max  
// update max if needed

8. **Test as you go**  
"Let me trace through with the example..."

**After coding:**

9. **Walk through your solution**  
"So this works by first..."

10. **Mention edge cases**  
"This handles empty arrays, negatives, duplicates..."

11. **Discuss time/space complexity**  
"This is O(n) time, O(1) space"

### The Practice Regimen

**If you have 3 months:**
- 2-3 problems a day
- Focus on patterns, not memorization
- Study solutions after trying
- Redo problems you got wrong

**If you have 1 month:**
- 1 problem a day minimum
- Focus on most common patterns
- Practice explaining out loud

**If you have 1 week:**
- Review patterns
- Do easy problems to build confidence
- Practice talking through solutions

**The patterns to know:**
- Two pointers
- Sliding window
- Hash maps
- DFS/BFS
- Binary search
- Dynamic programming (basics)

### The Behavioral Prep

**Have stories for:**
- Conflict with teammate
- Technical challenge you solved
- Failed project and what you learned
- Time you had to learn quickly
- Disagreement with manager
- Helping a struggling teammate

**STAR method for each:**
- **S**ituation: Context
- **T**ask: What you needed to do
- **A**ction: What you actually did
- **R**esult: What happened (with numbers if possible)

### The Questions to Ask Them

**Don't ask:**
- Stuff on their website
- Basic benefits
- Vague "what's the culture like"

**Do ask:**
- "What does success look like in this role in 6 months?"
- "What's the biggest technical challenge the team is facing?"
- "How does the team handle technical debt?"
- "What's your favorite part about working here?"
- "What would make someone unsuccessful in this role?"

**Show you care about the work, not just getting hired.**

### The Mental Game

**Before the interview:**
- Review your projects
- Practice talking through them
- Sleep well
- Eat something
- Test your tech setup

**During the interview:**
- Breathe (seriously, you forget to breathe)
- It's okay to pause and think
- It's okay to say "I don't know, but here's how I'd figure it out"
- They want you to succeed (most of the time)

**After the interview:**
- Send thank you email within 24 hours
- Mention specific things you discussed
- Don't replay every mistake
- It's done, move on to next one

### When You Get Stuck

**Don't:**
- Panic and freeze
- Code in silence
- Give up
- Pretend you know something you don't

**Do:**
- "I'm not sure about the optimal approach, but here's a brute force solution"
- "Can I have a moment to think?"
- "I haven't seen this exact problem, but similar ones use..."
- Ask for hints if truly stuck

### The Rejection Handling

**You will get rejected. A lot.**

Not because you're bad.  
Because:
- Someone else had more experience
- Someone else knew someone
- Timing was wrong
- Budget changed
- They needed different skills
- Your interview style didn't match theirs
- Luck

**After each rejection:**
1. Feel feelings (they're valid)
2. Ask for feedback (rarely useful but try)
3. Identify one thing to improve
4. Apply to 5 more jobs
5. Keep going

### The Success Metrics

**Not this:**
- Getting every interview
- Never getting nervous
- Solving problems perfectly
- Knowing every algorithm

**This:**
- Getting 10-20% interview rate
- Communicating clearly under pressure
- Showing your thought process
- Improving with each interview

### The Real Preparation

**Technical skills:** 40%  
**Communication:** 30%  
**Confidence:** 20%  
**Luck:** 10%

You can control the first three. The last one evens out over multiple interviews.

### Bottom Line

Interviews are a skill separate from coding.  
The good news: Skills can be practiced.  
The bad news: You have to actually practice them.

**Nobody is naturally good at this.**  
Everyone who seems good has just practiced more.

# Parents Are Not Your Partners (Usually)

## For: Everyone who's been yelled at by a parent for doing their job

You're trying to teach their child.

The parent:
- Blames you for their kid's failing grade
- Demands you change the grade
- Calls you at 9pm
- CCs the superintendent on angry emails
- Shows up unannounced
- Undermines your authority
- Takes kid's word over yours
- Threatens to "go higher"

**Welcome to the parent problem.**

### The Partnership Myth

**What they tell you in training:**  
"Parents are your partners in education!"

**Reality:**
- Some parents are allies
- Some are absent
- Some are adversaries
- Most are too busy/overwhelmed to engage much

**True partnerships are rare. Manage accordingly.**

### The Framework: PROTECT

**P - Professional always**  
Even when they're not

**R - Respond, don't react**  
Stay calm

**O - Only during work hours**  
Boundaries on availability

**T - Track everything**  
Document all contact

**E - Email for paper trail**  
Get it in writing

**C - Call backup when needed**  
Loop in admin

**T - Take nothing personally**  
Their anger isn't really about you

### Parent Types

**The Invisible:**
- Never respond
- Don't come to conferences
- Kid has no support at home
- Makes teaching harder but not confrontational
- **Approach:** Document contact attempts, do what you can for kid, move on

**The Helicopter:**
- Emails constantly
- Wants daily updates
- Micromanages everything
- Anxious about every detail
- **Approach:** Set communication boundaries, weekly update instead of daily, train them on your schedule

**The Bulldozer:**
- Demands you fix everything
- "My child would never..."
- Blames you for kid's choices
- Threatens escalation
- **Approach:** Document, stay professional, involve admin early, stick to facts

**The Absent:**
- Checked out completely
- No response ever
- Kid basically raising themselves
- **Approach:** Accept you can't reach them, support student directly, document attempts

**The Underminer:**
- Bad-mouths you to child
- Tells kid they don't have to listen to you
- Actively sabotages your authority
- **Approach:** Document, stay professional with kid, admin involvement, don't engage

**The Reasonable:**
- Trusts you
- Supports at home
- Communicates appropriately
- Partners actually
- **Approach:** Cherish them, communicate regularly, collaborate genuinely

### Communication Boundaries

**Set clear expectations:**

**Email:**
- Response time: 24-48 hours
- Only during work hours
- Not on weekends/breaks
- Auto-response explaining this

**Phone:**
- Scheduled calls only
- During prep period
- School number, not personal
- If they call personal, don't answer

**In-person:**
- By appointment only
- Not before/after class
- With admin if contentious
- During work hours

**Your time off is off. Non-negotiable.**

### The Grade Complaint

**Most common:**  
"My child is failing. What are YOU going to do about it?"

**Their expectation:**
- You fix the grade
- You provide extra credit
- You change requirements
- You're responsible for their success

**Your reality:**
- Kid didn't do work
- Kid failed tests
- Grade reflects performance
- Not your fault

**Your response script:**

"[Child] is failing because [specific reasons: missing X assignments, failing tests with scores of Y]. 

Here's what I've already done: [contact home, offer help, accommodations, etc.].

Here's what [child] needs to do: [specific actions with deadlines].

I'm available [specific times] if [child] wants extra help.

The grade reflects work completed. [Child] can still improve by [specific path]."

**Stay factual. Document. Don't budge.**

### The Angry Email

**When you get the ALL CAPS EMAIL:**

**Don't:**
- Respond immediately
- Match their tone
- Defend yourself emotionally
- Send after-hours
- Get drawn into argument

**Do:**
- Wait 24 hours
- Show to colleague first
- Stay professional
- Stick to facts
- CC admin if threatening
- Keep it brief

**Template response:**

"Thank you for your email. I understand you have concerns about [issue].

[State facts only - no emotion]

I'm happy to discuss this further. Please contact me to schedule a time to talk.

[Professional close]"

**Don't JADE (Justify, Argue, Defend, Explain). State facts. End.**

### When Parents Go Over Your Head

**The move:**
- Doesn't like your answer
- Emails principal
- CCs superintendent
- Complains to board

**Your response:**

**Immediately:**
- Email admin with your version
- Provide documentation
- Professional summary
- Offer to meet

**In meeting:**
- Stay calm
- Facts only
- Documentation ready
- Let admin handle parent
- Don't get defensive

**After:**
- Document outcome
- Continue professional relationship
- Don't retaliate against student
- Learn if needed, but don't internalize

**CYA always.**

### The Entitled Parent

**Their mindset:**
- "I pay taxes, you work for me"
- "My child deserves..."
- "You need to..."
- "I demand..."

**Their tactics:**
- Intimidation
- Threats
- Name-dropping
- Escalation
- Legal threats (rarely followed through)

**Your stance:**

**Stay professional:**
- They want you to get emotional
- Don't give them ammunition
- Facts only
- "I understand you're upset"
- Don't argue

**Know your rights:**
- You don't work for them individually
- You follow school policy
- Admin should back you (hopefully)
- Union protects you

**Set boundaries:**
- "I won't continue this conversation while you're yelling"
- "Please email me your concerns"
- "Let's schedule a meeting with admin present"
- Walk away if necessary

### Conference Survival

**Before:**
- Review student's work
- Have grade book ready
- Specific examples
- Solutions prepared
- Admin on standby if needed

**During:**
- Start positive (find something)
- Present facts/data
- Avoid judgment of parenting
- Collaborative language
- Specific action steps

**If it goes south:**
- Stay calm
- "Let's bring in admin to help"
- End meeting if abusive
- Don't accept abuse

**After:**
- Email summary
- Document conversation
- Follow up on action items
- Cover yourself

### The Lawsuit Threat

**When they say:** "You'll hear from my lawyer!"

**Your response:**
- Stay calm (they almost never follow through)
- "I understand. Please have them contact the district."
- Immediately notify admin
- Document everything
- Don't discuss further with parent
- Continue treating student fairly

**Reality:**
- Very few actually sue
- If they do, district handles it
- Your documentation protects you
- This is why you document everything

### The Social Media Problem

**Parents who:**
- Post about you on Facebook
- Complain in parent groups
- Screenshot your public social
- Try to find dirt
- Organize against you

**Protection:**

**Lock down social media:**
- Private accounts
- Don't accept parent friend requests
- Google yourself
- Nothing public that's questionable
- Separate professional/personal

**Don't engage:**
- Don't respond to posts
- Don't defend yourself online
- Tell admin
- Document
- Stay offline

**Be smart:**
- Nothing online you wouldn't want admin to see
- No student photos without permission
- Professional presence or no presence

### Building Good Relationships

**With the reasonable parents:**

**Positive contact:**
- Email good news
- Call with praise
- Notes home about success
- Before problems arise

**Regular communication:**
- Weekly updates
- Newsletter
- Website/portal updated
- Proactive not reactive

**Partnership approach:**
- "I need your help with..."
- Ask their insight
- Collaborate on solutions
- Respect their knowledge of their kid

**These parents are gold. Invest in these relationships.**

### What You Can't Control

**You can't make parents:**
- Care about education
- Support at home
- Respond to communication
- Be reasonable
- Trust you
- Do their job as parent

**You can only:**
- Teach their child
- Communicate appropriately
- Document efforts
- Do your job professionally
- Protect yourself

**Accept what's not in your control.**

### Scripts for Common Situations

**Missing work:**  
"[Student] has missing assignments: [list]. They're available to make up during [times]. Please encourage [student] to come in."

**Behavior issues:**  
"[Student] had difficulty following expectations today. Specifically: [facts]. We addressed it by [intervention]. Please discuss [specific expectation] at home."

**Failing grade:**  
"[Student]'s current grade is [X] based on [specific scores/missing work]. They can improve by [specific actions]. I'm available [times] for extra help."

**Unreasonable request:**  
"I understand your concern. According to school policy, [explanation]. I'm not able to [do what they want], but here's what I can do: [alternative]."

**Hostile communication:**  
"I want to work with you to help [student], but I need our communication to remain respectful. Please email me to schedule a time to discuss this productively."

### Red Flags to Document

**Document if parent:**
- Threatens you
- Is verbally abusive
- Shows up unannounced repeatedly
- Contacts you outside work hours repeatedly
- Makes accusations
- Goes over your head
- Brings lawyer/media
- Harasses you online
- Creates hostile environment

**Tell admin immediately. Protect yourself.**

### When Student Gets Caught in Middle

**The problem:**
- Parent undermines you
- Student hears
- Now student won't respect you

**Your approach:**
- Don't bad-mouth parent to student
- Stay professional
- Enforce expectations anyway
- Build relationship with student separately
- Document if parent actively sabotages

**You can't control what happens at home. Control what you can in classroom.**

### Permission Slips

You're allowed to:
- Set communication boundaries
- Enforce school policy
- Not respond immediately
- Involve admin
- Refuse to meet alone with hostile parent
- Document everything
- Not accept abuse
- Keep personal life private
- Not be available 24/7
- Say no to unreasonable requests

### Bottom Line

**Some parents will support you.**  
**Some parents will fight you.**  
**Most will be somewhere in between.**

Your job is to teach their child. Not to make them happy.

**Stay professional.**  
**Set boundaries.**  
**Document everything.**  
**Don't take it personally.**

**Their anger usually isn't really about you anyway.**

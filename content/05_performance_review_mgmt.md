# Performance Review Season: Where Everyone Lies

## For: Everyone pretending this process makes sense

It's that time of year again.  
Performance review season.

Where you:
- Rate yourself (knowing it doesn't matter)
- Write accomplishments (that will be minimized)
- Set goals (that will change in 3 months)
- Wait for your rating (predetermined months ago)
- Get 3% raise (if you're lucky)

**And pretend this is "merit-based."**

### The Charade

**What they say:**  
"Performance reviews are about development and growth"

**What they are:**  
A bureaucratic process to justify compensation decisions already made

**The truth:**
- Your rating was decided in calibration meetings
- Without you there
- Based on factors you don't control
- Months before you submit your self-review

**The self-review is theater.**

### The Forced Distribution

**The system:**
- 10% can be "exceptional"
- 70% will be "meets expectations"  
- 15% will be "needs improvement"
- 5% will be "does not meet"

**The math:**
- Even if everyone is great
- Even if the team exceeded all goals
- Someone has to be in the bottom 15%

**Translation:**
- Your performance relative to objectives doesn't matter
- Your performance relative to your peers does
- It's a zero-sum game
- Someone has to lose

### The Self-Review Hell

**They want:** Specific examples with metrics and impact

**You have:**
- Vague recollection of what you did
- Spreadsheets you can't find
- Accomplishments you forgot to document
- Panic

**So you:**
- Make it up/exaggerate (everyone does)
- Use corporate buzzwords
- Inflate your impact
- Hope they don't fact-check

### The Ratings Translation

**"Exceptional" (10%):**
- You're a political genius
- Or you work for the right person
- Or you got lucky with a visible project
- Congrats on your 5% raise

**"Exceeds expectations" (15%):**
- You're good but not in the inner circle
- 4% raise
- Keep doing what you're doing
- (You won't be promoted)

**"Meets expectations" (70%):**
- You did your job
- Maybe even very well
- Doesn't matter
- 3% raise
- This is "average" (even when it's not)

**"Needs improvement" (15%):**
- Someone has to be in this bucket
- Might be you
- Might be performance
- Might be politics
- 0-2% raise
- Start looking for new job

**"Does not meet" (5%):**
- You're being managed out
- Update resume
- They're building a case
- Start job searching immediately

### The Calibration Meeting You're Not In

**What happens:**
- All managers in a room
- Stack ranking employees
- Fighting for their people
- Horse trading ratings

**The conversation:**
"I'll give your person a 3 if you give mine a 3"  
"Sarah deserves exceptional but we're out of budget"  
"Put him in needs improvement, we want him to leave"  
"She's great but not visible enough"

**Your fate decided by:**
- How much your manager fights for you
- What deals they make
- Who has more power
- Budget politics

**You're not there. You have no voice.**

### The Feedback That Doesn't Help

**Generic feedback you'll get:**
- "Work on executive presence"
- "Be more strategic"
- "Communicate more effectively"
- "Show more leadership"
- "Be more visible"

**What this means:**
- They can't articulate real issues
- Or the real issue is political
- Or they don't actually know your work
- Or this is boilerplate copy-paste

**Useful feedback is rare.**

### The Raise Insult

**The math:**
- Inflation: 3-5%
- Your raise: 3%
- Net effect: Pay cut

**But they frame it as:**
- "Competitive compensation"
- "Merit-based increase"
- "Rewarding performance"

**Meanwhile:**
- New hires get 20% more than you
- External market moved 10%
- You're now underpaid
- They wonder why you're looking

### The Goal-Setting Farce

**You have to set goals for next year.**

**The problem:**
- Strategy will change in 3 months
- Your priorities will shift
- Your manager might leave
- Company might reorganize
- Goals will be meaningless

**But you write them anyway:**
- Vague enough to claim success
- Specific enough to look legit
- Measurable but movable
- Basically fiction

**They'll be forgotten by February.**

### The Peer Feedback Politics

**They ask your peers to rate you.**

**What they're actually asking:**
"How much do you like this person?"

**Because:**
- Peers don't see your full work
- They rate based on interactions
- Political relationships matter
- Favors get returned

**The person who buys donuts might rate higher than the person who does better work.**

### The Manager-Employee Tension

**You're now on both sides:**

**Reviewing your team:**
- You know they deserve more
- You fight in calibration
- You lose or compromise
- You have to deliver bad news
- They lose trust in you

**Being reviewed:**
- You know the process is rigged
- You have to play along
- You hope your manager fights for you
- You might be disappointed

**No one wins.**

### The Timing Problem

**Performance period:** January-December  
**Review conversation:** February-March  
**Raise effective:** April  

**Meanwhile:**
- December work doesn't count
- January-March work is for next year
- You forget what you did in Q1
- The whole thing is delayed

**By the time you get feedback, it's 6 months old.**

### The Documentation You Should've Done

**Throughout the year you should've:**
- Kept a wins document
- Tracked metrics
- Saved praise emails
- Documented major projects
- Updated weekly

**What you actually did:**
- Nothing
- Relied on memory
- Scrambled in December
- Regretted it

**Start now for next year.**

### The Political Realities

**Getting "exceptional" requires:**
- High-visibility project
- Executive sponsorship
- Political capital
- Luck
- Or all of the above

**Not:**
- Just doing excellent work
- Quietly excelling
- Being dependable
- Helping others

**Visibility > Performance**

### The Bias Problem

**Studies show:**
- Women rated lower for same performance
- Minorities face harsher standards
- Older workers seen as "stagnant"
- Younger workers seen as "inexperienced"
- Introverts rated lower than extroverts

**"Objective" ratings aren't objective.**

**Bias is baked in.**

### The Pay Transparency Movement

**What's changing:**
- Some states require salary ranges
- More discussion about pay
- People comparing notes

**What this reveals:**
- Pay inequities
- External hires paid more
- Raises don't keep up with market
- The game was rigged all along

**Knowledge is power. Compare notes.**

### The Exit Strategy

**If you're consistently rated lower than you deserve:**

**Ask:**
- "What specifically would get me to the next level?"
- "What does exceptional look like in measurable terms?"
- "Who in my peer group exemplifies that?"

**If you get vague answers:**
- The issue is political
- Or they want you to leave
- Or they don't know

**Start looking.**

### The Stay-or-Go Calculation

**Stay if:**
- Compensation is competitive
- You're learning
- Promotion path is clear
- Manager advocates for you
- You believe in the mission

**Go if:**
- Compensation is below market
- Reviews are consistently unfair
- No path forward
- Manager doesn't fight for you
- Better options exist

**Do the math every year.**

### What Actually Helps

**During the year:**
- Document wins weekly
- Make work visible
- Build relationships with leadership
- Ask for feedback quarterly (not just annually)
- Understand the political landscape

**During review season:**
- Write detailed self-review
- Quantify everything
- Align accomplishments with company goals
- Prepare for the conversation
- Have market comp data ready

**After:**
- Process the feedback
- Decide if it's worth acting on
- Update your resume
- Reassess your situation

### The Perspective Shift

**From:** "This should be fair"  
**To:** "This is a negotiation"

**From:** "My work should speak for itself"  
**To:** "I need to make my work visible"

**From:** "I'm being judged on merit"  
**To:** "I'm being judged on perception and politics"

**This isn't surrender. It's strategy.**

### Bottom Line

Performance reviews are:
- Partially about performance
- Mostly about politics and budget
- Never as objective as claimed
- Useful mainly for documentation

**Your choices:**
- Play the game (document, make work visible, navigate politics)
- Accept the system (stay, know it's flawed, focus on the work)
- Leave the system (find somewhere better, if it exists)

**What you can't do:**
- Change the system
- Make it fair
- Opt out (if you want to advance)

**Know the game. Decide if you want to play.**

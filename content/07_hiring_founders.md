# Your First Hire Will Probably Be Wrong (And That's Okay)

## For: Everyone about to hire someone and terrified of screwing it up

You've been doing everything yourself.  
You and your co-founder can't keep up anymore.  
You need help.

**So you're about to make your first hire.**

And you have absolutely no idea what you're doing.

**Welcome to the club. Nobody does.**

### The Fear

**What if:**
- You hire the wrong person?
- You can't afford to pay them?
- They're better than you and take over?
- They're worse than you and slow you down?
- You have to fire them?
- You make a huge mistake?

**All of these will probably happen. Not might. Probably.**

### The First Hire Paradox

**You need:**
- Someone who can do everything
- Someone who's okay with chaos
- Someone who doesn't need management
- Someone who believes in your vision
- Someone you can afford

**This person doesn't exist.**

**You'll hire someone who has 3 of these 5.**  
**Then figure it out from there.**

### When to Actually Hire

**Too early:**
- You have 3 months runway
- You haven't found product-market fit
- You're not sure what you need
- You're hiring because other startups are

**Too late:**
- You and your co-founder are burning out
- You're missing opportunities
- Quality is suffering
- Customers are complaining

**Just right:**
- You have 12+ months runway for their salary
- There's more work than you can handle
- You know exactly what role you need
- You've been doing this work yourself and know what it takes

### What to Hire For First

**NOT:**
- Your weakness (you need to understand it first)
- A generalist who can do everything
- Someone just like you

**BUT:**
- Something you're spending too much time on
- Something critical that you're bad at
- Something that directly impacts revenue/growth

**Common first hires:**
- Engineer (if you're non-technical founder)
- Sales/BizDev (if you're technical founder)
- Designer (if product is visual and neither of you can design)

### The Founder vs. Employee Mindset

**You need someone who:**
- Takes ownership (acts like a founder)
- Executes without hand-holding
- Figures things out
- Cares about the outcome

**You'll probably get someone who:**
- Needs direction
- Asks what to do next
- Waits for permission
- Cares about doing their job

**This gap is the hardest part of early hiring.**

### The Equity Question

**Employee #1 expectations:** 5-10%  
**What you should actually give:** 0.5-2%

**Why the gap:**
- They want founder-level equity
- They have employee-level risk
- You need to save equity for future hires
- Dilution matters

**The deal:**
- Fair equity (0.5-2% with 4-year vest)
- Decent salary (as much as you can afford)
- Clear role and ownership
- Honest about risks

### The Salary Reality

**You can't:**
- Pay market rate
- Compete with big tech
- Offer amazing benefits
- Give job security

**You can offer:**
- Equity (that might be worth something)
- Learning and growth
- Ownership and impact
- Ground floor opportunity

**The people who work at startups value the second list more than the first.**

### The Interview Process

**Don't:**
- Wing it
- Only talk to one person
- Hire because you're desperate
- Skip reference checks
- Ignore red flags

**Do:**
- 3-4 interviews minimum
- Include your co-founder
- Give them a project/case study
- Check references (actually call them)
- Trust your gut

**Take your time. A bad hire costs 6+ months.**

### The Red Flags

**Run away if:**
- They badmouth previous employers aggressively
- They can't explain gaps in resume
- References are lukewarm
- They want to change everything immediately
- They don't ask questions about the company
- They need to know all the answers
- Your gut says no

**Ignore these at your own risk.**

### The Green Flags

**Great signs:**
- They ask about the vision and mission
- They did research on your company
- They have side projects/initiative
- They've worked at early-stage before
- References rave about them
- They ask thoughtful questions
- They're excited but realistic

### The Offer

**In writing, include:**
- Salary
- Equity (% and vesting schedule)
- Role and responsibilities
- Start date
- At-will employment (you can let them go)

**Don't:**
- Make verbal promises you can't keep
- Oversell the company
- Guarantee an outcome
- Hide the hard truths

**Be honest about what they're getting into.**

### The First Week

**What you expect:** They hit the ground running

**What actually happens:**
- They need onboarding
- They don't know your systems
- They ask 1000 questions
- They can't be productive yet

**This is normal. Budget for 2-4 weeks of ramp time.**

### The Management You've Never Done

**You now have to:**
- Give direction
- Provide feedback
- Have 1-on-1s
- Set expectations
- Give praise
- Give criticism

**And you've never managed anyone before.**

**Get a management book. Take a course. Ask for help.**

**Bad management kills good employees.**

### The 90-Day Window

**First 30 days:** Learning mode, lots of questions  
**Days 30-60:** Starting to contribute  
**Days 60-90:** Should be independently productive

**If by day 90 they're not pulling their weight, you have a problem.**

**Don't wait to address it.**

### When It's Not Working

**Signs:**
- Not meeting expectations after 90 days
- Constant hand-holding needed
- Quality issues
- Team friction
- Your gut says it's not right

**What to do:**
- Give clear feedback
- Set specific improvement goals
- Give them 30 days
- If no improvement, let them go

**Keeping a bad hire is worse than the awkwardness of firing.**

### How to Fire Someone

**The conversation:**
1. Be direct and quick
2. "This isn't working out"
3. Explain final pay/equity
4. Answer questions
5. End cleanly

**Don't:**
- Drag it out
- Give false hope
- Make it personal
- Badmouth them after

**It will suck. Do it anyway.**

### The Culture You're Building

**Your first hire sets the tone:**
- How you work
- What you value
- What's acceptable
- What's not

**Choose carefully.**

**Everyone you hire after will be compared to them.**

### The Growing Team

**After hire #1:**
- Hire #2 is easier (you've done it before)
- Hire #3-5 should fill different gaps
- By hire #5 you need some structure
- By hire #10 you need actual processes

**Don't wait until it's chaos to add structure.**

### The Remote vs. In-Person Question

**Remote:**
- Larger talent pool
- Lower costs
- Harder to build culture
- Requires discipline

**In-person:**
- Faster communication
- Easier culture building
- Higher costs
- Limited talent pool

**There's no right answer. Pick what works for your situation.**

### The Contractor vs. Employee Decision

**Contractor first if:**
- You're testing the role
- It's project-based
- You're not sure about budget
- You want flexibility

**Full-time if:**
- It's critical to the business
- You need commitment
- You want culture building
- You can afford it

**You can always convert contractor → employee.**

### What to Delegate

**Delegate first:**
- Tasks you do most frequently
- Things that don't require founder-level judgment
- Areas where someone else could be better
- Work that's taking too much of your time

**Keep for yourself:**
- Vision and strategy
- Key customer relationships
- Fundraising
- Major product decisions

**For now. This changes as you grow.**

### The Learning Curve

**Your first hire will teach you:**
- What questions to ask next time
- What to look for
- What to avoid
- How to onboard
- How to manage
- When to fire

**Every mistake is a lesson for hire #2, #3, #4...**

### Bottom Line

Your first hire will probably be imperfect.  
You'll probably make mistakes.  
You might even have to let them go.

**That's all normal.**

**The only way to learn to hire is to hire.**  
**The only way to learn to manage is to manage.**

**Start now. Iterate as you go.**

---

**If you need someone, start the process today. Write the job description. Post it. Don't wait for the perfect time. It doesn't exist.**

# Nothing Is Original (And That's Okay)

## For: Everyone paralyzed by the fear of being derivative

"Is this original enough?"  
"Someone's definitely done this before."  
"I'm just copying what already exists."

**Stop. This thinking is killing your creativity.**

### The Originality Myth

**The lie:** Real artists create something completely new  
**The truth:** Everything builds on what came before

**Even the most "original" artists:**
- Studied others obsessively
- Borrowed techniques
- Remixed influences
- Built on existing ideas

Picasso studied classical painting for years before breaking rules.  
Steve Jobs copied Xerox's interface ideas.  
Shakespeare borrowed plots from everyone.

**Originality isn't creating from nothing. It's combining influences in your unique way.**

### What Actually Happens

**You think:** "I need a completely original idea"  
**What actually works:** "I'll combine A + B in a way that feels like me"

**You think:** "Looking at others' work is cheating"  
**What actually works:** "Studying others teaches me what's possible"

**You think:** "If it's been done, I shouldn't do it"  
**What actually works:** "Everything's been done. My version is still valuable."

### The Framework: REMIX

**R - Reference widely**  
Study work you admire. Lots of it.

**E - Extract elements**  
What specifically do you like? Color? Composition? Concept?

**M - Mix influences**  
Combine 3+ different inspirations

**I - Inject your perspective**  
What's unique about YOUR take on this?

**X - eXperiment freely**  
Try combinations. Most will suck. Some won't.

### The Austin Kleon Truth

**"Steal Like an Artist" isn't about copying.**  
**It's about:**

1. Study work you love
2. Understand WHY it works
3. Apply those principles in your own way
4. Add your unique perspective
5. Create something new from old parts

**Everyone does this. Successful creatives just admit it.**

### What's Actually Original About You

Not your techniques (everyone learns those).  
Not your tools (everyone has access).  
Not your subject matter (everything's been photographed/designed/written).

**What's original:**
- Your combination of influences
- Your lived experiences
- Your perspective
- Your taste
- Your execution
- Your voice

**You don't need to invent a new color. You need to use colors in your way.**

### The Comparison Trap

**What you see:** Their polished, finished work  
**What you don't see:** The 50 artists they studied, the iterations they trashed, the years they practiced

**What you see:** Your rough ideas  
**What you forget:** Everyone's rough ideas look rough

**Stop comparing your process to their product.**

### Inspiration vs. Copying

**Inspiration:**
- "I love how they use light, I'll explore that"
- "Their composition is interesting, let me try that technique"
- "This feeling they created, I want to evoke something similar"

**Copying:**
- Recreating their exact work
- Using their signature style/technique
- Claiming their idea as yours

**The line:** Are you learning a principle or stealing a product?

### Dealing With "Someone Did This Already"

**So what?**

Someone wrote a love song before. We still write love songs.  
Someone designed a chair before. We still design chairs.  
Someone painted a portrait before. We still paint portraits.

**Your version is still valuable because:**
- It's yours
- For your audience
- In your context
- With your spin
- Right now

### Permission Slips

You're allowed to:
- Be influenced by others
- Study work you admire
- Use similar techniques
- Work in established styles
- Make "derivative" work while learning
- Find your voice by borrowing others' first
- Create something "unoriginal"
- Not reinvent the wheel

**You're not allowed to:**
- Copy someone's exact work and claim it
- Use their signature style commercially
- Plagiarize

**There's a line. Don't cross it. Everything else is fair game.**

### Finding Your Voice

**Your voice isn't something you create.**  
**It's something you discover by making a lot of work.**

**Process:**
1. Try everything
2. Notice what feels natural
3. Notice what people respond to
4. Double down on that
5. Repeat

**Your voice = the overlap between what you love making and what works for you**

### The Originality Checklist

**Before you panic about being unoriginal, ask:**

- Am I directly copying someone's exact work? (No = you're fine)
- Am I bringing my own perspective? (Yes = you're fine)
- Would 10 people make 10 different versions of this? (Yes = you're fine)
- Am I learning or stealing? (Learning = you're fine)

**If you're not plagiarizing, you're probably okay.**

### What Great Creatives Say

**"Good artists copy, great artists steal"** - Picasso  
(Meaning: make it so yours that the influence is invisible)

**"Nothing is original"** - Jim Jarmusch  
(Meaning: stop worrying and make things)

**"If you steal from one person, it's plagiarism. If you steal from many, it's research"** - Wilson Mizner  
(Meaning: wide influences = originality)

### The Real Problem

The real problem isn't lack of originality.

**It's:**
- Not making anything because you're afraid it's been done
- Paralysis from perfectionism
- Comparison killing your creativity
- Waiting for a "truly original" idea that'll never come

**Make the "unoriginal" thing. Make 100 of them. Your voice will emerge.**

### Bottom Line

**Originality is overrated.**  
**Execution matters more.**

Make the thing you want to make, even if it's been done.

**Your version, made by you, for your reasons, is original enough.**

Stop waiting for permission to create.

**Make it. Ship it. Repeat.**

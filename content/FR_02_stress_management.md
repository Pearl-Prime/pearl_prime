# First Responder Self-Help Master Script
## File 2: Stress Management - Tactical Decompression

### Operational Stress Is Not Weakness

You responded to a pediatric code. A fatal MVA. An officer-involved shooting. A structure fire with civilian casualties. A domestic that went sideways. A suicide completion.

Your body responded exactly as it was designed to. Your stress response kept you alive and functional. Now you need to process it before it processes you.

### The Physiology of It

**What Happens During a Critical Incident:**
- Adrenaline/cortisol flood your system
- Heart rate spikes (140+ BPM)
- Fine motor skills degrade
- Tunnel vision kicks in
- Time distortion occurs
- Memory encoding changes

**What Happens After:**
Your body wants to discharge that energy. If you don't, it stores as tension, hypervigilance, irritability, insomnia.

### Tactical Breathing (Use This On Scene)

**Box Breathing (4-4-4-4):**
- Inhale through nose: 4 seconds
- Hold: 4 seconds
- Exhale through mouth: 4 seconds
- Hold: 4 seconds
- Repeat until heart rate drops

**Works in:**
- The rig between calls
- Patrol car at a safe location
- Station bathroom
- Apparatus seat

### Post-Call Decompression Protocol

**Immediately After (0-30 minutes):**
1. **Physical discharge** - If safe, walk it off, do pushups, shake out your limbs
2. **Tactical breathing** - 5 rounds minimum
3. **Hydrate** - Your body is depleted
4. **Brief check-in** - "That was heavy" (acknowledge it)

**Within 24 Hours:**
1. **Move your body** - Gym, run, hit the bag, anything intense
2. **Talk to someone who gets it** - Partner, crew member, peer support
3. **Write it down** - Just facts, no judgment (helps memory processing)
4. **Normal routine** - Eat, shower, basic self-care

**Within 48-72 Hours:**
1. **Check in with yourself** - Sleep okay? Intrusive thoughts? Irritability?
2. **Debrief if available** - Formal or informal
3. **Consider peer support** - If it's still heavy

### The After-Action Internal Review

**Ask yourself (honestly):**
- Did I do what I could with what I had?
- Would I do anything differently with the same information?
- Am I carrying guilt for things outside my control?
- Is this normal acute stress or something more?

**If you're stuck on "I should have..."**
You made the best call you could in that moment with that information under those conditions. Monday morning quarterbacking yourself is unfair. You're human, not superhuman.

### Chronic vs. Acute Stress

**Acute Stress (Normal):**
- Tied to specific incident
- Symptoms improve with time/processing
- You can still function
- Sleep disruption is temporary

**Chronic Stress (Needs Attention):**
- Background hum of tension always present
- Multiple incidents compounding
- Sleep problems ongoing
- Relationships suffering
- Substance use increasing
- Can't shake it

### Shift Work Sleep Strategy

**The Reality:**
Rotating shifts destroy your circadian rhythm. You're fighting your biology. Perfect sleep is impossible.

**Damage Control:**
- **Night shift prep:** Dark room, blackout curtains, white noise, cool temp (65-68°F)
- **Post-night shift:** Sunglasses on drive home, melatonin (3-5mg), sleep immediately
- **Day sleep:** Treat it like night sleep (8 hours if possible)
- **Stay consistent:** Same sleep schedule on days off if possible

**Sleep Hygiene (The Basics):**
- No caffeine 6 hours before sleep
- No screens 1 hour before bed
- Magnesium supplement (helps sleep quality)
- Sleep app if needed (Calm, Headspace)

### The Station/Shift Decompression

**Between calls:**
- 5-minute resets (tactical breathing, walk, hydrate)
- Don't immediately jump to your phone
- Check in with crew: "Everyone good?"

**End of shift:**
- 10-minute debrief (formal or informal)
- "Anything from today we need to talk about?"
- Leave gear, not the weight (intentional transition)

### Physical Stress Management

**What Works:**
- High-intensity training (controlled aggression outlet)
- Martial arts/boxing (stress discharge + skill)
- Team sports (camaraderie + movement)
- Yoga (surprised? It works for tactical breathing)

**What Doesn't:**
- Ignoring it (compounds)
- Only using alcohol (becomes a problem)
- Staying isolated (you need your people)

### Substance Use Real Talk

**The Pattern:**
Couple beers after shift → Few more to sleep → Need it to decompress → Drinking alone → Problem

**The Line:**
- Using to enhance (beer with the crew) = Normal
- Using to cope (need it to feel okay) = Problem

**If you're questioning it:**
- Track it for 2 weeks honestly
- Notice if you're using to numb
- Ask someone you trust if they're concerned
- Consider talking to peer support or EAP

You're not weak if you're struggling with substances. This job creates the perfect storm for it. Get ahead of it.

### Crisis Stress Management (Mass Casualty, LODD, etc.)

**First 24 Hours:**
- Survival mode is okay
- Focus on immediate needs only
- Accept support from crew
- Don't isolate

**First Week:**
- Formal debriefing (CISM if available)
- Talk to peer support
- Maintain basic routines
- Don't make big decisions

**First Month:**
- Consider professional help if symptoms persist
- Lean on department resources
- Be patient with yourself
- Know this is a marathon, not a sprint

### When to Get Professional Help

**Red Flags:**
- Thoughts of self-harm
- Can't sleep more than 2-3 hours for weeks
- Substance use escalating
- Pushing everyone away
- Can't function at work
- Rage you can't control

**Resources:**
- IAFF Center of Excellence (firefighters)
- Copline (law enforcement)
- Code Green Campaign (EMS)
- Safe Call Now: 206-459-3020
- 988 (Suicide & Crisis Lifeline)

### The Daily Reset Ritual

**End of every shift (5 minutes):**
1. Physical: Shake it out, stretch, breathe
2. Mental: "I did what I could today"
3. Transition: "I'm leaving work behind"
4. Future: "Tomorrow is a new shift"

This job will test you. Managing stress isn't optional—it's tactical necessity for survival.

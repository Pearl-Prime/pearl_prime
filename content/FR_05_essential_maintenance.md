# First Responder Self-Help Master Script
## File 5: Essential Maintenance - Physical & Mental Upkeep

### Your Body Is Your Equipment
You wouldn't skip apparatus checks. Don't skip body maintenance.

The job demands:
- Physical strength
- Mental clarity
- Emotional stability
- Quick reactions
- Endurance

You can't deliver any of that running on empty.

### The Physical Toll Inventory

**What this job does to your body:**
- Back/knee/shoulder injuries (repetitive strain)
- Cardiovascular stress (adrenaline spikes)
- Sleep deprivation (shift work)
- Cancer risk (exposure to toxins)
- Hearing damage (sirens, equipment)
- Respiratory issues (smoke, fumes)
- Weight gain (stress eating, shift schedules)

You can't prevent all of it. You can mitigate some of it.

### Sleep: The Foundation of Everything

**The Problem:**
- 24-hour shifts
- Rotating schedules
- Interrupted sleep (calls)
- Hypervigilance preventing deep sleep
- Trading sleep for overtime

**The Reality:**
Less than 7 hours = impaired decision-making equivalent to 0.05% BAC
Chronic sleep debt = increased injury risk, health problems, mistakes

**Sleep Optimization for Shift Workers:**

**On 24s:**
- Nap when you can (even 20 minutes helps)
- Dark, cool sleeping quarters
- Earplugs, eye mask
- Don't drink caffeine after 1800

**After Night Shifts:**
- Blackout curtains at home
- White noise machine
- Tell family you're sleeping (do not disturb)
- Melatonin (3mg, 30 min before sleep)
- No alcohol (disrupts sleep architecture)

**Rotating Shifts:**
- Stick to sleep schedule on off days (yes, even weekends)
- Light exposure in morning (helps reset circadian rhythm)
- Avoid bright light before bed
- Consider light therapy box

**Can't Fall Asleep:**
- Don't lie there fighting it
- Get up, boring activity for 15 min
- Progressive muscle relaxation
- No screens

**Wake Up Exhausted:**
- Get checked for sleep apnea (common in first responders)
- May need sleep study
- CPAP can be career-saving

### Nutrition in a Job That Doesn't Allow It

**The Challenge:**
- Eating on the go
- Station food culture (pizza, wings, donuts)
- No time to prep meals
- Stress eating
- Irregular meal times

**Realistic Approaches:**

**Meal Prep (1-2 hours on off day):**
- Cook 3-4 meals at once
- Pack for shifts
- High protein, vegetables, complex carbs
- Portable (can eat cold if needed)

**Station Strategies:**
- Protein shakes (easy, quick)
- Pre-cut vegetables, hummus
- Nuts, jerky, hard-boiled eggs
- Keep your own food (don't rely on group orders)

**During Shift:**
- Eat before calls when possible
- Quick carbs before exertion (banana, crackers)
- Protein after (recovery)
- Hydrate constantly (water, not energy drinks)

**What to Minimize:**
- Excess caffeine (makes hypervigilance worse)
- Alcohol (sleep disruptor, depressant)
- Processed sugar (energy crashes)
- Heavy meals before sleep

**Supplement Basics:**
- Multivitamin
- Omega-3 (brain health, inflammation)
- Vitamin D (most first responders deficient)
- Magnesium (sleep, muscle recovery)
- Probiotics (gut health affects mental health)

*Always clear with doctor first*

### Physical Fitness (Not Optional)

**The Truth:**
You can't save others if you're out of shape.
The job requires physical capability.
Being fit reduces injury risk.

**Minimum Effective Dose:**
- 3x week strength training (30-45 min)
- 2x week cardio (20-30 min)
- Daily movement (walking, stretching)

**Functional Fitness for First Responders:**
Focus on job-specific movements:
- Deadlifts (lifting patients)
- Farmer carries (carrying equipment)
- Sled drags (dragging victims)
- Stairs/step-ups (climbing)
- Grip strength (holding equipment)
- Core work (injury prevention)

**On-Shift Workouts:**
- 15-minute circuits between calls
- Bodyweight: pushups, squats, planks
- TRX or resistance bands
- Never too exhausted to respond to call

**Injury Prevention:**
- Warm up (5 minutes)
- Proper lifting technique (always)
- Stretch after shift
- Address small pains before they become big ones
- Physical therapy when needed, not when broken

**Recovery:**
- Rest days matter
- Sleep (see above)
- Foam rolling, stretching
- Ice/heat for sore areas
- Massage or PT as needed

### Mental Fitness

**Your brain needs maintenance too:**

**Cognitive Training:**
- Read (fiction activates different brain areas)
- Learn new skills (language, instrument, hobby)
- Puzzles, strategy games
- Avoid autopilot (take different routes, try new things)

**Stress Management:**
- Daily breathing practice
- Weekly longer decompression (hike, hobby time)
- Monthly check-in with yourself
- Yearly mental health screen

**Mindfulness/Meditation:**
"I'm not a meditation person."
Neither are most first responders. Try anyway.

**Start simple:**
- 5 minutes morning
- Sit, breathe, notice thoughts
- Not about stopping thoughts, about not attaching to them
- Apps: Headspace, Calm, Insight Timer

**Benefits:**
- Reduces reactivity
- Improves focus
- Decreases anxiety
- Better sleep

### The Substance Use Talk

**The Honest Reality:**
First responders have higher rates of alcohol/substance use.

**Why:**
- Self-medication for trauma
- Cultural acceptance ("beer after shift")
- Easy access
- Stress relief
- Sleep aid (doesn't work, makes it worse)

**The Self-Assessment:**
- Do you drink every day?
- Do you need it to sleep/relax?
- Do you drink alone?
- Do you hide how much you drink?
- Has anyone expressed concern?
- Do you drink more than you used to?

**3+ yes answers:** You're self-medicating, not relaxing.

**The Standard:**
- 14 drinks/week max (men)
- 7 drinks/week max (women)
- No more than 3-4 in one sitting

If you're over this consistently, you're at risk.

**Getting Help:**
- AA (first responder meetings exist)
- SMART Recovery (alternative to AA)
- Therapy specializing in substance use
- Department EAP (confidential)
- Safe Call Now: 206-459-3020 (first responder specific)

**The Culture Change:**
You don't have to drink to decompress.
You don't have to participate in the station drinking culture.
Choosing not to drink isn't weakness.

### Annual Physicals (Do Them)

**Get checked:**
- Blood work (cholesterol, blood sugar, hormones)
- Blood pressure
- Heart health (EKG if indicated)
- Cancer screenings (age-appropriate)
- Hearing test
- Vision check
- Mental health screening

**Why it matters:**
Early detection saves lives. Including yours.

**Occupational Health:**
- Exposure tracking
- Lung function tests
- Toxicology monitoring
- Immunizations current

### Injury Response Protocol

**When hurt:**
1. Report it immediately (don't tough it out)
2. Get evaluated (even if minor)
3. Follow treatment plan (actually do PT)
4. Modified duty if needed (better than permanent damage)
5. Full recovery before returning to full duty

**The Culture Problem:**
"Real firefighters/cops/medics work through pain."

**The Truth:**
Working injured turns acute injuries into chronic disabilities.
Then you're done. Protect your career.

### The Longevity Plan

**You want 20-30 years in this job:**
- Maintain fitness throughout career
- Address injuries early
- Manage stress continuously
- Sleep as much as possible
- Eat reasonably well
- Don't abuse substances
- Process trauma as it comes
- Build life outside the job

**The Alternative:**
Burn out in 10 years, broken body, broken mind, broken marriage, wondering where it all went wrong.

### Off-Duty Life Quality

**You need:**
- Hobbies unrelated to the job
- Physical activities (hiking, sports, gym)
- Social connections outside first responders
- Mental challenges (learning, creating)
- Relaxation practices
- Time with family/friends
- Something that brings joy

**Why:**
Your identity can't be 100% the job.
When you retire (or when the job ends you), you need to still be someone.

### The Maintenance Schedule

**Daily:**
- 7+ hours sleep attempt
- Move your body
- Eat real food
- Hydrate
- 5-minute breathing

**Weekly:**
- 3x strength, 2x cardio
- One meaningful social connection
- Meal prep
- Recovery activity

**Monthly:**
- Mental health check-in
- Review substance use
- Assess pain/injuries
- Social activity outside work

**Yearly:**
- Full physical
- Mental health screening
- Reassess fitness baseline
- Life audit (am I maintaining or declining?)

### The Hard Truth

This job will take from you.
That's non-negotiable.

The only question is: How much are you going to let it take?

Maintenance isn't optional if you want to make it to retirement with your body, mind, and relationships intact.

Take care of your equipment.
You're the most important tool on the rig.

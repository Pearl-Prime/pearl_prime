# You're Playing the Long Game (Whether You Know It or Not)

## For: Everyone trying to figure out if tech is actually for them

You've been in tech for a while now.  
Maybe 6 months. Maybe 2 years. Maybe you're still trying to break in.

And you're starting to wonder: "Can I actually do this for the next 30 years?"

**Short answer: Not if you keep approaching it the way you are now.**

### The Harsh Truth

Most people don't leave tech because they're bad at it.

They leave because:
- They burned out trying to prove themselves
- They optimized for money over everything else
- They chased trends instead of fundamentals
- They never built a sustainable pace
- They forgot why they started

**Tech is a marathon. Stop running it like a sprint.**

### The Career Stages (Roughly)

**Years 0-2: Survival**
- Focus: Learn enough to be useful
- Goal: Get and keep a job
- Don't worry about: Being an expert
- Do worry about: Building solid fundamentals

**Years 3-5: Competence**
- Focus: Get really good at something
- Goal: Become the person others ask for help
- Don't worry about: Knowing everything
- Do worry about: Depth over breadth

**Years 6-10: Mastery**
- Focus: Understand systems, not just code
- Goal: Solve problems before they happen
- Don't worry about: Being the smartest
- Do worry about: Making others better

**Years 10+: Whatever You Want**
- Focus: Your choice (IC, management, teaching, startup)
- Goal: Impact that matches your values
- Don't worry about: What others think you should do
- Do worry about: What actually fulfills you

### What Sustainable Looks Like

**Not this:**
- Grinding 60-hour weeks
- Learning new frameworks every month
- Constantly job hopping for 10% raises
- Sacrificing health for career
- Working weekends to "stay relevant"

**This:**
- Consistent 40-hour weeks
- Learning what interests you
- Moving when it makes strategic sense
- Investing in your health
- Actually having a life outside code

### The Skill Investment Strategy

**Timeless skills (always valuable):**
- Problem solving
- System design
- Communication
- Debugging
- Reading other people's code
- Writing clear documentation

**Temporary skills (helpful but expire):**
- Specific frameworks
- Particular tools
- Current best practices
- Trendy architectures

**Invest 80% in timeless, 20% in temporary.**

### The Money Trap

**The chase:**
- Constantly moving for higher TC (total compensation)
- Optimizing every decision for max salary
- Sacrificing everything for stock options
- Comparing yourself to comp threads

**The reality:**
- Money matters. But it's not everything.
- The highest paying job isn't always the best job
- Quality of life > 20% more money
- You can't spend stock options on happiness

**Find your "enough" number. Then optimize for other things.**

### The Learning Paradox

**Early career:** Learn everything you can  
**Mid career:** Specialize in what you're good at  
**Late career:** Teach what you know

**The mistake:** Trying to do all three at once

### The Technology Treadmill

**New framework drops every month:**
- "Should I learn this?"
- "Will I fall behind if I don't?"
- "Everyone's using it..."

**The truth:**
- Most new tech won't matter in 5 years
- The fundamentals haven't changed in decades
- Learn new things when you need them, not because they exist

**React/Vue/Svelte will all be replaced. HTTP will still be HTTP.**

### The Career Paths (You Have Options)

**Individual Contributor:**
- Keep coding
- Get really good technically
- Solve complex problems
- Mentor others
- Can reach $300k+ as IC

**Management:**
- Less coding, more people
- Different problems
- Impact through others
- Not "better" than IC, just different
- Can reach similar comp

**Entrepreneur:**
- Build your own thing
- Higher risk, higher potential reward
- Freedom and stress in equal measure
- Not for everyone (and that's okay)

**Teaching/DevRel:**
- Help others learn
- Create content
- Build in public
- Usually lower comp but higher satisfaction

**All of these are valid. Pick based on what energizes you, not what impresses others.**

### The Burnout Prevention

**Warning signs:**
- Can't focus anymore
- Dreading opening your laptop
- Everything feels pointless
- Physical symptoms (headaches, stomach issues)
- Snapping at people
- Stopped caring about code quality

**The fix:**
- Take actual time off (not "work from vacation")
- Set real boundaries
- Find hobbies that aren't code-adjacent
- Talk to someone
- Maybe change jobs
- Maybe change careers (it's okay)

### The Compound Interest of Consistency

**Big gestures (don't compound):**
- All-nighters to ship features
- 80-hour weeks
- Sacrificing health for deadlines

**Small consistent actions (do compound):**
- Learning 30 minutes a day
- Writing clean code every time
- Asking good questions
- Building relationships
- Taking care of yourself

**Over 10 years? The consistent person wins every time.**

### The Network Reality

**Your network isn't:**
- How many LinkedIn connections you have
- How many Twitter followers you have
- How many conferences you speak at

**Your network is:**
- People who'll take your call
- People who'll refer you
- People you've helped
- People who've helped you
- People you actually know

**Build real relationships, not a contact list.**

### The Imposter Syndrome Evolution

**Year 1:** "I don't know anything"  
**Year 3:** "I know some things but not enough"  
**Year 5:** "I know a lot about this specific area"  
**Year 10:** "I know a lot about a little, and a little about a lot"  
**Year 15:** "I know enough to know how much I don't know"

**Imposter syndrome doesn't go away. You just get better at managing it.**

### The Real Metrics

**Don't measure success by:**
- Salary (beyond your "enough")
- Job title
- Company prestige
- Lines of code
- Hours worked

**Measure success by:**
- Do you still enjoy it?
- Are you learning?
- Are you helping others?
- Can you sustain this pace?
- Does it align with your values?

### The Exit Strategy

**You're allowed to:**
- Leave tech entirely
- Take a break
- Switch to a related field
- Go part-time
- Do contract work
- Retire early
- Try something completely different

**Tech isn't a life sentence. It's a tool to build the life you want.**

### The Permission You Need

**You don't have to:**
- Work at FAANG to be successful
- Code outside work hours
- Have a side hustle
- Be passionate about technology
- Constantly level up
- Sacrifice everything for your career

**You can:**
- Be a 9-5 developer and that's enough
- Have hobbies that aren't coding
- Care about your job without loving it
- Coast sometimes
- Prioritize family/health/life
- Be good enough

### What Makes Someone Last

**Not:**
- Raw talent
- Best education
- Fastest learner
- Hardest worker
- Most ambitious

**But:**
- Sustainable pace
- Continuous learning
- Adapting to change
- Building relationships
- Taking care of themselves
- Finding meaning in the work

### The 10-Year Test

**Ask yourself:**
- Can I see myself doing this in 10 years?
- At this pace?
- With these priorities?
- For these reasons?

**If the answer is no, what needs to change?**

### Bottom Line

Tech is a long game.

**Sprint:** Burn out in 2 years  
**Marathon:** Still going strong at 30 years

The people who last aren't the ones who go hardest.  
They're the ones who find a pace they can maintain.

**Choose sustainability over intensity.**  
**Every. Single. Time.**

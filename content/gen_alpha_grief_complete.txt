# GEN ALPHA STUDENTS - GRIEF SCENES
## Ages 12-16 | Middle School Context | 2026

---

## SCENE G-01 | The Empty Dog Bed
**Setting:** Living room, 7:42 AM, Saturday
**Engine:** Grief

Riley walks downstairs. The dog bed is still there.

Empty. Max died Tuesday. They buried him in the backyard. Her dad already took the food bowls away but couldn't move the bed yet.

Riley sits on the couch. Stares at the bed. The indent where Max used to sleep. The chewed corner. The blanket that still has his fur on it.

Her mom comes in. Sees her looking. "We can put it away if you want."

"Not yet."

Riley's cried three times this week. Once at the vet. Once at the burial. Once at 2 AM when she realized she'd never hear his tags jingling again.

Now she's just… hollow. The house is too quiet. No paws on hardwood. No snoring from the corner.

She goes to school Monday. Someone asks why she seems sad.

"My dog died."

"Oh that sucks. I'm sorry."

Conversation moves on. Everyone else's day keeps going.

Riley sits there, carrying a grief that feels too big for middle school, too small for anyone else to care about.

## SCENE G-02 | The Grandma Voicemail
**Setting:** Bedroom, 4:17 PM, Wednesday
**Engine:** Overwhelm

Kai's grandma died last week. Heart attack. Fast. No warning.

His mom saved the last voicemail. Played it at the funeral. Grandma calling about Kai's birthday, asking what he wanted for dinner.

Now it's on Kai's phone. Forwarded from his mom.

He's listened to it seven times. Each time thinking this will be the last. Each time pressing play again.

Her voice. Exactly like he remembers. Happy. Asking about school. Saying "love you, bubbeleh" at the end.

He knows he should delete it. Knows keeping it is just making it harder.

But if he deletes it, that voice is gone. Completely. Forever.

He presses play again.

His mom knocks. "Dinner."

He locks his phone. The voicemail still there. Still saved.

He goes downstairs. Sits at the table. His grandma's chair is empty.

Everyone eats in silence.

## SCENE G-03 | The Divorce Announcement
**Setting:** Kitchen table, 6:18 PM, Sunday
**Engine:** False Alarm

"We need to talk."

Zara's parents sit across from her. Both of them. Serious faces.

Her stomach drops. She knows what this is. She's seen this in movies.

"Your dad and I… we've decided to separate."

There it is.

Her mom keeps talking. Explaining. Using words like "amicable" and "still love you" and "nothing changes for you."

Everything changes for Zara.

Which house will she live in? Will she have two rooms? Two Christmases? Will her friends know? Will she have to explain?

Her dad's crying. Her mom's holding his hand.

Zara just… nods. Processes. Doesn't cry yet.

"Do you have questions?"

A thousand. None she can form into words.

"Can I go to my room?"

They let her.

She lies on her bed. Stares at the ceiling. Her phone buzzes. Friend asking about homework.

Her parents are getting divorced and someone wants to know the math assignment.

Life is still happening around her. For her, everything just broke.

## SCENE G-04 | The Friendship Breakup
**Setting:** School bathroom, 11:47 AM, Friday
**Engine:** Shame

Maya and Priya have been best friends since fourth grade. Were best friends.

Past tense now.

It wasn't one fight. It was a hundred small things. Priya making new friends. Maya feeling left out. Priya saying Maya's too sensitive. Maya saying Priya's mean now.

Last week Priya texted: "i think we need space"

Maya didn't respond.

Now they're in the same classes. Same lunch period. Same hallways.

Maya walks past her in the bathroom. They make eye contact. Both look away.

It hurts worse than Maya expected. She lost fights with Priya before. This feels different.

This feels like watching someone walk away on purpose.

Maya washes her hands. Leaves. Doesn't cry until she's in the stairwell where no one can see.

She's grieving someone who's still alive. Still here. Just not hers anymore.

## SCENE G-05 | The Uncle's Illness
**Setting:** Hospital waiting room, 2:34 PM, Saturday
**Engine:** Spiral

Tyler's uncle has cancer. Stage four. The kind where doctors use words like "palliative" and "making him comfortable."

Tyler's family drove three hours to visit. His uncle's sleeping. Hooked to machines. Doesn't look like himself.

Tyler sits in the waiting room. His mom's in the room with her brother. His dad took his little sister to get food.

Tyler scrolls his phone. Group chat making weekend plans. Someone posting memes. Normal life happening while his uncle's dying two rooms away.

He types: "can't hang today. family stuff"

Doesn't explain. Doesn't want to.

His friend responds: "all good bro"

Tyler locks his phone. Stares at the hospital TV. Some cooking show. People laughing.

His uncle's forty-six. Tyler's thirteen. He's learning that people die before they're supposed to. That there's no fair timeline.

That sometimes you sit in a waiting room and scroll your phone because looking at memes is easier than thinking about what's happening.

## SCENE G-06 | The School Shooting Drill
**Setting:** Classroom, 10:47 AM, Tuesday
**Engine:** Watcher

The lockdown alarm goes off. Everyone knows the drill.

Lights off. Door locked. Everyone against the wall. Silent.

Jordan's teacher checks the hallway. Pulls the shade. Sits with them.

They wait. Twenty minutes. Thirty.

This is practice. Just a drill. But Jordan's heart is racing anyway.

Because drills are for things that happen. And school shootings happen.

His school's never had one. But the next town over did. Two years ago. Three kids died.

Jordan knows their names. Saw their photos. They were his age.

The all-clear sounds. Lights come on. Everyone stands, stretches.

Teacher says, "Good job, everyone."

Jordan goes to lunch. Eats his sandwich. Talks to friends.

But something sits in his chest now. The knowledge that the drill exists because the thing it's practicing for is real.

He's twelve. He does active shooter drills like they're fire drills.

He's grieving a childhood that never felt fully safe.

## SCENE G-07 | The Best Friend Who Changed
**Setting:** Bedroom, 9:28 PM, Thursday
**Engine:** Grief

Dev's best friend didn't die. Didn't move away. Didn't do anything dramatic.

He just… changed.

Started hanging with different people. Got into vaping. Stopped texting back.

Dev sees him at school. They're not enemies. They're just… not friends anymore.

Tonight Dev's scrolling through old photos. Sixth grade. Seventh. Birthday parties. Sleepovers. Inside jokes they had for years.

That person's gone now. Replaced by someone who wears different clothes, talks different, laughs at different things.

Dev misses him. Misses who he was.

He texts: "remember when we built that fort in your backyard"

Three hours. No response.

Dev locks his phone. Knows the message won't get answered.

You can grieve someone who's still alive. Still walking the same halls. Still breathing the same air.

You can lose someone without them going anywhere.

Dev learned that this year. Learned that growing up sometimes means watching people grow into strangers.

And there's no funeral for that. No burial. No ritual.

Just a slow, quiet ache that no one teaches you how to name.

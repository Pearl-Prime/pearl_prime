# Nobody Cares About Your Product (Until You Make Them Care)

## For: Everyone who built it but nobody came

You spent 6 months building the perfect product.  
You launched on Product Hunt.  
You posted on Twitter.  
You told your friends.

**You got 12 signups. 3 of them were your co-founders.**

Welcome to the hardest part of startups: Getting anyone to give a damn.

### The Build It and They Will Come Myth

**You thought:**
- Make good product → people find it → instant success

**Reality:**
- Make good product → nobody knows it exists → crickets → grind → maybe eventually traction

**Distribution > Product Quality** (at least at first)

### Why Nobody Cares

**Hard truth:**
- Your product isn't that different from existing solutions
- People are busy solving their problems with current tools
- Switching costs are high
- You haven't given them a reason to try
- They don't trust you yet
- They don't even know you exist

**This isn't personal. This is physics.**

### The First 10 Users

**Won't come from:**
- Product Hunt launch
- Hacker News post
- Twitter thread
- Facebook ads
- SEO

**Will come from:**
- You personally asking people
- Reaching out 1-on-1
- Finding them where they already are
- Solving their problem in their face
- Begging (honestly)

**The first 10 users are always manual. Always.**

### The Do Things That Don't Scale Phase

**What worked for early Airbnb:**
- Founders went door-to-door in NYC
- Took professional photos of listings themselves
- Crashed on hosts' couches

**What worked for early Stripe:**
- Patrick Collison personally onboarded every customer
- Would sit with developers and integrate it for them

**What will work for you:**
- Whatever manual, unscalable thing gets you users
- Yes, it's exhausting
- Yes, you have to do it anyway

### Where Your First Users Actually Are

**Not:**
- Waiting for your launch
- Searching for your solution
- On Product Hunt

**But:**
- In specific communities (Reddit, Discord, Slack groups)
- Complaining about current solutions
- Using bad workarounds
- Answering questions on forums

**Go where they are. Don't wait for them to come to you.**

### The Outreach That Works

**Bad outreach:**
"Hey! Check out my new app [link]"

**Good outreach:**
"Saw your post about [specific problem]. I built something that might help. Would you be willing to try it and give feedback? No pressure if not."

**The difference:**
- Specific
- Acknowledges their problem
- Asks for help (not a sale)
- Low pressure

### The Feedback Loop

**Your first 100 users aren't customers. They're researchers.**

**Ask them:**
- "What problem were you trying to solve?"
- "What almost made you not sign up?"
- "What's confusing?"
- "What's missing?"
- "Would you pay for this?"

**Then actually change things based on what they say.**

### The Rejection Reality

**To get 10 users, you'll:**
- Reach out to 200 people
- Get 50 responses
- Have 20 conversations
- Get 10 to sign up
- Have 3 actually use it

**95% rejection rate is normal.**

**Don't take it personally. It's a numbers game.**

### The Engagement vs. Signup Trap

**Vanity metric:** 500 signups!  
**Real metric:** 12 active users

**What matters:**
- Not how many signed up
- But how many came back
- And how many would pay

**10 engaged users > 1,000 dead signups**

### The Community Approach

**Find where your users congregate:**
- Specific subreddits
- Discord servers
- Slack communities
- LinkedIn groups
- Twitter lists

**Then:**
- Participate genuinely for weeks
- Provide value, don't pitch
- Build relationships
- Eventually mention what you're building
- Share as a community member, not a marketer

**Spam gets banned. Value gets attention.**

### The Content Strategy

**Don't:**
- Write about your product features
- Post "We just launched!" everywhere
- Talk about yourself

**Do:**
- Write about the problem you're solving
- Share insights from customer conversations
- Tell stories about how people use it
- Teach something valuable
- Build in public (progress, struggles, learnings)

**Help people first. Sell second.**

### The Referral Engine

**After someone uses and likes your product:**

"Who else do you know who has this problem?"

**Then ask:**
"Would you be comfortable introducing me?"

**Your best customers are your best sales team.**

**But only if you:**
- Solve their problem
- Make it easy to refer
- Thank them when they do

### The Paid vs. Organic Dilemma

**Early stage:**
- Organic (manual outreach, content, community)
- Learn who your customers actually are
- Figure out messaging that works
- Understand the value prop

**Later stage:**
- Paid (ads, sponsorships, growth marketing)
- Scale what's already working
- Predictable CAC

**Paid channels won't save a bad product.**

### The Influencer Trap

**You think:** "If [big account] tweets about us, we'll blow up"

**Reality:**
- Most influencer mentions don't convert
- Their audience isn't your customer
- It's a vanity spike, not sustainable growth
- You're better off with 10 engaged users than 10,000 tire-kickers

**Unless the influencer is your exact target customer with your exact target audience.**

### The Launch Paradox

**Your launch will:**
- Feel like a huge deal to you
- Be totally invisible to everyone else
- Get some initial spike
- Then drop off immediately

**Launches don't make companies. Consistent execution does.**

Plan for:
- Day 1: Small spike
- Day 7: Back to normal
- Month 1-6: Grind
- Month 6+: Maybe starting to see consistent growth

### The Problem Definition

**If users aren't signing up, ask:**

Is the problem:
- **Discovery:** They can't find you → work on distribution
- **Understanding:** They don't get what you do → work on messaging
- **Value:** They don't see why it's better → work on positioning
- **Trust:** They don't trust you yet → work on credibility
- **Product:** It doesn't actually solve the problem → pivot

**Fix the right problem, not just any problem.**

### The Activation Puzzle

**The journey:**
1. Land on your site (100 people)
2. Understand what you do (60 people)
3. Sign up (20 people)
4. Complete onboarding (10 people)
5. Use the core feature (5 people)
6. Come back next week (2 people)

**Find where people drop off. Fix that first.**

### The First Dollar

**Getting first user ≠ Product-market fit**  
**Getting first paying customer ≠ Product-market fit**

**But:**
- First user = proof someone cares
- First dollar = proof someone values it

**Celebrate these milestones. Then get back to work.**

### The Expectations vs. Reality

**You expected:** Viral growth from day 1  
**You got:** Slow, manual grind

**You expected:** Users begging to pay  
**You got:** Users asking for more free features

**You expected:** Hockey stick growth  
**You got:** Lumpy, inconsistent traction

**This is normal. Adjust expectations, keep grinding.**

### The Daily Grind

**What early-stage distribution looks like:**

**Every day:**
- Reach out to 10 potential users
- Follow up with 5 previous conversations
- Ship one small improvement
- Post one piece of content
- Have 2-3 user conversations

**Do this for 6 months. Then see where you are.**

### The Pivot Signal

**Might need to pivot if:**
- After 100 conversations, no one wants it
- Users sign up but never come back
- Free users won't convert to paid ever
- The problem isn't painful enough
- The market is too small

**But give it time. 3-6 months minimum.**

### What Traction Actually Looks Like

**Not:**
- Viral explosion
- Everyone wants it
- Overnight success

**But:**
- Slow, steady week-over-week growth
- Users telling other users
- Some people willing to pay
- Retention improving
- Churn decreasing

**5-10% WoW growth sustained over 6 months = real traction**

### Bottom Line

Your first 100 users won't find you.  
You have to find them.

**One by one.**  
**Manually.**  
**Exhaustingly.**

There's no shortcut.  
There's no growth hack.  
There's just the grind.

**But:**
If you solve a real problem for those first 100 users,  
They'll tell the next 100.  
And those will tell the next 1,000.

**That's how it works. It just takes longer than you think.**

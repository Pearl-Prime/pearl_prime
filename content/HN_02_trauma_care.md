# Healthcare Nurse Self-Help Master Script
## File 2: Trauma-Informed Self-Care - Process What You Witness

### The Trauma No One Talks About
You see death, suffering, violence, abuse, and medical trauma regularly.
You're expected to just clock out and be fine.

You're not fine. That's not weakness. That's a normal human response to abnormal circumstances.

### What Nurses Are Exposed To

**Death and dying:**
- Expected deaths (still hard)
- Unexpected deaths (traumatic)
- Pediatric deaths (devastating)
- Prolonged suffering you can't relieve
- Coding patients repeatedly
- Pronouncing deaths
- Preparing bodies

**Violence and abuse:**
- Patient aggression (hitting, biting, spitting)
- Sexual harassment/assault from patients
- Verbal abuse (patients and families)
- Threats to your safety
- Witnessing domestic violence
- Child abuse cases
- Elder abuse

**Medical trauma:**
- Horrific injuries
- Gruesome wounds
- Surgical procedures
- Disfigurement
- Suffering you can't prevent
- Pain you can't adequately control

**Moral injury:**
- Providing care you know is inadequate
- Not having time/resources to do it right
- Being forced to choose between patients
- Following orders you disagree with
- Watching preventable suffering

**Cumulative trauma:**
All of the above, repeatedly, for years.

### Understanding Compassion Fatigue

**It's not the same as burnout.**

**Burnout:** Work stress, feeling overwhelmed, exhausted
**Compassion Fatigue:** Vicarious trauma, numbness, inability to feel empathy

**Symptoms:**
- Emotional numbness ("I don't feel anything anymore")
- Avoiding certain patients/situations
- Irritability with patients
- Cynicism
- Detachment
- Difficulty sleeping
- Intrusive thoughts about work
- Hypervigilance
- Reduced ability to feel joy
- Feeling nothing when you should feel something

**The progression:**
1. High empathy, lots of caring
2. Overwhelming exposure to trauma
3. No processing or recovery time
4. Empathy starts to hurt
5. Self-protection through numbness
6. Complete compassion shutdown

**You're not a bad nurse. You're traumatized.**

### Secondary Traumatic Stress (STS)

**What it is:**
PTSD symptoms from exposure to others' trauma.

**Symptoms:**
- Intrusive memories of patient situations
- Nightmares about work
- Avoiding reminders of traumatic cases
- Hyperarousal (startling easily, always on edge)
- Negative changes in thoughts/mood
- Re-experiencing trauma when triggered

**Common triggers:**
- Patients who remind you of traumatic cases
- Certain procedures
- Smells, sounds from previous traumas
- Similar diagnoses/presentations
- Anniversaries of difficult cases

**This is a real diagnosis. You need real treatment.**

### Immediate Trauma Processing (After Hard Shifts)

**Within 1 hour of shift ending:**

**Physical release:**
- 10-minute walk before driving home
- Scream in your car
- Shake your body (literally, shake it out)
- Cold water on face/hands
- Change clothes immediately

**Mental download:**
- Voice memo to yourself about the shift
- Write it out (then destroy if needed)
- Text a nurse friend: "Rough one today"

**Transition ritual:**
- Stop somewhere between work and home
- Physical marker (take off badge, deep breath)
- Say out loud: "I'm leaving work. I'm going home."

**What NOT to do:**
- Go straight home and pretend you're fine
- Immediately tell family every detail
- Drink to numb it
- Doom scroll
- Isolate completely

### Daily Decompression Protocol

**End of every shift (even "normal" ones):**

**15 minutes before you leave:**
- Chart completed
- Brain dump (write down anything lingering)
- Hand off concerns to oncoming nurse
- Mentally close patient chart

**Leaving the building:**
- Physical gesture (remove badge, wash hands)
- One deep breath
- "My shift is over. Their care is now someone else's responsibility."

**Driving home:**
- No work calls
- Music that shifts your state
- Or silence to process

**Entering home:**
- Shower immediately (wash off the shift)
- Change into home clothes
- 10 minutes alone before engaging with family

### Processing Specific Traumatic Events

**After a particularly hard case:**

**Same day:**
- Debrief with coworker who was there
- Don't minimize ("It's just part of the job")
- Name it: "That was traumatic"
- Physical release (cry, rage, shake)

**Within 24 hours:**
- Write the full story
- Include what you saw, felt, thought, did
- Include what you wish had been different
- Optional: destroy the writing after

**Within 72 hours:**
- Talk to someone safe (therapist, peer support, trusted colleague)
- Don't keep it locked in
- Process before it embeds

**Ongoing:**
- Notice if you're avoiding triggers
- Notice if you're replaying it
- Get EMDR or trauma therapy if it persists

### Moral Injury Specific Processing

**When you couldn't provide the care you wanted to:**

**The feeling:**
Guilt, shame, anger, betrayal by the system.

**The process:**

**1. Name it:**
"I was put in a position where I couldn't [what you wanted to do] because [system limitation]."

**2. Separate responsibility:**
"The system failed. I did what I could with what I had."

**3. Grieve it:**
"This wasn't right. I wish it had been different."

**4. Channel it:**
"I can't change what happened. I can advocate for change so it doesn't happen again."

**This is different from clinical trauma. It needs specific processing.**

### Vicarious Trauma Protection

**Before shifts:**
- Remind yourself: "Their pain is not my pain to carry"
- Set intention: "I can care without carrying"
- Visualize boundary: "I hold space. I don't absorb."

**During shifts:**
- After intense cases, reset (bathroom break, deep breath)
- Physical grounding (feel feet on floor)
- Internal mantra: "This is their experience, not mine"

**After shifts:**
- Consciously release (visualize setting down weight)
- Gratitude for what went well
- Acknowledge hard parts without dwelling

### Building Trauma Resilience

**What helps:**
- Regular therapy (especially EMDR or trauma-focused)
- Peer support groups
- Physical exercise (processes cortisol)
- Nature time
- Creative expression
- Meaning-making (why you do this work)
- Connection with non-healthcare people
- Spiritual practice (however you define it)

**What doesn't help:**
- Suppressing it
- "Toughening up"
- Substance use
- Avoiding all triggers
- Never talking about it
- Pretending it doesn't affect you

### When to Get Professional Help

**Immediately if:**
- Suicidal thoughts
- Self-harm urges
- Substance abuse spiraling
- Complete inability to function
- Violent thoughts toward patients

**Soon if:**
- Symptoms of PTSD (intrusion, avoidance, hyperarousal)
- Compassion fatigue interfering with patient care
- Complete emotional shutdown
- Regular nightmares about work
- Avoiding work because of specific trauma

**Eventually if:**
- General burnout
- Mild compassion fatigue
- Wanting to process cumulative experiences
- Needing tools to cope better

**Resources:**
- Find trauma-informed therapist
- EMDR specifically for healthcare workers
- Nurse-specific support groups
- Employee assistance programs (EAP)

### Peer Support That Actually Works

**What you need:**
Other nurses who get it.

**Where to find:**
- Coworkers you trust
- Nursing online communities
- Formal peer support programs
- Nurse support groups (local or online)

**What makes it helpful:**
- No judgment
- Understanding without explanation
- Validation
- Shared dark humor
- Practical advice
- "Me too" moments

**What makes it unhelpful:**
- Toxic positivity
- One-upping trauma
- Gossip instead of support
- No boundaries (trauma dumping without consent)

### The Boundary Between Caring and Carrying

**You can:**
- Care deeply about patients
- Feel empathy
- Be moved by their stories
- Wish you could do more
- Grieve difficult outcomes

**You cannot:**
- Carry their pain home
- Take responsibility for their suffering
- Cure all their problems
- Save everyone
- Prevent all deaths

**The practice:**
"I care about you. I cannot carry your pain. I will do my best within my scope."

### Self-Compassion for Nurses

**You need to hear:**
- You're not weak for struggling
- This job is legitimately hard
- You're human, not a robot
- You're allowed to have limits
- You can't pour from empty
- Caring for yourself isn't selfish
- You deserve the compassion you give others

**The practice:**
When you're hard on yourself, ask:
"Would I say this to another nurse in my situation?"
If not, don't say it to yourself.

### Creating Trauma Processing Rituals

**Daily:**
- End-of-shift decompression (15 minutes)
- Physical transition (shower, change clothes)
- One thing that brought meaning today

**Weekly:**
- One activity that has nothing to do with nursing
- Physical release (exercise, dance, etc.)
- Connection with someone who fills your cup

**Monthly:**
- Therapy session (if you have one)
- Review: What's the cumulative load?
- Assess: Do I need more support?

**Yearly:**
- Bigger decompression (vacation)
- Career check: Is this sustainable?
- Gratitude for what you've witnessed and learned

### The Reality of Trauma Work

**Nursing is trauma work.**

Even if you're not in ICU, ER, or trauma unit.
Med-surg has trauma. Peds has trauma. Psych has trauma. LTC has trauma.

**You witness human suffering as your job.**

This will affect you.
It should affect you (you're human).

**The question isn't IF it affects you.**
**The question is: Are you processing it or suppressing it?**

Suppression leads to compassion fatigue, burnout, PTSD.

Processing leads to sustainable practice.

Choose processing.

Get support.

You can't do this alone.

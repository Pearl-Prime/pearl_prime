# Healthcare Nurse Self-Help Master Script
## File 7: Exit Strategy - Know Your Options (Stay or Leave with Intention)

### The Question You're Afraid to Ask
"Can I leave nursing?"

**Answer: Yes. Absolutely yes.**

Your nursing degree doesn't own you.
Sunk cost fallacy isn't a reason to stay miserable.

### Assessing If It's Time to Leave

**Ask yourself:**

**About your current situation:**
- Do I dread every shift?
- Is my physical/mental health suffering?
- Have I tried different specialties/settings?
- Am I using substances to cope?
- Is my life outside work suffering?
- Do I have recurrent thoughts of quitting?

**About the future:**
- Can I see myself doing this in 5 years?
- Is this sustainable long-term?
- What would happen if I stayed?
- What would I do if I left?

**3+ yes to first section = time to seriously consider leaving**

### The Decision Tree

**Option 1: Stay in Current Role**
- If: It's temporarily hard but generally sustainable
- Action: Implement all strategies from this guide
- Timeline: 3-6 months to see improvement

**Option 2: Change Within Nursing**
- If: It's the specialty/setting, not nursing itself
- Action: Research, shadow, apply to different roles
- Timeline: 6-12 months to transition

**Option 3: Leave Bedside, Stay in Nursing**
- If: It's bedside nursing specifically
- Action: Explore non-bedside nursing roles
- Timeline: 6-12 months, may need additional training

**Option 4: Leave Nursing, Stay in Healthcare**
- If: It's nursing but you like healthcare
- Action: Adjacent careers using nursing background
- Timeline: 1-2 years, may need additional education

**Option 5: Leave Healthcare Entirely**
- If: It's the entire field
- Action: Career change, different industry
- Timeline: 1-3 years, likely need new training

**All options are valid.**

### Non-Bedside Nursing Careers

**Still nursing, different application:**

**Case Management:**
- Coordinate care, authorize services
- Mostly desk work, phone calls
- Regular hours typically
- Less acute stress
- Requires experience (usually 2+ years)

**Nurse Educator:**
- Teach nursing students or staff
- Can be academic or hospital-based
- Often requires Master's degree
- Regular schedule
- Different type of stress

**Nurse Informaticists:**
- Work with electronic health records
- Technology + nursing knowledge
- Mostly desk work
- May need additional certification
- Growing field

**Legal Nurse Consultant:**
- Review medical cases for law firms
- Research, expert testimony
- Use clinical knowledge differently
- May need certification
- Often remote work possible

**Utilization Review:**
- Review care for insurance/hospitals
- Determine medical necessity
- Regular hours
- Less patient contact
- Can be remote

**Infection Control:**
- Prevent/manage infections
- Hospital safety role
- Some patient interaction
- Regular hours
- Certification available

**Quality Improvement:**
- Improve patient outcomes/safety
- Data analysis
- Project management
- Less direct patient care

**Pharmaceutical/Medical Device Sales:**
- Use clinical knowledge
- Sales/relationship building
- Can be lucrative
- Travel often required
- Very different from bedside

**Telehealth:**
- Remote patient care
- Phone/video consultations
- Work from home
- Growing rapidly
- Different skills needed

### The Financial Planning

**Before you quit:**

**Save:**
- 3-6 months expenses minimum
- Ideally 6-12 months
- Consider severance, unemployment
- Health insurance costs

**Calculate:**
- Current income
- Future income (realistic)
- Gap period income needs
- Transition costs (education, etc.)

**Reduce:**
- Unnecessary expenses
- Create lean budget
- Build cushion

**Don't:**
- Quit impulsively without plan
- Assume it'll work out
- Ignore financial reality

### The Transition Plan

**6-12 months before leaving:**
- Research options thoroughly
- Network in new field
- Update skills if needed
- Save aggressively
- Develop exit plan

**3-6 months before:**
- Apply to positions or programs
- Build resume for new career
- Practice interviewing
- Finalize finances
- Give notice (when job secured)

**After leaving:**
- Mourn the loss (yes, even if you hated it)
- Allow transition time
- Be patient with learning curve
- Build new professional identity
- Don't second-guess

### The Emotional Component

**Leaving nursing is grief:**

**You might grieve:**
- Identity as a nurse
- Years of education/training
- Relationships with coworkers
- Sense of purpose
- The idea of who you thought you'd be

**This is normal.**

**Give yourself:**
- Time to process
- Permission to feel loss
- Support through transition
- Patience with yourself

### The "But I'll Waste My Degree" Myth

**No.**

**Your nursing degree gave you:**
- Critical thinking skills
- Medical knowledge
- Professional experience
- Transferable skills
- Personal growth

**None of that is wasted.**

**Education is never wasted.**
**Even if you don't use it the way you planned.**

### Addressing the Judgment

**People will say:**
- "You're quitting?"
- "What a waste"
- "Nursing shortage and you're leaving?"
- "You just need to try harder"
- "What about your patients?"

**Your response:**
"I made the decision that's best for my health and my life."

**Then stop explaining.**

**You don't owe anyone justification.**

### The Keep Your License Option

**Consider:**
Keeping your license active even if not nursing.

**Why:**
- Can return if you want
- Keep options open
- Maintain credential
- Relatively easy (CEUs, renewal fee)

**Or don't:**
If you're done, you're done. That's okay too.

### The Decision Timeline

**Don't decide when:**
- In crisis
- After terrible shift
- When exhausted
- Emotionally volatile

**Do decide when:**
- Calm and rational
- After trying other options
- With financial plan
- With support system
- After serious reflection

**Give yourself time.**
**But not so much time you suffer unnecessarily.**

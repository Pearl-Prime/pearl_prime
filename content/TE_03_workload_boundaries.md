# Your Job Is Impossible - Stop Trying to Do It All

## For: Everyone grading papers at 11pm on Sunday

The job description says:
- Teach 6 classes
- Grade assignments
- Create lesson plans
- Differentiate for all learners
- Attend meetings
- Contact parents
- Professional development
- Committee work
- Supervision duties
- Data entry
- IEP accommodations
- Respond to emails

**That's at least 60 hours of work for 40 hours of pay.**

### The Math Doesn't Math

**Contract hours:** 7:30am - 3:30pm (40 hours/week)

**Actual job:**
- Teaching: 25-30 hours
- Planning: 10-15 hours
- Grading: 5-10 hours
- Meetings: 3-5 hours
- Emails: 2-4 hours
- Parent communication: 2-3 hours
- Admin tasks: 2-4 hours
- Professional development: 2-3 hours

**Total: 51-74 hours/week**

**You're expected to do 60+ hours of work in 40 hours. While also being creative, engaging, and patient.**

**It's structurally impossible.**

### What Actually Has to Get Done

**Tier 1: Must do (legally/contractually required)**
- Show up to teach
- Lesson plans (basic)
- Grade major assignments
- Attend required meetings
- Legal accommodations (IEPs, 504s)
- Mandatory reporting
- Required documentation

**Tier 2: Should do (keeps job functional)**
- Most grading
- Parent communication (major issues)
- Email (somewhat) responsive
- Basic classroom management
- Some differentiation

**Tier 3: Nice to do (if you have energy)**
- Amazing lesson plans
- Detailed feedback
- Extra decorations
- Pinterest-perfect activities
- Going above and beyond
- Clubs/extra duties

**Tier 4: Not your job (seriously)**
- Providing supplies
- Unpaid tutoring
- 24/7 availability
- Solving all student problems
- Making up for system failures

**Do Tier 1. Try for Tier 2. Tier 3 if you have bandwidth. Tier 4? Never.**

### The Grading Problem

**The reality:**  
If you give meaningful feedback on every assignment for 150 students, you'll spend 30+ hours per week grading.

**The math:**
- 150 students
- 2 assignments per week
- 10 minutes feedback each
- = 50 hours of grading

**That's more than a full-time job. Just grading.**

**Sustainable approaches:**

**Option 1: Grade less**
- Not every assignment needs a grade
- Practice work = completion credit
- Save detailed feedback for major assignments

**Option 2: Streamline feedback**
- Rubrics (fast and clear)
- Common comments bank
- Focus on 1-2 areas per paper
- Not every error needs marking

**Option 3: Peer and self-assessment**
- Students evaluate each other
- Reflective self-grading
- You moderate, not grade everything

**Option 4: In-class grading**
- Grade during work time
- Spot-check instead of comprehensive
- Immediate feedback

**Option 5: Accept less feedback**
- Some work gets completion grade
- Choose quality over quantity
- Your sanity matters more

**Perfect grading is unsustainable. Pick what you can maintain.**

### The Planning Trap

**Pinterest-teacher fantasy:**
- Themed decorations
- Elaborate activities
- Differentiated everything
- Original content daily

**Sustainable reality:**
- Reuse lessons from last year
- Modify existing resources
- Simple but effective activities
- Good enough planning

**Your choices:**
- Spend 20 hours planning amazing lessons, burn out by October
- Spend 5 hours planning solid lessons, survive the year

**Students need a functional teacher more than they need perfect lessons.**

### The Email Boundary

**The problem:**  
Parents/admin/students email at all hours. Responding creates expectation of 24/7 availability.

**The solution:**

**Set email hours:**
- Check twice per day max (start of day, end of day)
- Respond during work hours only
- 24-48 hour response time is reasonable

**Auto-response:**
"I check email twice per day during work hours. I'll respond within 24-48 hours. For urgent matters, contact the office."

**Stop apologizing:**
- "Sorry for the delay" enables unrealistic expectations
- You're responding in a reasonable timeframe
- No apology needed

**Filter aggressively:**
- Unsubscribe from non-essential lists
- Folders and rules
- Address patterns, not individual emails

**Your personal time is not email time.**

### The Sunday Scaries

**What happens every Sunday afternoon:**
- Dread sets in
- Anxiety rises
- You start working
- There goes your weekend

**Breaking the cycle:**

**Saturday boundary:**
- No school work Saturday (full day off)
- Recharge completely
- Do non-teacher things

**Sunday cap:**
- Max 2-3 hours of school work
- Set timer
- When timer's done, you're done
- Even if not finished

**Sunday evening ritual:**
- Something that marks transition
- No work after 6pm
- Prep Monday clothes/lunch
- Early bedtime

**Monday will come whether you're ready or not. Rest anyway.**

### Systems That Save Time

**Lesson planning:**
- Templates for unit plans
- Repeat successful lessons
- Colleague collaboration (share the load)
- Borrow and adapt (you're not reinventing the wheel)

**Grading:**
- Rubrics for everything
- Grade in batches
- Timer for each batch
- Stop when time's up

**Classroom management:**
- Clear procedures (less energy spent)
- Student jobs (delegate)
- Routines eliminate decisions

**Communication:**
- Weekly class newsletter (answers common questions)
- Template emails
- Batch parent contact

**Admin tasks:**
- Do minimum required
- Templates for everything
- Designated time block
- Don't let it bleed into teaching

**Set it up once, reap benefits all year.**

### The Summer Myth

**What people think:** "You get summers off!"

**Reality:**
- Mandatory professional development
- Curriculum planning
- Classroom setup
- Second job to make ends meet
- Recovering from burnout
- Maybe 4-6 weeks actual break

**Protecting summer:**
- Don't work first two weeks (minimum)
- Set boundaries on summer PD
- Schedule actual vacation
- Rest without guilt
- Remember you're not paid for summer

**Summer is recovery time, not free labor.**

### When Work Invades Home

**The bleed:**
- Grading at night
- Planning on weekends
- Thinking about students constantly
- Work anxiety during personal time
- Can't separate

**Creating separation:**

**Physical boundary:**
- Work space at home (if needed)
- Put work away when done
- Out of sight helps out of mind

**Time boundary:**
- No work after X time
- No work on Y day
- Phone goes away
- Email apps deleted from phone

**Mental boundary:**
- Transition ritual (drive different route, change clothes, etc.)
- "I'm done for today"
- Let thoughts come and go without engaging
- Therapy if intrusive

**Ritual:**
- Same action every day to mark end of work
- Makes brain recognize transition
- Consistency is key

### Signs You're Doing Too Much

**Check yourself:**
- Working 60+ hours regularly
- No weekend without work
- Grading past 9pm routinely
- Constant stress/anxiety
- Health problems
- Relationship strain
- Dreading work
- Crying about work
- No hobbies anymore
- Resentment building

**Multiple boxes checked = time to cut back dramatically**

### The Bare Minimum Week

**When you're drowning, here's the absolute minimum:**

**Monday-Friday:**
- Show up
- Teach (even if basic)
- Grade only what's urgent
- Respond to crisis emails only
- Leave when contract hours end

**Weekend:**
- Zero work Saturday
- Max 2 hours Sunday
- Rest

**This is enough when you need it to be.**  
**Survival mode is valid.**

### What To Cut First

**When you need to reduce:**

1. Perfect lesson plans → Good enough plans
2. Detailed grading feedback → Rubrics and brief comments
3. All extra duties → Only required
4. Immediate email response → 24-48 hours
5. Weekend work → Minimal or none
6. After-hours availability → Contract hours only
7. Elaborate activities → Simple but effective
8. Original content → Borrowed and adapted

**Cut before you break.**

### Bottom Line

**The job is designed to be impossible.**

You can't do it all.  
No one can.  
Anyone who claims to is lying, burning out, or independently wealthy.

**Your choices:**
- Do everything poorly while destroying yourself
- Do essential things well while maintaining sanity

**Choose option 2.**

**You're not failing. The system is.**

**Set boundaries. Protect your time. Do what's sustainable.**

**A burned-out teacher helps no one.**  
**A boundaried teacher can actually keep teaching.**

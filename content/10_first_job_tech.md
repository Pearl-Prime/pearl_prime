# Your First Dev Job Will Humble You (And That's the Point)

## For: Everyone about to start or just started their first tech role

You got the job.  
You celebrated.  
You updated LinkedIn.  
You told everyone.

Now you're on day 3 and you have no idea what anyone is talking about.

**Welcome to being a junior developer. It's supposed to feel like this.**

### The First Day Reality

**What you imagined:**
- Writing amazing code
- Solving cool problems
- Being productive immediately
- Impressing everyone

**What actually happens:**
- Spend 4 hours setting up your laptop
- Don't understand half the meetings
- Can't figure out how to run the codebase
- Ask where the bathroom is 3 times

**This is normal. Everyone goes through this.**

### Week 1: Total Confusion

**You'll spend it:**
- Reading documentation you don't understand
- Attending meetings using words you don't know
- Setting up tools you've never heard of
- Pretending you understand more than you do

**What you should actually do:**
- Take notes on EVERYTHING
- Ask what acronyms mean
- Screenshot the README
- Write down who does what
- It's okay to be lost

### Week 2-4: Slightly Less Confused

**You'll be:**
- Making your first small changes
- Breaking things (then learning how to fix them)
- Asking "dumb" questions (they're not dumb)
- Still using Google for basic syntax

**The truth:**  
Senior devs are also Googling basic syntax. They just do it faster.

### The Codebase Will Terrify You

**What you see:**
- 500,000 lines of code
- Files you don't recognize
- Patterns you've never seen
- Comments in languages you don't speak
- Legacy code from 2009

**What you need to know:**
- Nobody understands the entire codebase
- Not even the people who wrote it
- You only need to understand the part you're working on
- It gets less scary with time

### Your First Ticket

**The ticket:** "Fix the bug in the user service"

**Your internal monologue:**
- What's the user service?
- Where is the user service?
- What's a service?
- How do I even run this locally?
- Who do I ask?
- Will they think I'm incompetent?

**The process:**
1. Spend 2 hours finding the code
2. Spend 3 hours understanding the code
3. Spend 30 minutes fixing it
4. Spend 1 hour figuring out how to test it
5. Create PR
6. Get feedback
7. Realize you broke something else
8. Fix that
9. Finally merge after 3 days

**This is exactly how it's supposed to go.**

### What Nobody Tells You

**Everyone expects you to be slow.**  
They hired you knowing you'd need training.  
Your job isn't to be fast. It's to learn.

**You're not supposed to know things.**  
That's why you're junior. If you knew everything, they'd pay you senior money.

**Questions don't make you look dumb.**  
Not asking questions and staying stuck makes you look dumb.

### The Imposter Syndrome Amplified

Before: "I'm not good enough"  
After getting hired: "They're going to find out I don't belong"  
After first week: "How did I fool them into hiring me?"  
After first month: "Any day now they'll realize their mistake"

**Reality check:**  
If you're doing the work, learning, and improving, you belong. That's the only qualification.

### What Good Looks Like (First 3 Months)

**Not this:**
- Shipping features solo
- Making architectural decisions
- Knowing the best practices
- Never asking questions

**This:**
- Completing small tickets with guidance
- Learning the team's workflow
- Asking good questions
- Not making the same mistake twice
- Understanding one small area well

### The Feedback You'll Get

**"Take your time"**  
Translation: You're going too fast and making mistakes. Slow down.

**"Ask more questions"**  
Translation: You're stuck and we can tell. Please ask for help.

**"Good start, here are some improvements"**  
Translation: This works but isn't how we do things. Here's our way.

**"Let's pair on this"**  
Translation: This is complex. Let me teach you.

**None of this means you're failing.**

### The Code Review Terror

**Your first PR:**
- 47 comments
- "Let's discuss this approach"
- "Why did you choose this method?"
- "This could be simplified"
- 7 different people weighing in

**How it feels:** Like you failed an exam  
**What it actually is:** Free education from experienced devs

**How to handle it:**
- Read all comments carefully
- Ask questions if you don't understand
- Don't defend your code, learn from feedback
- Make the changes
- Thank the reviewers

**Code review is teaching, not judging.**

### What to Focus On

**First Month:**
- Understanding the dev environment
- Learning the team's workflow
- Reading code more than writing
- Building relationships
- Taking good notes

**Months 2-3:**
- Completing small features independently
- Understanding how pieces connect
- Contributing in code reviews
- Asking fewer "where is this" questions
- Asking more "why do we do this" questions

**Months 4-6:**
- Owning small features end-to-end
- Spotting potential issues
- Proposing solutions (not just reporting problems)
- Helping newer people than you

### The Relationship Building

**Beyond your manager:**
- Your onboarding buddy
- The person who answers your questions
- The person who's friendly in Slack
- The person who invites you to lunch
- The person who explains things well

**These relationships matter as much as your code.**

### What to Ask

**Week 1:**
- "How do I set up my environment?"
- "What's the process for [basic task]?"
- "Where can I find [documentation]?"
- "Who should I talk to about [topic]?"

**Week 2-4:**
- "Why do we do it this way?"
- "What's the context behind this decision?"
- "Can you walk me through this code?"
- "Is this the right approach?"

**Month 2+:**
- "What would happen if we [alternative approach]?"
- "How would you handle [scenario]?"
- "Should I consider [edge case]?"
- "What am I missing?"

### The Mistakes You'll Make

**Technical:**
- Break the build
- Delete something important
- Commit to wrong branch
- Push sensitive data (briefly panic)
- Take down staging (learn from it)

**Social:**
- Talk over people in meetings
- Miss a meeting
- Forget to update your ticket
- Ask something already explained
- Not read the docs first

**All of these are fixable and everyone has done them.**

### When You Feel Overwhelmed

**Remember:**
- They don't expect you to know everything
- Learning is literally your job right now
- Everyone who works there was once where you are
- It takes 6-12 months to really feel comfortable
- Struggling means you're learning

### The 1-on-1s

**Use them for:**
- "Am I meeting expectations?"
- "What should I focus on learning?"
- "How can I be more effective?"
- "Is there anything I should do differently?"

**Don't use them for:**
- Only talking about tasks
- Complaining without solutions
- Avoiding feedback

### Signs You're Actually Doing Well

- Your PRs are getting fewer comments
- People ask for your opinion in meetings
- You complete tickets without constant help
- You understand more standup jokes
- New hires ask you questions
- You catch your own mistakes before review

### Signs You Need to Adjust

- Same feedback repeatedly
- Missing deadlines consistently
- Not asking questions when stuck
- Avoiding code reviews
- Not participating in meetings

**If you notice these, talk to your manager.**

### The 6-Month Mark

By month 6, you should:
- Feel somewhat comfortable with the codebase
- Complete small features independently
- Know who to ask for what
- Understand the team's patterns
- Not panic when assigned a ticket

**You still won't know everything. That's fine.**

### Bottom Line

Your first dev job is school with a paycheck.

You're not supposed to be good yet.  
You're supposed to get better.

**The difference between juniors who succeed and juniors who struggle isn't talent.**  
**It's the willingness to be uncomfortable while learning.**

---

**Now show up tomorrow, ask your questions, make your mistakes, and learn from them. That's the job.**

# Parents of Teens Self-Help Master Script
## File 6: Guide - Lead Without Controlling

### The Shift
You used to be able to direct their every move.
Now you can only influence from the sidelines.

This feels like losing control.
It is.

But influence is more powerful than control anyway.

### The Guide vs. Manager Mindset

**Manager (what worked when they were younger):**
- Makes all decisions
- Directs all activities
- Solves all problems
- Controls outcomes
- Micromanages

**Guide (what works now):**
- Helps them make decisions
- Suggests possibilities
- Asks questions
- Accepts outcomes
- Trusts process

**The transition is painful but necessary.**

### The Big Conversations (How to Have Them)

**Topics you need to discuss:**
- Sex and relationships
- Drugs and alcohol
- Mental health
- Social media safety
- Consent and boundaries
- Money and responsibility
- Future planning
- Values and beliefs

**How NOT to do it:**
- One awkward talk and done
- Lecture format
- When they're in trouble
- When you're angry
- With judgment and shame

**How to do it:**
- Multiple small conversations
- Dialogue, not monologue
- Proactively, not reactively
- When everyone's calm
- With curiosity and respect

### The Sex and Relationship Conversation

**They need to hear from you:**

**About sex:**
- Factual information (yes, even if school covers it)
- Your values (without imposing)
- Safety information (protection, consent)
- Emotional component (it's not just physical)
- That they can come to you

**The approach:**
"I want to talk about sex and relationships. This might feel awkward, but it's important."

**Cover:**
- Consent (enthusiastic, ongoing, can be withdrawn)
- Protection (pregnancy and STI prevention)
- Emotional readiness
- Pressure (from partners or peers)
- Healthy vs. unhealthy relationships
- That porn isn't education
- Questions they might have

**Your values:**
You can share your values without requiring they adopt them.

"Our family believes [X]. You'll make your own choices. I want you to be safe and respect yourself and others."

**The ongoing part:**
Not one talk. Ongoing conversations.

### The Substance Use Conversation

**The reality:**
- Many teens will try substances
- Saying "just don't" rarely works
- Harm reduction beats abstinence-only
- Information is protective

**What to say:**

**Your position:**
"I don't want you using drugs or alcohol. Your brain is still developing and substances affect it differently."

**The reality:**
"I know you might make different choices than I want. If you do, here's how to be safer."

**Harm reduction info:**
- Never drive or get in car with impaired driver (call me instead)
- Don't drink from open containers (risk of drugs added)
- Stay with friends, don't separate
- Know what you're taking (fentanyl in everything now)
- Start small, wait to see effects
- Have a sober friend

**The "call me" policy:**
"If you're ever in a situation that feels unsafe, call me. I'll come get you, no questions asked that night. We can talk about it later, but your safety comes first."

**Follow through on this promise.**

### The Mental Health Conversation

**Normalize talking about it:**
"Mental health is just health. Everyone struggles sometimes."

**Share (appropriately):**
"I've dealt with [anxiety/depression/stress]. Here's what helped me."

**Check in regularly:**
"How are you doing, really? Not just 'fine' - actually."

**Warning signs to discuss:**
"If you ever feel [hopeless/suicidal/like hurting yourself], I need you to tell me or another trusted adult."

**Remove stigma:**
"Therapy isn't for 'crazy people.' It's for everyone. I'd support you seeing someone if you wanted."

**Resources:**
Make sure they know:
- Crisis text line (text HOME to 741741)
- 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline
- School counselor
- Trusted adults they can talk to

### The Social Media Safety Conversation

**What they need to know:**

**Privacy:**
"What you post is permanent. College admissions and employers will see it."

**Predators:**
"Not everyone online is who they say they are. Never meet someone from online without telling me."

**Cyberbullying:**
"If someone's harassing you online, tell me. Save the evidence. We'll handle it."

**Mental health:**
"Social media can make you feel bad about yourself. If it does, talk to me or take a break."

**Sexting:**
"Sending nude photos is illegal if you're under 18. Even if you trust the person now, you don't control where they go."

**Your monitoring:**
"I will occasionally check your accounts. Not to spy, but to keep you safe."

### The Future Planning Guide

**College/Career discussions:**

**Don't:**
- Project your dreams onto them
- Push them toward specific career
- Make it all about prestige/money
- Stress them out with constant pressure

**Do:**
- Ask what they're interested in
- Expose them to different options
- Discuss pros/cons of different paths
- Support their exploration
- Help with practical planning

**The questions to ask:**
- What do you enjoy doing?
- What are you good at?
- What kind of lifestyle do you want?
- What matters to you?
- How can I support your goals?

**The money conversation:**
Be honest about what you can/can't pay for.
Early. Not surprise them senior year.

### The Values Transmission

**They're questioning your values. This is normal.**

**Your role:**

**Share yours:**
"Here's what I believe and why."

**Don't impose:**
"You'll form your own beliefs. I hope you keep [core value] but I can't make you."

**Model them:**
Your actions teach more than your words.

**Allow questioning:**
"Why do you believe that?" is a good question, not disrespect.

**Discuss differences:**
"We see this differently. Tell me your perspective."

**The things they usually keep:**
- Values you modeled consistently
- Values you explained (not just declared)
- Values they saw working in your life
- Core values (honesty, kindness, respect)

**The things they often reject (temporarily):**
- Religious beliefs (may return later)
- Political views (may explore opposite)
- Career expectations
- Lifestyle preferences

### The Academic Support Balance

**The problem:**
Too much pressure → anxiety, rebellion
Too little support → underperformance

**The sweet spot:**

**Do:**
- Provide structure (study space, routine)
- Help them learn organization
- Be available for help if asked
- Monitor grades generally
- Intervene if significant drop
- Connect them to resources (tutor, etc.)

**Don't:**
- Do their homework
- Manage every assignment
- Make school your relationship's only topic
- Put all value on grades
- Compare them to siblings/peers

**The message:**
"I care about your education. I'm here to support you. The work is yours to do."

### The Friend Situation Navigation

**When you don't like their friends:**

**Safety issue (drugs, dangerous behavior):**
"I'm concerned about [specific behavior]. Here's the boundary."

**Not safety, just not your preference:**
Let it go. They need to navigate friend dynamics.

**Middle ground (bad influence but not dangerous):**
"I've noticed [behavior]. I'm concerned. Let's talk about it."

**Bullying situation:**
- Take it seriously
- Document everything
- Contact school
- Consider legal action if severe
- Support them through it
- May need therapy

**Toxic friendship:**
- Point out patterns ("I've noticed this person makes you feel bad")
- Ask questions ("How do you feel after hanging out with them?")
- Don't forbid (usually backfires)
- Be there when it implodes

### The Romantic Relationship Guide

**When they start dating:**

**Your role:**
- Set boundaries (curfew, honesty about where they are)
- Meet the person they're dating
- Discuss healthy relationships
- Watch for red flags
- Support them through heartbreak

**Red flags in their relationship:**
- Isolation from friends/family
- Changing drastically to please partner
- Constant drama/crying
- Partner is controlling
- You see abuse signs

**Intervention:**
"I'm concerned about [specific behavior]. Can we talk about what a healthy relationship looks like?"

**If they won't listen:**
Can't forbid (drives them together).
Stay connected. Be safe landing place for when it ends.

**The heartbreak support:**
- Validate their pain
- Don't minimize ("puppy love")
- Be available
- Don't badmouth the ex (tempting but unhelpful)
- Help them learn from it

### The Mistake Response Framework

**When they screw up (and they will):**

**1. Address the immediate situation**
"Okay, this happened. Let's deal with what needs dealing with now."

**2. Cool-down period**
Don't have the big conversation while everyone's emotional.

**3. The debrief (later, when calm)**
"Let's talk about what happened."

Questions to ask:
- What were you thinking? (genuinely curious)
- What happened?
- What would you do differently?
- What did you learn?
- What happens next?

**4. Consequence (if appropriate)**
Related to the behavior.
Reasonable.
Enforced.

**5. Move forward**
"Okay. Let's learn from this and move on."

**Don't:**
- Bring it up repeatedly
- Use it as ammunition later
- Make them feel permanently bad
- Withdraw love

### The Asking Questions Approach

**Instead of telling, ask:**

**When they have a problem:**
"What do you think you should do?"
"What are your options?"
"What happens if you choose X?"

**When they make a decision you disagree with:**
"Help me understand your thinking."
"Have you considered [consequence]?"

**When they're struggling:**
"What would help?"
"What do you need from me?"

**This builds their problem-solving, doesn't create dependence.**

### The Information vs. Experience Balance

**You can share information:**
- "Research shows..."
- "In my experience..."
- "Here's what I've learned..."

**But some things they need to experience:**
- Heartbreak
- Failure
- Consequences
- Success earned on their own
- Problem-solving

**Your job:**
Provide information.
Create safe containers for experience.
Be there for the aftermath.

Don't prevent every bump in the road.

### The Influence You Still Have

**Even when they act like they don't care what you think:**

**They're watching:**
- How you handle stress
- How you treat others
- Whether you practice what you preach
- How you deal with failure
- Your relationships
- Your work ethic
- Your self-care

**Model what you want to see.**

**They're listening:**
Even when they roll their eyes.
Even when they argue.
Even when they seem not to care.

Your words matter. Choose them carefully.

### The Long-Term Vision

**You're preparing them for age 25, not age 18.**

Every guidance decision should ask:
"Will this help them be a functioning adult in 10 years?"

**The test:**
If they're still calling you to make every decision at 25, you guided wrong.
If they never call you at 25, you probably controlled too much and damaged the relationship.

**The goal:**
At 25, they can function independently AND they choose to include you in their life.

That's what good guidance creates.

Guide well.
Hold loosely.
Trust the process.

They're becoming who they're meant to be.
Your job is to help, not control the outcome.

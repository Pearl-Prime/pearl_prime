# First Responder Self-Help Master Script
## File 5: Emotional Processing - Deal With Trauma Before It Deals With You

### The Calls That Stay With You

You've run thousands of calls. Most blur together. But some are branded into your memory:

- The kid who didn't make it
- The domestic where you knew someone was going to die
- The fire you couldn't get them out of
- The shooting where you did everything right and they still died
- The suicide you found
- The one that looked like your own kid/parent/partner

These aren't just memories. They're weights you carry.

### What Trauma Actually Is

**Trauma isn't:**
- Being weak
- Overreacting
- Something only civilians experience
- Something you can just "get over"

**Trauma is:**
- Your brain's response to overwhelming experiences
- Normal reaction to abnormal events
- Physiological, not just psychological
- Something that requires actual processing, not just suppression

### The Cumulative Effect

**One Critical Incident:**
Your brain can usually process this with normal decompression, peer support, time.

**Multiple Critical Incidents Over Years:**
They stack. Each one adds weight. Eventually, what used to bounce off starts sticking. A "routine" call that shouldn't bother you suddenly breaks you.

This isn't weakness. This is cumulative trauma. Your cup is full.

### The Myth of "Leaving It at Work"

**What You Were Told:**
"Don't take it home. Leave it at work. Compartmentalize."

**The Reality:**
Your brain doesn't work that way. You can't unsee what you've seen. You can't unknow what you know. Compartmentalization is short-term survival, not long-term solution.

**What Actually Works:**
Process it. Talk about it. Integrate it. Acknowledge it changed you. THEN you can function with it.

### The Processing Protocol

**Immediate (Within 24 Hours):**

1. **Acknowledge:** "That was traumatic. I'm not fine. That's okay."

2. **Physical:** Move the stress out of your body
   - Intense exercise
   - Literally shake it out
   - Breathe intentionally

3. **Connect:** Talk to someone who gets it
   - Partner on the call
   - Peer support
   - Trusted crew member

4. **Document:** Write it down
   - Just facts, no judgment
   - What you saw, did, thought, felt
   - Helps memory consolidation

**Within 72 Hours:**

1. **Formal Debrief:** If available
   - CISM (Critical Incident Stress Management)
   - Informal crew debrief
   - Chain of command if needed

2. **Check Your Support System:**
   - Told someone at home?
   - Crew checked in on you?
   - Isolated or connected?

3. **Self-Assessment:**
   - Sleeping?
   - Eating?
   - Irritable?
   - Intrusive thoughts?

**Ongoing (Weeks/Months):**

1. **Monitor Symptoms:**
   - Nightmares?
   - Flashbacks?
   - Avoidance?
   - Emotional numbness?

2. **Get Professional Help If:**
   - Symptoms persist past 4 weeks
   - Getting worse, not better
   - Affecting work performance
   - Relationships suffering
   - Using substances to cope

### Moral Injury: The Invisible Wound

**What It Is:**
Not just "I saw something horrible." It's "I had to do something that violated my values" or "I couldn't do what I should have been able to do."

**Examples:**
- Had to pronounce a kid you were still trying to save (protocol)
- Couldn't go in because command said no (following orders vs. saving lives)
- Did CPR when you knew it was futile (family wanted you to try)
- Had to use force and questioned if it was necessary
- Followed policy that resulted in bad outcome

**Why It's Worse Than PTSD:**
PTSD is fear-based. Moral injury is guilt/shame-based. It attacks your sense of who you are as a person.

### Processing Moral Injury

**The Guilt Cycle:**
1. "I should have..."
2. "If only I had..."
3. "It's my fault..."
4. Rumination
5. Shame
6. Isolation

**Breaking the Cycle:**

**Step 1: Reality Check**
"With the information I had, the time I had, the resources I had, and the circumstances I faced, did I do what I reasonably could?"

Usually, the answer is yes.

**Step 2: Context**
"What were the constraints I was operating under?"
- Protocol
- Scene safety
- Resources available
- Information at the time
- Impossible choices

**Step 3: Accountability vs. Blame**
- Accountability: Owning your actions, learning from them
- Blame: Beating yourself up for things outside your control

Take accountability for what you actually control. Release blame for the rest.

**Step 4: Forgiveness**
"I forgive myself for being human in an impossible situation."

This takes time. It's not one conversation. It's ongoing work.

### The Talking Scripts

**When Someone Asks "Are You Okay?"**

**Don't Say:** "I'm fine." (Automatic shutdown)

**Try:** 
- "That was rough, but I'm processing it."
- "Not okay right now, but I will be."
- "Still working through it."

**When You Need to Talk:**

**To Crew:**
"That call is sitting heavy with me. Can we debrief it?"

**To Partner/Family:**
"I had a really hard call. I don't need you to fix it. I just need you to know."

**To Peer Support:**
"I'm struggling with [specific call]. I need to talk it through with someone who gets it."

### The Therapy Question

**Why First Responders Don't Go:**
- "I'm not weak"
- "They won't understand"
- "It goes in my record"
- "I'll lose my job"
- "My crew will think I'm soft"
- "I can handle it myself"

**The Reality:**
- Therapy is tactical processing, not weakness
- First responder-specific therapists exist
- EAP is confidential in most departments
- Untreated trauma WILL end your career eventually
- Your crew wants you healthy

**Types That Work for First Responders:**

**EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing):**
- Evidence-based for PTSD
- Doesn't require talking through whole trauma
- Reprocesses stuck memories
- Usually 8-12 sessions

**CPT (Cognitive Processing Therapy):**
- Specifically for trauma
- Addresses stuck points in thinking
- Good for moral injury
- Structured protocol

**PE (Prolonged Exposure):**
- Gradually confronts avoided memories
- Effective for PTSD
- Requires commitment
- Can be intense

**First Responder-Specific Therapy:**
- West Coast Post-Trauma Retreat
- IAFF Center of Excellence
- Revive Counseling
- Badge of Life

### The Peer Support System

**What It Is:**
Trained first responders who provide emotional support after critical incidents.

**When to Use It:**
- After any call that's sitting wrong
- When you're questioning yourself
- Before it becomes a crisis
- As a bridge to professional help

**It's Not:**
- Therapy (they're not clinicians)
- A replacement for professional help
- Mandatory reporting (in most programs)
- A sign of weakness

### The Warning Signs You're Not Processing

**Behavioral:**
- Increased substance use
- Withdrawing from people
- Aggressive/irritable at home
- Taking unnecessary risks
- Avoiding certain types of calls

**Emotional:**
- Numb/disconnected
- Rage outbursts
- Can't feel positive emotions
- Constant anxiety
- Feeling hopeless

**Physical:**
- Can't sleep
- Nightmares
- Headaches/body pain
- Appetite changes
- Exhaustion that doesn't improve with rest

**Cognitive:**
- Intrusive thoughts
- Can't concentrate
- Memory problems
- Constantly reliving calls
- Suicidal ideation

**If you have more than 3 of these for more than 4 weeks:**
This is PTSD. Get professional help. This isn't something you can will away.

### The Self-Care That Actually Works

**After a Hard Call:**
- Don't go straight home and drink alone
- Do process with crew, then go home
- Don't ignore it and hope it fades
- Do acknowledge it was hard
- Don't expect to be "over it" in a day
- Do give yourself time

**Long-Term:**
- Regular exercise (proven to help PTSD)
- Maintain sleep hygiene
- Stay connected to people
- Hobbies outside work
- Professional help when needed
- Spiritual practice if that's your thing

### The Cultural Barrier

**The Culture Says:**
- Suck it up
- Don't show weakness
- Handle it like your predecessors did
- Asking for help is failure

**The Reality:**
- Your predecessors died younger, divorced more, and suffered in silence
- The culture is changing
- Strength is asking for help before you break
- The strongest thing you can do is take care of yourself

### Processing the Unspeakable

**Some calls are too heavy to describe:**
- Mass casualty
- Kids
- Line of duty death
- Personally triggering

**You don't have to put it into words to process it:**
- Art/music
- Physical activity
- EMDR doesn't require detailed description
- Just being with someone who was there

### The Timeline

**Normal Trauma Processing:**
- Week 1: Intense emotions, disrupted sleep, replaying it
- Week 2-4: Emotions settling, sleep improving, less intrusive thoughts
- Month 2+: Integrated memory, less emotional charge

**If That's Not Happening:**
Get help. The timeline stops mattering. What matters is you're stuck and need professional intervention.

### The Permission Slip

**You have permission to:**
- Be affected by what you see
- Cry about a call
- Feel grief for strangers
- Need help processing
- Not be okay after horror
- Take time to heal

**This doesn't make you:**
- Weak
- Unfit for duty
- Less of a first responder
- Broken

**It makes you:**
- Human

Process it or it will process you. Choose the first option.

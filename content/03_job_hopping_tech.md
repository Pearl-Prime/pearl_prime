# Loyalty is a Two-Way Street (And Companies Forgot Their Side)

## For: Anyone feeling guilty about leaving a job

Let's get one thing straight: Your company isn't loyal to you.

They'll lay you off in a Zoom call with your camera off while posting record profits.

So why are you feeling guilty about leaving for a better opportunity?

### The Old Rules Are Dead

Your parents' generation:
- Worked one job for 30 years
- Got a pension
- Had job security

Your generation:
- Will have 10+ jobs in your career
- Gets a 401k match if you're lucky
- Has "security" until the next round of layoffs

The social contract broke. You don't owe them loyalty they won't return.

### Job Hopping Isn't Disloyal - It's Strategic

**Old thinking:** "I should stay at least 2 years or my resume looks bad"

**New reality:** Staying too long costs you money, growth, and opportunities.

**The math:**
- Internal raise: 3% if you're lucky
- New job: 20-40% increase
- Staying 5 years: You're now 50%+ underpaid

You're not job hopping. You're career managing.

### When It Makes Sense to Leave

**Leave if:**
- You're not learning anymore
- Your compensation is below market
- The culture is toxic
- Your manager sucks (and won't change)
- Better opportunity appears
- You're bored
- The company is struggling
- You just want to

**Don't leave if:**
- You're learning a ton
- Compensation is fair
- People invest in your growth
- You're building something meaningful
- You genuinely enjoy it

Notice what's NOT on the "don't leave" list: Time served.

### The Guilt is Manufactured

"But my team needs me..."  
They'll backfill your role. They always do.

"But I owe them..."  
You gave them work. They gave you money. That's the transaction.

"But it looks bad..."  
To who? HR at companies that don't deserve you?

"But what about loyalty..."  
Where was their loyalty during the last layoff?

### What Good Managers Actually Think

Good managers don't want you to stay out of guilt. They want you to stay because it's your best option.

When you leave for a better opportunity, good managers:
- Congratulate you
- Ask if there's anything they could've done differently
- Offer to be a reference
- Stay in touch

Bad managers guilt-trip you. That's how you know you're making the right choice.

### How to Leave Well

**Give notice:** 2 weeks is standard (but if they're toxic, they don't deserve it)  
**Document everything:** Make transition smooth  
**Don't burn bridges:** Tech is small, be professional  
**Don't overshare:** "I'm pursuing a new opportunity" is enough  
**Don't apologize:** You're making a business decision, not betraying them

### The Real Career Strategy

**Years 0-2:** Learn everything, build skills  
**Years 2-4:** If not growing/paid fairly, move  
**Years 4+:** Only stay if you're building equity (literal or career)

Every 2-3 years, ask: "Is this still my best option?"

If the answer is no, start looking.

### What They Don't Want You to Know

Companies LOVE hiring people who've worked at 5+ companies. It means:
- You've seen different systems
- You bring diverse perspectives
- You've learned to adapt quickly
- You know what good looks like

Your "job hopping" is their "diverse experience."

### Your Only Real Loyalty

To yourself.  
To your growth.  
To your compensation.  
To your future.

Everything else is negotiable.

### Bottom Line

You're not a employee for life. You're a contractor with a longer contract.

When the contract stops serving you, end it.

**No guilt. No apologies. Just growth.**

---

**The best time to start looking? When you're comfortable. Never wait until you're desperate.**

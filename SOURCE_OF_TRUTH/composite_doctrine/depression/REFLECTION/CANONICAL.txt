
## COMPOSITE_DOCTRINE v01
---

---
The heaviness you are carrying is a state your body is in, not the truth of who you are.

Sit for a moment, if you can, with the weight that is here. Notice that there is the heaviness, and then there is something that is aware of the heaviness. They are not the same. The weight is real, and it is sitting on the body right now. But the part of you reading these words, the part that can notice the weight at all, is not itself heavy. It is the place where the heaviness is happening.

This matters because depression speaks in a particular voice, and that voice says: this is who you are now, this is permanent, this is the truth. The voice is convincing. It does not feel like a symptom from the inside. It feels like clarity finally arriving.

But consider what the voice actually is. When the body's energy is low, the mind does not report low energy. It reports a worthless self and an empty world. Those reports are produced by the state, the way a fever produces chills. They are not a clear view of your life. They are what a depleted system says.

You do not have to believe the verdict to feel the weight. The weight asks for care, for rest, for help, for gentleness. It does not ask to be taken as the final word on your worth.

What is the heaviness today? And underneath it, quietly, what is the part of you that is noticing it — the part that was here before this state arrived, and will still be here when it lifts?

## COMPOSITE_DOCTRINE v02
---

---
You do not have to feel like it before you do the small thing.

There is a quiet rule you may have been waiting on without naming it: that you will act once the motivation arrives. Once you feel ready, you will get up. Once you feel like it, you will reach out. And so you wait, and the feeling does not come, and the waiting itself becomes another weight.

Notice the trap in that. In this state, the usual order has reversed. The motivation does not arrive first and then produce the action. It is the other way around. The small action comes first, and the faint motivation, if it comes at all, follows behind it.

So the question is not how to make yourself want to. The question is what is the smallest thing you could do without wanting to. Not the whole list. Not the life rebuilt. One glass of water. One step outside the door. One curtain opened to let the light in.

These are not trivial because they are small. They are how the loop breaks. Each tiny completed act sends a faint signal back to a depleted system: something is still possible here.

You will probably not feel like it. That is expected. Feeling like it is downstream. What is one small thing you could do in the next few minutes, not because you want to, but simply because it is small enough to be done? Do that, gently, and let the feeling catch up in its own time.

## COMPOSITE_DOCTRINE v03
---

---
What you are calling laziness is a body running on almost nothing.

Notice the word you reach for when you look at the undone things. The dishes, the messages, the tasks that used to take no thought at all. The word that arrives is probably lazy. You are being lazy. You should be able to do this. Everyone else manages.

Sit with how that word lands. It implies a choice — that the energy to act is sitting right there, and you are simply refusing to use it. Is that true? Or is it closer to the truth that the energy itself is gone, that the fuel which once powered these ordinary acts is running dangerously low?

A body in this state is not declining to act. It is depleted. The chemistry that makes effort feel worthwhile has gone quiet. The reward that once arrived at the end of a finished task no longer comes. So the system, sensibly, stops spending fuel it does not have on actions that return nothing.

A phone at two percent is not a lazy phone. A car low on fuel is not a lazy car. And you, in this state, are not a lazy person. You are a depleted one.

See what changes when you let the accurate word replace the cruel one. Shame and pressure are what you do to laziness, and they make everything worse. Care and a lighter load are what you offer depletion. Which has the body actually been needing from you? What would it mean to lower the bar today to the size the fuel can actually reach?

## COMPOSITE_DOCTRINE v04
---

---
The pull to disappear is part of the illness, not a reasonable answer to it.

Notice the instinct to withdraw. Cancel the plan. Leave the message unanswered. Stay in, pull the covers up, let the world recede to a safe and silent distance. The instinct feels protective. There is no energy for people, and being seen like this feels like too much.

The instinct is understandable. It is also one of the ways the state deepens itself. Your nervous system was never built to settle on its own. It comes back toward steadiness, in large part, through contact with other steady nervous systems — a calm voice, a shared meal, the ordinary presence of someone who is not in crisis. Withdraw, and you remove one of the few things that actually helps.

And then the isolation argues for itself. The longer it lasts, the more reaching out feels like climbing a wall. The voice insists you would only be a burden, that no one wants to hear from you. That voice is the symptom, not the truth.

This is not a demand to force yourself into a crowded room you have nothing left for. It is an invitation to resist the disappearing in the smallest survivable dose. One message. One short call. Sitting near another person with no obligation to be cheerful. Letting one trusted person know how things actually are.

What is the smallest thread back to another person you could keep from breaking today? Not the whole reconnection. Just one thread, however thin.

## COMPOSITE_DOCTRINE v05
---

---
The thoughts that arrive in the heaviest hour are the least reliable ones you have.

You may have noticed that the heaviness has a rhythm. The morning, often, is the worst — a dread on waking, before the day has offered anything to be heavy about. And then, sometimes, the hours pass and the fog thins, and by evening something is almost bearable, only for the next morning to return you to the bottom.

This pattern points to something physical. The chemistry that regulates mood rises and falls across the day, and in this state its lowest point tends to land in the early hours. The morning dread is not, mostly, a true reading of your life. It is the felt experience of the chemistry sitting at its floor.

This is worth holding onto, because of what the lowest hour does. The thoughts that arrive there are the darkest the cycle produces — that nothing will improve, that there is no point, that you should give up. They feel the most true precisely when they are the least reliable.

So here is a practice worth keeping: do not trust the verdicts of the lowest moments, and do not make any large or irreversible decision inside them. Let those hours pass before you let any conclusion feel final.

When the heaviness is at its worst, can you remember that it is reporting on the chemistry of this particular hour, not delivering a forecast for your life? The afternoon is a different country. You have been there before, and you will reach it again.

## COMPOSITE_DOCTRINE v06
---

---
Your worth did not drop on the days the body could not produce.

Notice the quiet equation you may have been running, probably since long before any of this arrived. It says your value depends on what you produce — that you are worth something when you are useful, achieving, contributing, and worth less when the output stops.

Now notice what depression does to that equation. It stops the output. The work slows. The contributions thin. And so, if you hold the equation, you arrive at a crushing conclusion: that you have become worthless, a drain, a burden taking up space you have not earned.

Sit with whether that conclusion is actually true, or whether it is a false premise colliding with a low state. Is worth really a wage paid for productivity? Awarded for a good week and revoked for a flat one? Or is the value of a life something that does not move when the capacity to act moves?

A person who cannot get out of bed is not worth less than the same person at full strength. The capacity changed. The worth did not. This is not a comforting phrase to recite. It is the ground any recovery has to stand on, because as long as your worth feels like it is on the line, every flat day becomes evidence of failure, and the failure deepens the very state that caused it.

When the voice says you are worthless because you are producing nothing, you do not have to prove you are secretly productive. You can refuse the premise itself. What would today feel like if your worth were simply not up for measurement — if it held steady at full, no matter what the body managed?

## COMPOSITE_DOCTRINE v07
---

---
You are meant to survive and tend this, not win a war against it.

Notice how you have been framing the heaviness. There is a good chance it has become an enemy — something to beat, to conquer, to push through by force of will. The framing borrows the language of strength, and it sounds right. But feel what it actually does to you. Every day the heaviness remains becomes a day you are losing. Anything short of total restoration counts as defeat. And the willpower the fight demands is exactly the fuel this state has drained.

There is a different stance, and it is not giving up. It is closer to tending. A depleted system is not beaten back to health by being attacked. It is carried, gently, back toward function by being cared for.

So the work is not to win a battle. It is to get the body through a low stretch — to lighten its load, to hold a little structure, to keep one thread to another person from breaking, to refuse the verdicts of the worst hours, to move a little, to reach a little, and to wait the state out while tending it.

Notice what this changes about what counts as enough. Under the fight, only total victory counts, and so you fail daily. Under the tending, getting through today is the achievement. Surviving the morning is the achievement. One small thing done, one structure kept, one person reached — each is a real success, not a fraction of a victory still owed.

What would it be like to stop fighting the heaviness today and simply tend the body that is carrying it? The lift comes, in time and unevenly, to the ones who keep the system going rather than exhausting it in a war it was never going to win that way.

Teacher Intake Survey
Purpose: capture how you teach, what you see work, and how transformation actually happens in your experience.
There are no right answers. Short answers are fine. Examples are extremely helpful.
________________


SECTION 1 — HOW SHE SEES THE PROBLEM (Pattern Recognition)
These questions extract problem framing, which is the backbone of your chapter roles.
1. When people come to you, what do they think their problem is — and what is it usually actually about?
(Example: “They say anxiety, but it’s really avoidance of feeling.”)
People often come to me believing their problem is an external circumstance—a relationship, a situation, or a feeling that’s disrupting their happiness or sense of ease. What’s usually happening beneath that is an inner blockage at the emotional, energetic, or soul level. They’re trying to change the outer world to fix an inner experience, when the real shift comes from reconnecting inward and releasing what’s keeping them stuck at a deeper level.
   2. What is the most common mistake people make when trying to fix this problem on their own?
When people live solely in the mind, they often lose touch with what the heart is holding. I help clients reconnect to the heart-mind relationship, uncover what is truly weighing on their soul, and realign with a path that supports clarity, healing, and their highest expression of life.

   3. What do people usually try before they find you? What doesn’t work about that?
Before finding me, people often try traditional therapies, self-help tools, or various forms of distraction to manage their discomfort. While these approaches can offer temporary relief, they tend to stay on the surface—helping people cope rather than truly resolve what’s underneath. What doesn’t work is the avoidance of deeper fears, unresolved hurts, and emotional patterns that continue to operate in the background until they are consciously acknowledged and addressed.
      4. What belief keeps people stuck the longest?
The belief that something outside of them needs to change before they can feel better keeps people stuck the longest. This leads them to focus on fixing circumstances, other people, or symptoms rather than turning inward to address deeper emotional and soul-level patterns. Over time, this can evolve into resignation or apathy—when things have “been this way” for so long that hope for change quietly fades. Many people also believe they’ve already tried everything, and the weight of prolonged frustration creates a fear of going deeper or an unwillingness to try again in a new way—preventing the very shift that’s possible when approached with fresh awareness.

SECTION 2 — HOW CHANGE ACTUALLY HAPPENS (Mechanism)
This is where you extract mechanism_proof material.
         5. In your experience, what actually causes change — insight, practice, timing, safety, repetition, something else?

Lasting change happens when what is truly obstructing the natural flow of awareness and inner light is seen, felt, and released. Insight opens the door, but transformation occurs when there is enough safety, presence, and willingness to meet what’s been hidden or protected for a long time. From there, change unfolds organically—not through force, but through allowing what is already true to come back into alignment.

         6. What usually has to happen before someone is ready to change?

(A moment, realization, failure, exhaustion, etc.)
People are usually ready to let go of what’s blocking them when the ways they’ve been coping no longer work and the cost of staying the same becomes greater than the fear of going inward. 

         7. What small shift makes the biggest difference early on?
The smallest shift with the greatest impact is realizing that we are not our thoughts or emotions—we are the awareness that holds them. When people stop collapsing their identity into the mind and body and reconnect to this deeper awareness, profound change and deep healing become possible. 
         8. What do people often misunderstand about progress or healing?

People often misunderstand thinking that healing is linear and slow, when in reality it can happen far more quickly than expected once the root is seen and met. Healing is also frequently misunderstood as something abstract or “airy,” when it’s actually deeply practical and woven into everyday life. It’s not separate from our purpose, it’s a fundamental part of why we’re here: to grow, expand, and evolve through what we heal.

SECTION 3 — TURNING POINTS (Critical for Your System)
These answers become turning_point stories.
            9. Can you describe a moment when someone suddenly “got it”? What changed right then?

There was a moment when they suddenly “got it” — they went quiet, and it was as if a weight had lifted. In that stillness, they were speaking and seeing from a deeper, more connected part of themselves, and you could feel a shift in how present, grounded, and aligned they had become in that instant.

            10. What’s a common turning point you’ve seen repeatedly across different people?
A common turning point is when people give themselves permission to truly listen to their inner voice, rather than letting the inner critic dominate. In that moment, they begin to act from an integrated, soulful place rather than a purely head-driven space, and their decisions, presence, and energy shift profoundly as a result.
               11. What do people usually do differently after that turning point?
After that turning point, “the doing” comes naturally. What once felt effortful or forced begins to flow with ease and fluidity, as their actions in life start to align with the inner clarity and connection they’ve cultivated. Choices, relationships, and daily habits all begin to be natural and integrated.

SECTION 5 — PRACTICES (Without Asking for “Techniques”)
This avoids the “teach me your method” trap and instead extracts usage rules.
                           16. Do you use practices (breath, awareness, journaling, movement, etc.)? If yes, when do you introduce them?
Yes, I introduce practices—such as breath work, awareness exercises, journaling, and movement—based on each client’s readiness and openness. I tailor the timing and approach to meet them where they are, ensuring the practices support deeper insight, integration, and a sense of safety rather than feeling forced or overwhelming.
                           17. How do you know when a practice is helping vs. when it’s being used to avoid something?

I know a practice is helping when it fosters awareness, clarity, and alignment—it opens space for insight and allows the client to connect more fully with themselves. A practice is being used to avoid something when it becomes a way to numb, distract, or stay in the mind rather than engaging with what’s really arising.

                           18. What’s one practice you use sparingly — and why?

One practice I use sparingly is regression or memory recall work. While it can be profoundly insightful, it can also bring up intense emotions or experiences too quickly if a client isn’t fully ready. 

                           19. What’s something simple that works better than people expect?

Something simple that works better than people expect is listening from the heart. When we truly tune in—beyond the words or surface-level thinking—people feel seen and understood in a deeper way. That simple act creates trust, opens space for insight, and often leads to profound clarity and connection far more quickly than complex techniques.

SECTION 6 — LANGUAGE SHE USES (Critical)
These answers become system phrases, not quotes.
                              20. Is there a sentence you say so often you’re tired of hearing yourself say it?

You are not your thoughts

                              21. Is there a phrase you avoid because it sounds right but doesn’t help?

I avoid phrases like “you shouldn’t feel that way.” They might sound reassuring, but they dismiss the person’s experience and shut down real exploration and healing.

What words feel safest for people when they’re struggling? What words make them shut down?

People feel safe with compassionate, nonjudgmental, and validating words. Language that pushes, criticizes, or tells them what to do too soon usually makes them shut down.

SECTION 7 — BOUNDARIES & ETHICS (Very Important)
This keeps your system aligned with her values.
                                 23. What do you never promise people?

I never promise something I cannot deliver or fully give my word to. 

                                 24. What responsibility do you believe belongs to the teacher — and what belongs to the person?

The teacher’s responsibility is to create a safe, compassionate, and grounded space, to guide awareness, provide tools and practices, and help illuminate patterns or blind spots that may be holding someone back. The person’s responsibility is to engage with that guidance, show up honestly, and take ownership of their own inner work. 

                                 25. What would you not want someone to think after working with you?

People can think whatever they’d like, I’d just hope they share that opening and honestly with me vs hiding something.

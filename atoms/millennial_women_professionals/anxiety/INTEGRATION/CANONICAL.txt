

## INTEGRATION v01
---
integration_mode: BODY-LANDED
reframe_type: BODY-FACT
---
The tightness in my chest isn't a warning system. It's just muscle contracting from old instructions. My body learned to be ready for a threat that never came. But muscles can learn new patterns. I've spent the week noticing where the tightness lives—the base of my throat, the knot between my ribs—without trying to fix it. Just noticing. Yesterday I felt the tightness and instead of bracing, I breathed into it. It didn't get worse. It got quiet. My body is learning that safety is possible. That nothing terrible happens when I'm relaxed. That I can be soft. This is slower work than I wanted, but slower is teaching me something that speed never could.
I'm learning to trust my body.
---

## INTEGRATION v02
---
integration_mode: COST-VISIBLE
reframe_type: MECHANISM
---
The anxiety has been useful. It kept me alert, kept me checking and rechecking, kept me safe from mistakes. But the cost of safety is my sleep. Is my presence with people I love. Is my ability to taste my food. I've been trading presence for protection. That math worked when the threats were real. It doesn't work when the threats are invented. I'm sitting with this choice: keep the exhausting vigilance or let some things be uncertain. Uncertainty isn't the same as danger. I know this in my mind. My body is slower to believe it. So I'm practicing. One hour of not checking. One task I don't redo. One conversation where I don't predict disaster. My body is learning that mistakes aren't fatal.
The cost of constant vigilance is everything.
---

## INTEGRATION v03
---
integration_mode: STILL-HERE
reframe_type: BODY-FACT
---
I am here and my heart is racing. Both are true. The racing doesn't mean I should run. The tightness doesn't mean something is wrong. I can sit in this body, with these sensations, and still be safe. The anxiety lives here, but it doesn't own the space. There are other things here too: the chair holding me, my hands finding my lap, my breath that comes even when I'm scared. I've learned the difference between sensation and danger. My hands shake when I'm afraid and when I'm cold and when I've had too much coffee. Shaking is just shaking. It's not an emergency. I'm learning to be present with my body the way I'd be present with a friend who's frightened—curious, gentle, not trying to fix anything.
My body and I are becoming friends.
---

## INTEGRATION v04
---
integration_mode: QUESTION-OPEN
reframe_type: MECHANISM
---
What if I've been misreading the signals? What if the racing heart isn't predicting disaster but just processing input? What if my anxiety is doing exactly what it's designed to do—making me aware of threat—and the problem is that I've never learned to quiet the signal once the threat is gone? I don't have answers yet. I'm learning that I don't need them before I can rest. The question is enough. Something in my body settles when I ask the question instead of assuming the answer. What if this feeling passes? What if I don't have to fix it? What if I can sit here and wait and it will change on its own?
Curiosity quiets what certainty cannot.
---

## INTEGRATION v05
---
integration_mode: BODY-LANDED
reframe_type: COST-VISIBLE
---
I can feel my feet on the floor. The pressure is real. The cold air on my skin is real. The coffee still warm in my hands is real. These things are real in the way that my imagined disasters are not. I've spent so much time in my head inventing futures that I forgot to notice that right now is solid. Right now my body is safe. Right now I'm breathing. The anxiety will come back, but when it does I'll remember this: that real things—my feet, my breath, this chair—are more real than the stories in my head.
My body knows what my mind forgets.
---

## INTEGRATION v06
---
integration_mode: SOMEONE-ELSE
reframe_type: BODY-FACT
---
I'm learning what it would look like if I treated my anxiety the way I'd treat someone I care about who was scared. Not dismissing. Not forcing them to be brave. Not saying they're overreacting. Just: you're scared. That's real. And you're also safe. Both are true. You can be scared and safe at the same time. We can sit with this together. There's no rush to fix it. When I talk to myself this way, my body softens. There's less bracing. The anxiety is still there, but I'm not fighting it anymore. I'm just keeping it company while it passes.
I'm learning to be my own safe person.
---

## INTEGRATION v07
---
integration_mode: STILL-HERE
reframe_type: MECHANISM
---
The anxiety taught me something: I can survive discomfort. I've been surviving it all along. Every moment I thought I couldn't bear the tightness, the racing, the catastrophic thoughts—I bore it. I'm still here. That's data. The fear was lying about how fragile I am. I'm not fragile. I'm quite sturdy. I can feel my anxiety and still show up to work. I can feel my anxiety and still be kind to people. I can feel my anxiety and still make breakfast. The feeling doesn't determine my capability. That's the discovery.
Fear and function can exist together.



## HOOK v01
---

---
Your body is exhausted. Your mind is running tomorrow like a rehearsal. You know this is counterproductive. You know that being awake at 2am calculating worst-case scenarios is not preparation—it's self-sabotage. You do it anyway because stopping feels like surrender.
---

## HOOK v02
---

---
The dread starts before bedtime. You can feel it arriving as your to-do list comes into focus, as you think about the conversations that might happen, the mistakes you might make, the things you've forgotten to do. Sleep is not an option tonight. Sleep is something that happens to other people.
---

## HOOK v03
---

---
You're lying in the dark doing mental work you couldn't finish during the day. Solving problems, replaying conversations, planning contingencies. Your body is pressed against the mattress. Your mind is somewhere else entirely. You're both exhausted by the displacement.
---

## HOOK v04
---

---
The anxiety doesn't arrive as fear. It arrives as a to-do list. You're not lying awake afraid. You're lying awake organizing. The fact that you're supposed to be sleeping doesn't feel relevant to the work that needs to be done.
---

## HOOK v05
---

---
You got in bed thinking tonight would be different. That today's exhaustion would win and you'd actually sleep. By 11pm you knew it wouldn't. By midnight you stopped believing it. By 2am you're operating under a different set of assumptions about what's possible for your body.
---

## HOOK v06
---

---
The night stretches out. Each hour feels longer than the last. You're checking the time less to see how much you've slept and more to confirm that you haven't slept, that the night is as bad as you feared. The clock becomes evidence.
---

## HOOK v07
---

---
Your mind generates threats that aren't immediate. Things that might happen next week, next month, next year. Your nervous system treats them like they're happening now. Your body mobilizes for something that doesn't exist except as a thought. The thought keeps you awake so you can protect against it.
---

## HOOK v08
---

---
You've tried everything—breathing techniques, meditation apps, warm milk, that thing where you tense and release muscles. Nothing works when the actual problem is that your brain has a job it hasn't finished. Your nervous system won't let you rest until the work is done.
---

## HOOK v09
---

---
You know what would help: letting go, trusting that things will unfold, believing that you can handle tomorrow without having analyzed it in advance. You also know yourself well enough to know you're not capable of that level of surrender. So you stay awake instead.
---

## HOOK v10
---

---
The exhaustion is real. The inability to sleep is also real. Both of these are true simultaneously. You're tired enough that your body hurts. You're anxious enough that your mind won't shut off. The gap between these two is where you spend the night.
---

## HOOK v11
---

---
Other people apparently just go to bed and sleep. They make a decision and move forward. They don't spend the night replaying it, imagining variations of it, preparing for the ways it might not work. You've never understood how they do that.
---

## HOOK v12
---

---
The anxiety is about loss of control, but you tell yourself it's about preparation. If you just think about it enough, you'll be ready enough, and then you'll be safe enough. The only problem is: there's no such thing as enough, and the thinking doesn't make you safer. It just keeps you awake.
---

## HOOK v13
---

---
You went to bed anxious. You're still anxious, except now it's layered with exhaustion and the frustration of not being able to sleep, which is its own source of anxiety. The anxiety about anxiety is sometimes worse than the original anxiety.
---

## HOOK v14
---

---
Your brain is offering you a false choice: stay awake and work on the problems, or sleep and wake up unprepared. You're choosing the lesser of two terrible options. You're not choosing the one thing that would actually help, which is sleep despite the unfinished business.
---

## HOOK v15
---

---
The competence you've built during the day goes dormant at night. At night, you're unsure. At night, everything feels fragile. At night, you're convinced that you're going to fail at things you've already succeeded at. The darkness seems to require rethinking everything.
---

## HOOK v16
---

---
You're not afraid of sleep. You're afraid of the morning after the night where you didn't sleep, where you'll be running on fumes trying to do things that require presence. So you stay awake preparing for a condition you're creating through staying awake. It's circular.
---

## HOOK v17
---

---
The dread before bed is the tell. It's your nervous system saying: "Everything is a threat. We can't rest. We need to stay alert." The dread is the signal that sleep isn't going to come easily. So you lie in bed bracing for the insomnia you predicted.
---

## HOOK v18
---

---
You have a life that feels good during the day. During the night, it feels precarious. During the night, you're convinced it's all balanced on something fragile that could tip. Your brain is trying to shore it up through thinking. It's not working.
---

## HOOK v19
---

---
The body wants to rest. The mind is not ready to stop working. You're trapped in the negotiation between what your body needs and what your anxiety is convinced is more important. The anxiety usually wins. The body pays the price.
---

## HOOK v20
---

---
You're trying to think your way out of a problem that thinking created. Your overactive mind is keeping you awake. Your response is to use your mind to solve it. This is backwards. But you don't know how to operate any other way.
---

## HOOK v21
---

---
The night becomes a metaphor for your life—full of things you can't control, problems you can't solve while lying down, futures you can't predict or prevent. The night is where your normal coping strategies fail, and you have to sit with things as they actually are.
---

## HOOK v22
---

---
Sleep anxiety is not about sleep. It's about the unraveling that happens when you stop performing your competence and actually have to just exist. Night is when the performed version of yourself goes offline, and you meet the anxious version instead.
---

## HOOK v23
---

---
You set a goal to be asleep by 11pm. By 10:45pm you're already anxious about whether you'll make it. The very goal to relax and fall asleep has made it harder to relax and fall asleep. You're working too hard at something that's supposed to be involuntary.
---

## HOOK v24
---

---
The scenarios your brain generates at night are increasingly unlikely, increasingly catastrophic, increasingly beyond your control. But they feel real in the dark. The darkness makes everything feel true.
---

## HOOK v25
---

---
You're lying next to someone sleeping (or alone, if alone). Either way, you're isolated with your thoughts. The isolation of 3am, the time when everything feels true and nothing feels possible, becomes the environment where sleep can't exist.
---

## HOOK v26
---

---
You made a decision during the day that you're now relitigating at night. The night makes it feel wrong. The night makes everything feel wrong. Morning will probably render it fine again, but you have to live through the night first.
---

## HOOK v27
---

---
The anxiety is about certainty. You're lying awake trying to achieve a level of certainty about tomorrow that you can't achieve while lying down. You can't achieve it at all, actually, but your body doesn't know that yet.
---

## HOOK v28
---

---
You've had insomnia before. You know what it feels like. You're afraid of experiencing it tonight. The fear of insomnia makes you tense, which makes sleep harder, which confirms the fear. You're engineering the very thing you're trying to prevent.
---

## HOOK v29
---

---
Other parts of your life have gotten easier with competence. Sleep is the opposite. The more anxious you are about it, the harder it is. The more you try, the worse it gets. This is the domain where effort is counterproductive.
---

## HOOK v30
---

---
The anxiety travels in both directions: worries about the future keep you awake, and being awake makes worries about the future feel more inevitable, more true, more certain to happen. By 4am, you're convinced of things that seemed unlikely at 10pm.
---

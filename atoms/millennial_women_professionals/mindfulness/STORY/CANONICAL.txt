Devon joins the client call four minutes early, the way he always does, and opens Slack "just to check one thing." The one thing becomes six messages. By the time the call starts, his camera is on but he's still typing a reply in his head to a comment from #product-feedback. The client asks a direct question. Devon says "could you repeat that" — the phrase he's used three times this month. He catches himself doing it again, mid-sentence, and instead of apologizing internally he just stops. Puts both hands flat on the desk. Feels the chair under him, the actual chair, not the metaphor of one. Looks at the client's face on the screen instead of his own thumbnail. The question gets repeated. This time he hears the whole thing, first try. He answers it in one clean sentence, no scrambling. After the call he doesn't feel transformed. He feels like someone who was gone and came back, one time, on purpose. He writes himself a note: hands on desk, look at their face, not yours. Small. Repeatable. Not a fix, just a return he now knows how to do again.

## STORY v02
---
---
Mei is six weeks into her first job and has developed a private ritual of nodding during meetings while composing Slack replies in her head. In today's stand-up, her manager asks what's blocking her. Mei realizes, mid-nod, that she doesn't actually know — she was drafting a message to a friend about weekend plans. She says "sorry, can you ask again," out loud, for the first time ever instead of faking a smooth answer. Nobody reacts badly. The meeting continues. Something in her chest unclenches slightly. That afternoon she notices the pattern has a shape: her attention leaves the room the second something gets slightly uncomfortable — a question she doesn't have a great answer to. She doesn't try to stop the wandering. She just starts noticing the exact moment it happens, the little flinch before the drift. By Friday she's caught it four times. Still drifts. But now she knows the doorway it walks through, and knowing the doorway means she can, sometimes, just not walk through it.

## STORY v03
---
---
Tariq is presenting quarterly numbers to a room of people, half in person, half on Zoom tiles. Two minutes in, he notices he's watching himself in the gallery view instead of looking at his slides. He's grading his own facial expressions. He forces his eyes to the shared screen — actual content, not his own face — and something shifts, like a camera refocusing. He finishes the slide clean. Old habit pulls him back to his own tile twice more during the twenty-minute deck. Each time he notices a beat later than the last. After the meeting, his manager pings him: "good energy today." Tariq almost doesn't believe it, because internally it felt scattered the whole time. But the numbers landed and the room tracked with him. He realizes the audience never saw the parts where he left. Only he did. That gap — between how present you feel and how present you actually were — becomes the thing he starts paying attention to, more than the feeling of scatter itself.

## STORY v04
---
---
Aisha WFH's on a couch that used to be just a couch and is now also her office, and during the all-hands she's folding laundry with the video off, telling herself multitasking is fine because no one can see. Her name gets called for a shoutout. She fumbles the "thank you," half a beat too slow, laundry still in her hands. It's fine, nobody notices. But she notices. That night she sets one rule for herself: video meetings, no laundry, no second screen, hands empty. Not because it's virtuous. Because she wants to be findable inside her own meetings, the way you can find a channel by knowing which frequency it's on. The first meeting under the new rule is boring and her hands feel weirdly empty and she almost breaks the rule in the first ten minutes. She doesn't. By the third meeting the itch is smaller. She's not present the whole time. But she's present enough times in a row that the meeting has a shape in her memory afterward, instead of a blur with her name shouted somewhere inside it.

## STORY v05
---
---
Sam has two monitors and, during the sprint planning meeting, opens a second monitor's worth of Twitter "for one second." Twenty minutes pass in the meeting without Sam absorbing a single ticket estimate. When it's time for Sam's team to weigh in, the silence stretches half a second too long. A teammate, Jules, covers for them without being asked. Sam feels the specific shame of being caught not-quite-caught. Afterward Sam doesn't swear off screens forever — that lasted about a day, last time. Instead Sam moves the second monitor to face the wall during meetings. Small, almost silly. But it removes the reflex glance, the drift-by-default. The next planning meeting, Sam's attention still wanders — to a rehearsed joke, to a lunch order — but it wanders in the room, in thought, not into another app entirely. Jules doesn't have to cover for anyone. Sam counts that as the whole win.

## STORY v06
---
---
Priya gets a Slack DM mid-meeting from her manager, just three words: "got a sec?" Her stomach does the thing. For the rest of the call — nine more minutes — she is not in the call. She's rehearsing four different versions of whatever conversation is coming, none of which turn out to be right, because when she finally gets on the call her manager just wants to ask if she can move a deadline. All that rehearsal, wasted on a fake future. Priya starts noticing this pattern has a name in her own head now: "the meeting after the meeting" that hasn't happened yet. She starts testing something small — when the ping lands, she reads it once, decides it can wait until this call ends, and puts the phone face down. Not always successful. But some days it works, and on those days she notices she actually remembers what was said in the meeting she was supposedly attending the whole time.

## STORY v07
---
---
Noah, new to the team, spends his first few 1:1s performing engagement — leaning in, nodding at 1.2x normal speed — while internally drafting the Slack message he'll send afterward defending a decision no one has criticized yet. His manager, mid-sentence, says his name to get his attention back. Noah flushes. Instead of apologizing three times, which is his instinct, he just says "sorry, lost the thread — can you say that last part again." His manager repeats it, unbothered. Noah realizes later that the imagined criticism, the one he was defending against in his head, was never coming. He'd spent the meeting defending himself in a courtroom that existed only between his ears. The real meeting, the one with an actual person asking an actual question, had been waiting the whole time, patient, right there.

## STORY v08
---
---
Layla works from a kitchen table now, and during back-to-back Zooms she's developed a tell: she taps her pen when her mind has left the room. Today, in a client review, she notices the tapping starts up on its own, the way a nervous habit announces itself before the thought does. She sets the pen down, deliberately, like putting down a phone. Feels her feet on the floor under the table. The client is mid-sentence about a deliverable date. She catches the back half of the sentence, not the front, and has to ask a clarifying question that reveals she missed something. It's a little embarrassing. But it's less embarrassing than answering confidently to a sentence she never heard, which is what she used to do. She starts treating the pen-tap as a doorbell — something ringing to tell her she's not home, so she can go let herself back in.

## STORY v09
---
---
Ben's team does "cameras on" Fridays, which he privately resents, because his face has nowhere to hide when his attention leaves for the group chat about weekend plans. Today he decides, as an experiment, to try actually staying — just for this one meeting, just to see. It's uncomfortable almost immediately, like a muscle he hasn't used complaining on the first rep. He drifts twice in the first ten minutes anyway. But each time, he comes back faster than the last, the way a rubber band returns quicker on the second stretch. By the end of the meeting he's said two things out loud he wouldn't have thought to say if he'd been half-gone the way he usually is. Nobody in the meeting knows any of this happened. To them it just looked like Ben, present, contributing. Which, Ben realizes, is maybe all presence ever looks like from the outside — invisible until it's the reason something good gets said.

Grace is in her second job now, past the point where she can blame everything on being new, and she notices in today's leadership sync that she's been performing a very convincing simulation of listening while replaying a Slack exchange from this morning where a peer got credit for her idea. The resentment is loud enough that she misses an actual question directed at her. Her manager has to say her name twice. ## STORY v11

Marcus, six months in, has started measuring his meetings by how many times his Slack badge count changes during them — a private, slightly obsessive metric. During today's roadmap review, the badge jumps from 3 to 7 and he feels the pull, physical, like a string tugging his eyes sideways. He doesn't look. He notices the pull instead, names it "the pull," and keeps his eyes on the shared screen. It takes real effort, more than he expected for something so small. The badge count doesn't matter and he knows it doesn't matter, and knowing that doesn't make the pull weaker. What makes it survivable is just not following it, once, on purpose, and finding out the meeting is still there when he stays.

Zoe's onboarding buddy warned her: "the first month, you'll be in every meeting and present in none of them." Zoe laughed it off, then lived it — three weeks of nodding through meetings she couldn't summarize an hour later. Today, in a meeting about a project she actually cares about, she notices herself already halfway into the rehearsed-reply spiral before the meeting's even ended. She stops mid-spiral. Just stops. Looks at the actual person talking, a coworker named Dev explaining a bug. She catches the whole explanation, first time in weeks. It's such a small thing that she almost doesn't register it as a win. But it's the first meeting she can fully describe to herself afterward, and that fact alone tells her something has shifted, quietly, underneath the noise.

## STORY v13

Amara's manager gives feedback in a 1:1 — gentle, specific, actually useful — and Amara's mind immediately leaves to go rehearse a defense, even though nothing requires defending. She catches herself constructing an argument against a criticism that was never made. She takes one breath, on purpose, mid-conversation, which feels strange and slightly performative the first time she does it. Then she asks her manager to repeat the last point, not because she needs to stall, but because she genuinely didn't catch it. Her manager repeats it. This time it lands as what it actually was: useful, not an attack. Amara leaves the 1:1 with a real takeaway instead of an imagined battle scar, and notices that the second was heavier to carry than the first, even though only one of them was real.

Jordan works two calls back to back most days and has started arriving to the second one still fully occupied by the first — a comment a stakeholder made that didn't sit right, replayed on loop. In today's second call, a teammate directly asks Jordan a question and gets an answer that's technically responsive but clearly assembled from autopilot. The teammate's face does a small confused thing. Jordan notices the face before noticing the disconnect that caused it. That's new — usually the drift goes unnoticed entirely. Jordan says, "sorry, that didn't land — let me actually answer that," and does, this time from the actual room. It's a two-second repair. The teammate doesn't remember it five minutes later. Jordan does, because it's the first time this week the repair happened during the meeting instead of as a guilty replay after it.

## STORY v15
---
---
Chen has a running joke with himself that his body has excellent meeting attendance and his mind has none. Today, presenting to a director two levels up, he feels the old pull toward the version of himself narrating his own performance — "you sound nervous," "they can probably tell." He notices the narrator. Instead of arguing with it or believing it, he just turns his attention, bodily, to the actual next sentence he needs to say. The narrator doesn't stop completely. But it gets quieter, the way a second conversation in a loud room fades when you commit to the one in front of you. He finishes the presentation having said what he meant to say, mostly. The narrator's commentary, in the end, changed nothing about how it went. Just how it felt while it was happening.

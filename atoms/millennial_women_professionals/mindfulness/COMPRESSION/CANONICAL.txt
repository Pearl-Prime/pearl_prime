## COMPRESSION v01
---
---
Half-presence costs you the meeting's memory, not its outcome — you attend, answer, nod, and still can't recall it an hour later, because attention is what encodes an event and yours was elsewhere. The mind drifting to Slack or a rehearsed reply isn't a flaw; it's the baseline behavior of unattended attention. The fix isn't gripping focus tighter — that was never available for a full thirty minutes to anyone. The fix is shortening the drift-to-return gap through repetition: notice you've left, ground physically (hands, feet), look at the real speaker, repeat. Presence is the return, not an unbroken state. Track catches, not stretches of unbroken focus.
---

## COMPRESSION v02
---
---
Attention doesn't split between a meeting and a Slack thread — it switches, fully, each time, and every switch costs a re-orientation beat. Switch often enough and you spend most of a meeting relocating yourself instead of absorbing anything, which is why you can attend fully and remember almost nothing. The wandering itself isn't fixable; it's automatic, aimed at whatever feels most unresolved. What's trainable is the gap between drifting and noticing. Shrink that gap through repeated small returns — physical anchor, then eyes on the actual speaker — and presence stops being a discipline problem and becomes a rep you get faster at, meeting after meeting.
---

## COMPRESSION v03
---
---
The rehearsed reply and the replayed comment both share a mechanism: your attention chases whatever feels most unresolved, real or imagined, and a meeting is simply where it goes to do that chasing. Fighting the chase head-on fails, because the chase is automatic. What works is catching it — noticing, without narrating why, that you've left — and walking back to one physical anchor and the actual person talking. Ten short catches in a single meeting beat one long stretch of white-knuckled focus that collapses by minute six. The skill isn't staying. It's returning, repeatedly, faster each time.
---

## COMPRESSION v04
---
---
Presence isn't a state you hold; it's a correction you keep making, the way a compass wobbles and resettles rather than locking onto true north and staying still. The wobble — mind to Slack, mind to a manager's ambiguous comment, mind to the next meeting — is not the failure. The absence of correction is. Build the correction as a rep: notice the drift, anchor to something physically present, redirect attention to the real voice in the room. Repeat this across meetings, not perfectly, just repeatedly, and the gap between leaving and returning shortens on its own, which is the entire mechanism of getting better at this.

## COMPRESSION v05
---
---
What you lose to half-presence isn't visible in the moment — nobody watching you nod can tell you're actually drafting a Slack reply in your head. What you lose shows up later, as a meeting with no retrievable memory attached to it, because attention is the thing that writes an event down and yours wasn't there to do the writing. The fix isn't more willpower aimed at staying; willpower doesn't hold attention still for anyone. The fix is training the catch — noticing you've left, faster each time, and returning to one concrete physical anchor and the real person speaking. Repetition shortens the gap. That gap is the entire skill.

## COMPRESSION v06
---
---
Unattended attention drifts by design, toward whatever feels most urgent — a Slack ping, an ambiguous comment, a meeting looming next on the calendar — whether or not it's actually urgent. This isn't a personal failing; it's the default setting. The trainable part isn't preventing the drift. It's shrinking the time between drifting and noticing, through the same small rep every time: notice, anchor physically, look at the real speaker. Do this across enough meetings and the noticing gets faster, sometimes almost automatic. That's the entire arc of getting better at presence — not fewer drifts, just quicker returns.

## COMPRESSION v07
---
---
A meeting attended in body but not attention leaves no trace, because the mechanism that would have recorded it — your focus — was busy elsewhere, usually defending against criticism that was never actually given or rehearsing a reply nobody asked for yet. The fix isn't suppressing the pull toward those things; the pull is automatic and largely involuntary. The fix is noticing the pull has won, again, and walking back — hands on the desk, eyes on the real face — as many times as the meeting requires. Each return is a complete rep, not a partial failure. Presence is built entirely out of these small, repeated corrections.

## COMPRESSION v08
---
---
Every switch of attention — from the meeting to the Slack thread and back — carries a hidden re-orientation cost, which is why a mind that toggles constantly during a call absorbs far less than half of it, not close to half. This tax is invisible in the moment and explains the specific, common experience of finishing a meeting you technically attended with almost nothing retained. The remedy isn't fewer toggles by force of will; toggling is automatic. The remedy is catching each toggle sooner — noticing, anchoring physically, returning attention to what's actually being said — until the average catch-time shortens through sheer repetition across real meetings.

## COMPRESSION v09
---
---
Treat presence as a metric of return-speed, not attention-length. Nobody holds unbroken focus for a full meeting — the mind drifts to Slack threads, rehearsed replies, replayed comments, as a baseline behavior, not a personal defect. What separates someone who seems consistently present from someone who doesn't is how quickly they notice the drift and walk back, not whether they drift at all. Build this by treating every noticed drift as a rep: ground physically, then redirect to the real speaker. The gap shortens with repetition. That shortening gap is the entire, only trainable form of "focus" available to anyone.

## COMPRESSION v10
---
---
The specific cost of half-presence is retrospective, not immediate: you don't feel yourself missing the meeting while it's happening, you just find, an hour later, that there's nothing there to recall, because the meeting was never encoded — attention does the encoding, and yours was in a Slack thread. This isn't solved by trying harder to concentrate, since concentration was never a static state to begin with. It's solved by practicing the return: noticing the departure, using a physical anchor to relocate yourself, and looking at the actual person talking. Repeated across enough meetings, the noticing gets faster. That's the whole mechanism, and it's the only one that was ever actually available.

## COMPRESSION v11
---
---
Your attention chasing a Slack ping mid-meeting is the same mechanism as an old nervous system checking a rustle in the grass — it reads unresolved as urgent, automatically, regardless of actual stakes. You cannot switch this reflex off. What you can do is shorten the interval between the reflex firing and you noticing it fired, through the exact same small rep every time: notice, ground in something physical, return attention to the real conversation. This interval shrinks with practice the way any noticing-based skill shrinks its own lag — not dramatically, but reliably, meeting after meeting, rep after rep.
---

## COMPRESSION v12
---
---
Presence fails silently — you can perform full engagement (nodding, camera on, verbal cues) while being functionally absent, and nothing visibly breaks except your own later memory of the meeting. The mechanism: attention encodes experience, and divided or absent attention encodes nothing, leaving a hollow spot where a memory should sit. The fix bypasses willpower entirely and targets the catch instead — noticing the departure sooner, returning to a physical anchor and the actual speaker, over and over. Presence, correctly understood, is this return, repeated until the gap between leaving and coming back becomes small enough not to matter.

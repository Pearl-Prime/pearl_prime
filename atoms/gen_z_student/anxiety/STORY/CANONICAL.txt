Priya sits cross-legged on her dorm bed with her laptop balanced on a pillow and her jaw clenched so tight her molars ache. The group project meeting starts in ten minutes. She opens the shared doc and sees three blank sections assigned to teammates who stopped responding in the group chat two days ago.
Her chest tightens. She types a message — "Hey, are you all set for tonight" — then deletes it. The punctuation feels aggressive. She rewrites it with an exclamation point. That feels desperate. She locks her phone and presses her palms against her thighs until the trembling stops.
She carries other people's silences in her body. She learned this in high school, absorbing every unread text as evidence of rejection. Now, in a shared apartment with four strangers who became half-friends, the pattern runs deeper. When someone goes quiet, her ribcage compresses around the space their voice left behind.
The meeting link appears. She clicks it. Three black squares. No cameras. Her stomach drops. She unmutes and says hello to dead air. Her voice sounds thin, a wire pulled too taut.
One person joins late, apologizes, talks about their own deadline. Priya nods, takes on two extra sections, and says it is fine. Her shoulders stay welded to her ears for the rest of the night. She finishes the project at 3 a.m., alone in the blue glow of her screen, neck locked, pulse still running too fast for a body sitting perfectly still.
She gets an A. The grade means nothing to the knot that lives between her shoulder blades. It fed on every hour she spent performing calm while her nervous system screamed.

Marcus stands in the campus dining hall with a full tray and a heartbeat that hammers against his sternum. The room buzzes — forks scraping plates, conversations overlapping, someone laughing too loud near the soda fountain. His vision narrows to the space between tables, calculating paths the way other people calculate tips.
He picks a corner seat facing the wall. His hands shake when he sets down the tray. He eats fast, mechanically, tasting nothing. A bead of sweat rolls down his temple. No one looks at him. He checks anyway, three times in two minutes, glancing over his shoulder with a neck so stiff it clicks.
This started freshman year. The first dining hall meal without his family, surrounded by strangers who already seemed to know each other. His throat closed around a bite of pasta and he spent ten minutes in the bathroom convincing himself he could swallow.
Now, second year, the ritual hardened into architecture. He memorizes the dining hall's least crowded hours. He eats at 4:47 p.m. or 8:15 p.m., never during the rush. His roommate thinks he has a weird schedule. Marcus lets that story stand because it costs less than the truth.
The truth lives in his chest: a steel band that tightens whenever bodies crowd together and voices layer into noise. His doctor calls it generalized anxiety. Marcus calls it Tuesday.
He finishes the tray, buses it, and walks back to the dorm with his fists pressed into his jacket pockets. His knuckles ache from gripping. The night air hits his face and his lungs release one fraction. Just one. The rest stays locked until the dorm door closes behind him and he stands alone in the dark room, breathing like a man who just surfaced from deep water.

Jess stares at her phone on the library desk. Her best friend Mara texted thirty minutes ago — "wanna grab food" — and Jess typed and deleted four responses. Her thumbs hover, frozen. Her stomach churns with something that looks nothing like hunger.
She wants to go. She also wants to disappear into the carpet. The tension between those two impulses locks her body in place: shoulders rigid, breath shallow, the muscles along her spine humming like a plucked string.
She sends "yeah give me 20" and immediately regrets it. Twenty minutes means she has to shower, change, appear human. Her reflection in the library window shows someone whose face looks calm. Her pulse tells a different story — a staccato drumline behind her ribs.
Jess walks to the dining hall and finds Mara at their usual table. She smiles. The smile costs effort, a conscious lift of facial muscles against the gravity of dread. Mara talks about a professor, a party, a guy from econ. Jess nods in the right places while monitoring her own body for signs of collapse.
Her hands stay in her lap so Mara cannot see them shake. She orders soup because it requires the least chewing and her jaw already aches from clenching during the walk over. Every laugh she produces travels through a checkpoint in her chest before it reaches her mouth.
Mara says, "You seem chill tonight." Jess holds her spoon steady and says, "Yeah, good day." The lie sits in her throat like a stone. She swallows it with broth.
Later, alone in her dorm, she lies on the narrow bed and her body finally releases. Tears slide sideways into the pillow. Not from sadness. From the sheer muscular exhaustion of pretending that existing among people costs her nothing.

Kai opens the exam booklet and the words scatter. Letters rearrange themselves on the page like insects startled by light. His pen hovers. His hand trembles — a fine vibration visible only to him, or so he hopes. The lecture hall smells like dry-erase markers and collective dread.
He knows this material. He quizzed himself forty-seven times on the flashcard app. He recited mechanisms in the shower until his roommate banged on the bathroom door. The knowledge exists in his brain, filed and organized. His body refuses to let it out.
His throat constricts. A steel wire pulls from his solar plexus to his jaw. He reads the first question again and recognizes every word individually but cannot assemble them into meaning. Sweat pools at the base of his spine.
He closes his eyes. Counts his own heartbeats. One. Two. Three. At seven, he opens his eyes and writes the first sentence — wrong, clumsy, but present on the page. The second sentence comes easier. The wire in his chest loosens by one degree.
Kai learned something this semester that textbooks never taught him. His anxiety does not attack his intelligence. It attacks the bridge between knowing and showing. He sits in the gap, building the crossing plank by plank, one shaking sentence at a time.
He finishes the exam with four minutes left. His hand cramps. His shirt sticks to his back. He turns it in and walks out into cold air that fills his lungs like the first breath after being held underwater. A familiar ache settles behind his eyes — the hangover of a nervous system that just ran a marathon while his body sat in a plastic chair.
He texts his sister: "Survived." She sends back a fist emoji. He presses the phone against his chest and stands still on the quad, letting his heartbeat slow to something that resembles rest.

Dani arrives early to every lecture. Not from discipline — from the specific terror of walking into a full room and feeling two hundred sets of eyes track her to an empty seat. The early arrival erases that possibility. She picks the aisle seat, third row from the back, and arranges her notebook and pen with the precision of someone defusing a small bomb.
Students filter in. Each new body in the room adds weight to the air she breathes. Her shoulders inch upward. By the time the professor starts talking, her traps are knotted ropes pressed against her earlobes.
She takes notes compulsively — not to study later but to give her hands a job. Idle hands become visible hands, and visible hands tremble. The pen moves across the page in tight, controlled script. She presses too hard. The ink bleeds through to the next page.
A classmate leans over: "Did she say the quiz is Thursday or Friday." Dani's throat seizes. She points to the line in her notes. The classmate nods and turns away. Dani's heart pounds as though she just sprinted a hallway.
After class, she walks the long route back to her apartment — the one that avoids the crowded campus center. Her legs feel hollow, drained of something her body burned through during seventy-five minutes of sitting still and appearing fine. She unlocks her door, drops her bag, and sinks into the couch.
The cushion absorbs her weight. Her shoulders descend by slow fractions. She stares at the ceiling and breathes through her mouth, long and ragged, like an athlete cooling down from a race no one else ran.

River laces up their running shoes in the shared apartment at 6 a.m. Their roommate sleeps behind a closed door. The apartment is silent except for the refrigerator hum and the small sounds River's body makes: a cracking ankle, a jaw that pops when it unclenches.
The anxiety arrived in middle school — a permanent resident that claimed the space behind their sternum and never paid rent. For years, River tried to evict it. Therapy helped name it. Medication filed down its sharpest edges. Running taught them to live alongside it.
They step outside. Cold air stings their nostrils. Their chest is already tight — the familiar morning compression, like a hand pressing flat against their breastbone. They start slow. Their feet hit pavement in a rhythm that their heart gradually matches.
By the half-mile mark, the tightness shifts. It does not vanish. It redistributes. The pressure in their chest migrates to their legs, their lungs, their pumping arms. Anxiety becomes fuel for motion. The body that held fear now holds exertion, and the two feel almost identical from the inside, but one builds and the other burns.
River rounds the campus track. The library looms on their left, the building where they spent last night immobilized by a reading list that felt like a death sentence. In daylight, while running, the building shrinks to its actual size. Brick and glass. Not a threat.
They finish two miles and stop, bent over, hands on knees, gasping. Sweat drips from their nose onto the track's red surface. The tightness in their chest returns — softer now, a background hum instead of a siren.
River walks back to the apartment, showers, and sits at their desk. The reading list still exists. The anxiety still exists. The difference is spatial: where fear occupied the entire room last night, it now fits in a single chair. River sits in the other chair and opens the first article. Their hands hold steady enough to turn the page.

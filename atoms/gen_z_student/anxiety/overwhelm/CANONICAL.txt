Marcus opens his planner on Sunday night and the week spreads out like a landslide. Genetics midterm Tuesday. Lab report due Wednesday. Financial aid appeal deadline Thursday. Group project meeting Friday. His part-time job three nights in between. He writes everything out in columns, thinking organization will make it manageable. The columns fill both pages. His hand stops moving. His chest is already tight, a compression he's learned to recognize as the moment his capacity reaches its ceiling. He closes the planner. He does not know which item to start with. He opens his laptop and stares at the browser for four minutes before closing it. The week hasn't started and he's already operating at a deficit. The tightness spreads from his chest to his jaw. He sits at his desk in the dark and tells himself to just pick one thing. His body stays still.

COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Kofi is halfway through his biochemistry reading when his roommate knocks on the open door to ask about dinner. It is a normal question. Kofi's response is completely disproportionate—his shoulders lock, his vision narrows, and his voice comes out flat and clipped. The roommate leaves. Kofi stares at his textbook. He knows he was rude. He also knows that the knock came when he had eleven pages left in the chapter, two problem sets unopened, and a tutor session in ninety minutes he hasn't prepared for. His capacity was already at its edge. The knock was just the thing that found no room. He texts his roommate an apology. The apology costs him three minutes he doesn't have. His chest still hasn't released. The remaining eleven pages look the same as when he started.

## TURNING_POINT v03
---

BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Priya leaves the library at midnight having completed one of the four tasks she came to finish. She sits on the bench outside and lets herself feel the failure of the evening instead of pushing past it. She had tried to work on all four things simultaneously and finished none of them well. The biology paper is half-drafted. The calculus problem set is untouched. She is not okay. Her eyes sting. She isn't sure if it's tiredness or something she doesn't have language for yet. She makes a decision that feels counterintuitive: she will go to sleep and start with one thing in the morning. The decision doesn't feel triumphant. It feels like giving up on three of the four things, which her body registers as failure even though her rational mind understands it as triage. She stands up. The anxiety is still there. She walks toward her dorm with her hands pressed into her jacket pockets, feeling the cost of having limits, which she has spent the semester trying to pretend she doesn't have.

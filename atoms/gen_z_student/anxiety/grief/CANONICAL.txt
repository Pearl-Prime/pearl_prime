Nadia moves through her Tuesday morning on autopilot. Alarm, coffee, walk to campus, sit in the lecture hall. Her grandfather died six weeks ago. The semester didn't pause. She didn't ask for an extension because she didn't think she was allowed to, or maybe she didn't think she deserved one. The lecture hall fills around her and she opens her laptop and the grief sits in her chest like a stone she has agreed to carry without telling anyone. A classmate in the row ahead is laughing with a friend. Nadia watches this and her throat tightens in a way that has nothing to do with anxiety about the exam. It has to do with the fact that her grandfather used to call every Sunday and no one will call this Sunday. The lecture starts. She takes notes. The stone doesn't move.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02

COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Luz went home for her aunt's funeral in October and returned to campus ten days later with a week of missed lectures. She emailed professors. They responded with condolences and PDF slides. She sat in her dorm trying to catch up and found that the words wouldn't stay on the page. Her eyes moved and retained nothing. Her roommate asked how she was doing and Luz said "okay" because explaining felt harder than carrying it. She missed a quiz. She submitted a lab report with citations she knows are incomplete. Her chest aches constantly—not in a way she can identify as sadness exactly, more like pressure, like her sternum is being pressed from the inside. She is falling behind and grieving at the same time and the combination costs more than either alone. She has not cried since the funeral. The pressure keeps building.

## TURNING_POINT v03
---
path: story_atoms/gen_z_student/anxiety/grief/turning_point/micro/v03.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Remy finds his mother's voicemail from three months ago, before she got sick. Just a check-in. "Call me when you're free, love you." He presses play in the campus library bathroom with the door locked and listens to it twice. His hands shake. He sits on the cold floor against the stall door and breathes. He hasn't told his study group why he's been missing morning sessions. He keeps saying he has work shifts. The lie is easier than the truth, which is that some mornings he wakes up and the weight in his chest makes the walk across campus feel impossible. He stands up and runs water over his wrists until the shaking subsides. He has a group presentation in forty minutes. He will show up. His face will do what it needs to do. The voicemail will stay in his phone. The cost of carrying this is the distance it creates between his real self and the one his classmates see. He splashes water on his face and walks out.

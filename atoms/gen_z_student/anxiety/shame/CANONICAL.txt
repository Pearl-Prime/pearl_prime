Selin freezes during the seminar when the professor calls on her. She knows the reading. She read it twice. But the question lands and everything empties out. She says "I'm not sure" and looks down at her notebook. The professor moves on. Selin writes nothing for the next twenty minutes. The shame settles in her chest like something heavy and hot. She doesn't feel nervous right now. She feels exposed—like the failure wasn't about the question but about something fundamental, some lack that the professor and every other student in the room just witnessed. She knows this reading of the moment isn't accurate. She reads it anyway. On the walk home, she replays her two words and feels them shrink smaller with each repetition. "I'm not sure." The worst part is that she was sure. She just couldn't speak.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02

IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Arjun fails his first physics lab practical and has to retake it. The retake room has four other students. He knows two of them from lecture. They smile at him and he smiles back and his stomach is a closed fist the entire time. He passes the retake with an eighty-two, which is fine. The number means nothing. What stays is the act of being in the retake room, visible as someone who failed the first time, sitting under fluorescent lights while his classmates took it once and passed. The shame doesn't care that eighty-two is fine. The shame is about the original failure being public. He avoids the two classmates he recognized for the next three weeks, taking the long route between lecture and his dorm. The long route adds twelve minutes to every commute. He does not notice himself doing this until he checks his phone one evening and sees how late it is.

## TURNING_POINT v03

COST_TYPE: internal

---
Marcus goes to office hours for the first time in the semester. He stands outside the door for four minutes before knocking. Inside, the professor is alone, working through a stack of papers, and she looks up and says "Hi, what can I help with?" without any indication that she has judged the lateness of his arrival in the semester. Marcus asks his question. His voice is steady. His hands grip his notebook at his sides. She explains it twice, differently each time, and the concept finally connects. He writes it down. He thanks her and leaves. Outside, he stands on the building steps and his chest is doing something complicated—relief, but also something that stings, because the question he just asked was one he had been too ashamed to bring in for weeks. He could have had this explanation eight weeks ago. The cost of shame was eight weeks of compounding confusion. He walks to the library and opens the chapter from the beginning.

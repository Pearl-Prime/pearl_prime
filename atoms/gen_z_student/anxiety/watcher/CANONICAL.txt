Marcus notices himself noticing. He is sitting in the lecture hall, heart rate elevated, hands folded on the desk, and there is a part of him that watches the anxiety the way you might watch a weather pattern from a window. He sees his own jaw clench. He observes his own shallow breathing. He registers his own inability to take notes for the past seven minutes. The watching doesn't help. Knowing you're anxious doesn't reduce the anxiety. His chest stays compressed. His hands stay cold. The observing self and the suffering self exist simultaneously and the split costs something—a doubling of the experience, the anxiety itself plus the exhausting awareness of it. The professor writes an equation on the board. Marcus watches himself not writing it down.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02

COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Selin attends her friend's birthday dinner and spends the evening watching herself perform. She sees herself lean forward and laugh. She observes herself ask questions, refill her glass, look engaged. The performance is good. She knows how to do this. But she is never fully inside it—there is always a back row in her mind where she sits and watches the social mechanics run, cataloguing what she says and how it lands, measuring her contributions against her noise-to-signal ratio. Her shoulders are tight through the whole meal. She doesn't eat much. When she gets home she lies on her bed and the exhaustion hits all at once. Not tiredness. The specific depletion of having been simultaneously present and absent for three hours. Her friends had a good time. Selin was there and wasn't there, and the gap between those two things sits in her chest when the room goes quiet.

## TURNING_POINT v03
---
path: story_atoms/gen_z_student/anxiety/watcher/turning_point/micro/v03.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Remy is mid-panic in the campus health center waiting room when he realizes he has been narrating his own panic. He watches himself grip the chair arms. He notices himself counting the number of other students in the room. He observes his own leg bouncing. For a moment he tries to stop the narration—just feel the panic without the commentary—and the attempt itself adds another layer. He gives up. He lets the watcher watch. He lets the panicking body panic. He breathes through his mouth, slow and audible, and after six minutes his heart rate drops enough that he can fill out the intake form. He is still watching himself fill it out. The pen moves steadily. His hands are still cold. The watching never stops—he understands that now, sitting in the waiting room with the form half-complete. The cost is living in two places at once, neither of which is ever fully his.

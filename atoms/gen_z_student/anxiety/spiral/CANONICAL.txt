Nadia checks the course drop deadline at 2 a.m. and her thoughts start moving faster than she can track. If she drops the class, she'll fall below full-time status. If she falls below full-time status, her financial aid recalculates. If her financial aid recalculates, she can't pay rent. If she can't pay rent, she has to move home. If she moves home, she loses the summer internship she applied for. She's three steps from her original question and already at a catastrophic ending. Her palms are damp against her phone screen. Her breathing has gone shallow without her noticing. She reads the same line of the financial aid policy four times and processes nothing. The spiral is running independent of evidence. The drop deadline is three weeks away. None of the cascade has happened. Her chest doesn't care.

---
Yuki misses a morning lecture and the spiral starts before she gets out of bed. The attendance policy allows three absences. This is her second. She calculates: one absence left for the semester, finals during her flight home, she might have to choose between the family holiday and her grade. Her pulse rises. She opens the syllabus on her phone to check if the holiday flight date could constitute an excused absence. The words blur. She has been lying in bed for twenty minutes building an architecture of disaster from a single Tuesday absence. Her roommate knocks to say breakfast is ready. Yuki says she'll be there in a second. She won't. She'll spend another thirty minutes in the spiral, unable to eat, unable to get up, stomach locked around the worry, until the original problem—one absence—is buried under twenty hypotheticals she can't resolve. Breakfast will be cold.

## TURNING_POINT v03
---
path: story_atoms/gen_z_student/anxiety/spiral/turning_point/micro/v03.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Luz catches herself on the third what-if and stops. Her philosophy professor just handed back papers and she got a B-minus. She was already moving through the cascade—B-minus drops her GPA, GPA drops below scholarship threshold, scholarship revoked, she can't return next semester—when something slows the chain. She has been here before. She recognizes the acceleration. She sits with the B-minus on her lap and breathes and tries to stay inside the actual event: a grade on one paper. She does not succeed completely. The spiral slows but doesn't stop. Her chest is still tight. Her hands grip the paper. But she names what's happening—"I'm spiraling off one paper"—and the naming costs her something strange, a kind of grief for the part of herself that wanted the catastrophe to be real. She sits with the B-minus for a long time. The tightness doesn't leave. She puts the paper in her folder and opens the next assignment.

## COMPRESSION v01
---

---
Anxiety is a false fire alarm wired into your nervous system. It rings in the dining hall. It rings before exams. It rings when the group chat goes quiet. Your body responds identically to social discomfort and genuine danger — chest compression, shallow breath, locked jaw. The work is not silencing the alarm. The work is learning to hear it ring and check the room before you run. Most rooms contain no fire. Your body needs repetitions of that truth before it believes.

## COMPRESSION v02
---

---
Your brain generates catastrophic predictions. Your body executes them as physical reality — tight throat, rigid shoulders, trembling hands. The thought "I will fail this exam" becomes a clenched fist before you finish thinking it. Interrupting the cycle means catching the translation in progress: thought becomes sensation, sensation becomes behavior, behavior becomes avoidance. Name the thought before it reaches your muscles. That naming creates a half-second gap. Inside that gap, you choose.
---

## COMPRESSION v03
---

---
Three anxiety patterns dominate student life. Anticipatory: the dread that precedes events by hours or days, stealing sleep and appetite. Social: the monitoring of every word spoken and every face in the room, burning energy meant for thinking. Perfectionist: the refusal to submit work that falls below an impossible standard, producing paralysis disguised as high standards. Most students carry all three. Recognizing which pattern runs the current moment tells you which body part to check: stomach for anticipatory, shoulders for social, jaw for perfectionist.

## COMPRESSION v04
---

---
The group chat pings. Your chest tightens. You check the message. It is benign. Your chest stays tight. This is the signature of anxiety: the trigger and the response decouple. The feeling outlasts its cause because your nervous system runs on momentum, not logic. Knowing this changes nothing physically — your chest still compresses. Knowing this changes everything strategically — you stop searching for a reason and start addressing the sensation directly. Hand on chest. Slow exhale. The body responds to touch and breath faster than to reason.

## COMPRESSION v05
---

---
Avoidance is anxiety's business model. Skip the dining hall, eat alone, feel temporary relief. Skip office hours, email instead, feel temporary relief. Skip the party, stay in the dorm, feel temporary relief. Each avoidance buys comfort now and sells capacity later. The world shrinks by one room, one conversation, one opportunity per transaction. Reversing the pattern demands tolerating discomfort without purchasing relief. Sit in the dining hall for ten minutes. The discomfort peaks and descends. Your body learns that survival is possible without escape.
---

## COMPRESSION v06
---

---
Sleep debt and anxiety form a feedback loop that runs the average student's nervous system into the ground. Anxiety delays sleep — the body stays hypervigilant past midnight, cataloging tomorrow's threats. Sleep loss heightens anxiety — the under-rested brain amplifies threats by measurable neurological factors. Breaking the loop requires attacking one side. A fixed wake time anchors the cycle regardless of when sleep arrived. Feet on the floor at the same hour, every day, even after a 3 a.m. spiral. The body clock resets. Anxiety keeps its volume but loses its overtime shift.

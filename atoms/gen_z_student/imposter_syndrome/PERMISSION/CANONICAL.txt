## PERMISSION v01
---

---
You have permission to accept the compliment. Let it land in your chest without deflecting. Your professor meant the words. Your hands did the work. The praise belongs to you. Set down the shield. The compliment is not an attack.

You have permission to not know the answer in the study group. Silence is not evidence of stupidity. Your closed mouth gathers information. Learning requires gaps. Your empty page fills at its own pace. Confusion precedes clarity every single time.

You have permission to celebrate the ninety-four. The score reflects hours of effort your body remembers. Your sore back from studying. Your tired eyes from reading. The two-point gap means nothing. The work means everything. Your body earned this grade.

You have permission to take up space in the room. Your chair holds you. The table has room for your elbows. Spread your notebook open. Uncross your arms. Your body belongs in this classroom exactly as much as every other body present.

You have permission to be a beginner. First-year students do not owe expertise. Your confusion is appropriate. Your stumbling is developmental. Your shaking voice in the presentation proves you stood up and spoke. Beginners begin. That is the entire job description.

You have permission to close LinkedIn. The curated highlight reel of other people's summers carries zero information about your worth. Your thumbs deserve rest. Your stomach deserves peace. Set the phone down. Look at the ceiling. Breathe into your belly. Return to your own lane.

## PERMISSION v02
---

---
You have permission to put your name first on the group project document. Open the file. Type your name. The author order is a small lie about contribution. Your hours of work are documented in the version history. Claim them in the byline.

You have permission to ask the professor to repeat the question. Lift your hand slightly. Speak. The repetition is a free service. Pretending you heard it the first time is a tax you keep paying.

You have permission to log how many hours a paper actually took you. Open a notes app. Type the hours. The number is data against the doubt that says you were lazy. The doubt rewrites history. The notes app does not.

You have permission to ignore the LinkedIn announcement of a peer's prestigious internship. Close the tab. Feel the room get quieter. Their announcement is for their network, not your peace of mind. Their summer is not your scoreboard.

You have permission to say "I do not know" in the seminar without softening it. Press your tongue against your teeth. Release. The four-word answer is complete. Padding it with apology only signals that not-knowing is shameful. It is not.

You have permission to keep the rejection letter in the drawer instead of the screenshot folder. Close the drawer. Walk away. The rejection is one decision by one committee on one day. The screenshot folder is a museum you keep visiting. Stop curating it.

## PERMISSION v03
---

---
You have permission to tell your tutor "I do not understand the previous chapter either." Sit down. Open the book. The tutor's job is not to assess your prior preparation. It is to teach the gap. Name the gap from the start.

You have permission to ask for a deadline extension because the work is hard, not just because of an emergency. Email the professor. Type the reason. Difficulty is a real reason. Most professors prefer honest difficulty over manufactured emergency.

You have permission to claim the A-minus as your work even if your roommate got an A. Walk into your room. Set the paper on the desk. Their grade did not affect your grade. The comparison only happens because you keep running it. Stop running it.

You have permission to take a class pass-fail. Walk to the registrar. Fill out the form. The transcript will show pass-fail. Future-you will not retroactively wish you had taken the harder grading option. Future-you will be grateful for the bandwidth.

You have permission to say "this is my first attempt" before showing your work. Open the laptop. Click share. The disclaimer is honest, not weak. First attempts are how all expertise starts. The audience adjusts expectations accordingly.

You have permission to celebrate getting the average score. Walk to the dining hall. Order the food you wanted. Average means you cleared the bar set by everyone in the room. Average is not failure. The class is sorted by curve, not by your inner critic.

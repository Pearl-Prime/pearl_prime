## STORY v01
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: cognitive_distortion — discounting positive evidence
IDENTITY_STAGE: foreclosure — identity defined by external validation
COST_TYPE: academic_withdrawal
COST_INTENSITY: high
---
Kai sits in the third row of his honors seminar, textbook open, notes color-coded, and his stomach tight as a fist. The professor poses a question about trade policy. Kai knows the answer. He rehearsed it at 6 a.m. with a cold cup of coffee in his dorm room. But his throat constricts when the professor's gaze sweeps his row. He drops his eyes to the page.
Two seats away, Marcus raises his hand and delivers the same argument Kai prepared. The class nods. The professor writes the point on the board. Kai's chest caves inward. He catalogs this as proof: Marcus belongs here, and Kai stumbled in through a clerical error.
After class, Kai passes the professor's office. The door stands open. He keeps walking. His shoulders climb toward his ears. He skips the study group that evening and rewrites his notes alone, adding nothing new, just rearranging what he already knows as if the formatting will make him legitimate.
By midterms Kai stops attending office hours entirely. His grade drops from an A to a B+. He reads the transcript update and feels relief — the number finally matches what he believes about himself. His jaw unclenches for the first time in weeks. The cost is invisible to everyone except Kai: he chose a grade that confirmed his fear over a performance that challenged it.

## STORY v02
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: attribution_bias — success externalized, failure internalized
IDENTITY_STAGE: moratorium — actively questioning competence
COST_TYPE: social_isolation
COST_INTENSITY: medium-high
---
Priya gets the research assistant position over forty other applicants. She reads the email three times, and her hands tremble against the phone screen. By the second lab meeting she has convinced herself the professor picked her because no one better applied.
She arrives thirty minutes early to every session. She triple-checks her data entry. Her neck aches from hunching over the laptop. When the postdoc praises her literature review, Priya's face flushes and she deflects the compliment with a laugh that comes out too loud. She attributes the praise to politeness, not accuracy.
Her lab partner, Tomás, invites her to a weekend study session. Priya declines. Her ribs tighten at the thought of him discovering gaps in her knowledge. She declines again the following week. Tomás stops asking. Priya eats lunch alone in the library stacks and tells herself she prefers the quiet.
By December, Priya has produced the most thorough dataset in the lab. The professor nominates her for a conference presentation. Priya stares at the nomination and her stomach drops. She drafts a refusal email, deletes it, drafts another. She accepts, but only after rewriting her abstract eleven times. The isolation she built to protect her secret now walls her off from the people who already see her as competent.

## STORY v03
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: hypervigilance — constant scanning for exposure
IDENTITY_STAGE: diffusion — no stable self-concept in academic setting
COST_TYPE: perfectionism_paralysis
COST_INTENSITY: high
---
Diego opens his laptop at 11 p.m. to start a paper due in thirty-six hours. He writes a sentence, reads it, and deletes it. His jaw tightens. The sentence sounded like something a real student would write, and that mismatch between the words and what he believes about himself freezes his fingers.
He spends two hours adjusting margins, reformatting citations, and reorganizing his outline. His eyes burn. His shoulders lock. At 1 a.m. he has a pristine document structure and zero content. He closes the laptop and lies on his bed staring at the ceiling, his pulse thumping in his temples.
The next morning Diego sits in the library and watches classmates type steadily. His throat tightens. He opens his document again and writes three paragraphs in a burst, then rereads them and highlights every phrase that sounds too confident. He softens each claim until the argument dissolves into qualification.
He submits the paper ten minutes before the deadline with a body that reads like an apology. The professor returns it with a B and a note: "You have stronger ideas than you let yourself argue." Diego reads the comment, and his chest aches. He folds the paper into his bag and does not look at it again. The mechanism is precise: he scans for evidence of fraud, finds none, and treats the absence itself as proof he has not been caught yet.

## STORY v04
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: comparative_anchoring — measuring self against idealized peers
IDENTITY_STAGE: foreclosure — worth determined by rank
COST_TYPE: health_deterioration
COST_INTENSITY: high
---
Mina checks the honors program leaderboard every Monday. She holds her breath as the page loads. Her name sits at fourteenth out of sixty. Her ribs compress. She does not register that fourteenth is exceptional; she registers only the thirteen names above hers.
She cuts her sleep to five hours to add a second study session after midnight. Her hands shake during her 8 a.m. lecture. She grips her pen hard enough that her knuckles ache. When her roommate asks if she slept, Mina lies and says seven hours.
By week eight, dark circles ring her eyes. She skips meals to study through lunch. Her stomach growls in the seminar room and she presses her forearm against her abdomen to muffle it. The professor assigns a group project, and Mina volunteers for the largest section because she cannot trust anyone else to match the standard she believes is baseline.
She finishes the semester at eleventh on the leaderboard. Her GPA rises by a tenth of a point. Her doctor notes elevated cortisol and early signs of an ulcer. Mina reads the lab results in the campus health center, and her chest tightens — not from the diagnosis, but from the fear that slowing down will expose the gap between her ranking and her ability.

## STORY v05
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: emotional_suppression — masking distress to maintain facade
IDENTITY_STAGE: moratorium — testing whether vulnerability is survivable
COST_TYPE: relationship_erosion
COST_INTENSITY: medium
---
Jalen presents his capstone proposal to four faculty members. His voice holds steady. His slides advance on cue. His hands grip the podium edge hard enough to blanch his knuckles, but no one in the audience can see that. The committee approves the proposal unanimously.
In the hallway his advisor claps his shoulder and says the idea is ambitious. Jalen's stomach knots. He smiles, nods, and walks to the bathroom where he braces both hands on the sink and lets his breath shudder out. His reflection stares back, and he searches its eyes for the fraud he is certain the committee missed.
His girlfriend, Reese, texts him congratulations. Jalen replies with a thumbs-up emoji. She calls. He lets it ring. She texts again asking if he is okay. He types "all good" and sets the phone face-down on his desk. His chest feels hollow.
Over the following weeks, Jalen deflects every conversation about the capstone. Reese stops bringing it up. His friends learn not to praise him because the praise makes him flinch. The gap between the competent person others see and the imposter Jalen believes himself to be widens with every suppressed reaction, and the people closest to him slowly stop trying to cross it.

## STORY v06
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: origin_narrative — first-gen status as evidence of illegitimacy
IDENTITY_STAGE: moratorium — renegotiating class identity in elite space
COST_TYPE: opportunity_avoidance
COST_INTENSITY: high
---
Sana is the first person in her family to attend a four-year university. She carries that fact like a stone in her sternum. Orientation week, she listens to classmates name their parents' alma maters, and her throat closes. Her mother cleaned offices to pay for the application fee. Sana sits in a mahogany-paneled lecture hall and feels the wood itself rejecting her.
She earns an A on her first political science essay. Her professor writes "exceptional analysis" in the margin. Sana reads it in the stairwell and her eyes sting — not from pride, but from the certainty that the professor grades first-gen students on a curve of sympathy. She folds the essay into the bottom of her backpack.
The department announces a summer fellowship in Washington, D.C. Sana's advisor emails her directly and encourages her to apply. Sana opens the application, reads the first prompt, and her hands go cold. The prompt asks her to describe her qualifications. She stares at the blinking cursor for forty minutes, then closes the tab.
She tells her advisor she decided the fellowship does not align with her interests. Her advisor accepts the explanation. Sana walks back to her dorm with her fists jammed in her jacket pockets, her jaw set. The opportunity disappears. The stone in her sternum stays. She chose the certainty of avoidance over the risk that someone in D.C. would discover she does not speak the language of people who belong in those rooms.

## PIVOT v01
---

---
Imposter syndrome is not a character flaw. It is a pattern your brain built to keep you safe inside unfamiliar rooms. The pattern runs on outdated evidence. You can update the evidence without dismantling the alarm system overnight.
---

## PIVOT v02
---

---
That tight feeling in your chest before you speak in seminar is not proof you do not belong. It is your nervous system responding to perceived threat. The threat is social, not physical. Name it, and the grip loosens by a fraction — enough to act.
---

## PIVOT v03
---

---
Your brain discounts every A and magnifies every stumble. This is not honesty. It is a filtering error. The fix starts with recording what the filter deletes: the compliment you deflected, the answer you knew, the paper that earned praise you refused to absorb.
---

## PIVOT v04
---

---
No one in that lecture hall arrived fully formed. Every student you admire carries a private catalog of doubts. The difference is not confidence. The difference is whether the doubt gets the final vote on your behavior.
---

## PIVOT v05
---

---
Belonging is not a feeling. It is a set of actions: showing up, contributing, revising, returning. Your body will send distress signals in those rooms long after you have earned the right to sit in them. Act first. The feeling recalibrates later.
---

## PIVOT v06
---

---
You built a courtroom in your skull and appointed yourself both defendant and prosecutor. The jury never convenes. The verdict arrives before the evidence. Interrupt the trial by collecting data your prosecutor ignores.
---

## PIVOT v07
---

---
Avoidance feels like protection. It functions as proof. Every office hour you skip, every hand you do not raise, every fellowship you decline becomes a line item on the case against yourself. Action is the only testimony that disrupts the record.
---

## PIVOT v08
---

---
The admissions committee read your file. The professor called on you. The lab accepted your application. These are not accidents. Your nervous system insists they are, because certainty of fraudulence feels safer than the vulnerability of deserving something good.
---

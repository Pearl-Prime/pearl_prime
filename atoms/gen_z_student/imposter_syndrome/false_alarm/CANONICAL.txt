I worked with a sophomore I'll call Priya, pre-med, 3.9 GPA, the kind of transcript advisors put on the department website. She told me the acceptance email to the summer research program arrived on a Tuesday between classes. She read it standing in the hallway outside her organic chemistry lecture. Her hands went cold first, then her stomach dropped, the exact sequence her body used for a pop quiz she hadn't studied for. Not for good news. For good news her body used the bad-news sequence, because some older wiring did not distinguish between "you got in" and "now they will find out you shouldn't have." She put the phone in her pocket and walked into lecture and took notes for fifty minutes without absorbing a single word, and nobody around her could have told the difference.

A junior I'll call Devon told me about the group project where his name got listed first as lead. He said his chest tightened the moment he saw the slide — not because the assignment was hard, but because "lead" meant a spotlight, and a spotlight meant eventually somebody would look closely enough to see the gaps he was sure were there. He described sitting in the library that night re-reading the syllabus for the fourth time, not because he'd forgotten anything, but because rereading felt like the only way to catch the mistake he was certain was coming. There was no mistake. There was a well-prepared student checking work that was already sound, running a threat scan against a threat that didn't exist.

A first-generation student I'll call Marisol told me she kept a running list on her phone of "things I got away with" — a scholarship renewal, a professor's compliment on a paper, a callback for a competitive internship. She wasn't proud of the list. She kept it the way you'd keep evidence, in case someone eventually asked her to account for how she'd ended up somewhere she wasn't supposed to be. The list had eleven items on it by spring break. Eleven separate moments her nervous system had logged as "unexplained," each one small, each one adding weight to a story that had nothing to do with her actual record.

Priya's research advisor asked her a clarifying question in week two of the summer program — a completely ordinary question, the kind advisors ask everyone — and her body reacted like the fire alarm had gone off in the building. Heart rate up, palms wet, a hot flush up the back of her neck, the specific physiology of being caught. She answered the question correctly. She always answered the questions correctly. That was the part that never registered as data: the alarm fired regardless of the actual answer, because the alarm wasn't measuring her competence, it was measuring whether attention was on her. Attention was the trigger. Not performance. The two had gotten fused so early that her body could no longer tell them apart.

Priya's turn came from a strange place: a lab mate, mid-pipetting, said offhand, "wait, you get nervous even when you know the answer?" — not mocking her, just surprised, because to everyone else Priya looked like the calmest person in the room. That single sentence did something a hundred journal-prompt reassurances hadn't. It made the mismatch visible from outside her own head: the alarm and the actual event were two separate things, running on two separate tracks, and only one of them was information anyone else could see. She started, that week, timing the spike — how long between the trigger and the fade — and noticing, with some detachment, that it fell every single time whether or not anything bad happened. The falling was the proof. Nothing had to be fixed. The alarm just had to be outlasted, repeatedly, until her body updated the model.

Devon finally kept an office-hours appointment by making a small, almost embarrassing deal with himself: he would not knock, he would just stand in the doorway until the professor noticed him.

Selin is in her first law school seminar. Everyone else seems to have read cases they weren't assigned. Someone mentions a law review article from last year as though everyone knows it. Selin writes down the citation she didn't recognize. Her classmates sound fluent in a language she is still learning to spell. She checks their bios on the class portal: three did pre-law majors, one worked at a law firm, one has a parent who is a judge. Selin has a literature degree and a lot of determination. She sits with the comparison and it says: they belong here and you are guest. Her pen moves across her notes taking down every word she doesn't already know. Her jaw is set. Her back is perfectly straight. She will learn every word on this list. The list is six words long by the end of class. The class meets twice a week.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02
---
path: story_atoms/gen_z_student/imposter_syndrome/comparison/mechanism_proof/micro/v02.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Arjun's paper gets selected for the undergraduate research journal. He tells his roommate. His roommate says "that's amazing." Arjun says thank you and goes back to his desk and the first thing he does is look up the other selected papers. Their abstracts are tighter than his. Their methodologies are more sophisticated. One author has already presented at a regional conference. Arjun reads his own abstract again. It seems smaller than it did when he submitted it. He tries to hold both things: the selection is real, and the comparison is running anyway. The selection is evidence he belongs. The comparison is evidence of a gap. He sits with the contradiction and his chest tightens. He opens his next paper assignment. He works for two hours. He is not comforted. He is working.

## TURNING_POINT v03
---
path: story_atoms/gen_z_student/imposter_syndrome/comparison/turning_point/micro/v03.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Priya asks a question in her graduate seminar that she has been not-asking for three weeks. The question is: "Can you explain why we're using this methodological framework rather than an alternative?" Her voice is steady. The professor engages with the question for ten minutes. Three students take notes. After class, a classmate says "that was a good question." Priya walks to her car in the parking structure and sits in the driver's seat. She spent three weeks comparing herself to peers who all seemed to already know why, and she was doing that instead of asking. The asking cost one moment of visible not-knowing. The not-asking cost three weeks of compounding confusion. She knows this accounting is real. She also knows the comparison will be back next week with new evidence. Her hands are on the steering wheel. She drives home and writes down the methodological framework she now understands.

## REFLECTION v01
---
family: origin_narrative
voice_mode: direct_address
mechanism_emphasis: attribution_bias
---
You earned your seat. Your brain disagrees. It scans every room for evidence that you slipped through a gap in the system, and it finds that evidence because the search is rigged. Every success gets reclassified as luck. Every struggle gets filed as confirmation.
This is attribution bias operating at full speed. Your stomach drops when the professor praises your work because your internal accountant refuses to credit the deposit. The praise goes into a suspense account labeled "they're being nice." The C+ on a quiz goes straight to the ledger labeled "who you really are."
The fix is not confidence. Confidence is a downstream effect. The fix is auditing the books. Write down one thing you did this week that required skill, not luck. Do not soften it. Do not append a disclaimer. Your nervous system will resist — your chest will tighten, your jaw will clench. That resistance is the mechanism defending itself. Log the entry anyway. Do it again tomorrow. The ledger corrects slowly, one honest line at a time.

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: hypervigilance
voice_mode: observational
mechanism_emphasis: scanning_for_exposure
---
The scanning starts before class. It intensifies during discussion. It peaks in the minutes after you speak, when your brain replays your sentences and tags each one for errors the room definitely noticed. Your neck stiffens. Your face heats. The replay runs on a loop that has no natural endpoint.
This is hypervigilance borrowed from threat detection and aimed at social performance. Your nervous system treats the seminar room like a minefield. Every comment is a step. Every silence from classmates is a detonation you have not yet identified.
The scan itself is the problem, not what it finds. Thirty seconds of exposure in discussion does not require forty minutes of post-mortem analysis. The cost of the scan exceeds the cost of the error it hunts. Notice the moment the replay begins — that tightness in your throat, that heat behind your eyes. Label it: "The scan started." You do not need to stop it. You need to stop treating its output as court-admissible evidence.

## REFLECTION v03
---
family: perfectionism_paralysis
voice_mode: direct_address
mechanism_emphasis: all_or-nothing_standards
---
You rewrite the sentence because it sounds too smart for someone like you. Then you rewrite it because it sounds too simple. Your fingers hover. Your shoulders lock. The cursor blinks on a page that never accumulates enough words because every draft gets tried for fraud before it reaches the end of a paragraph.
This is perfectionism wearing imposter syndrome's mask. The standard you set is not high — it is impossible. You demand prose that simultaneously proves your intelligence and conceals any ambition that someone smarter might find laughable. No sentence survives that filter.
Lower the bar to "submitted." A completed assignment that earns a B teaches you more about your capacity than a pristine outline that never becomes a paper. Your hands will shake when you hit submit on work that feels imperfect. Let them shake. The tremor is your nervous system adjusting to a world where you produce things instead of rehearsing them.

## REFLECTION v04
---
family: social_comparison
voice_mode: observational
mechanism_emphasis: comparative_anchoring
---
Marcus raises his hand without hesitation. Priya speaks in full paragraphs. Tomás references papers you have not read. Your brain builds a composite from their best moments and measures your interior against that highlight reel. The comparison is structurally unfair. You already know this. Knowing does not stop the mechanism.
Comparative anchoring works because it feels like objectivity. You believe you are simply observing reality: they perform, you pretend. But you are selecting data. You did not see Marcus delete his answer twice before raising his hand. You did not see Priya's hands trembling under the desk. You saw finished products and compared them to your rough draft.
The intervention is not to stop comparing. That instruction fails every time. The intervention is to compare accurately. Track your own performance the way you track theirs — with the same generous eye, the same benefit of the doubt. Your ribs will loosen when the comparison includes your full record instead of your worst moments.

## REFLECTION v05
---
family: emotional_suppression
voice_mode: direct_address
mechanism_emphasis: masking_distress
---
You smiled when the committee approved your proposal. Your knuckles turned white on the podium. You texted a thumbs-up when your girlfriend congratulated you. Your chest felt hollow for the rest of the night. The mask works. It works so well that the people who care about you stop asking what is underneath.
Suppression is the imposter's most expensive tool. It protects the facade by severing the connection between your experience and the people who witness your achievements. Every deflected compliment, every shrugged-off praise, every "it was nothing" erodes the bridge between you and the humans who see you clearly.
The practice is small and uncomfortable: when someone offers recognition, hold still. Do not laugh it off. Do not redirect. Let your chest tighten. Let the discomfort land. Say "thank you" and stop talking. Two words. The silence afterward will feel unbearable. It is also the first moment the mask drops, and the person across from you gets to see the real reaction. That visibility is the antidote suppression prevents.

## REFLECTION v06
---
family: opportunity_avoidance
voice_mode: observational
mechanism_emphasis: preemptive_withdrawal
---
The fellowship application sits open in a browser tab for four days. The cursor blinks on the first prompt. Your hands go cold every time you read the word "qualifications." On day five you close the tab and tell your advisor the program does not fit your interests. Your jaw sets. Your fists press into your pockets. The relief arrives instantly.
Avoidance masquerades as preference. It rebrands fear as discernment. "I chose not to apply" sounds autonomous. "I could not tolerate the possibility of being seen as unqualified" does not. But the second sentence is the honest one, and your body knows it — that is the stone in your sternum, the tightness that does not dissolve after the tab closes.
Every declined opportunity feeds the case file your brain builds against you. The logic is circular: you avoid the thing that terrifies you, then cite your empty resume as evidence you do not deserve it. Break the loop at the action step. Fill in one field. Save the draft. Return tomorrow. Your hands will stay cold. Fill in the next field anyway. The application does not need warm hands. It needs a submitted form.

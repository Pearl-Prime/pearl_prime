
## SCENE v01
---

---
The crisis text arrives at midnight and your chest goes flat before you finish reading it. You care about this person. You know you do. You also know your tank has been empty for two weeks. You type back something warm and true and your hands are steady and your face in the laptop screen looks like a person who is present. The ache in your sternum is invisible. No one on the other end of the chat sees what it costs.

You sit in the weekly check-in with your mentee and your phone buzzes twice with messages from two other people who need something. You silence the phone. Your mentee is talking about housing stress. You nod and ask a follow-up question and somewhere in your chest the warmth has been replaced by a dull pressure. You have been here so many times this semester that the shape of the conversation feels like a template. You follow the template. Your mentee feels heard. You feel hollow.

The RA on-call shift ends at 6 a.m. Four resident knocks. Two actual crises. Six hours of performing calm. You sit in the quiet of the duty room with a cold cup of coffee and your shoulders won't come down from where they've been lodged near your ears since 11 p.m. You have your own paper due at noon. You have the kind of tiredness that sleep doesn't touch.

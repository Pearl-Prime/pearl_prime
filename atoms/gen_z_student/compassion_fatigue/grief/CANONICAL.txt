Nadia used to be the person in her friend group who could hold a crisis for someone else without it depleting her. She answered late-night calls, sat on dorm floors while people cried, texted back immediately when someone said they were not okay. She was good at this. She doesn't know when it changed. Now her phone buzzes with a message from a friend who is struggling and Nadia reads it and feels something that doesn't resemble care—more like a flat exhaustion, a low-grade dread, a wish that the problem could belong to someone else tonight. She hates this about herself. The hate is the grief: something she valued about who she was is no longer reliably present. She types a caring response anyway. The response is accurate. The warmth behind it is borrowed from a reserve that isn't there anymore.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02

COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Luz sits across from her first-year mentee who is describing housing anxiety and financial stress and a family situation at home. Luz nods. She asks follow-up questions. She is doing the job she signed up for. Her chest is perfectly flat. She cares about this person in abstract—she knows this in the same way she knows facts about history—but the warmth that used to accompany her caring has gone somewhere she can't reach. After the meeting, she sits in the hallway and feels disgusted with herself. She became an orientation leader because she wanted to be the kind of person who shows up for others. She cannot tell if she is still that person or if she has been performing it for so long that the distinction has collapsed. Her mentee looked at her with trust that Luz may not have earned.

## TURNING_POINT v03
---

BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Kofi cancels his shift at the campus food bank for the third time this semester. He lies in bed after sending the cancellation text and the grief hits him cleanly, without defense. He joined this organization his first week on campus because he wanted to matter outside his own problems. He showed up every weekend for two semesters. Now he cancels. He is not sure if this is moral failure or physical depletion. He suspects it is both, that they are not separate, that running empty for long enough makes depletion feel like failure and failure feel like character. He sits up in bed. He does not reschedule. He opens the window and lets the cold air in and holds his face in it for a minute. The grief doesn't go anywhere. He accepts that he can't give what he doesn't have, and the acceptance costs him the identity of someone who could always give anyway.

Simone does not remember the last time someone asked how she was and actually waited for the answer. She is the one who waits. She is the one in the group chat who notices when someone goes quiet for two days and sends the check-in text that nobody else thinks to send.

It is 12:47 a.m. and her roommate Destiny is crying again. Not the sharp kind of crying — the long, low, exhausted kind that comes from a semester that has been one bad thing after another. Simone sits cross-legged on Destiny's bed with her back against the wall and her face arranged in the expression that says I am here, I am not going anywhere, you are safe to fall apart.

Her jaw aches. She has been holding this particular expression for four months. The muscles on both sides of her face are tight from the sustained performance of calm. She looks like she is paying full attention. She is paying full attention. And something underneath the attention is sending a signal her mind will not stop to name.

Later, lying in her own bed, she stares at the water stain on the ceiling and waits for sleep. It does not come. Her heart beats fast without cause. Her thoughts cycle through the things Destiny said and the things she didn't say and whether tomorrow will be better and what she should text in the morning and whether the text will be enough.

She does not notice, in this moment, that she has spent the last three hours holding someone else's pain so carefully that she forgot to put hers down somewhere accessible. It is still in her chest, compacted. She fell asleep thinking about Destiny. She woke thinking about Destiny. At some point the boundary between care and absorption dissolved so quietly she missed the crossing.

She gets up. She makes coffee. She texts the check-in text. She is exhausted in a way that nine hours of sleep will not resolve. Her body is trying to tell her something. It has been trying for weeks. She does not yet have a word for what it is saying.

Diego is the one who picks up. Every time. His friends know this about him the way they know his class schedule — as background fact, unexamined.

The call from Mateo comes at 11 p.m. on a Thursday. Mateo's voice is tight and fast, talking about the breakup, talking about his grade on the midterm, talking about calling his mom and what she said and how it made it worse. Diego sits at his desk with his stats homework open in front of him and walks Mateo through the same reassurances he offered two weeks ago about different content.

He is good at this. He has always been good at this. He knows when to ask a question and when to let silence do the work. He knows the tone that says you are heard without saying you need to stop talking now. He learned it early, in a house where someone always needed managing.

He finishes the call at 12:20 a.m. and looks at the stats homework. The motivation he had at 9 p.m. is gone. He submits what he has, which is not enough, and tells himself he will make up the points on the next assignment.

He does not register anything as wrong. He is just tired. He is always tired. Mateo is fine now, which means the call was worth it. Diego turns off the desk lamp and lies on his bed with his clothes on.

Three months later, Diego turns off notifications for a week and feels his shoulders descend from somewhere near his ears to their actual anatomical position. He does not connect this to the phone calls. He thinks he just needed a break from his phone. He is not wrong. He is also not seeing the full picture.

Amara has a name for it now. She learned it in a women's health course she took to fill a distribution requirement — compassion fatigue — and she sat in the lecture with a stillness that the student next to her might have read as boredom. It was recognition.

She had been the emotional center of her friend group for two years. She was twenty. She had held her roommate through a sexual assault disclosure, her best friend through a suicide scare, her home friend through a parent's cancer diagnosis, all while finishing her junior year of a biochemistry degree. She did not think of this as extraordinary. She thought of it as what you do for people you love.

The recognition came with a specific physical sensation — a loosening in her chest, like a knot untying one strand. The word fit what she had been feeling: not depressed exactly, not burned out from academics exactly, but emptied in the specific way of someone who has been pouring without refilling for long enough that the bottom of the vessel is visible.

After class she sat outside on a concrete bench and looked at the campus around her. Students walked to class. The sun was low and made long shadows across the path. She felt, for the first time in months, that something had been named and therefore could be addressed. Named things have edges. Things with edges can be handled.

She made a counseling appointment. She told the counselor the same thing she told her friends, but in reverse — she spoke and someone listened and she did not monitor anyone else's reaction while she spoke. The unfamiliarity of the dynamic made her pause twice. She had forgotten this direction of care existed.

Recovery took the rest of that semester. She gave less in some friendships. The friendships survived. Some of them deepened, because honesty creates more intimacy than accommodation. She is not done with the work. But she is no longer empty and mistaking it for fine.

The dorm suite has five people. Yuki is the one with the tissues. Literally — she keeps two boxes in her room and has offered them to four of the five suite-mates at some point this semester. She does not track this as a pattern. She just notices that people come to her.

She is funny about it with herself, privately. "Yuki's office is open," she thinks, when she hears the familiar knock after midnight. She does not tell anyone about the joke because that would sound like a complaint, and she is not complaining. She cares about these people. She chose this suite. She chose the people in it, more or less, and they are going through real things.

What she does not tell anyone: she cannot watch a movie anymore without scanning it for emotional content that might upset her. Her nervous system has been running a threat-assessment protocol for so long that she now applies it to fiction. Sad scene incoming — her body registers it before the scene lands, bracing.

She has been waking up at 4 a.m. for six weeks. Just waking up, not from a nightmare, just awake in the dark with her heart running fast and nothing specific attached to the feeling. She gets up and drinks water and sits on the floor of the common room where it is quiet.

She does not tell the suite about this. She is aware of the irony — she knows the interior lives of four people better than her own, and she cannot answer the basic question of how she is without pulling up a blank page.

The night she tells her campus counselor about the 4 a.m. wakings, she cries for twelve minutes straight — not about any of her friends, about herself, which feels strangely unfamiliar and also exactly right.

Theo scrolls back through his texts on a Sunday afternoon when nothing is urgent. He is looking for something — a conversation about his own life, a thread where someone asked him how midterms went, a check-in that moved in his direction for once.

He scrolls for a while. He finds plenty — long, winding threads where he asked the questions, followed up, pushed for more, held the story. He is a good listener. His thumbs remember the phrases: "that makes sense," "you're not overreacting," "do you need anything from me right now."

He does not find what he is looking for. He is not sure that surprises him.

He puts the phone down and looks at his ceiling. His chest feels like something heavy is resting on it — not painful, just present, a weight that arrived so gradually he cannot name the date it settled in. He thinks it might be loneliness, but that doesn't quite fit. He is surrounded by people. He knows things about them no one else knows.

Loneliness is the wrong word. What is closer to the truth is something like: he has been in every story as the witness and in none of the stories as the subject. He has presence everywhere and interiority nowhere. He is the room other people's feelings happen in.

He is twenty years old. He has not yet found the language for this. But on a Sunday afternoon when nothing is urgent, he feels the contour of something true about his life and sits with it quietly for a while before the next text arrives.

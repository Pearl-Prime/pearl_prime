Selin has answered seventeen texts from three different friends since Thursday and it is Sunday morning and she has not started her political theory essay. She sits at her desk looking at the blank document and notices that she is slightly resentful of the seventeen texts—not of the people, who are struggling with real things, but of the weight of the caring itself. The resentment frightens her. She has always measured herself partly by her availability to others. The frightened feeling sits in her chest alongside the resentment alongside the essay that hasn't started. She opens the document and types a sentence. The sentence is technically complete but she knows it is hollow—her brain is still tracking three separate people's problems while trying to form an argument about Rousseau. She deletes the sentence. She has capacity for exactly one thing right now and she doesn't know which one it is.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02

COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Marcus is on the phone with his younger brother for the second time this week. The brother is having a hard semester at his own school. Marcus listens. He asks questions. He offers what he can. After forty minutes, they hang up. Marcus sits on the edge of his bed with his hands loose in his lap. He has his own hard semester happening right now—three incomplete readings, a financial aid form he keeps not submitting, a group project partner who has gone silent. He has been holding space for his brother's hard semester while standing in his own. His chest is full of something that feels less like love right now and more like depletion. He knows these are not opposites. He also knows he won't not answer next time his brother calls. That's the cost: there is no exit from caring when you care, and caring when you are empty changes what caring feels like.

## TURNING_POINT v03

MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Remy tells his best friend he needs two days where he doesn't respond to group chat. He has been the emotional center of a friend group through a difficult month and his own nervous system is running on empty. His friend says "of course, take care of yourself." Remy puts his phone in his desk drawer and goes to the library alone. He studies for three hours. His chest doesn't loosen the way he expected. The break from the group chat is necessary and correct and his body doesn't feel the correctness. He feels the absence—all the threads he is used to holding, now untended—and there is a low-grade guilt running underneath the quiet. He turns back to his notes. The guilt is not a verdict. He finishes the chapter. His hands are steadier than they were when he sat down, which is a small thing but a true one, and today a true thing is enough.

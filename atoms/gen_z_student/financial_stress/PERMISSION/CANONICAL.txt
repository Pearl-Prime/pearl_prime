## PERMISSION v01
---

---
You have permission to name the housing insecurity out loud to someone who can help. Press your feet into the floor. Feel the ground. The fact that you might not have the same roof next month is not a shameful secret. It is a material crisis. The financial aid office has emergency funds. The words you need to say are: "I have a housing situation and I need to know what options exist."

You have permission to eat the free food at the campus event even if you are not there for the event. Fill the plate. Your body needs the calories regardless of what you came for. Food insecurity is a campus reality that universities acknowledge with food pantries and dining hall surplus programs and late-night free meals. You are not stealing. You are using infrastructure built for exactly this.

You have permission to call home and ask for money. Feel the knot in your stomach before you dial. The knot is made of love and pride and not wanting to be a burden. It is also between you and a resource that exists. The call costs you something. Hunger costs you more. Dial.

You have permission to feel the full weight of what you are managing. You are paying rent, eating within a number that does not allow for fullness every day, working hours that pull from study time, and showing up to class and performing normalcy in an environment that assumes a baseline you are below. That is hard. It is allowed to be hard.

You have permission to skip the textbook purchase and tell the professor why. Drop your shoulders. Feel the resistance in your neck. "The book is not in my budget this semester — is there a library copy or a loaner I can use?" is a sentence that professors hear more often than they tell you. Most have an answer. The answer exists. The sentence just has to leave your mouth.

You have permission to feel embarrassed about asking for help and ask anyway. The embarrassment is not information about whether to ask. It is information about the stigma your environment has deposited around financial need. The embarrassment and the request can coexist. Walk to the financial aid office embarrassed. Ask the embarrassing question. The answer is for you, not for anyone watching.

## PERMISSION v02
---

---
You have permission to eat less than you want and also tell someone that is happening. Both. Not either-or. Eating less is the adaptation you are making right now. Telling someone is how the adaptation becomes temporary instead of permanent. The food pantry is open three days a week. The emergency fund application takes twenty minutes. Both require disclosing need to someone. You have permission to make that disclosure.

You have permission to be angry that a textbook costs more than your weekly food budget. Press your palms against your thighs. Feel the heat. The rage at the price structure of academic publishing is proportionate and correct. It should cost less. It does not cost less because of a system with no obligation to your food budget. Your anger is aimed at the right target.

You have permission to accept the free housing offer, the couch for a month, the double in the room that was supposed to be a single. Housing insecurity is not solved by pride. It is solved by accepting the available option while you work toward the stable one. Your dignity survives the couch. It does not require pretending the situation is fine.

You have permission to feel ashamed and apply for the emergency grant anyway. The shame is familiar and it has been used to keep you from resources for too long. Touch your sternum. Feel what is underneath the shame. Underneath it is a need that is real and that you did not create and that you deserve to have addressed. The grant exists for this exact situation. Apply.

You have permission to tell your academic advisor that your financial situation is affecting your coursework. Your advisor cannot solve the problem. They can connect you to people who have tools. They can note the context in your file. They can adjust expectations around deliverables when the situation is documented. They need to know. You need to tell them.

You have permission to reduce your course load for one semester to make the math work. Feel the resistance. Feel the way this sounds like falling behind. It does not have to be falling behind. It can be enrolling in what you can actually pay for and attend to. Finishing in five years while eating regularly is not failure. It is sustainable.

## PERMISSION v03
---

---
You have permission to feel the specific grief of financial stress in college. The version of college you imagined did not include choosing between a textbook and groceries. The gap between the imagined version and the actual version is a real loss. You are allowed to mourn it. The mourning does not mean you are doing it wrong. It means you are honest about what this costs.

You have permission to stop pretending your situation is fine to people who are not fine either. There are other students in this building who are managing what you are managing. The silence between you is not evidence that you are the exception. It is a shared social performance that nobody asked you to maintain and that nobody benefits from.

You have permission to use the full name of what is happening. Not "I'm a little tight this month" — housing insecurity, food insecurity, loan default risk. The precise language gets precise help. The softened language gets sympathetic looks and nothing actionable. Your situation deserves the exact words for it.

You have permission to receive without reciprocating immediately. When a friend covers a meal, when a classmate shares their dining swipes, when a professor waives a fee — you do not have to balance the ledger this week. Receiving is allowed to be asymmetrical during a crisis period. The reciprocity comes in a different season. Let this be the season of receiving.

You have permission to hold both things: this is hard and you will get through it. Neither cancels the other. The hardness is real and does not make the through-it impossible. The through-it is coming and does not require pretending the hard isn't here. Both are true simultaneously. Your chest can hold both.

You have permission to be scared about money without making it mean something permanent about your future. The scar of financial precarity in college is real. It is also not an indicator of what your financial life looks like at thirty-five. The numbers you are managing now are not the numbers that define you. They are the constraints of this particular season. Seasons change.

The rent is due on the first. It is the twenty-eighth. Camila has forty-three dollars in checking and she is doing the math for the fourth time today, as though it might change.

She knows the numbers. Forty-three dollars. Rent is her portion: three-eighty. Deposit already paid. The gap is three hundred and thirty-seven dollars and she has three days to close it or start a conversation she does not know how to begin with her roommate, who is from a family that would not understand the gap as a gap at all.

She calls her mom after her 2 p.m. class. Her mom picks up on the second ring. Camila says "I need to talk to you about something" and her voice does not shake until the third sentence. Her mom says "how much" before Camila finishes. The number comes out and lands in the silence. Her mom says "okay, I can do half." Half is not three hundred and thirty-seven. Her mom already knows that.

They talk for twenty minutes about nothing they are actually talking about. Camila says she is fine three times. Her mom says "I know you are" and it sounds like a prayer rather than a fact.

After the call Camila sits on the bench outside the library and looks at the campus — the brick buildings, the manicured lawn, the students moving between them with backpacks and coffees and lives that from the outside all look like the same level of okay. Her body is heavy and her stomach is empty because she skipped lunch.

She applies for the emergency fund that night. She has been looking at the form for six weeks. The form takes nineteen minutes. She hits submit and feels simultaneously relieved and exposed and furious that relief required this level of performance of her need. She sleeps six hours. She pays rent on the first.

The bio professor assigns a textbook that costs two hundred and forty dollars new, one hundred and sixty used, and "not available in the library system." Owen checks the library anyway, twice. He finds a two-edition-old copy that differs in chapter order and some updated research. He uses it.

He does not tell the professor. He does not tell his lab partner, who already bought the new edition and highlights sections in yellow and offers to share. He says "I've got mine" and keeps using the old edition. He is forty pages behind the class pagination by week three.

His lab partner Elena is kind without being intrusive. She does not ask why Owen always shows up to study sessions with a different book. She does ask, once, whether he wants the PDF she found online, and the way she asks it — without ceremony, without eye contact — tells him she knows something. He says yes.

He opens the PDF that night on his laptop and feels the tightness in his sternum release by one notch. The material is the same as the new edition. His grade does not suffer. The two hundred and forty dollars stays in his account where it has to stay.

He does not ever tell anyone that the textbook cost was a problem. He finds this fact, at the end of the semester, slightly ridiculous — that he spent four months in a mild state of low-level stress about a PDF that a classmate sent him for free in the third week. The cost of pride, he thinks. The cost of pride.

Jade figured out, gradually, that the campus assumed things about her that were not true. It assumed she had a laptop charger she could afford to replace. It assumed she had a credit card for the study abroad deposit. It assumed she had parents who could be emergency contacts in a financial sense, not just a logistical one.

She was a first-generation student at a school that used the phrase "first-generation student" in its mission statement and had a two-hundred-dollar orientation fee.

She navigated the gap for two years the way she had learned to navigate gaps since childhood: quietly, resourcefully, without letting it show. She knew the name of every scholarship with a small applicant pool. She knew which professors kept office hours on Thursdays and which would accept a late assignment with an honest explanation. She knew the food pantry hours and she used them and she felt, each time she used them, a flicker of something she identified as shame and then reidentified, over time, as evidence that the institution's indifference was leaving a mark.

The reidentification happened during a financial coaching session she attended on a roommate's suggestion. The coach asked her to describe her relationship to money. She said "vigilant." The coach wrote it down. They talked for an hour. At the end the coach said "you have been managing a financial situation that most of your peers have never encountered, and you've done it well." Jade said "not always." The coach said "well enough to stay enrolled. That counts."

She finished her degree. She graduated with debt and a 3.7 GPA and a skill set that included not just her major but the harder-to-name curriculum: knowing how to ask for what you need with precision, how to adapt without apology, how to hold difficulty without letting it define your ceiling. She does not recommend the difficulty. She acknowledges, carefully and without sentimentality, that she is different because of it.

The group gets Thai food on Thursday nights. Isaiah has been to three of the last twelve Thursdays. He rotates his excuses: headache, early class, tired. They are all variations on the same truth, which is that the cheapest item on the menu is fourteen dollars and he cannot spend fourteen dollars on dinner when the month is tight.

He likes these people. He chose to live with them because they felt easy, and they are easy, which makes the performance of financial normalcy around them both low-stakes and exhausting. They have never once said anything about money that indicated awareness that it might not be the same for everyone. The assumption is background radiation — present everywhere, acknowledged nowhere.

He considered telling one of them. Considered it specifically in October, when the month was tightest and he skipped two Thursdays in a row and one of them asked if he was doing okay. He said yes. He was not okay. He was managing — not in crisis, but managing in the specific way of someone for whom the baseline is already compressed.

The following Thursday he went. He ordered the cheapest thing and split a dessert with someone and laughed at a joke and felt, for two hours, like the version of himself who was not doing math in the background of every social experience. The meal cost thirteen dollars. He tracked it against his remaining budget. It was worth it. He decided it was worth it.

He did not tell any of them. He tells himself he will, someday, when the situation is past tense and less exposing. He may be right. He is twenty years old and managing something significant and choosing his moments carefully. That is not weakness. It is self-knowledge.

The dining hall swipe runs out on the 20th. Mia has seven days left in the month and twelve dollars in cash and the knowledge that her mother cannot send anything until the second. She has been here before. Her body remembers it before her mind does — the low-grade headache, the lighter sleep, the way the day gets longer when you are managing hunger on top of everything else.

She goes to the campus food pantry for the first time. She has known about it for two semesters. The door is unmarked except for the room number. Inside: a student worker, a table with dry goods, a refrigerator. No one looks up when she comes in.

She takes two cans, a box of crackers, a bag of rice, and a packet of instant oatmeal. She carries the bag out in her backpack and goes to her next class. The class is on global food systems. The professor talks about food deserts and structural poverty. Mia takes notes and thinks about the weight of the bag in her backpack and does not say anything.

After class she cooks the rice in the communal kitchen and eats it standing up at the counter because her roommate is at the library and the kitchen is empty. The rice is plain. It fills the hollow in her stomach. Her concentration returns.

She goes to the food pantry two more times that month. By the third time the shame is smaller. Not gone — smaller. The student worker who checks people in learns her name. Learning her name makes it easier. She does not know why. Something about being seen as a person rather than a need. The distinction is small and it matters entirely.

## REFLECTION v01
---

---
Raise your hand right now. Not in a classroom. Just here. Six inches above your thigh. Notice the shoulder. It tenses. The trapezius muscle fires a signal of effort even though the hand weighs almost nothing. Your body treats visibility as load-bearing work.
Every time you stayed silent in class, your shoulder made a deal with your hand: stay down, stay safe. The shoulder absorbed the tension of the words you swallowed. It contracted so the hand would not rise. This was protection. It was also expensive. Your shoulder has been paying the bill for semesters of silence.
Hold the hand up for ten more seconds. The shoulder burns. Good. That burn is the cost of being seen — measured in muscle, not metaphor. The hand in the air proves nothing to a professor. It proves something to the shoulder: the body can hold the weight of visibility and continue to function.
Lower the hand slowly. Feel the shoulder release. That release is what happens after courage. Not during. After. The body braces, the hand rises, and then — only then — the shoulder gets to rest.

## REFLECTION v02
---

---
Say a word. Out loud. Right now. Not a sentence. One word. Notice where it begins. The chest pushes air. The throat shapes it. The mouth releases it. Three body parts conspiring to make you audible.
Every time you chose silence in a room full of people, those three systems stood ready and received a cancellation order. The chest held its breath. The throat narrowed. The mouth pressed shut. Your body ran a dress rehearsal for speaking and cancelled the show before opening night.
Speaking in class, in meetings, in conversations that matter — the act begins in the chest. The lungs fill. The diaphragm drops. Air moves upward. Your throat opens or it does not. Courage lives in the half-second between the chest pushing air and the throat deciding to let it pass.
Say another word. Louder. Feel the chest commit. Feel the throat open. The sound that crosses your lips is proof that the three systems still function. Silence was a choice. The architecture for sound never dismantled itself. It waited.

## REFLECTION v03
---

---
Stand up. Feel the transfer — from sitting to standing. Your legs accept your full weight. Your stomach shifts as your center of gravity changes. This is what courage feels like in the body: a transfer of weight from the known position to the exposed one.
Every time you stayed seated when you wanted to speak, your legs kept your weight in the chair. The chair felt safe. The standing position felt like a spotlight with a heartbeat. Your stomach voted to stay put because vertical meant visible.
Standing now, notice your legs holding you. They bear load without complaint. Your stomach adjusts to the new altitude. The body adapted in less than a second. The dread lasted longer than the actual transition.
Courage is not the absence of the stomach drop. It is the willingness to stand up while the stomach still falls. Your legs did not wait for your gut to settle. They extended. They bore weight. The stomach caught up three seconds later. The body figures it out in motion, never in theory.

## REFLECTION v04
---

---
Press your palms together. Push. Feel the resistance — left hand pressing right, right hand pressing back. Equal force. Neither hand wins. This is what courage feels like before it moves: two forces locked in opposition. The desire to act presses forward. The fear of exposure presses back.
Your face knows this pressure. It holds neutral in classrooms when your brain screams to react. It smiles at networking events while your hands sweat through a handshake. The face manages a public campaign while the hands run a private one.
Release the palms. Feel the heat between them. That warmth came from friction — two surfaces working against each other. Courage generates the same heat. The act of pushing through fear produces energy the body registers as temperature change. Your face flushes before a presentation. Your hands warm before a difficult conversation. The heat is not malfunction. It is friction.
The next time your face burns and your hands heat up before something that matters, know the warmth by its real name: two forces meeting, and one of them choosing forward.

## PERMISSION v01
---

---
You have permission to raise your hand without a perfect answer. Lift your arm now. Feel the shoulder joint rotate. Your hand in the air signals willingness, not mastery. The classroom belongs to the curious, not just the certain.

You have permission to walk into a room where you know no one. Place your hand on the doorframe. Feel the wood. Your body enters rooms the same way regardless of who occupies them. One foot, then the other. The mechanics of arrival do not require a social invitation.

You have permission to change your major, your path, your mind. Press your palms flat on your desk. Feel the surface that held your old plans. The desk does not care what you study. It holds whatever you place on it. So does your future.

You have permission to tell someone "that hurt" without softening the sentence. Touch your sternum. Feel the bone. Your pain is not a negotiation. Reporting it accurately costs less than burying it permanently. Let the sentence stand without a disclaimer.

You have permission to try and fail publicly. Grip the edge of your chair. Feel the frame hold steady. Public failure embarrasses. Private stagnation erodes. Embarrassment fades in days. Erosion takes years. Choose the shorter pain.

You have permission to be the only one who thinks differently in the discussion. Press your feet hard into the floor. Feel the ground hold. Consensus is comfortable. Dissent is generative. Your singular perspective adds a dimension the room lacks. Occupy that dimension.

## PERMISSION v02
---

---
You have permission to ask the professor a question after class instead of in front of everyone. Pack your bag slowly. Walk to the lectern. The private question is still a question. The audience was never the point. Understanding is.

You have permission to apply for the scholarship even if you do not feel qualified. Open the application. Type your name. The committee decides who is qualified. Your job is to give them something to read. Self-disqualification is silent withdrawal.

You have permission to introduce yourself to the person you have been meaning to talk to. Walk over. Extend your hand. The introduction takes nine seconds. The avoidance has cost you weeks. Spend the nine.

You have permission to email a researcher whose work you read. Open your inbox. Type two sentences about the paper. Researchers want their work read. The email costs them nothing to ignore. They often reply.

You have permission to disagree with the TA in front of the class. Raise your hand. Speak. Your reading of the text is data. Multiple readings sharpen the discussion. The TA is paid to hear you, not to be unchallenged.

You have permission to ask for feedback on a draft before it is graded. Hand the paper to the professor in office hours. Watch them flip it open. The grade is not the goal. The improvement is. Feedback before the grade is how the improvement happens.

## PERMISSION v03
---

---
You have permission to tell your friend you have been hurt by what they said. Sit across from them. Press your palms together. The feedback feels confrontational. The unspoken hurt would have ended the friendship slowly. The conversation ends it slowly only if you are wrong. Find out.

You have permission to admit you do not know what you want to do after graduation. Lean back in the chair. Feel the support. The career advisor cannot help you find a path you have already pretended to be on. Drop the pretend. The advisor's job starts at "I do not know."

You have permission to ask someone out who probably will say no. Walk up. Speak the question. The data point of "no" is information you can use. The data point of "never asked" is the one you carry forever.

You have permission to tell your family you do not want their version of the future for you. Stand at the kitchen counter. Feel the edge under your hands. The conversation is harder than the silent compliance. The compliance was hardening into resentment. Speak before the resentment becomes unrecoverable.

You have permission to sit with someone whose grief makes you uncomfortable. Pull a chair next to theirs. Place your hand near theirs without touching. The discomfort is not damage. It is the cost of being a person whose presence matters. Pay the cost.

You have permission to walk into the office hours of the professor whose class is destroying you. Knock. Step inside. The professor has heard versions of this conversation every semester. Yours is not the worst one they have heard this week. Speak.

## STORY v01

Kai sat in the back row of Political Theory for eleven weeks. Knew the material. Read every assignment twice. Highlighted passages in three colors. Participation grade: zero. Not because Kai lacked opinions. Because the hand refused to rise.
It started freshman year. A wrong answer in Intro to Sociology. The professor corrected him in front of ninety people. His chest burned for three hours. After that, his hand developed its own policy: stay down, stay safe, stay invisible.
By junior year, the pattern had its own architecture. Kai's brain would assemble a response. His arm would tense at the shoulder. Then the chest wall would send a veto — a sharp contraction behind the sternum that pinned the hand to the desk. The whole sequence took two seconds. Nobody saw it. Everyone saw the silence.
Week twelve. The professor asked about democratic backsliding. Kai's hand moved before the chest could intervene. His arm reached vertical. His fingertips opened toward the ceiling. The professor called his name. Kai's chest detonated — heat flooding his ribs, pulse slamming against his collarbone.
He spoke. Six sentences. Voice tight but audible. The words left his mouth and did not destroy him. The professor nodded. A student across the aisle looked over. Not mocking. Just noticing.
Kai's hand shook when he lowered it. His chest still burned. But the burn carried a different signature — not the scorch of shame. The heat of a muscle used for the first time in two years. He raised his hand again the following Thursday. The chest still protested. The hand overruled.

## STORY v02
---

---
Dani avoided the counseling center for three semesters. Her roommate went. Her RA recommended it. The building sat next to the library — she passed it every day. Her legs would accelerate past the entrance. Her stomach would tighten at the sign that read "Student Wellness."
The avoidance had logic. Entering meant admitting the anxiety was real. Her legs understood this before her brain did. They rerouted automatically — adding thirty seconds to the library walk to bypass the door entirely. The stomach confirmed the decision each time with a clench of agreement.
November of sophomore year. A panic attack in the library bathroom. Hands gripping the sink. Legs buckling. Stomach in revolt. Dani sat on the tile floor and stared at the wall until her breathing returned to something her body could manage.
The next morning, she walked toward the counseling center. Her legs slowed at the usual point. Her stomach performed its contraction. She stopped. Stood on the sidewalk for forty-five seconds. Then her left foot crossed the threshold, and her right foot followed.
The intake form took nine minutes. Her stomach churned through every checkbox. Her legs bounced under the chair. The receptionist handed her a tissue she did not yet need.
She sat in the waiting room. Her legs stopped bouncing. Her stomach released one degree. The courage was not in the conversation that followed. It lived in the forty-five seconds on the sidewalk — the pause between the body's learned route and the new direction her feet chose. Her legs remembered that choice for the rest of the semester.

## STORY v03
---

---
Sena sat in the group project meeting and said nothing for forty minutes. Four teammates debated the presentation structure. Sena saw the flaw in the argument — a missing data point that would collapse the thesis. Her throat held the correction like a splinter lodged sideways.
She had learned silence early. High school debate team. A judge told her she spoke "too aggressively." Her jaw snapped shut that afternoon and developed a default setting: locked. College reinforced it. Group settings triggered the lock. Her throat narrowed. Her jaw set like concrete. The words existed, fully formed, behind her teeth. They stayed there.
Forty-two minutes into the meeting, the teammate presenting the data said something factually wrong. Sena's jaw unclenched on its own — a physical reflex, not a decision. The words fell out: "That number is from the 2019 dataset. The current figure reverses the trend."
The room went quiet. Sena's throat constricted. Her jaw tried to re-engage the lock. But the teammate pulled up the source, checked, and nodded. "Good catch."
Two words. Her throat released. Her jaw softened at the hinge. The correction did not land as aggression. It landed as competence.
Sena spoke four more times in the remaining hour. Each time, her throat tightened first — the old gate checking credentials. Each time, the words passed through anyway. The jaw never fully relaxed. But it moved from locked to guarded, and guarded was enough. She left the meeting with her throat aching. The ache felt different from silence. It felt like use.

## STORY v04
---

---
Marco wrote the email to his academic advisor seventeen times over three weeks. Each draft lived in his drafts folder. Each one explained that his father's illness made the semester impossible. Each one asked for help. His hands typed and deleted, typed and deleted. His face flushed every time he re-read his own words.
The shame lived in his hands. They froze over the send button the same way they froze over exam questions — not from ignorance, but from exposure. Sending the email meant a person with authority would read his private collapse. His face burned at the thought. His hands pulled back from the keyboard.
Week fourteen. Three missed assignments. GPA in freefall. Marco opened draft number seventeen. His hands trembled over the trackpad. He read the email once. Moved the cursor to send. His face heated to a color he could feel without a mirror.
He clicked.
The confirmation banner appeared: "Your message has been sent." His hands went cold. His face stayed hot. He closed the laptop and pressed his palms against his cheeks — holding the heat, containing the exposure.
The advisor replied in two hours. No judgment. A plan. Incomplete grades. Extended deadlines. A path through the wreckage that Marco's hands had been too paralyzed to build alone.
Marco saved the reply in a folder labeled "proof." Not proof of accommodation. Proof that his hands could send the hardest email of his life and the screen did not shatter. His face cooled by evening. His hands stopped shaking by the next morning.

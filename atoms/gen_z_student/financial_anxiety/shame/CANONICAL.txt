Luz buys the cheap store-brand granola bars from the grocery store and hides them in her backpack when she eats in the library. She knows no one is watching her eat. She knows it doesn't matter. She does it anyway because the store-brand packaging says something she doesn't want visible. In the campus café her classmates order lattes and bagels without looking at the prices and Luz orders drip coffee and does the math before she speaks. Her jaw tightens at the counter. She carries this small math everywhere: every group lunch, every birthday, every shared Lyft where she calculates her share twice before agreeing. The shame isn't logical—she is in school, she is here, she is managing. But managing looks like hiding, and the hiding is tiring in a way the actual money problem isn't.

IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Arjun's parents call to ask about the winter break flight home. He has been avoiding the topic because the flight this year is three hundred dollars more than they expected and his parents will pay it, and he doesn't want them to pay it, and he also can't afford not to go home. The conversation runs exactly as he feared: his mother says "we'll figure it out" in a voice that means they will find money they don't easily have. He thanks them and hangs up and sits with the shame, which is not about the three hundred dollars exactly—it's about being twenty-one and still requiring rescue, still adding weight to people who are already carrying weight. He opens his laptop to look for holiday shifts at the campus café. His shoulders are tight. His neck aches from the phone call. The shifts are posted. He signs up for four.

## TURNING_POINT v03
---

MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Nadia tells her friend group she can't do the ski trip. Not a soft excuse—an actual reason: "It's outside my budget right now." Her hands are cold when she says it. The table pauses. One friend says "That sucks, we'll miss you." Another says "We could look for alternatives." The conversation moves on. Nadia sits with what's left. She named the thing she has been managing in private for a year—a budget, a limit, a financial reality that her classmates mostly don't have. It was survivable. No one looked at her differently. Her chest is still tight, the shame still present, not as evidence but as habit—the body's insistence on bracing for judgment after the judgment already didn't come. She will order the drip coffee again at the next café meetup. The shame will be there. But the secret is a little less heavy than it was before she said the word "budget" at a table where people care about her.

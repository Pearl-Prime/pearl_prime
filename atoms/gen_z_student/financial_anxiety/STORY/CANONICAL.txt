The group text says Cancun, spring break, split a house for five nights, three hundred each plus flights. Nadia reads it sitting on the edge of her bed at 8 a.m. and feels her stomach do the calculation before her mind can interfere. Three hundred plus flights. She checks her balance. Already knows what it says but checks it anyway.

She types "might not be able to swing it this year" and immediately feels the follow-up question forming on the other end — why, what's wrong, you have to come. She deletes the message and types "let me check" instead.

Let me check is a stall. She has checked. The number is clear. She is going to spend the next two weeks finding the gentlest way to decline, which will take more energy than the trip itself.

She mutes the thread and goes to her 8 a.m. seminar. She sits in the second row. The professor is talking about global economic inequality and Nadia takes notes and thinks about her loan balance growing at 6.8 percent and thinks about the word "inequality" in the mouth of a woman who makes two hundred thousand dollars a year to discuss it.

After class she calculates her next two weeks: the shift Thursday, the shift Sunday, the financial aid disbursement not until the 15th, groceries for fourteen days on what is currently in her account. The math works if she skips two lunches. She has skipped lunches before.

She texts her mom about the Cancun thing. Her mom says "I'm sorry baby" and the two words land in Nadia's chest like a warm stone. Not the trip. That is gone. But the "I'm sorry" — that she carries back to campus like something to hold when the numbers run too cold.

Marcus checks his email for the financial aid notification three times before 9 a.m. It has not arrived. The portal shows "pending review," which is what it showed yesterday and the day before, which means he cannot register for spring courses until it resolves, which means the sections he needs might fill before he can enroll.

He goes to class. He sits in the back. The professor talks about the Reconstruction era and Marcus hears the words and his body nods and something behind his sternum runs a different calculation quietly, like a second browser tab he cannot close.

He is on a Pell grant and a merit scholarship and a subsidized loan and he still works twelve hours a week at the campus rec center front desk. His roommate does not work. His roommate went to Cabo in January for a friend's birthday and came back with a hat. Marcus never asked how much the hat cost. He did not want to know.

The financial aid email arrives at 2:43 p.m. His phone buzzes and he opens it standing outside the library. The number is the same as last semester. He exhales for what feels like the first time in three days. His shoulders drop by a fraction he can actually feel.

He does not notice, as he stands there in the cold air feeling the relief, that he has spent seventy-two hours with a low-grade alarm running in his body, the kind that affects sleep and concentration and the quality of everything without ever landing on a specific thought. He just knows he can breathe now. He registers for spring courses. He goes to the rec center for his shift. He does not tell anyone about any of it.

Sofia learned to do the math invisibly. It started in middle school — calculating whether she could accept a birthday party invitation based on the gift expectation, whether she could join the field trip, whether the school supply list was manageable or whether she would need to go to the teacher quietly and ask for help.

In college the math became more complex. Tuition, rent split four ways, the textbook for bio that was ninety dollars used or two hundred and forty new, the transportation home for Thanksgiving, the co-pay at the student health center, the late fee she paid once and swore she would never pay again.

She became excellent at financial problem-solving. She knew every free event on campus. She knew which dining hall ran a late-night surplus. She knew the food pantry hours without having to look them up. She told herself this was resourcefulness. It was resourcefulness. It was also exhaustion, dressed in competence.

The conversation that changed something happened in her junior year, with a financial aid counselor she visited because her mother had lost her second job in two years. The counselor reviewed her file and asked questions Sofia had not been asked before: not "what do you need" but "what have you been doing without that most affects your ability to attend to your coursework?"

Sofia paused. The question assumed a cost she had been minimizing. She started listing. By the fourth item she was crying, which surprised her, because the items were practical. The counselor did not minimize them. She took notes.

By the end of the meeting Sofia had an emergency grant, a food pantry referral, and a note in her file for the coming semester. The money was a relief. The larger thing was the hour of being seen accurately — tallied in full, with someone holding the complete number without flinching.

The worst part, Jonah thinks, is not the money. It is the performance around the money.

He has a script for social situations involving cost. At restaurants: "I'm not that hungry, I'll just get an appetizer." At bar nights: "I'm good for now, I'll get the next one" — there is no next one. At group dinners when someone says "let's just split it evenly" for a check where he ordered the cheapest item: he pays his portion of everyone else's salmon and says nothing.

The script is fluent. He has been running it since high school, when the gap between his family's finances and his friends' became visible enough to navigate around. He is good at it. No one has ever called him on it, which means it works, which means he is paying a social toll no one sees.

He tallies this on a Thursday in March while sitting in the campus writing center pretending to review his essay draft. His loan balance at current rate of interest will reach approximately X by the time he graduates in fourteen months. He has done this calculation so many times it runs automatically, like a screensaver.

A classmate he knows slightly sits down across the table and opens her laptop. She is wearing AirPods and does not look up. Jonah watches her and performs a quick involuntary class-inference — new laptop, brand name backpack, AirPods, not working based on her schedule that he somehow knows secondhand.

He catches himself doing it and feels the specific discomfort of having been resentful of someone who did nothing to him. Closes his essay. Puts in earbuds. The writing center is warm and quiet. He works for two hours without checking his balance. He counts this as a success.

Bea's stomach drops every time she opens the app. She knows the number. She checked it four hours ago. It has not changed. She opens it again.

The app says she has enough for groceries and gas to her Thursday shift and the utility split her roommates voted on last month and not much else until the 18th. The 18th is nine days away. Nine days is a long time when the math is tight.

She is a junior. She is good at school. She has a 3.6 GPA and a professor who asked her to consider graduate school and she laughed in a way that the professor did not understand and that she did not explain.

She buys the store-brand everything. She eats before social events so she does not have to spend money at them. She owns one good blazer for professional occasions and wears it until it pills. These are solutions. She is a good problem-solver.

The problem-solving runs a cost she does not track because tracking it would be too expensive. Her sleep is lighter than it should be. She wakes at 4 a.m. and lies in the dark doing arithmetic. Her concentration during the first hour of class on days after bad nights is lower than she reports to herself.

She has never told a professor why she sometimes submits work late. She has never told her advisor that the spring research opportunity she turned down was turned down because of money, not scheduling. She has never let the full picture be visible in any room on this campus. The maintenance of that invisibility is its own kind of exhaustion, steady and quiet, running just below the surface of everything she does.

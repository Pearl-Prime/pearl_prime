Every morning, before the day demands anything, place your hand on your chest. Feel your heartbeat. Count five beats. Each beat pumps blood through a body that carries loss and still functions. Do not rush past this moment. Depression tells you the morning is pointless. Grief tells you the morning hurts. Your heart tells you neither narrative stops the blood from circulating. After five beats, move your hand to your belly. Take one deep breath. Feel the expansion against your palm. This breath entered a body that loved someone enough to break when they left. That capacity for love lives in the same lungs that take this breath. You did not lose the ability to love when you lost them. You carry it in every inhale. Stand up. Feel your weight transfer to your feet. The day begins not with productivity but with acknowledgment. Your body marked the loss before the alarm even rang. Honoring that mark takes ten seconds and costs nothing. It changes the texture of the morning from numb to noticed.

Once a week, write a single line to the person you lost. Not a letter. Not a journal entry. One line. "I heard that song in the dining hall." "Your birthday is next month and I do not know what to do with it." "I got a B on the paper and wanted to tell you first." Write it on paper. Press the pen down. Feel the ink move through the point. The line does not require a response. It requires a witness, and you are that witness. After writing, fold the paper. Place it somewhere specific: a drawer, a box, a book. Touch the container. Feel its surface. The line lives somewhere physical now. Grief that stays entirely internal becomes ambient, formless, overwhelming. Grief that lands on paper occupies a specific space. Over weeks, the container fills. The accumulation is not sadness. It is conversation. Ongoing, one-sided, and real. The person cannot read these lines. Your hand still writes them. The writing is for the hand, for the body, for the part of you that refuses to stop talking to someone who stopped answering.

The grief wave hits in public. Your eyes sting. Your throat locks. Your chest caves inward. You stand in the dining hall or the campus bus or the library and the world keeps moving while you fracture silently. Do not fight the wave. Press your feet into the floor. Hard. Feel the pressure travel up through your ankles, your shins, your knees. Grounding through the feet gives your nervous system a point of contact with the present while grief pulls you into the past. Place one hand on a surface — a table, a railing, a wall. Feel the temperature. Cool surfaces work best. The cold sends a sensory signal that competes with the emotional flood. Breathe through your nose. Three counts in. Six counts out. The extended exhale activates the vagus nerve and slows the cascade. The wave crests. It does not stay at crest forever. Your feet remain on the floor. Your hand remains on the surface. The public space did not witness your internal earthquake. And if it did, earthquakes are natural events. They require no apology.

Keep one object that belonged to them or reminds you of them within arm's reach in your dorm room or apartment. A photo. A hoodie. A mug. A playlist saved on your phone. Once a day, touch the object. Hold the mug. Feel its weight and temperature. Press the hoodie fabric between your fingers. Scroll to the playlist and read the song titles without pressing play. This daily contact maintains a physical thread between your present self and the relationship that ended. Grief counselors call this continuing bonds. The bond did not sever when the person left. It transformed. It lives now in objects, in sensory memory, in the way your hand reaches for a mug that carries their thumbprint in your imagination. The daily touch is not wallowing. It is tending. The same way you water a plant or charge a phone, you tend the bond. The object absorbs your attention for thirty seconds. In that half-minute, grief has a container, a texture, a weight that your hands can hold. Held grief hurts differently than floating grief. It hurts with edges. Edges you can eventually set down.

Once a week, walk to a spot on campus where you feel their absence most strongly. The bench where you sat together. The building where you got the call. The dining hall where you shared a meal. Go there on purpose. Sit down or stand still. Press your feet into the ground. Feel the surface beneath you — concrete, grass, tile. Place your hand over your heart. Breathe five slow breaths. In through the nose. Out through the mouth. Let the place hold you and the memory simultaneously. Your nervous system associates this location with pain. Visiting it voluntarily, with your feet grounded and your breathing controlled, rewrites the association. Not from pain to joy. From ambush to pilgrimage. You choose to come here. You choose to feel the absence here rather than being ambushed by it in the library at midnight. Intentional grief is not heavier than accidental grief. It is more contained. After five breaths, stand or walk away. Feel your legs carry you out of the space. You arrived, you felt, you left. The place remains. You remain. Both hold the same memory differently.

Someone says the wrong thing about your loss. "They are in a better place." "Everything happens for a reason." "At least you had time together." Your blood heats. Your jaw locks. Your fists clench at your sides. Before you respond from rage or collapse into the hurt, press your thumb hard into the center of your opposite palm. Feel the pressure point activate. That sharp sensation pulls you out of the reactive loop. The person in front of you means well and understands nothing. Both statements are true. You do not owe them a gracious response. You also do not need to educate them in this moment. A simple response works: "Thank you" or "I appreciate you saying something." These phrases close the exchange without opening your wound to someone who lacks the tools to help. After they walk away, shake your hands out. Roll your shoulders. The anger stores itself in your forearms and your jaw. Release both. Unclench your teeth. Open your fingers. The wrong words came from outside. Your grief lives inside, protected by the body that holds it. No platitude can reach the real loss. It only grazes the surface. The depths belong to you alone.

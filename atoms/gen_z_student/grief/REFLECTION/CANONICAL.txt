## REFLECTION v01
---

---
Grief at twenty hits different because nobody expects it. The campus keeps moving. The dining hall stays open. Your friends plan spring break while your chest holds a funeral no one attended. You placed your hand there just now. Feel the warmth. That heat is not fever. It is the engine of a heart that kept beating through the worst week of your life.
Your hands held a phone that delivered the news. Those same hands turned in assignments, swiped into the dining hall, gripped a steering wheel home for the service. They performed function while carrying fracture. Notice them now. The steadiness in your fingers. The fact that they still open and close on command.
Grief does not ask for your academic calendar. It arrives in the body like a transfer student — no orientation, no map, no assigned seat. Your chest absorbed it. Your hands carried it. And right now, the warmth between your palm and your sternum is evidence that you survived the semester of your life.

## REFLECTION v02
---

---
You stopped talking about it. Not because the grief faded. Because every time you opened your mouth, the words came out wrong. "I am fine" became the default password. Your throat memorized the lie. Your jaw clenched to keep bigger truths locked behind your teeth.
Drop your jaw now. Feel the release in the hinge near your ears. That loosening travels down into your throat, opening a corridor that has been sealed for weeks. You do not have to speak. Just notice the space.
Grief in college hides behind productivity. You attend class. You submit papers. Your jaw stays tight because relaxation feels dangerous — like the whole structure might collapse if one bolt loosens. But the jaw was never a load-bearing wall. It was a clamp.
Release the clamp. The building stands. Your throat softens. The words you swallowed do not need to come out today. They just need room to exist somewhere other than behind your molars.

## REFLECTION v03
---

---
Your stomach has been speaking a language you did not study. Nausea before class. Appetite vanishing at dinner. A churning that starts when you see their name in old texts. The gut processes loss the way it processes everything — through contraction and release.
Sit with your spine against the chair. Feel each point of contact. The chair holds you the way the ground holds a building — without opinion, without condition. Your stomach can soften against that support.
You did not sign up for this elective. Grief at this age feels like an enrollment error — wrong class, wrong semester, wrong life. But your body enrolled anyway. Your stomach registered every absence. Your spine curved under the weight and straightened again each morning.
Notice the chair holding your back right now. You do not have to generate your own support. The surface beneath you already committed to the job. Let your stomach unclench one degree. Then one more.

## REFLECTION v04
---

---
Your eyes scanned for them in every crowd for the first month. The dining hall. The quad. The parking lot. Your visual system ran a search pattern it built over years of friendship, and it refused to update the database. The forehead tightened every time the search returned empty.
Touch your forehead now. The space between your eyebrows holds a knot made of looking and not finding. Your eyes performed their job — scanning, tracking, locating — but the target left the grid. The forehead contracted to compensate, as if squinting harder could bring them back.
Close your eyes. Let the search pause. The darkness behind your eyelids is not emptiness. It is the first rest your visual system has taken since the loss. Your forehead can release because the search is not cancelled. It is paused. You will always look for them in rooms. That impulse is love wearing the clothes of attention.
Rest your eyes. Let your forehead smooth. The search will resume when you open them. For now, let the muscles that scan for the missing take one full breath off duty.

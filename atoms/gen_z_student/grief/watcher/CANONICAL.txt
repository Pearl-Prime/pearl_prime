
## RECOGNITION v01

Nadia watches herself grieve. She stands at her best friend's graduation party three months after her own mother's death and she observes herself smiling and accepting a glass and congratulating her friend. The performance is accurate. She is happy for her friend in the way she can be happy for things right now, which is at a slight remove, through glass. The watcher notes: she is dressed appropriately, she is making eye contact, she is here. The watcher also notes: the grief is still going, quietly, underneath all of this, unreachable by the party. She goes home at 10 p.m. and closes her door and the watching self and the grieving self are finally in the same room. She lies down without taking off her shoes. The ceiling is the ceiling. She doesn't sleep for a while. Both selves lie there together.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02
---

MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Marcus is in grief counseling and he watches himself be in grief counseling. The counselor is good. The counselor asks good questions. Marcus hears himself answer with accurate sentences about his brother. He observes himself answering. He notes that the answers are true but that he is delivering them from somewhere behind himself, watching his own mouth form the words. The counselor asks "What does it feel like right now, in your body?" Marcus watches himself pause. He watches himself try to access something that the watching is blocking. He says "tight. My chest." This is true. The counselor nods. Marcus watches the counselor nod. After the session he walks across campus and the autumn light hits the library steps and for one second the watching stops and the grief is just the grief, unnarrated, immediate. Then the watcher returns. But for one second it was just him and the grief and the light.

## TURNING_POINT v03
---

BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Yuki plays the voicemail from her father at the beginning of his last hospitalization. She has listened to it three times in the six months since he died, always waiting for it to do what she hoped: crack something open, release something that would move through her and finish. It doesn't. She listens to his voice and she watches herself listening. She registers that the voice is warm. She registers that the message is ordinary—just telling her he's proud of her, that he'll talk to her when he's feeling better. The watcher notes: the voice is present, the person is not, the grief is here. She puts the phone down. Her hands are still. Her eyes are dry. The watcher won't step aside. She closes her eyes and stays in the chair for five minutes and lets the grief occupy the space without narrating it. She doesn't fully succeed. She partially succeeds. Her breath slows. The voicemail stays in her phone. She will listen again. The cost is still the same cost every time: the person she wants to hear is not coming back.

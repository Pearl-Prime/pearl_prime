## TAKEAWAY v01
---

---
Grief does not follow a syllabus. It arrives between classes, mid-bite, mid-sentence. Press your hand to your sternum. Feel the ache settle there. The ache proves the love was real. Pain and love share the same address in your chest.

You do not grieve on a schedule. The wave hits during a Tuesday lab or a Saturday morning shower. Grip the nearest surface. Feel its solidity. Waves recede. They always recede. Your body stands on the shore and endures the water.

Continuing to function does not mean you have moved on. Touch the base of your throat. Feel the pulse. Your heart pumps for the living, including you. Functioning and grieving run on parallel tracks. Both are real. Both are happening.

No one teaches you how to grieve at twenty. The campus offers tutoring for calculus but not for loss. Place your feet on the floor. Feel the ground. You are learning the hardest subject without a textbook. Every day you survive it counts as passing.

The urge to be fine for other people costs energy you cannot spare. Place your hand on your chest. Feel the weight beneath your ribs. Authenticity costs less than performance. Let the people around you see the weight. Some of them will help carry it.

Missing someone is not a problem to solve. It is a condition to carry. Press your palms together. Feel the heat between your hands. That warmth belongs to a body that loved and lost and continues to generate heat. The carrying is the honoring.

## TAKEAWAY v02
---

---
Grief arrives in the body as weight before it forms as thought. The chest is heavier than yesterday. The arms feel useless. The legs resist forward motion. This is not depression and it is not weakness. It is your nervous system processing the reality that something real was lost. Weight is the accurate physical response.

Grief does not follow a timeline. Your body may feel fine for a week and then be struck by a song or a smell and return to acute loss. The returning wave is not regression. It is the nervous system completing a processing cycle it paused. Feel the wave arrive. Let it move through. It moves through.

The throat tightens when the loss tries to speak. Tears form or resist forming depending on where your body stores the grief. Neither response is wrong. Press your knuckle gently against the space below your lower lip. That small pressure sometimes allows the throat to open. Grief that cannot move through the body stays in the body.

Eating changes during acute grief. The stomach contracts. Food feels irrelevant or impossible. Your body reallocates its resources to the processing of loss. Eat something soft. Feel it move down your throat. Softness meets the contracted stomach more gently than hard food. Your body still requires fuel even while it grieves.

Grief and love occupy the same neural architecture. The loss activates the same circuits that the connection did. Your chest hurts because the same place that held the warmth is now processing the absence. The pain is the love without a destination. It has not disappeared. It has changed form.

Sleeping with something that belonged to the person or relationship you lost is not childish. It is a real somatic strategy. Your skin registers warmth, texture, and scent. The nervous system reads these inputs as proximity. Proximity reduces distress signals. The object is a bridge between what was and what your nervous system still needs.

## TAKEAWAY v03
---

---
Grief in a college environment has no formal container. There is no bereavement leave for a friendship ending. No ceremony for losing a version of yourself you had planned to become. Your body does not know the grief is unofficial. It grieves the unofficial loss with the same physiological intensity as the recognized ones.

Academic grief — the failure of a plan you had built an identity around — produces a specific physical sensation. The future the body was braced for disappears and the bracing has nowhere to land. Feel where your body is holding that unspent readiness. Shoulders, jaw, hands. Shake them out. Release the preparation for a future that changed.

Numbness is a grief response. Your body suspended sensation to manage the acute phase. The numbness is not indifference. It is protective rationing of available processing capacity. When sensation returns — and it does — it may arrive as irritability, hunger, or the sudden ability to cry at something unrelated. The return of sensation is progress.

Grief isolates because explaining it requires energy the grieving body does not have. Other people's discomfort with loss compounds the isolation. Sit near another person without requiring them to say anything useful. Feel their presence without obligation. Proximity without performance is a low-cost antidote to isolation. You do not need a good listener. You need another body in the room.

The grieving body needs permission to find moments of ease without interpreting the ease as betrayal of the loss. Laughing at a meme does not mean the grief is resolved. Your nervous system requires contrast — regulation and dysregulation alternating. The moment of ease is not a moral failure. It is a physiological necessity the body found on its own.

Eventually, the grief changes texture. The sharp edge rounds. The weight redistributes. You carry it differently rather than less. Notice when the texture begins to shift. Feel it in your chest or stomach. That shift is not forgetting. It is integration. Your body is learning to hold the loss as part of its ongoing architecture.

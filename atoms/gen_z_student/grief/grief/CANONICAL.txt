
## RECOGNITION v01

Luz's grandmother died in September and it is now January and the grief is not done. She had expected grief to have a shape—an acute phase and then a decline. Instead it lives in the ordinary. The dining hall smells like the soup her grandmother made every Sunday and Luz stands with her tray and can't move for a moment. The smell dissipates. She gets her food. She sits down. The moment passed and also it didn't—it sits in her chest for the rest of the afternoon, a low ache that the semester runs over without stopping. She has not cried in six weeks. She is not okay. She is going to lecture and turning in assignments and eating and sleeping and doing what the calendar says, and she is not okay, and these two facts exist simultaneously in her body in a way that no one around her can see.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02

COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Remy is in the library when his cousin texts that his uncle passed away. He reads the text twice. His uncle had been sick for a year. The death is not a surprise. He puts the phone face down and stares at his notes for ten minutes. He is in the middle of an annotated bibliography for a history paper. He picks up his pen. The pen doesn't move. He is aware of the gap between what he is expected to do—continue, produce, deliver the bibliography by Friday—and what his body wants, which is something he can't name cleanly, something adjacent to going home, something about not being in a library. He packs up. He walks to the dorm. He calls his mother. They talk for an hour. Afterward he sits in the hallway outside his room on the floor and doesn't move for a while. The bibliography is still due Friday. The grief doesn't care about Friday.

## TURNING_POINT v03
---
path: story_atoms/gen_z_student/grief/grief/turning_point/micro/v03.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Kofi writes his deceased father's name in the acknowledgments section of his thesis proposal. He has been working on the proposal for three months. He added the acknowledgment at 2 a.m. on a Tuesday and then sat very still at his desk for a long time. His father died two years ago, midway through Kofi's first semester. He never told most of his classmates. He didn't know how to carry it alongside the normal operations of being a student. Writing the name—seeing it in the document, on the page, attached to the work—does something he didn't expect. His chest opens around an ache he has been keeping compressed. His eyes sting. He doesn't cry. He keeps the name in the proposal. He saves the document. He closes the laptop and sits with the ache, which is real and which the thesis proposal now holds a small portion of. The rest of it is still his to carry. But less of it is invisible now.

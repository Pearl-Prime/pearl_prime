## COMPRESSION v01
---

---
The empty chair in the lecture hall is not a metaphor. It is a fact your chest absorbs every Monday, Wednesday, Friday. Grief at twenty arrives without a syllabus. No office hours. No curve. Your sternum compresses at the absence the way a sponge compresses at pressure — absorbing what it contacts. You kept attending. You kept sitting in the row next to the gap. Your chest held the weight of a missing person and still expanded enough to breathe through ninety-minute lectures.
---

## COMPRESSION v02
---

---
Your hands typed "I am okay" fourteen times this week. Your throat swallowed the real answer every time someone asked. The body runs a parallel grief — one your Instagram story never shows. Your hands know the truth because they trembled over the keyboard at 2 AM writing the eulogy. Your throat knows because it closed at the funeral and has not fully reopened since. These body parts carry a record the mind keeps trying to archive. Let the hands shake. Let the throat stay narrow. The body grieves on its own clock, and that clock ignores every deadline on your calendar.
---

## COMPRESSION v03
---

---
The dining hall triggers it. Their favorite meal on the rotating menu. Your stomach flips at the sight of mac and cheese on a blue tray. Grief encodes itself in sensory memory — smells, sounds, the specific yellow of cafeteria lighting. Your gut registered the association before your conscious mind caught up. You do not need to eat there today. You do not need to override the stomach. Walk to a different building. Eat somewhere your body has no file on them.
---

## COMPRESSION v04
---

---
Your backpack weighs twenty pounds. Your grief weighs more. Your shoulders hunch forward on the walk to class because the body organizes itself around the heaviest load. The spine curves to protect the chest — the organ most exposed to the absence. You carry both weights across the same campus, up the same stairs, into the same rooms. Notice the shoulders right now. They climbed toward your ears the day you found out, and they have not returned to neutral. One millimeter of drop is enough. Let the shoulders descend one millimeter. The backpack stays. The grief stays. But the muscles exhale.

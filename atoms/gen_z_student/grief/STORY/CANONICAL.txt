## STORY v01
---

---
Maya kept her phone on the desk face-up during every lecture. Not to scroll. To see the name. Her older brother texted every morning at 7:12 — never earlier, never later. A single thumbs-up emoji. His way of saying: still here.
October 14th, the text stopped.
She sat in Introduction to Psychology three days after the funeral. The professor discussed attachment theory. Maya's chest compressed into a fist. Her hands flattened against the desk, fingertips white. The girl next to her offered gum. Maya tried to say thank you. Her mouth opened. Nothing crossed the threshold.
She walked to the bathroom. Locked the stall. Pressed both palms against the cold metal door. Her body shook from the sternum outward, rings of tremor radiating through muscle and bone. She did not cry. The grief sat deeper than tears could reach — lodged behind her ribs like a stone swallowed whole.
The academic advisor suggested a reduced course load. Maya refused. Stopping felt like dying too. She attended every class. Sat in the same seat. Kept the phone face-up. The screen stayed dark at 7:12 every morning, and every morning her chest caved at the silence.
Week six, she placed her hand on her own sternum in the library. Not because anyone told her to. Because her body demanded contact. She pressed down. Felt her heartbeat push back against her palm. One thump. Then another. Her brother could not text. But her chest still answered.

## STORY v02
---

---
Jordan learned about the accident from a push notification. Not a phone call. Not a knock on the door. A local news alert while standing in the burrito line at the campus food court. His best friend's name in a headline. His stomach ejected upward into his throat.
He dropped the tray. Beans and rice hit the tile. Students turned. Jordan stood in the fluorescent light, phone screen burning a rectangle into his retina. His throat sealed shut. The cashier asked if he was okay. His legs answered by walking him out the door and across the quad in bare November air without a jacket.
Three weeks later, Jordan sat in the counseling center waiting room. The pamphlets said "grief support." His stomach twisted at the word. Grief belonged to grandparents. To cancer wards. Not to twenty-year-olds killed by a red light runner at 11 PM on a Tuesday.
The counselor asked him to locate the feeling in his body. Jordan pointed at his gut. She nodded. She did not try to explain it away. She asked him to breathe into the spot. He inhaled. The exhale shattered into fragments — not a cry, something more animal. A sound that started in his belly and clawed up through his throat and escaped as a bark he did not recognize.
He sat with it. The counselor sat with him. No homework. No workbook. Just a stomach that finally told the truth and a throat that let the truth leave. Jordan attended every session that semester. He never called it therapy. He called it "the place my stomach talks."

## STORY v03
---

---
Priya found out during finals week. Her mother called at 6 AM — never a good sign. Her grandmother, gone. The woman who braided her hair every morning before primary school. The hands that smelled like cardamom and coconut oil. Finished.
Priya's legs buckled at the edge of her dorm bed. She sat on the floor. Her spine curved forward until her forehead touched the carpet. She stayed there for an hour. Her roommate stepped around her, silent, placing a glass of water within reach.
The calculus final happened twelve hours later. Priya's legs carried her to the exam hall on autopilot. Her spine held her upright in the plastic chair. Her hand wrote equations her brain could not parse. She turned in the paper and walked to the campus garden. Sat on cold stone. Let her spine collapse again.
Grief in college travels through the legs first. They forget how to commit to a direction. They carry you to class and then to the bathroom and then to a bench where you sit until the sky changes color. Priya noticed this — the wandering. Her body refused straight lines for weeks.
She started walking on purpose. One lap around the track each evening. Not running. Walking. Feeling her legs agree to a path and complete it. Her spine stacked one vertebra at a time during those laps. Grief did not leave. But her body relearned how to move in a straight line while carrying it.

## STORY v04
---

---
Elias cried in the library. Not the quiet floor. The main atrium. Lunchtime. Full capacity. His face crumpled without permission while reading a text from his dad: "Mom's scan came back. She has weeks."
His face burned. Tears dropped onto the open textbook, warping the page on cellular mitosis. The girl across the table glanced up, then looked away. Elias pressed his palms over his eyes. His chest heaved in silence — grief on mute in a public building.
He trained his face to stay flat after that. Practiced neutral expressions in the dorm mirror. Locked his chest tight during study groups. Smiled at parties. His face became a screen door — structure visible, everything behind it exposed to anyone who looked closely enough.
His RA noticed. Not the tears. The performance. She said, "You hold your face like a fist." Elias felt his jaw drop. Someone had read the mask without needing to see behind it.
She did not offer solutions. She offered her office. A door that closed. Elias sat in the swivel chair, and his face let go. Eyebrows up. Mouth loose. Cheeks slack. His chest unlocked with a sound like a cabinet opening. The grief poured across his face — not as tears this time, but as heat. A flushing that started at his sternum and climbed to his forehead.
He sat in that office four more times that semester. Each visit, his face softened faster. The mask thinned. His chest opened wider. Grief kept its schedule. But his face stopped lying about it.

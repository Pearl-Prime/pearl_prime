Marcus's professor asks him to help organize the departmental open house and his stomach drops before he can form a thought. He has the afternoon free. The task sounds like two hours of work. His nervous system treats the request like a demand he cannot refuse, like saying no would shatter something essential between him and the professor, like his entire academic standing rests on this single yes. He says yes. He hangs up and stares at his laptop. He had planned to use the afternoon to start his thesis proposal draft, which is due in three weeks and which he hasn't begun. The open house is not a crisis. His yes was not necessary. He knows this. His body did not give him time to know it before the word was already out.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02
---

BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Selin's roommate asks if she can borrow Selin's class notes before the history exam. Selin's chest seizes. The notes are hers. She spent eight hours on them. She also needs them to study tonight. Her body runs the threat scenario immediately: if she says no, the roommate will be upset, and then the apartment will be tense, and then the next three months of shared living will be poisoned. The catastrophe is complete before she assesses whether it's accurate. She photographs all forty pages and sends them. Her phone shows 11:47 p.m. She studies from memory until 1 a.m., missing the visual organization she built into her notes. She gets a lower score than she expected. The roommate thanks her and never mentions it again. The relationship is exactly the same. The notes mattered only to Selin, and she gave them away in response to a threat that was never there.

## TURNING_POINT v03
---

BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Arjun pauses before saying yes to a peer who wants to borrow twenty dollars. He holds the pause for two seconds. He feels the familiar urgency—the sense that the delay is already a micro-rejection, already damaging something. He stays in the pause. He checks his account balance in his head: rent is due in a week and he's three shifts away from covering it. He says "I'm tight right now, I can't." His pulse spikes. His hands grip his phone. His friend says "Oh, no worries, I'll ask someone else." They keep texting. The conversation moves forward. Arjun sits with the aftermath of the no: his chest tight, his pulse still elevated, his body insisting something went wrong. Nothing went wrong. The cost is not the friendship. The cost is that holding his own limit felt exactly like a catastrophe, even when it wasn't.

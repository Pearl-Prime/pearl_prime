
## RECOGNITION v01

Luz skips a birthday party for a classmate she doesn't know well. She had a research presentation the next morning and couldn't afford the late night. She texted an apology and stayed in her room. Three days later, she is still replaying the decision. She rehearses alternate versions where she went and performed being fine. The shame is not logical—she made a reasonable choice—but it sits in her sternum like something she owes everyone in the room she didn't enter. She passes the classmate on campus and her pulse jumps. The classmate waves. Luz waves back. Nothing has changed. The shame doesn't update on new evidence. It decided she was selfish and she continues to pay the sentence regardless of the verdict.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02
---

BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Nadia tells her mother she can't come home for fall break because she has two exams the following Monday. The call ends. Nadia sits on the edge of her dorm bed with her phone in her lap and her throat tight. She made the right call. The exams are real, the studying is necessary, the travel would cost two full days she doesn't have. She knows this. She also feels like a person who chose grades over her family, and the feeling doesn't yield to the reasoning. She eats dinner alone and finds it hard to taste anything. Her mother texts: "I understand, study hard." The kindness makes it worse. Nadia opens her laptop and stares at her notes without reading them. The boundary she held cost more than the exams required. She pays the difference in silence, in the dining hall, alone.

## TURNING_POINT v03
---
path: story_atoms/gen_z_student/boundaries/shame/turning_point/micro/v03.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Yuki tells her thesis advisor she needs to push their weekly meeting to the following week. She has the flu. She sends the email and immediately begins composing the apology that will accompany it—three drafts before she sends a single line. The advisor responds in six minutes: "Of course, feel better." Yuki reads the reply and something shifts. The shame was already there before the response, installed in advance, anticipating punishment that didn't come. She sets her phone down. She is still sick. Her throat still aches. But she notices the gap between what her body expected—rejection, disappointment, consequences—and what arrived. The gap is not relief exactly. It's something more disorienting. She has spent four years assuming her limits would cost her relationships. The evidence is accumulating that this is not true. The assumption remains. The cost is still high.

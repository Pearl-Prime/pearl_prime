## STORY v01
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 4
---
Mia sits on the edge of her twin bed, knees pressed together, listening to her roommate invite four friends into their shared dorm room at 10 p.m. on a Wednesday. Her jaw clenches the instant the door opens. Bodies fill the narrow space between desks. Someone drops onto her desk chair without asking. Her chest compresses around a protest she cannot release.
She has an organic chemistry exam in fourteen hours. The textbook lies open on her lap, pages blurring under the overhead light someone just dimmed for ambiance. Her shoulders climb toward her ears. She breathes through her nose in tight, controlled pulls.
Her roommate glances over. "You're welcome to join." The sentence sounds like an invitation. Mia's body reads it as a verdict: participate or become the problem. Her throat constricts around the word no. She smiles, shakes her head, presses earbuds in deep. The bass from the speaker vibrates through her mattress.
She studies for three hours inside a room that does not belong to her. Her neck locks at an angle that will ache for two days. She finishes the chapter with her molars ground flat and her fists curled around a highlighter she squeezed until the cap cracked.
At 1 a.m. the friends leave. Mia lies in the dark. Her body releases in slow, shuddering increments — shoulders dropping, jaw loosening, chest expanding into the silence. The exam is in seven hours. She lost three of them to a boundary she could not speak. Her body paid the invoice in tension her muscles will carry into the testing room.

## STORY v02
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 4
IDENTITY_STAGE: pre_awareness
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 5
---
Jordan checks Venmo and counts: eighty-seven dollars sent to friends this semester, none returned. His stomach knots. He sits in the campus dining hall, alone at a corner table, scrolling through the transaction history with his jaw locked and his thumbnail pressing crescents into his palm.
It started small. A coffee here. A late-night food run there. "I'll get you back" became the phrase that entered his ears and dissolved before it reached his memory. His chest tightens around a truth he refuses to assemble: the paying is not generosity. The paying is rent he charges himself for belonging.
His phone vibrates. A floormate asks if he can cover a ticket to the campus comedy show. Twenty dollars. His ribs compress. He has twenty dollars. He also has a textbook charge posting Friday that will overdraft his account. His thumbs hover over the keyboard. His throat dries.
He types "yeah no worries" and hits send. The Venmo notification chimes. Twenty dollars gone. The sound produces a sensation he cannot name — something between relief and a door closing. His shoulders drop one degree because the social math balanced. His bank account math collapsed.
He walks back to the dorm, hands shoved in his jacket pockets, knuckles white. The night air hits his face. His chest stays compressed. He sits on his bed and opens the banking app, watching the number sink below what the textbook costs.
Jordan closes the app and stares at the ceiling. His body holds two truths: he cannot afford to keep saying yes, and he cannot locate the muscle that forms the word no. Somewhere between his ribs, a boundary tries to exist. He rolls over and smothers it with the pillow.

## STORY v03

IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
COST_TYPE: relational
COST_INTENSITY: 4
---
Aisha opens the shared Google Doc and counts the slides. Eighteen. She built fourteen of them. Her group project has four members. The math is not complicated. Her jaw clenches as she scrolls through the two slides Marcus contributed — bullet points copied from the first Google result, unformatted, misspelled.
She texts the group chat: "Hey, are you all adding to the doc tonight." No punctuation at the end because punctuation feels aggressive and her chest already constricts around the confrontation she cannot afford. Three read receipts. No replies.
Her shoulders rise. She sits in the library, alone at a table near the window, and her fingers hover over the keyboard with a tremor she hides by pressing her wrists against the desk edge. She could message the professor. She could name the imbalance. Her throat seizes around both options.
Instead she builds the remaining four slides. Her neck locks at an angle that sends pain down her right arm. She formats Marcus's slides because submitting uneven work reflects on her and her body rejects that possibility with a visceral clench below her sternum.
The presentation earns a 94. The professor praises the group's cohesion. Aisha sits in the back row and her face holds a smile while her trapezius muscles burn. Marcus high-fives her in the hallway. "Great job, team." Her jaw snaps shut on the correction. The word team travels through her body like ground glass.
She walks to her apartment and stands in the shower until the hot water runs out. Her shoulders descend one vertebra at a time under the stream. She built a project for four people using one body. The grade belongs to the group. The cost belongs entirely to the space between her shoulder blades.

## STORY v04
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 4
IDENTITY_STAGE: self_claim
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 4
---
Leo stands in the doorway of his own dorm room and hesitates. His roommate's girlfriend sits on his bed — his bed — watching something on a laptop propped against his pillow. The room smells like her perfume. His jaw tightens so fast his teeth click.
He has asked twice this month. Polite, careful, the sentences sanded down until no edge remained. "Hey, would it be cool if she maybe didn't use my bed." Both times his roommate laughed and said "Sure, bro" and changed nothing. Leo's chest compressed tighter each time, the boundary bouncing off a wall of casual dismissal.
Now he stands in the doorway with his backpack cutting into his shoulder and his throat closing around attempt number three. His body catalogs the cost of speaking: awkward silence, possible confrontation, the label of being difficult. His body also catalogs the cost of silence: his own bed, colonized; his own space, erased; his pulse running too fast for a body standing in a doorframe.
He sits at his desk with his back to the couple. His shoulder blades press together like a door closing. He opens a textbook and reads the same paragraph four times while his jaw works in small, grinding circles.
That night, after the girlfriend leaves, Leo lies on sheets that smell like someone else's shampoo. His fists clench under the blanket. The ceiling offers no advice. His chest holds a sentence he has rehearsed for three weeks: "This is my space and I need you to respect it." Eight words. His throat locks around every one.
He sets an alarm for 7 a.m. Tomorrow he speaks the sentence or he loses the room to a version of himself that never existed — the version who does not mind, who goes with the flow, who trades his own bed for the appearance of being easy to live with.

## STORY v05
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 3
---
Taryn opens her planner and blocks two hours on Saturday afternoon for silence. She writes it in pen. She underlines it. Her shoulders relax one fraction for the first time all week.
By Friday night the group chat fills with plans. Brunch at 11. Shopping at 1. Pregame at 5. Every message is addressed to the floor — the collective "you all coming" that functions as a summons her body cannot ignore. Her chest tightens with each notification. She watches the blocked hours on Saturday shrink under the weight of social expectation.
She types and deletes six responses. "I'm in" feels like a lie. "I can't" feels like an exit from the group. Her jaw aches from holding both truths simultaneously. She locks her phone and presses her palms flat against her desk until the trembling stops.
Saturday arrives. She goes to brunch. She goes shopping. She stands in a dressing room with her forehead against the mirror, breathing through her mouth, shoulders rigid. The two hours she blocked exist only as ink on a page she will not open.
By 5 p.m. she sits on someone's floor at the pregame, knees pulled to her chest, smiling when addressed, silent otherwise. Her body vibrates with a low frequency only she detects — the hum of a boundary dissolved in real time.
She walks home at midnight. Alone on the sidewalk, her shoulders finally drop. Her jaw unclenches. The silence she needed wraps around her like a coat. She had it all along. She just never gave herself permission to wear it while other people watched.

## STORY v06
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: integration
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
---
Noor sits in the campus coffee shop with her phone face-down on the table. The group chat pinged four times in the last ten minutes. She knows because her watch buzzed against her wrist. She leaves the phone where it lies. Her jaw is loose. Her shoulders rest two inches below their usual station.
Six months ago this scene played differently. Every ping activated a sequence: grab phone, read message, calculate social cost of ignoring it, respond within sixty seconds. Her chest used to compress at the first vibration. Her thumbs used to move before her brain approved the message.
The shift started small. One unanswered text. The guilt arrived on schedule — a hot wire across her sternum. She sat with it. The wire cooled. The friendship survived. She filed that data point and repeated the experiment.
Now she carries a practice her therapist calls selective responsiveness and Noor calls breathing room. She responds to messages during two windows per day. Outside those windows, the phone rests face-down. Her nervous system no longer treats silence as social death.
The barista calls her name. She collects her coffee with steady hands. A friend slides into the opposite chair and says, "You never text back fast anymore." Noor holds her cup, meets the eyes across the table, and says, "I respond when I have the space to mean what I say." The sentence exits her throat without resistance. Her chest stays open.
The friend pauses, then nods. The conversation continues. Noor sips her coffee and feels the absence of the old chest compression — the hollow where urgency used to live, now filled with something quieter. Not emptiness. Capacity. The boundary she built does not keep people out. It keeps her body in the room long enough to show up as someone she recognizes.

## COMPRESSION v01
---

---
A boundary is a sentence your body speaks before your mouth does. Jaw clenches. Shoulders rise. Chest compresses. Those signals arrive the instant someone asks for your time, your space, your money. The signal is the boundary — already formed, already present, already accurate. The gap between the body's no and the mouth's yes is where resentment accumulates. Closing that gap does not require confrontation. It requires listening to the flinch and letting the flinch finish its sentence out loud.

## COMPRESSION v02
---

---
Student life operates on an unspoken contract: shared space means shared access. Your room, your notes, your emotional bandwidth become communal resources the moment you move into a dorm. That contract was never negotiated. Your body signed it under duress — the duress of wanting to belong. Renegotiating starts with one admission: the open door cost you sleep, money, energy, and the physical comfort of occupying your own space without apology. The renegotiation is not hostile. It is honest. Honesty starts in the body, in the jaw that unlocks long enough to say "not tonight."
---

## COMPRESSION v03
---

---
Three boundary failures dominate college life. The lending pattern: money, notes, meal swipes leave your hands faster than they return. The availability pattern: your door stays open, your phone stays answered, your presence stays guaranteed. The labor pattern: group work settles on the person least willing to watch the grade suffer. Each pattern stores its cost in the body — stomach for lending, shoulders for availability, jaw for labor. Naming the pattern and its physical address gives you a target. Address the body first. The words follow.

## COMPRESSION v04
---

---
Saying no activates a threat response identical to physical danger. Your heart races. Your palms sweat. Your throat constricts. The nervous system reads social refusal as exile, and exile once meant death. That ancient wiring runs underneath every dorm room negotiation, every group chat reply, every borrowed twenty dollars you cannot afford to lose. Understanding the wiring does not disable it. Understanding tells you the fear is inherited, not earned. The roommate who hears your no will not banish you from the species. Your body needs repetitions of that survival before it updates the threat model.
---

## COMPRESSION v05
---

---
People-pleasing is boundary collapse in slow motion. It begins with small surrenders — a favor here, a swallowed opinion there — each one so minor that the cost registers only in the body. A tighter jaw. A stiffer neck. A chest that compresses one millimeter further each week. By midterm the body holds a semester of accumulated concessions and the mind calls it exhaustion. The exhaustion is structural. It lives in muscles that spent months holding a shape designed by other people's expectations. Recovery demands the muscle learn its own shape again — the posture of a body that occupies its own outline.
---

## COMPRESSION v06
---

---
The boundary paradox: you fear that limits will shrink your social world, but the absence of limits already shrank it. Without boundaries, you avoid the dining hall because someone will ask for a favor. You dread the group chat because it delivers obligations disguised as invitations. You stay in your room not for rest but for the only privacy you can claim without negotiation. Limits do not subtract connection. They filter it. The people who remain after you say "I cannot do that" are the people who see you as a person. The rest saw a resource. Your body knew the difference all along. The jaw always clenched around the ones who came to collect.

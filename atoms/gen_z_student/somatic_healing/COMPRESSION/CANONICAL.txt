## COMPRESSION v01
---

---
The tension backpack weighs nothing and everything. Your shoulders carry it to every class — the invisible load of deadlines, social performance, family expectations, and the low-grade hum of a news cycle that never stops. The trapezius muscles forgot their resting position because the load never gets set down. Somatic healing for a college body starts with the shoulders because they are the first to climb and the last to drop. One conscious release per hour. One deliberate drop. The shoulders will climb again. Let them. Then drop them again. Healing is not a single release. It is a rhythm of holding and letting go.

## COMPRESSION v02
---

---
Your jaw holds the academic transcript of your stress. Midterms etch themselves into the masseter. Finals grind into the molars. The jaw clenches during Zoom lectures, during group chats, during the 11 PM scroll through social media that was supposed to be relaxing. The muscle does not distinguish between a differential equations problem and a comment section argument — both register as threat, both trigger the clamp. Somatic healing reaches the jaw through awareness first, release second. Touch the hinge. Notice the tension. Part the lips. Let the teeth separate. The jaw built its pattern over semesters. It will not unlearn in a day. But it can unlearn in a thousand small releases between now and graduation.

## COMPRESSION v03
---

---
Shallow breathing is the unofficial respiratory mode of every college campus. The chest pumps air like a bellows on high speed — short strokes, minimal depth. The diaphragm, the dome-shaped muscle designed to pull air deep into the lungs, sits idle. Your body chose this pattern because depth requires safety, and the semester offers very little of that. Retraining the breath means retraining the body's relationship with safety. One deep belly breath per hour. One deliberate descent of the diaphragm. The chest will keep its shallow habit. Let it. Then send one breath below the ribs.

## COMPRESSION v04
---

---
The chair is the most destructive piece of furniture on campus and you sit in it eight to twelve hours a day. Your hips lock. Your lower back rounds. Your glutes atrophy while the hip flexors shorten into cables that pull your pelvis forward every time you stand. The body was not built for this position at this duration. Somatic healing for the hips requires one intervention: stand up. Frequently. Push the hips forward. Stretch the front of the thighs. Remind the joints that they rotate in a circle, not just a right angle. The chair will be there when you return. Your full range of motion might not be.

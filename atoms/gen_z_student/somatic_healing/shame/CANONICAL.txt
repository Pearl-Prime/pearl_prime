
## RECOGNITION v01

COST_TYPE: internal

Luz notices that her chest is hot when she is ashamed. Not metaphorically—actually warm, a flush that spreads from her sternum upward when she does something she judges herself for. She failed a quiz today. The quiz was worth three percent. The heat is there anyway, spreading from her chest to her throat. She is sitting in the campus café and no one around her knows about the quiz or the three percent or the heat. She puts her hands flat on the table. The table is cool. The contrast helps a little. She has been living in this body for twenty years and didn't know until now that shame runs up from her chest like heat. She files this away. Not as something to fix—just as something she knows now. Her body is talking. She is beginning to hear what it says.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02
---
path: story_atoms/gen_z_student/somatic_healing/shame/mechanism_proof/micro/v02.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Selin sits in her wellness counselor's office and is asked to describe where she feels a difficult emotion in her body. She has been talking for forty minutes about a conflict with a friend. She is asked to locate the conflict in her body. She pauses. Her chest, obviously, is tight. She also notices her left shoulder is slightly higher than her right—has been the whole session—and her left hand has been pressed against her thigh. She hadn't noticed any of this while she was talking. She was in her head, in the narrative, while her body was doing its own version of the story without her. The counselor asks what the left shoulder wants. Selin doesn't know. She tries to lower it. It resists. Her throat tightens. She says "I think I'm angry." She hadn't known she was angry. The shoulder knew.

## TURNING_POINT v03
---
path: story_atoms/gen_z_student/somatic_healing/shame/turning_point/micro/v03.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Priya stands in the shower for ten minutes after a hard week and lets the water run hot on the back of her neck. This is not efficient. She has reading due tonight and a meeting in two hours. She stands there anyway. The heat loosens something in the muscles along her cervical spine. Her shoulders drop fractions. She notices the pressure in her sternum ease by a small but real amount. She has spent the week treating her body like a machine that processes stress silently while she focuses on output. The shower is not a solution. It is ten minutes of acknowledging that there is a body here, that it has been carrying something, that heat on the back of the neck is a form of kindness she can offer it. She turns off the water. The meeting is still in two hours. The reading is still due. Her neck aches less. She gets dressed and opens her laptop and the cost of the ten minutes is the ten minutes, which she would spend again.

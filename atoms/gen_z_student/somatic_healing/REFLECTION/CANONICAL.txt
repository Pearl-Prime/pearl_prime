## REFLECTION v01
---

---
Your shoulders have been on duty since orientation. Drop them now. Feel the distance open between your ears and your shoulder blades. That gap is real estate your body forgot it owned. The neck stretches into space it has not occupied in months.
Hold the tilt. The left side of your neck pulls — a line of tension from behind the ear to the top of the shoulder. That line is the cable your body strung to keep your head upright during all-night study sessions and 8 AM lectures and difficult conversations with your parents over FaceTime. The cable did its job. It held.
Switch sides. Tilt left. Feel the right cable stretch. Notice how the shoulders want to creep back up — the body defaults to guard position the moment attention wanders. Catch them. Press them down. The distance between ear and shoulder is a measurement of how much tension you are willing to release right now.
You do not need to release all of it. One inch of drop changes the blood flow to the neck. One stretch reminds the muscles that they have a resting length, not just a working one.

## REFLECTION v02
---

---
Your jaw just released a grip it has been holding since midterms. Notice the weight of the lower jaw hanging from the hinge. The masseter muscle — the strongest muscle in the human body by some measures — just clocked out for the first time in weeks.
The tongue drops. You did not realize it was pressed to the roof of your mouth. That press is a reflex — the tongue braces against the palate during concentration, during stress, during the nightly scroll through notifications before sleep. It pushes upward as if holding the skull together from the inside.
Let it rest behind the lower teeth. Feel the slack in the jaw. The lower face softens. The temples release pressure you thought was a headache but was actually muscular. The jaw and the skull and the temples form a tension triangle — lock one corner and the other two follow.
You just unlocked one corner. The triangle loosens. The face feels five degrees lighter. This is not relaxation in the spa sense. This is a body part returning to neutral after months of unnecessary effort.

## REFLECTION v03
---

---
The chest hand moves. The belly hand stays still. This is the breathing pattern of a body in sustained alert — air enters the upper chamber, cycles fast, exits fast. The diaphragm does not participate. The belly remains flat and guarded.
Push the breath lower. Inhale toward the belly hand. Force the belly to push outward against your palm. The first attempt may fail. The second might produce a flicker of movement. The diaphragm is not broken. It is dormant — a muscle that stopped receiving the signal to contract because the upper chest handled the emergency shifts alone.
Your body chose this pattern for good reason. Shallow breathing keeps the system in ready mode — prepared for the next alert, the next deadline, the next notification vibration against the desk. The pattern worked. But the cost accumulated: tight ribs, compressed stomach, sleep that never reaches deep cycle.
The belly hand moves now. Even a centimeter of rise proves the diaphragm still fires. Breathe into that hand five more times. Each breath trains the signal path back online.

## REFLECTION v04
---

---
Your hips adjusted before you finished reading the instruction. The left hip locked to bear your full weight. The right hip released. This is how quickly the body reorganizes — faster than thought, faster than decision.
Your hips spend most of the academic day locked at ninety degrees. Seated in lecture. Seated in the library. Seated on the dorm bed. The joint that evolved for walking, running, climbing, and squatting spends its college years folded in a chair. The hip flexors shorten. The glutes forget their job description. The lower back fills in for muscles that went dormant.
Stand on both feet again. Press your hips forward an inch. Feel the front of the hips stretch — the hip flexors pulling against a length they abandoned. That pull is not injury. It is a muscle remembering its full range.
Your hips carry you between every class, every meal, every social event. Give them thirty seconds of range each hour. The seated position will not destroy them. But the stretch reminds them they are hinges, not brackets.

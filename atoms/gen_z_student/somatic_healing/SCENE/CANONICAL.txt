## SCENE v01
---

---
You sit in the library staring at the same paragraph for ten minutes. Your neck aches. Your shoulders hover near your ears. Your breath lives in the top two inches of your lungs. Press your palms flat on the textbook. Feel the page under your skin. Drop your shoulders one inch. Feel the muscles release like a held fist opening. The paragraph did not change. Your body's relationship to the chair did.

The campus bus lurches through a turn. Your hand grips the overhead bar. Your jaw clenches with the motion. Your whole body braces against the movement. Release your jaw. Feel your teeth separate. Let your tongue drop from the roof of your mouth. The bus turns. Your body sways. Swaying is not falling. Your hips absorb the motion the way a tree bends in wind. Rigid breaks. Flexible survives.

You lie in your dorm bed at midnight. Your body is exhausted but your legs twitch. Your calves buzz with electricity that has nowhere to go. Your fists ball under the pillow. Press your heels into the mattress. Hard. Hold for five seconds. Release. Feel the muscles go slack. The buzz drains downward. Your legs stored the day's tension like batteries. The press-and-release discharges them.

The dining hall noise hits your nervous system like a wall. Forks scraping plates. Chairs dragging. A hundred conversations overlapping. Your shoulders rise. Your stomach tightens. Your breath goes shallow. Place your hand on the table. Feel the vibration of the room through the wood. Press your feet into the floor. The noise is sound, not threat. Your body learned to armor against sensory input. The armor weighs more than the noise.

You walk across campus after a difficult exam. Your stride is choppy. Your hips lock. Your arms hang stiff at your sides. Stop walking. Stand still for three seconds. Feel your weight distribute between both feet. Roll your ankles in small circles. Feel the joint loosen. Start walking again. Your stride lengthens by an inch. Your arms swing. The exam tension stored itself in your gait. The pause returned your walk to your body.

Your roommate slams the apartment door. You flinch. Your whole body contracts: shoulders up, belly tight, hands clenched. The startle response fires and lingers. Place both hands on your stomach. Feel the muscles gripping. Breathe into the grip. Feel your belly push against your hands. The door slammed three seconds ago. Your body still braces ten seconds later. The breath tells the muscles the impact is over. Release arrives on the exhale.

Marcus fails his first calculus midterm. Not by a small margin. He stands in the hallway after getting the exam back and looks at the number at the top of the page and then looks at it again. He knows the difference between "I failed this exam" and "I am a failure." He knows this difference intellectually. In his body, right now, in this hallway, the distinction is not available. The number at the top of the page has organized itself into a statement about what he is rather than what happened on Tuesday. He folds the exam and puts it in his bag. He walks to his next class. He does not tell anyone. He sits in the lecture hall and takes notes and the number sits in the bag beside him and radiates the thing it's become. After class, he walks to the tutoring center. He does not know yet if this will help. He is going anyway.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02

MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Selin is dropped from a student organization she spent two semesters trying to join. The email is brief and professional. She reads it twice. Her throat tightens. She was selected for the final round. She made it to the interview. She prepared for two weeks. The email says the committee was impressed but went in a different direction. The different direction is not her. She closes her laptop and stares at the wall. The shame runs its argument: if she were more impressive, they would have chosen her. If she were smarter, more prepared, more something, the direction would have included her. She knows the committee's decision is not a measurement of her worth. She cannot make herself know this right now. She opens her laptop again and deletes the email and then goes to the deleted folder and reads it once more. Her hands are cold. The afternoon is long.

## TURNING_POINT v03
---
path: story_atoms/gen_z_student/self_worth/shame/turning_point/micro/v03.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Arjun talks to his advisor about the grade that's been sitting in him wrong for a month. He had framed it to himself as a logistics question—"I want to understand the rubric"—but in the room, sitting across from his advisor, the real question surfaces: "I'm struggling to believe I belong in this program after that result." His advisor is quiet for a moment and then talks for a while. Arjun listens. His hands are flat on the table. He cannot tell if the conversation is helping in real time. He thanks his advisor and walks out. On the campus steps, he stands in the afternoon light and something shifts by a small fraction. Not the grade. Not the shame. His posture. He straightens his shoulders. The shame is still there, still running its assessment of him. But he said it out loud to someone who didn't agree with it. That costs him the story that he was the only one who knew the truth.

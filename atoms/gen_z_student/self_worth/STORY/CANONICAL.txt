IDENTITY_STAGE: erosion
COST_TYPE: social_withdrawal
COST_INTENSITY: high
STORY_TYPE: character_study
STORY_TYPE_SOURCE: heuristic_v1
---
Mia sits cross-legged on her dorm bed at 11:47 PM. Her phone glows against her face. She scrolls through a former lab partner's LinkedIn update — research fellowship at MIT, full funding. Her chest sinks. She puts the phone down. Picks it up again.
She used to raise her hand in organic chemistry. She used to walk into office hours like she belonged there. Now her throat tightens every time she opens a classroom door. Her shoulders curl inward before she reaches her seat.
The comparison started small. One post, one pang. Then it became a ritual. Every night, Mia catalogs evidence that she falls short. Her stomach knots tighter with each swipe. She stops texting her study group. She eats lunch alone in the library stairwell because the dining hall holds too many people who seem certain of themselves.
Her grades stay decent. Nobody notices the withdrawal. That makes it worse. Her ribcage feels compressed, like her lungs forgot how to fully expand. She writes "I am enough" on a sticky note and presses it to her laptop. It peels off by Thursday.
The cost lands in her body first. Jaw clenched through lectures. Hands cold during exams. A permanent hunch that started as protection and became posture. Mia shrinks one scroll at a time, and the feed never runs out.

## STORY v02
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: external_validation_dependency
IDENTITY_STAGE: fragmentation
COST_TYPE: creative_paralysis
COST_INTENSITY: medium-high
STORY_TYPE: character_study
STORY_TYPE_SOURCE: heuristic_v1
---
Jordan opens a blank Google Doc for the third time today. The cursor blinks. Nothing comes. Two years ago, Jordan wrote short films that won campus awards. Now the document stays white.
It started when a classmate's short went viral on TikTok. Jordan's chest locked. Breathing went shallow. The thought landed hard: anything Jordan creates will get compared. So Jordan stops creating.
The paralysis spreads. Jordan skips the film club meeting. Then the next one. Then drops the screenwriting elective. Each withdrawal tightens the knot below the sternum. Jordan's shoulders stay pinned near the ears now, a permanent brace against judgment that has not arrived yet.
Jordan scrolls through other students' reels instead. Stomach drops with each one. The talent on screen looks effortless, which makes Jordan's effort feel like proof of inadequacy. Throat constricts. Jordan closes the laptop and lies flat on the dorm floor, spine pressed to carpet, staring at the ceiling.
Friends still call Jordan talented. The word bounces off. Jordan's body already decided the verdict — hands won't move toward the keyboard, gut clenches at the thought of sharing work. The creative instinct still pulses somewhere behind the ribs, but Jordan built a wall of comparison around it. Nothing gets in. Nothing gets out.

## STORY v03
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: achievement_addiction
IDENTITY_STAGE: hollowing
COST_TYPE: identity_collapse
COST_INTENSITY: high
STORY_TYPE: character_study
STORY_TYPE_SOURCE: heuristic_v1
---
Priya checks her GPA tracker before brushing her teeth. 3.91. Her stomach tightens. Two classmates in her pre-med cohort carry a 4.0. She feels the number like a bruise on her sternum.
She stacks achievements the way other people stack plates. Dean's list. Research assistant. Hospital volunteer. Each one lands and the relief lasts forty seconds before the next comparison swallows it whole. Her chest never fully loosens.
Between classes, Priya stands in the campus bathroom and grips the sink. Her knuckles go white. She watches her face in the mirror and tries to locate the version of herself that exists outside the transcript. Nothing surfaces. Her jaw aches from nighttime clenching she does not remember.
She earns another A. Posts it to her story. Watches the view count. When it plateaus, her throat closes. The achievement only matters if it registers on someone else's screen. Without the external signal, Priya's ribs feel hollow — a cathedral with no congregation.
Her roommate asks what she does for fun. Priya opens her mouth. Closes it. The question sits in her chest like a stone. She cannot name a single thing she enjoys that does not produce a metric. Her body carries the weight of an identity built entirely from output. The foundation cracks in silence.

## STORY v04
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: impostor_internalization
IDENTITY_STAGE: masking
COST_TYPE: relationship_erosion
COST_INTENSITY: medium
STORY_TYPE: character_study
STORY_TYPE_SOURCE: heuristic_v1
---
Dex laughs the loudest at the table. Nobody guesses the performance. Between jokes, Dex's stomach hardens into a fist. The laughter covers the sound of constant internal subtraction.
Dex got into the honors program on a strong essay. Now, surrounded by students who seem to speak fluently in theories Dex never encountered, the chest tightens daily. Dex memorizes phrases from readings and repeats them in seminar like borrowed clothes that never quite fit.
After class, Dex walks back to the dorm and the mask drops. Shoulders collapse. Breathing goes ragged. Dex sits on the bed and scrolls through classmates' posts — conference presentations, published papers, casual brilliance broadcast in 280 characters. Each one lands like a verdict.
Dex stops inviting people to hang out. The risk of being seen up close feels too high. Friends text less. The phone goes quieter. Dex tells the mirror this proves nothing, but the body disagrees — hands tremble during presentations now, voice catches on the first sentence of every contribution.
The loneliest part: Dex knows the mask works. Professors praise the participation. Peers respect the humor. But behind the ribs, Dex carries the certainty that one honest moment will collapse the whole structure. So the performance continues, and the distance grows.

## STORY v05
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: scarcity_belief
IDENTITY_STAGE: contraction
COST_TYPE: opportunity_avoidance
COST_INTENSITY: medium-high
STORY_TYPE: character_study
STORY_TYPE_SOURCE: heuristic_v1
---
Kai stares at the internship application for fourteen minutes. The cursor sits in the "Why are you a strong candidate" box. Kai's fingers hover above the keyboard. Nothing types. The chest compresses.
The belief runs deep: there is only so much success to go around, and other people already claimed their share. Kai watches a classmate celebrate an offer on Instagram. Throat tightens. Shoulders fold. The application tab stays open for three days, then Kai closes it without submitting.
This pattern started sophomore year. A rejection email from a summer program arrived the same day a friend posted their acceptance. Kai's stomach dropped through the floor. The body learned the lesson before the mind named it: trying leads to public evidence of falling short.
Now Kai avoids anything with a visible outcome. Drops the leadership application. Skips the career fair. Sits in the back row. The world gets smaller one avoided opportunity at a time. Kai's posture mirrors the contraction — hunched in the library carrel, spine curved, chin tucked.
Kai's advisor notices the withdrawal and sends an email. Kai reads it, feels the ribs clench, and does not reply. The cost of being seen by someone who expects potential feels heavier than the cost of disappearing. So Kai keeps shrinking the map of what counts as safe.

## STORY v06
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: perfectionism_spiral
IDENTITY_STAGE: paralysis
COST_TYPE: health_deterioration
COST_INTENSITY: high
STORY_TYPE: character_study
STORY_TYPE_SOURCE: heuristic_v1
---
Sana rewrites the same paragraph for the sixth hour. Her eyes sting. Her neck locked into a forward crane two hours ago. The essay is due at midnight. She deletes the paragraph again.
The standard lives inside her like a second skeleton. Every sentence gets measured against the best writing she has ever read online. Her stomach churns. Her jaw clenches. The gap between what she produces and what she imagines she should produce widens with every keystroke.
Sana skips dinner. Then breakfast. Her body runs on cold brew and the specific anxiety that lives behind her breastbone. She submits the essay at 11:58 PM and immediately feels the crash — not relief, but emptiness. Her hands shake. The work is out, and now it belongs to someone else's judgment.
She checks the grade portal seventeen times the next day. Her throat tightens each time the page loads. When the A-minus appears, her chest does not open. It constricts. The minus sign weighs more than the A.
Sana's roommate finds her crying in the shower at 2 AM. Not dramatic sobs — a quiet leak, shoulders shaking, water masking the sound. Her body processes what her mind refuses to name: the version of success she chases does not include rest, satisfaction, or a finish line. It only includes the next assignment and the next chance to fall short of the image in her head.

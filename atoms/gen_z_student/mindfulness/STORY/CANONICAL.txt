Priya sits in the back of Intro Psych with her laptop open to notes and, behind it, a second tab of the group chat about tonight's plans. She tells herself she's doing both. Forty minutes in, the professor cold-calls her. She looks up and the question hangs there and she has nothing — not confusion, just absence, like she was never actually in the room to receive it. She mumbles something. He moves on, kind about it, and that kindness is worse. Walking out, she checks: forty-one messages in the chat, zero of them essential, and zero memory of the lecture's second half. That night she tries something small. Next lecture, phone face-down in her bag, not on the desk. Not perfect — her mind still drifts twice — but both times she catches it and comes back, and for the first time in weeks she leaves a lecture able to say what it was actually about. Not enlightenment. Just a girl who was in the room for once.

## STORY v02
---
---
Marcus works the front desk at the rec center three nights a week, and he's gotten good at looking present while being anywhere else. Tonight a student asks him a simple question about locker rentals and Marcus answers wrong, distracted, still replaying a group project meeting where he said something dumb. The student has to ask twice. Something about the second ask lands differently than usual — the small embarrassment of being caught not-there. He doesn't overhaul his life. He just tries, for the rest of the shift, to actually look at the next person who walks up before answering. Five people. Five actual look-ups. It costs him nothing and by the end of the shift he notices he doesn't feel as fried as he usually does at close, because half his usual exhaustion, he realizes, was running two channels at once instead of one.

## STORY v03
---
---
Devon is failing to read the same twelve pages of his textbook for the fourth night running, in the library cubicle he's claimed as his, and every time he notices his eyes have moved without his brain, he gets furious at himself, which pulls him further out. Tonight, instead of the fury, he tries something a friend mentioned offhand: when you catch the drift, don't scold it, just say "back" in your head and re-land on the actual sentence. He does this maybe fifteen times in one page. Fifteen times feels like failure. But on page two, it's nine times. Page three, six. He doesn't finish the reading fully present — nobody does — but he finishes it having actually absorbed most of it, which is further than he's gotten all week, and it wasn't because he stopped drifting. It's because he got faster at coming back.

## STORY v04
---
---
Aisha's roommate Ren has been talking for two straight minutes about a fight with her sister, and Aisha is nodding, and is also, simultaneously, drafting in her head the text she wants to send her internship recruiter. She catches herself mid-nod — she has no idea what Ren just said. She could fake it. Instead she says, "wait, I got pulled somewhere else, can you say that last part again" — bracing for it to be awkward. Ren just repeats it, unbothered. What surprises Aisha is how much lighter she feels admitting it instead of performing listening she wasn't doing. She starts doing this more: catching the drift out loud instead of hiding it. Her friendships don't get deeper because she has some new technique. They get deeper because she's actually there for more of them than she used to be.

## STORY v05
---
---
Tomas has his interview prep spreadsheet open, his internship application deadline in six hours, and instead of writing he's replaying a conversation with his roommate from three days ago, turning it over and over for no productive reason. He notices the loop the way you notice you've been driving five minutes without remembering the road. He doesn't try to force the loop to stop — he's tried that, it never works. He just picks one line of his cover letter and reads it out loud, slowly, forcing his attention onto the actual words on the actual screen. The old conversation tries to pull him back twice more in the next ten minutes. Both times he reads a line out loud again. He finishes the paragraph. It's not his best writing ever. It's finished, and it's his, and that's the whole difference between tonight and the last three nights.

## STORY v06
---
---
Nina waits tables — well, works the dining hall serving line — three lunch shifts a week, and she's noticed she can do the entire shift on autopilot: scoop, greet, scoop, greet, four hours gone, and afterward she feels weirdly more tired than shifts where she's mentally sharp. One Tuesday she experiments: she picks one thing — the actual sound of the ladle hitting the tray — to anchor to whenever she notices she's checked out. She still drifts constantly; campus jobs are built for drifting. But each time the ladle sound pulls her back, just for a few seconds, and by the end of the shift, for the first time, she remembers actual faces of actual students instead of a four-hour blur. She's not transformed. She just spent four hours being somewhat more here than gone, and it changes how tired "tired" feels.

## STORY v07
---
---
Jae has a 300-level seminar where the professor talks in long unbroken paragraphs, and Jae's found that fifteen minutes in, his mind reliably drifts to a rotating cast of worries — his GPA, his ex, whether he left his straightener on. He used to think this meant the class was boring or he had a bad brain. What changes things is smaller than he expects: he starts keeping one finger pressed lightly on the edge of his notebook, and every time he notices he's drifted, he presses harder for one second before writing the next thing the professor says, whether or not it's important. The finger-press isn't magic. It's just a cue his body understands faster than his thoughts do. By week four he's not drifting less. He's returning faster, and the seminar stops being a blur he sits through and starts being something he can actually recall on the walk home.

## STORY v08
---
---
Lily's family calls every Sunday and every Sunday she half-listens while doing something else — folding laundry, scrolling, once even doing a problem set mid-call. Her mom, on a random Sunday, says "you haven't heard a word I said, have you," not even annoyed, just tired. Lily denies it and then, walking to her next class, has to admit she genuinely couldn't repeat the last five minutes. The next Sunday she tries an experiment: no second task, phone in the other room, just the call. It's harder than she expects — her attention still wants to leave four or five times in twenty minutes. But she notices each pull and comes back, and at the end her mom says, unprompted, "that was a nice talk," and Lily realizes her mom could feel the difference even without being told what changed.

## STORY v09
---
---
Oscar's lab partner Fei has started calling him out — gently, but pointedly — every time Oscar asks her to repeat an instruction he clearly wasn't listening to the first time. It happens three lab sessions in a row. The fourth session, before they start, Oscar says something he's never said to a classmate: "I've been checked out a lot, I'm going to actually try to be here today." It feels vulnerable and a little dumb to announce. But naming it seems to work almost like a promise he can't quietly break. He catches himself drifting twice during the two-hour lab and both times returns immediately, cued by his own words from twenty minutes earlier. Fei doesn't say anything about the difference. She just doesn't have to repeat herself once, and that, to Oscar, is proof enough.

## STORY v10
---
---
Camille takes the same fifteen-minute walk to campus every day and has never once arrived remembering the walk — she arrives having relived some argument or rehearsed some conversation that hasn't happened yet. One morning, half as an experiment and half out of boredom with her own mental reruns, she decides to try naming five things she can actually see on the route. She only makes it through two before her mind drags her back to the rehearsed conversation. She doesn't fight it — she just starts the list over. By the third attempt she gets to five. She arrives at her first class having actually seen the campus for six minutes of a fifteen-minute walk, which sounds small until she realizes that's six more minutes than she's had in the entire semester.

## STORY v11
---
---
Ben's internship interview is over video, in his dorm room, and mid-interview his mind starts narrating a running commentary on how badly he's doing, which pulls him out of actually hearing the interviewer's next question. He asks her to repeat it — twice — and hates himself a little each time. Afterward, debriefing with his roommate, he realizes the problem wasn't nerves exactly. It was that the moment he noticed he was doing badly, he left the room mentally to go argue with himself about it, which guaranteed he'd keep doing badly. He writes himself one instruction for next time, taped above his desk: notice the spiral, then look at the actual person's actual face. Next interview, the spiral starts on schedule around minute ten. He looks at the interviewer's face. He comes back. He doesn't get every question perfect, but he's there for all of them, which he wasn't last time.

## STORY v12
---
---
Sofia's group chat with her floor is a constant low hum of notifications, and she's noticed she checks it mid-sentence, mid-lecture, mid-hug-hello with a friend she hasn't seen in weeks. The floor friend visiting for the weekend actually says out loud: "you've checked your phone four times since I got here twenty minutes ago." Sofia is embarrassed but grateful someone said it. She doesn't delete the app or do anything dramatic. She just puts the phone in her backpack, zipped, for the rest of the visit. She notices the itch to check it at least six times in an hour. Each time, she notices the itch and doesn't act on it, and stays in the actual conversation instead. Her friend, at the end of the visit, says it felt like the best hang they'd had all year. Nothing else changed except where Sofia's attention was.

## STORY v13

---
Malik has a scholarship review meeting with his advisor, high stakes, and spends the first ten minutes of it mentally drafting his defense against questions she hasn't asked yet, missing half of what she's actually saying. She has to stop and say, "Malik, are you with me?" — not unkindly, but it lands hard. He apologizes, and instead of doubling down on trying to seem composed, he says, "sorry, can we start that part again, I got in my head." She does. This time he catches his mind trying to run ahead twice, and both times pulls it back to her actual sentence. He leaves the meeting having heard the actual feedback instead of the feedback he was bracing for, and it turns out the two were different in a way that matters for his next semester.

## STORY v14
---
---
Grace studies at the same library table every night with her friend Yuki, both on laptops, ostensibly working, and Grace has noticed she can "study" for three hours and retain almost nothing, because half her brain is always monitoring Yuki's screen, the room, the door. One night she tries closing her laptop lid partway, just enough to remove the peripheral pull, and commits to one rule: when she notices she's monitoring the room instead of reading, she puts her finger under the actual sentence she's on. She does this constantly at first, almost every paragraph. By hour two it's every few pages. She doesn't study longer that night. She studies less time overall and remembers more of it, and for once walks out of the library not vaguely anxious about what she missed.

## STORY v15
---
---
Theo's part-time job is answering emails for the admissions office between classes, and he's mastered the art of typing responses while his mind is fully elsewhere, which is how he sends one reply with the wrong attachment to a prospective family. His supervisor flags it, not harshly, just directly. Theo's first instinct is to work faster to compensate. Instead he tries something counterintuitive: before hitting send on anything, he reads the actual email once, out loud in his head, word by word, no skimming. It costs him maybe ten extra seconds per email. He sends eleven emails that shift. Zero mistakes. He clocks out having done less total typing-while-elsewhere and more total actually-doing-the-task, and for the first time all semester doesn't lie awake wondering what he might have gotten wrong.

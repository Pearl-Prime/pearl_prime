## COMPRESSION v01
---
---
Half-presence costs double: you lose the content and pay switching-fatigue for a mind running two channels. Attention drifts toward unresolved charge (the fight, the grade, the notification) — that's default wiring, not weakness. Presence isn't a held state; nobody maintains it continuously. It's a repeated return — notice the drift, land on a concrete anchor (a sentence, a sound, a face), skip the self-criticism, continue. Fix: pick one anchor before the task, treat every noticed drift as a rep, not a failure. Frequency of drift matters less than speed of return.
---

## COMPRESSION v02
---
---
The mind goes to the group chat, the grade-worry, the replayed conversation because those carry unresolved charge; a lecture or a meal doesn't compete on its own. The result isn't efficient multitasking — it's serial thrashing, worse at both, exhausting in a specific way. The fix isn't "focus harder," which has no mechanism. It's: choose one small physical anchor in advance, notice when you've left without narrating it, and return to the anchor immediately. Presence is the return, repeated, not an unbroken state. Everyone who looks focused is just returning faster than you can see.

## COMPRESSION v03
---
---
Divided attention doesn't average two tasks — it alternates between them, losing ground on both and paying a fatigue tax on the switching itself. That's why half-present studying or half-present dinners leave you flatter than fully-present short versions would. The lever: an anchor (a word, a sensation, an actual face) chosen before the task, touched every time you notice a drift, with zero self-criticism attached. Presence isn't achieved and held — it's returned to, over and over, for as long as the task runs. Drift count is normal; return speed is the trainable variable.

## COMPRESSION v04
---
---
Your mind isn't malfunctioning when it leaves lecture for the group chat — unresolved tension out-competes stable material by default, in everyone. The cost is real though: comprehension needs continuous attention to build, and the fatigue of toggling between two partial tracks is worse than either task alone. Fix: stop trying to eliminate the pull. Pick one anchor, and every time you notice you've left, return to it once, without commentary. Presence is a return you make repeatedly, not a state you hold — the only skill here is getting faster at coming back.

---
Half-attention isn't a discount version of two tasks — it's a tax on both, paid in missed content and switching fatigue. Attention chases unresolved charge (a fight, a pending grade, an ambiguous text) over calm, ongoing material, which is normal wiring, not a personal failure. The mind drifts toward unresolved tension because that's what unanchored attention tracks by default; it's universal, not a personal deficit.

## INTEGRATION v01
---
---
Tomorrow, pick exactly one recurring block of your day — your worst lecture, or the dining hall, or the walk to campus — and give your attention one small, specific anchor before you start: the professor's actual next sentence, the actual taste of the first bite, the actual feeling of your feet on the pavement. Don't try to hold it the whole time. You won't, and that's fine — nobody does. Just notice every time your mind leaves for the group chat, the grade-worry, the replayed conversation, and each time you notice, return to the anchor once, without narrating how long you were gone or how bad it is that you drifted. That narration is itself another drift, so skip it entirely. If you catch yourself five times in fifty minutes, that's five successful reps, not five failures — the counting itself is proof the skill is working, because you can't return from a drift you never noticed. By the end of the block, don't grade yourself on how present you stayed. Grade yourself only on whether you came back each time you noticed you'd left. Do this once tomorrow in one single context. Don't try to fix your whole attention span in a day — that's not how the skill builds, and expecting it will just give you one more thing to feel behind on. One block, one anchor, however many returns it takes. That's the entire assignment, and it's small enough to actually finish.

## INTEGRATION v02
---
---
Tomorrow, before your hardest class or your next real conversation with your roommate, decide in advance what you'll use as your anchor — a breath, the weight of your pen, the other person's actual face. Then just start the class or the conversation like normal, expecting your mind to leave, because it will. When you notice it's gone — mid-sentence, three slides in, wherever — don't stop and assess how bad it was. Just touch the anchor once and keep going. You might catch it twice. You might catch it ten times. Both are fine; the number isn't the point, the returning is. If you skip a whole stretch without noticing at all, that's normal too — you'll notice eventually, and when you do, the return still counts, late or not. The only rule for tomorrow is this: no self-criticism when you notice you've drifted. Criticism is just a second, sneakier form of leaving — you go from the group chat to a mental courtroom about the group chat, which is no more present than the original drift. Just come back, plainly, the way you'd glance back at a road you'd drifted off of. Try this in one class, one meal, one conversation tomorrow — not your whole day. Small and specific is the only version of this that actually gets tried twice.

## INTEGRATION v03
---
---
Carry this into tomorrow as a single low-stakes experiment: pick one meal, and eat it with your phone somewhere you can't see it — not off, just out of the sightline — and give your attention one job, the actual taste of the food. You will lose the thread. Something will pull you — a memory, a worry about tomorrow's deadline, an itch to check your phone anyway. When you notice you've left, don't treat it as evidence the experiment failed. That noticing is the entire point; it's the moment the skill exists. Just return to the next bite, actually taste it, and keep eating. If you do this once and drift back nine times in fifteen minutes, that's nine reps, and nine reps in one meal is more attention-training than most people do in a week of trying to "focus better" in the abstract with no anchor at all. Don't extend this to every meal starting tomorrow — that's a recipe for abandoning it by Thursday. Just the one meal, tracked loosely, no scorecard. Notice what it's like to actually finish something and remember having done it, instead of looking down at an empty plate with no memory of the food being there at all.

## INTEGRATION v04
---
---
Tomorrow, in whichever class drains you the most, try this exactly once: sometime in the first ten minutes, silently name your anchor — it can be as simple as "the professor's voice" or "my hand on the pen." For the rest of the class, don't try to force unbroken attention; just watch for the moment you notice you've left, however far into the group chat or the grade-spiral or the replayed conversation you've gone. The instant you notice, return to the anchor, once, and keep taking notes. Don't count how many times this happens as a score against yourself — just let it happen as many times as it happens. At the end of class, before you stand up, ask yourself one question: could I say, in one sentence, what the last ten minutes were actually about? If yes, that's the entire experiment working. If not, that's fine too — it just means tomorrow's rep continues into the next lecture. This isn't about fixing your attention span across the whole semester starting today. It's about one class, one anchor, and whatever number of returns it takes to leave that room having actually been in it for some real portion of the time.

## INTEGRATION v05
---
---
Tomorrow, during your next group project meeting or roommate conversation, try this once: before you speak, actually look at the person talking — not your notes, not your phone, their face — and treat that as your anchor. When your mind starts drafting your response before they've finished, or drifts to something unrelated entirely, notice it and bring your eyes and attention back to their actual words, mid-sentence if you have to. You won't catch every drift. You'll probably miss some entirely and only realize afterward you weren't really there for a stretch of it. That's fine — the goal isn't a flawless meeting, it's more returns than you'd otherwise make. If the meeting is twenty minutes, aim for nothing except noticing when you've left and coming back, however many times that is. Afterward, don't grade the whole interaction. Just notice one thing: did you walk away actually knowing what was said, or reconstructing it from fragments like usual? That difference, however small it feels tomorrow, is the entire mechanism working, one meeting at a time, starting with just this one.

## INTEGRATION v06
---
---
Take this into tomorrow as one specific rep: your commute or walk to your first class, with headphones out or music off, just once. Pick one small anchor — the sound of your own footsteps, or five actual things you can see along the route. You'll get pulled almost immediately into some rehearsed conversation or replayed argument; that's guaranteed, not a sign anything's wrong. When you notice the pull, don't fight the content of the thought — just return your attention to the anchor, footsteps or the next visible thing, and keep walking. If your mind wanders off again ten seconds later, that's not a failure of the exercise, that's the exercise. Do this for one walk, not every walk starting today — one is honest, sustainable, and enough to notice something. When you arrive, ask yourself only this: did I see any part of this walk, or did I arrive having missed the entire thing again? Whatever the answer, it's data, not a verdict on you as a person, and it's the only feedback you need to try it again the next time you remember to.

## INTEGRATION v07
---
---
Tomorrow, at your campus job or wherever you do repetitive, autopilot-friendly work, try this exactly once during one shift: pick a small recurring physical motion — the scanner beep, the ladle hitting the tray, whatever repeats — and use it as your return cue. Every time that motion happens, treat it as a check-in: are you actually here, or have you been gone for the last several minutes without noticing? If you've been gone, don't spiral about the lost time. Just notice, and be present for the very next repetition of that motion, and then see how long you stay before drifting again. You might drift immediately. You might stay present for three or four cycles. Both outcomes are just information about where you're starting from, not a verdict on your focus as a person. By the end of the shift, notice one thing: do you feel the specific blank, foggy tiredness of having been gone the whole time, or do you feel differently, even slightly? That single comparison, felt rather than reasoned about, is the entire integration for tomorrow — nothing more elaborate required.

## INTEGRATION v08
---
---
Tomorrow night, during whatever studying you already have planned, try one small adjustment: before you open the textbook or the problem set, name out loud (even just under your breath) the actual sentence or problem you're about to start with. That's your anchor. When you notice, twenty seconds or twenty minutes later, that your eyes have kept moving but your comprehension checked out somewhere back at the top of the page, don't reread from the beginning in frustration. Just find the actual last sentence you can genuinely remember, and continue from there, present this time. You'll likely need to do this many times in one session — that's not the exercise failing, that's the exercise happening, over and over, exactly as it's supposed to. Don't measure success by how long you stayed unbroken. Measure it by whether, at the end of the session, you can actually summarize what you read in your own words, roughly, without flipping back through it first. If you can, the returns worked, however many of them there were.

## INTEGRATION v09
---
---
Try this exactly once tomorrow: during your next call or video chat with a parent, sibling, or someone you talk to out of habit more than full attention, close whatever else is open — the laptop, the second app, the problem set — for the duration of the call only. Give your attention one anchor: their actual voice, the specific words, not the general gist you're used to half-catching. You will still drift; a call is long and your mind is trained to multitask through it. When you notice you've drifted, don't perform having listened — just say, if needed, "sorry, can you say that again, I got distracted," and actually listen to the repeat. Do this as many times as it takes across one call. At the end, notice whether the other person seems different — lighter, more heard, less like they're talking into static — without asking them directly. That felt difference, even faint, is the entire proof the mechanism works, and it's worth one call's worth of effort tomorrow to actually notice it.

Repeat this every single time you notice you've left, for one session tomorrow, without trying to extend the practice to your whole day. The point isn't to eliminate the pull toward the group chat or the worry — that pull will still be there next week and the week after. The point is only to get faster at noticing you've left and quicker at coming back, one session at a time. By tomorrow evening, you should be able to name at least one specific detail from that session you wouldn't normally have caught. That detail is the whole proof the rep worked.

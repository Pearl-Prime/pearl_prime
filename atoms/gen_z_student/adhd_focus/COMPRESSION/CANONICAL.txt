## COMPRESSION v01
---
---
The intention-action gap means knowing you need to write the essay and actually writing it are two separate events, bridged only by a visible, physical first verb — not "write the essay" but "type the thesis I already said out loud." Attention runs on salience, not importance, so urgent tasks need to be made loud and visible, not just true. Time blindness means duration has to be externalized onto a timer, since your internal clock won't reliably announce two hours disappearing. Working memory drops un-anchored intentions, so capture every "remember to" the instant it occurs. The fix across all of it is the same shape: make the next step small, physical, and visible, and let that step carry you instead of waiting to feel ready.
---

## COMPRESSION v02
---
---
Repetitive academic tasks like flashcard decks or discussion posts don't get harder as you go — your attention just naturally disengages from what's already processed and reaches for novelty instead, which is why deck five feels heavier than deck one despite being identical work. The fix isn't more discipline; it's introducing a small deliberate change partway through — a different study spot, reading aloud — to refresh engagement without changing the actual task. Combined with batching a fixed number before a short break, this replaces the exhausting relaunch-every-item pattern with real momentum.
---

## COMPRESSION v03
---
---
Tasks with multiple valid entry points — a lab report, an essay — create an invisible extra decision layered on top of the actual writing, and that decision, not the writing, is usually where the freeze lives. Pre-deciding one fixed, arbitrary order and using it every time removes the choice permanently, trading an "optimal" order you'd have to re-derive constantly for a "good enough" order you never reconsider.
---

## COMPRESSION v04
---
---
Unbounded tasks — "study for finals," "work on the thesis" — have no natural edge, so they expand to fill any time you give them while also giving your initiation system nothing to grab onto, which is why weeks of low-grade dread can produce almost no actual work. A fabricated container — twenty-five minutes, one deliverable, nothing else required — works nearly as well as a real deadline, because what was missing was never time, it was a boundary.
---

## COMPRESSION v05
---
---
Emotional flooding fuses a task to the feeling about the task, so a dreaded professor email or hard conversation gets blocked not by not knowing what to say but by a wave of anticipated shame jamming the initiation mechanism itself. Reasoning about the task doesn't reach the flooded part of the system. The fix is shrinking the action below the flood's trigger threshold — read only the first line, no obligation to respond.
---

## COMPRESSION v06
---
---
The physical objects around you are more powerful initiation triggers than any plan held only in language, which is why "I'll do it tonight" fails reliably while a document left open in your direct path succeeds reliably. Building your environment — the portal pinned open, the textbook open on the desk, the app on your home screen — does the reminding your memory won't do consistently.
---

## COMPRESSION v07
---
---
Sitting down at your desk and actually starting the work are two separate, non-adjacent events for an ADHD brain, which is why you can be physically present with everything loaded and still not be working, sometimes for the whole evening. The fix is a deliberate bridging action — touch the keyboard, say the first sentence out loud — whose only job is converting proximity into motion.
---

## COMPRESSION v08
---
---
Working memory functions like a whiteboard being quietly erased rather than one that reliably holds information, so a plan formed at 9 a.m. is often genuinely gone by 3 p.m. Since college life runs almost entirely on exactly these un-anchored intentions, the fix is treating your own memory as unreliable by design and capturing every "remember to" onto a visible external surface the instant it appears.
---

## COMPRESSION v09
---
---
Task-switching costs compound across a college day built almost entirely of forced switches — a lecture, a club meeting, a group project text, a dining hall conversation — and each switch has an invisible rebuilding cost that isn't counted the way clock-time is. The fix is sequencing, not willpower: protect the highest-context task with a hard boundary right after your last class, before switching costs accumulate.
---

## COMPRESSION v10
---
---
Attention in ADHD runs on salience — what's loud, novel, or immediately felt — rather than on a rational importance ranking, which is why a genuinely low-stakes task can out-compete a real deadline simply by being more visible and more immediately satisfying right now. The fix is manufacturing salience for what actually matters: bright visible reminders, visible countdown timers, social accountability instead of private intentions.
---

## COMPRESSION v11
---
---
The gap between a task's difficulty and its actual completion rate is often explained by the absence of a first physical action, not by the size or complexity of the task itself — six flashcard decks and one essay both stall for the same reason: no visible five-second-doable first step sitting at the front of them. The universal fix is naming the literal first verb and doing only that, trusting that motion, once started, tends to continue on its own.
---

## COMPRESSION v12
---
---
Waiting for the first sentence to arrive polished before typing anything is a specific, common trap that looks like perfectionism but is really about treating a first draft as though it must already be a final draft. The fix is a structural permission slip: deliberately allow the first pass to be bad, typos and all, removing the option of waiting for a polished version that was never going to arrive before you began.

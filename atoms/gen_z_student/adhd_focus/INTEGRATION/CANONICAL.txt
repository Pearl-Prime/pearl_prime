## INTEGRATION v01
---
---
Tomorrow, before you sit down to whatever's been stalling — the essay, the problem set, the form you keep opening and closing — write the literal first physical verb on a sticky note before you start: not "write the essay" but "type the thesis I said out loud at dinner." Put it where you'll see it the second you open your laptop. You don't need the whole assignment solved tonight. You need one verb, executed once.
---

## INTEGRATION v02
---
---
Tomorrow, pick the one task on your list that has no natural stopping point — the flashcard decks, the discussion posts, the practice problems — and give it an artificial one before you begin: a timer, a fixed count of two, a progress bar you watch move. Your attention system needs to see the task shrinking, not just trust that it's shrinking somewhere in the abstract.
---

## INTEGRATION v03
---
---
Tomorrow, notice the first moment you catch yourself reorganizing your desk, scrolling your phone, or watching one more video instead of starting the real task, and instead of judging it, treat it as useful information: that's the freeze announcing itself. Just name the smallest true first action out loud — "open the doc," "read line one" — and do only that.
---

## INTEGRATION v04
---
---
Tomorrow, right after your last class, protect the next hour for whichever assignment needs the deepest, most sustained attention — usually the essay or the lab report — before the dining hall conversation, before the club meeting that will cost you the rebuild.
---

## INTEGRATION v05
---
---
Tomorrow, if there's an email, a form, or a conversation you've been avoiding because it feels bigger than it probably is, don't commit to handling all of it — just open it and read the first line, nothing else, no obligation to respond yet.
---

## INTEGRATION v06
---
---
Tomorrow, for whatever multi-entry-point task keeps stalling you — the lab report, the essay, the professor's email — pre-decide one arbitrary but fixed order you'll use every time, and stop re-litigating which piece to start with. Methods first, always. The apology first, always.
---

## INTEGRATION v07
---
---
Tomorrow, put the thing you keep forgetting to do — the form, the reply, the group project piece — somewhere physical and visible the moment it occurs to you, not at the end of the day when you try to remember everything at once. A sticky note on your laptop, a running note on your phone.
---

## INTEGRATION v08
---
---
Tomorrow, before you start the task you've been dreading, deliberately lower the bar for the first pass: type in lowercase, don't fix a single typo, write one sentence you know is clumsy on purpose. The freeze often isn't fear of the task — it's waiting for a polished opening line that was never going to arrive before you started typing anyway.
---

## INTEGRATION v09
---
---
Tomorrow, move the physical object connected to your hardest task into your direct path — the assignment portal pinned open in a browser tab, the textbook open on the desk instead of on a shelf, the flashcard app on your home screen.
---

## INTEGRATION v10
---
---
Tomorrow, when you sit down at your desk to finally do the work, notice if sitting down and starting feel like two separate events — because for your brain, they usually are. Build a small bridge between them: touch the keyboard, say the first sentence out loud, type one word you'll delete later.
---

## INTEGRATION v11
---
---
Tomorrow, if a repetitive task starts feeling heavier the further into it you get — deck five harder than deck one — introduce one small deliberate change partway through: a different spot in the library, a different pen, reading a few out loud.
---

## INTEGRATION v12
---
---
Tomorrow, before you start whatever open-ended task has been generating quiet dread without producing any actual work — studying for finals, the thesis proposal you keep meaning to start — give yourself a fake but firm container: twenty-five minutes, one deliverable, nothing more required.
---

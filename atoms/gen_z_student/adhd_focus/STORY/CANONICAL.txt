## STORY v01
---
---
Marcus sits with the essay argument clear in his head and the doc blank beside it. He reorganizes his desktop icons. He refills his water bottle. He reads three unrelated texts twice. Then his roommate, doing his own homework at the same table, says: "just write the thesis sentence you told me at dinner, word for word." Marcus types the sentence he said out loud two hours ago. The next sentence takes less time. By paragraph two his hands have found the rhythm and stopped asking his brain for permission first. He finishes at one a.m., later than planned but done, and realizes the freeze was never about the whole essay. It was about the first sentence not existing yet on the page.

## STORY v02

---
Priya stares at the blank problem set until the cursor blink starts to feel aggressive. She gets up for a snack she doesn't want. She straightens her desk into a neater desk. Her lab partner texts: "just redo the TA's example with different numbers, that's basically problem one." Priya opens her notes from lecture, copies the TA's method, and swaps in her own numbers. It works. Problem two follows the same shape. By problem four her hands have found the rhythm. She finishes with time to spare before her lecture, and understands the problem set was never missing information — the first line of work was.

## STORY v03
---
---
Dana opens the flashcard app and picks the smallest deck, telling herself she'll just look, no real studying yet, just glancing through it. She glances through it. Without deciding to, she starts actually testing herself on card three. She has started studying without a moment where she decided to start studying. By deck two she's in a groove. She gets through four of six decks before midnight, more than she expected, and understands: she wasn't avoiding studying. She was avoiding the moment of opening the app with full intent to grind through it. Glancing first removed that moment.

## STORY v04
---
---
Theo rereads his own research notes for the eleventh minute and his teammate, on a call for the group project, says, "just say out loud what your slide is supposed to say, I'll type it while you talk." Theo talks. His teammate types almost exactly what he says. The slide fills in from his own spoken explanation, not from some imagined perfect version of the section. It's done in fifteen minutes once someone else's fingers moved while his mouth did the explaining he was already capable of doing.

## STORY v05
---
---
James opens the response box a fourth time and, instead of trying to write the whole response, types one blunt sentence: "I think the author's argument falls apart in chapter three because the evidence doesn't match the claim." True, easy, already fully formed in his head. He writes the next sentence expanding on it. By sentence four he's not stopping between them anymore. He submits with five minutes to spare, more than he's written in the ten minutes he usually spends staring at the box before section starts.

## STORY v06
---
---
Renee's roommate asks, casually, what's actually stopping her from replying to the advisor's email. Renee explains out loud which classes she wants and why, easily, in one breath — she's known for over a week. Her roommate says, "so just type that." Renee opens the email and types almost exactly what she just said out loud. She hits send before she can reread it a sixth time. The reply she'd been dreading for eight days took ninety seconds once the words existed somewhere first.

Ms. Whitfield rereads the last worksheet question for the fourth time and doodles instead of answering. Her professor, walking by, says, "just write one word, you can build the sentence around it after." Ms. Whitfield writes "osmosis." ## STORY v09
---
---
Priya opens the financial aid draft a fifth time, this time with a visible timer set for twenty minutes on her phone. Seeing the countdown changes something — there's now an end point she can see, not just a task with no edges. She fills in the first missing field before checking the timer again. Twelve minutes left. She keeps going. When the timer rings she has the whole form done, rougher in places than she'd like but real. She hadn't needed more time or more energy. She'd needed the invisible task to become a visible, shrinking number she could watch move.

## STORY v10
---
---
Marcus opens the professor's email again — the one about a missed deadline, unread for six days — and, instead of imagining the whole confrontation, reads only the first line. It says: "Wanted to check if everything's okay, happy to give you an extension if you need one." Not the disciplinary conversation he'd built in his head. He writes back two sentences in ninety seconds and hits send before he can reread them six more times. The reply he'd been dreading for six days took less time than the dread itself had cost him each day it sat there.

---
Theo rereads the discussion prompt a fifth time and catches himself doing it — actually notices the loop for what it is. He says out loud, to his empty room, "I'm rereading instead of typing." Naming it out loud breaks something small. He types the first post badly, run-on sentences and all, and does not fix them. He does the next one. Somewhere around post two the pace picks up, and he submits all three with four minutes to spare before midnight.

James scrolls through an old group chat from last semester at eleven-forty at night, a chat that has nothing to do with tonight's peer review, and knows exactly what he's doing and why it isn't helping. He closes it. He texts his current group: "sending my feedback now, is there a specific section you want me to focus on." Renee logs into the housing portal a third time, this time with her phone timer set for five minutes, visible beside the keyboard. Diallo looks at the six empty sections in the shared group doc and scrolls his phone instead, then eats a snack, then finds himself staring at the doc anyway, laptop open, doing nothing. His roommate, doing homework nearby, says, "just do the section you already researched, you literally have the sources open in another tab." One section.

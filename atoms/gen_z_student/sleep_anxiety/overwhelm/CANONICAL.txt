
## RECOGNITION v01

Marcus is running on five nights of under six hours. His body has reached a new baseline where tired is just how things are. He sits in the library at 2 p.m. and the words on the screen swim slightly. He blinks and they stop. He has three assignments due this week, two shifts at the café, and a financial aid meeting tomorrow. His to-do list is not the problem. He has had lists like this before and executed them. The problem is that the list is being processed by a brain running on deficit, and every decision takes longer and every paragraph requires more re-reading and every email needs to be checked twice. He has been awake for sixteen hours. He needs six more hours to finish the reading. He calculates this against the hour he needs to sleep to function tomorrow. The calculation doesn't resolve. He keeps reading. His eyes ache.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02

MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Kofi finishes his problem set at 4 a.m. and sets his alarm for 8 a.m. He gets to his 9 a.m. lecture and cannot track the argument the professor is making. The concepts are ones he knows. The sleep deficit has put them behind a pane of glass. He writes notes that look right but he knows aren't going in. After class his friend asks if he wants to get lunch and Kofi says he has to study, which is true, but also because lunch requires social processing his brain doesn't have capacity for. He eats a granola bar at his desk and stares at the afternoon's reading. He reads the first page four times. The words are real words. They are just not making it through. He knows what four hours of sleep costs by now. He is paying it in increments across the afternoon. The reading is due tomorrow. He will finish it. He will not remember it.

## TURNING_POINT v03
---
path: story_atoms/gen_z_student/sleep_anxiety/overwhelm/turning_point/micro/v03.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Priya goes to bed at 10:30 p.m. with three things unfinished. She has calculated that a full night's sleep will make her more effective in the morning than another three hours of diminishing-return work tonight. This calculation is correct. Her body doesn't accept it. She lies in the dark and her mind runs the unfinished items: the draft section, the unanswered email, the chapter she hasn't started. She breathes slowly and deliberately. Her shoulders won't flatten against the mattress for the first thirty minutes. Then, in degrees, they do. She wakes at 6:30 a.m. and the unfinished items are where she left them and she starts the draft section with a speed she didn't have at 10 p.m. She finishes it before her 9 a.m. class. The email takes five minutes. The chapter she'll start tonight. The cost of last night's decision is the low-grade guilt she carried until she fell asleep. The benefit is what she built with her actual brain this morning.

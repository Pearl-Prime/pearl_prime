Dani wakes at 2 a.m. and cannot get back to sleep. The spiral starts: if she doesn't sleep, she'll be exhausted for her presentation. If she's exhausted, she'll fumble. If she fumbles, her professor will grade her down. If her grade drops, her GPA drops below the scholarship threshold. She is twenty-three minutes away from her presentation, which is in seven hours, and she has already arrived at losing her scholarship. Her palms are damp against the sheets. She turns over. The spiral continues in the new position. She reaches for her phone. She knows she shouldn't check her phone. She checks it anyway. It is 2:23 a.m. She reads two news articles to derail the spiral. The articles about tuition costs reenter the spiral at a new angle. She puts the phone down. The ceiling is there. She counts to ten. She counts to ten again.

Selin has been awake since 3 a.m. and it is now 4:45 a.m. and the spiral has covered: tomorrow's meeting with her thesis advisor, her unanswered emails from last week, whether she's in the right major, whether she's in the right school, whether the choice she made two years ago to transfer was the right one, her parents' financial situation, whether she will be able to pay rent after graduation, what she will do if she can't find a job in her field, and whether her field was the right field. She started with a thesis meeting and arrived at the entire architecture of her future. Her jaw aches from clenching. Her neck is rigid. She is exhausted and fully alert simultaneously. The spiral has been thorough. It has covered everything. There is nothing left to spiral about. She lies in the dark and waits for morning and the exhaustion and the alertness continue side by side.

## TURNING_POINT v03
---
path: story_atoms/gen_z_student/sleep_anxiety/spiral/turning_point/micro/v03.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Remy writes himself a letter at 3 a.m. He has been in the spiral for an hour. He addresses it to the version of himself at 8 a.m., when the day will have started and the night will have ended. He writes: "You made it. The thing you were spiraling about at 3 a.m. is happening now or it has already happened and you have more information than I do. I am writing from inside the worst of it. You are reading from after it." He folds the letter and puts it on his desk. His chest is still tight. The spiral is still running. But the letter is on the desk—something from the worst of the night addressed to the morning-self who will be okay. He closes his eyes. His breathing doesn't slow for another twenty minutes. At 3:43 a.m. something releases and he slides into sleep. He reads the letter at 8 a.m. and the tightness from the night is already fading, which is what nights do, and what he couldn't believe at 3.

## STORY v01
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: avoidance_loop
IDENTITY_STAGE: pre-contemplation
COST_TYPE: academic_performance
COST_INTENSITY: escalating
---
Mara opens TikTok at 11:30 PM, planning to scroll for ten minutes. Her jaw tightens the instant her thumb hovers over the lock button. She keeps scrolling. Her roommate's breathing slows into sleep rhythm across the room, and the contrast makes Mara's pulse spike — everyone else drifts off while she stays trapped in the bright rectangle.
She rolls onto her side. Her neck cramps from the angle but she holds the phone closer. A video about productivity hacks plays on mute. Her eyes burn and her temples throb but the alternative — dark room, loud thoughts — sends a cold spike through her sternum.
At 2:14 AM she types "how to stop thinking at night" into Google. She reads three articles. Her eyelids droop but her heart slams against her ribs every time she starts to fade. Her body jerks awake. She grabs the phone again.
The alarm hits at 7 AM. Mara's eyes feel packed with sand. She sits through organic chemistry with her pen motionless, the professor's voice arriving from underwater. Her hand shakes around her third coffee by noon. She tells her friend she just didn't sleep well. She does not mention the phone, the scrolling, the way her chest seizes when the room goes dark.
That night she opens TikTok at 11:15 PM. Fifteen minutes earlier than yesterday. Her jaw locks into position. The cycle tightens its grip.

## STORY v02
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: hypervigilance_pattern
IDENTITY_STAGE: contemplation
COST_TYPE: social_withdrawal
COST_INTENSITY: compounding
---
Jordan lies in bed at midnight and monitors his own breathing. In, hold, out — the YouTube video said 4-7-8 counts. His diaphragm hitches on the hold. His shoulders climb toward his ears. He starts the count over. His heart rate spikes at each restart.
His roommate Caleb asks if he's okay. Jordan says he's fine. His voice comes out thin and tight. He turns toward the wall and presses his phone against his chest, feeling it vibrate with a notification he won't check because checking means admitting he's still awake.
By 1 AM Jordan's legs twitch under the sheets. His eyes track a crack in the ceiling paint. His stomach churns acid from the dining hall pizza that sits like concrete behind his ribs. He picks up the phone and opens the sleep tracker app. It shows four hours and twelve minutes of sleep debt from this week alone. The number makes his throat close.
He starts skipping the Wednesday night study group. Too tired. He eats dinner alone in his room because conversation demands energy his body stopped manufacturing. His texts back to friends shrink to single words: "yeah," "sure," "busy." His mother calls on Sunday and he lets it ring because his voice shakes when he's this depleted.
Jordan downloads another sleep app at 2:30 AM on Thursday. His hands tremble around the phone. He sets a new alarm. He does not sleep.

## STORY v03
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: catastrophic_projection
IDENTITY_STAGE: pre-contemplation
COST_TYPE: physical_health
COST_INTENSITY: acute
---
Priya's chest seizes at 11 PM like clockwork. She stands in the communal bathroom brushing her teeth and watches the tension arrive in the mirror — her shoulders hitch, her brow furrows, her grip on the toothbrush whitens her knuckles. Bedtime triggers her body like a fire alarm.
Back in her single dorm room she sits on the bed with her laptop open. Her fingers hover over an essay due Friday but the words blur. Her brain launches forward: fail this paper, fail the class, lose the scholarship, move back home. Each thought lands like a punch behind her sternum.
She slams the laptop shut. Her hands shake. She picks up her phone and opens Instagram. A friend posts a photo from a bar, smiling, relaxed. Priya's stomach drops. She scrolls faster. Her neck aches from the angle and a headache blooms at the base of her skull.
At 3 AM she gets up to pee and catches her reflection — dark circles carved under her eyes, skin dull and tight. Her heart hammers at the sight. She drinks water from the tap and her hand trembles against the faucet.
She sleeps from 4:15 to 6:45. Her body wakes feeling beaten. Her joints ache like she ran a marathon in her sleep. She dry-swallows ibuprofen and walks to her 8 AM lecture on legs that feel borrowed from someone else.

## STORY v04
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: control_seeking
IDENTITY_STAGE: preparation
COST_TYPE: emotional_regulation
COST_INTENSITY: chronic
---
Dev builds a sleep fortress every night. Blackout curtains. White noise app. Weighted blanket. Chamomile tea cooling on the desk. Blue-light glasses folded on the nightstand. He performs each step like a ritual, and his hands steady during the routine. The moment he lies down and turns off the lamp, his pulse explodes.
His chest tightens. His fingers grip the edge of the weighted blanket. He counts backward from one hundred but loses track at eighty-six because his brain replays a conversation with his advisor — the pause before she said "concerning." His jaw clenches so hard his ears ring.
He reaches for the phone to check the time. 12:07 AM. He puts it back. 12:09 — he checks again. His stomach knots at the two-minute gap. At 12:34 he opens Reddit and reads threads about insomnia. His eyes water from the screen glare.
The more tools Dev adds to his routine, the more his body resists. The weighted blanket makes him sweat. The white noise irritates his ears. Each failed solution stacks another layer of frustration in his chest. He lies awake cataloging everything he tried and everything that failed.
His girlfriend notices he snaps at small things — a loud chew, a door left open. Dev feels the irritability coil behind his ribs all day. He apologizes, then loses his temper again by evening. Sleep debt compounds into emotional debt. His hands shake when he makes the chamomile tea at 10 PM.

## STORY v05
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: identity_fusion
IDENTITY_STAGE: contemplation
COST_TYPE: identity_erosion
COST_INTENSITY: escalating
---
Aisha tells people she's a night owl. Her throat tightens around the lie every time. She stays up until 4 AM not because she thrives in darkness but because her body floods with adrenaline the moment she considers sleep. Her heart pounds. Her palms slick with sweat against the phone case.
She built her schedule around the pattern — no classes before noon, assignments done between midnight and dawn, social life compressed into afternoon hours. Her friends admire her discipline. Her advisor praises her late-night coding commits on GitHub. The identity fits like armor over a wound.
But at 3 AM in her apartment bedroom, Aisha's hands tremble over the keyboard. Her vision doubles from exhaustion. She blinks hard and her eyes sting like someone pressed sand into them. She stands, paces three steps to the wall and back, sits down. Her calves cramp from sitting cross-legged for six hours.
She tries sleeping at midnight once, on a Thursday. Her body rejects it — heart slamming, throat closing, legs restless under the sheets. She lasts forty minutes before retreating to her desk. The relief of returning to the screen floods her limbs like warm water.
Aisha's doctor asks about her sleep. She says she averages six hours. The real number hovers near four. Her tongue feels thick with the lie. Her blood pressure reading prints high. She folds the paper into her bag and walks into the afternoon sun, squinting, her bones heavy with a tiredness she no longer knows how to name.

## STORY v06
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: emotional_suppression
IDENTITY_STAGE: pre-contemplation
COST_TYPE: relationship_damage
COST_INTENSITY: compounding
---
Kai handles everything during the day. Classes, part-time job at the campus bookstore, study sessions, group chats — his hands stay steady, his voice stays even. At 11 PM he closes his bedroom door in the shared apartment and his chest caves in like a punctured tire.
His roommates hear him through the thin wall, tapping at his phone. They stopped asking if he's okay after he said "just restless" three times in one week. His jaw locks the moment he lies flat. His body reads the horizontal position as a threat signal and floods him with cortisol. He feels the heat rise through his neck and face.
He turns onto his stomach. His heart thuds against the mattress. He opens YouTube and watches video essays about topics he won't remember — a three-hour documentary about bridges, a ranking of fast food chains. The content doesn't matter. The sound fills the space where his thoughts multiply.
By 2 AM his eyes stream tears from exhaustion and screen glare. He wipes them on the pillowcase and keeps watching. His throat aches with something he won't name. Every emotion he managed during the day pools in his chest now, pressing against his ribs.
His best friend, Dani, stops inviting him to morning hikes. Kai cancels too often, voice raspy with four hours of sleep. He sees the group photos later on Instagram — everyone smiling at a trailhead at 7 AM — and his stomach drops. He double-taps the photo and puts his phone face-down. That night he opens YouTube at 10:45 PM. Earlier again.

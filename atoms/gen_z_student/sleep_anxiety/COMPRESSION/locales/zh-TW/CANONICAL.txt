## COMPRESSION v01
---

---
The phone at 2 AM is not rest. It is a shield against the dark and the thoughts that live there. Your jaw locks, your chest tightens, your eyes burn — and you keep scrolling because stopping means feeling all of it. Sleep anxiety runs a loop: exhaust the body, terrify the mind, reach for the screen. Breaking it starts at the body. Unclench the jaw. Release the grip. Let the belly breathe before the brain argues.
---

## COMPRESSION v02
---

---
Your nervous system learned to treat bedtime as a threat. The cues stack — dark room, silence, horizontal body — and your heart rate spikes on cue. This is conditioning, not character. The body fires its alarm before a single anxious thought forms. Retraining targets the body first: progressive muscle release, slow exhale, feet pressed flat against the mattress. New physical data overwrites the old alarm. Each calm night deposits one clean signal into the nervous system's memory.
---

## COMPRESSION v03
---

---
Sleep debt hides in plain sight. The shaking hands around the coffee cup. The lecture that passes through your ears like static. The sharp text you sent at noon because your emotional buffer ran dry at 4 AM. The costs accumulate below awareness while the phone screen glows through another night. Accurate accounting — naming each cost out loud — pulls the pattern out of autopilot. The body keeps the ledger. Start reading it.
---

## COMPRESSION v04
---

---
The identity hardens fast. "I'm a night owl." "My brain just doesn't shut off." These labels feel stable but they seal the exit. A nervous system that activates at bedtime acquired that response — it carries a start date. Swapping permanent language for temporal language opens the door: "currently," "this semester," "since moving to the dorm." Precision breaks the identity seal. A pattern you learned is a pattern your body can unlearn through new physical experience.
---

## COMPRESSION v05
---

---
Every failed sleep hack adds another brick to the frustration wall. The weighted blanket. The melatonin. The breathing app. Each abandoned tool teaches the nervous system one more lesson: nothing works. The shift is not a better tool — it is a different target. Stop aiming at sleep. Aim at the locked jaw, the shallow breath, the legs buzzing with adrenaline. Address the body's alarm state and sleep arrives as a side effect, not a conquered goal.
---

## COMPRESSION v06
---

---
The scroll numbs the surface while the body keeps score underneath. Your neck cramps from the angle. Your eyes stream tears you blame on screen glare. Your chest holds a pressure you refuse to name. The phone fills the silence between consciousness and the anxious dark — but silence is where the nervous system resets. One move changes the sequence: put the phone down, name the body state out loud, and let the sensation exist for thirty seconds without fixing it. The gap between stimulus and scroll is where recovery begins.

## PERMISSION v01
---

---
You have permission to sit in the dining hall alone without pretending to be busy. Set your phone face-down. Place your hands on the table. Feel the surface. A person eating a meal is a complete scene. No props required.

You have permission to leave a party early without inventing an excuse. Stand up. Feel your weight shift to your feet. Your body knows when it has had enough. Honor that signal. The door exists for departures too.

You have permission to say three words instead of thirty. Press your tongue to the roof of your mouth. Release it. Short answers carry the same validity as long ones. Brevity is not awkwardness. It is efficiency.

You have permission to let a conversation land badly and still walk away whole. Touch the back of your hand. Feel the tendons shift. One stumbled sentence does not fracture you. Your body keeps moving past the moment even when your mind replays it.

You have permission to decline a group invitation without guilt. Place your hand on your chest. Feel your breath move under your fingers. Solitude chosen is not isolation suffered. Your nervous system knows the difference.

You have permission to show up and say nothing. Grip the strap of your backpack. Feel the weight on your shoulder. Presence does not require performance. Standing in the room counts. Sitting in the room counts. Breathing in the room counts.

## PERMISSION v02
---

---
You have permission to arrive at the party fifteen minutes late. Walk slowly. Feel the night air. The fifteen minutes give the room shape before you enter it. The host will not register the timing. Your nervous system will register the avoided peak.

You have permission to text your friend "I am here, where are you" instead of scanning the room. Pull out the phone. Type. The scan was tax. The text is a shortcut. Shortcuts are allowed.

You have permission to bring a book to the cafeteria and read between bites. Open the page. Hold the spine. The book is not antisocial. It is a permission slip the body grants itself for solo eating in public. People who matter will recognize the move.

You have permission to leave the lecture if your face goes hot. Pack your bag silently. Walk out the back. The body is sending a clear signal. The lecture will be there in the recording. Your in-person attendance is not the only access point.

You have permission to introduce yourself with just your first name. Speak it. Stop. The full bio is not required at the cocktail party. The first name is a complete unit of information. The other person's follow-up will guide the rest.

You have permission to skip the icebreaker question. Smile. Pass. The icebreaker is theater. The room moves on within seven seconds. Your pass is one moment of micro-discomfort against thirty minutes of avoided forced disclosure.

## PERMISSION v03
---

---
You have permission to like solitude and not call it antisocial. Walk along the path. Feel your feet. Solitude is a recovery state for the nervous system that has been managing groups all day. Calling it antisocial pathologizes a healthy regulation move.

You have permission to make one new friend per semester instead of fifteen. Sit at the table with one person. Listen. Friendship is high-bandwidth. The bandwidth required for fifteen casual acquaintances would have left no room for one real one. Choose deep over wide.

You have permission to leave a thread in the group chat unanswered. Lock the phone. Set it face down. The thread will resolve without you. Your silence is not rude. It is bandwidth allocation. Most threads do not require every member's input.

You have permission to attend the event for forty-five minutes and call it a full attendance. Look at your watch. Stand up. Walk out. The hosts measured attendance in entry, not duration. You attended. You leave when your nervous system says.

You have permission to tell someone "I am socially fried" without explaining further. Touch your throat. Speak. Friends with any social experience will recognize the phrase. The explanation invites debate. The phrase is a closed unit of communication.

You have permission to skip the alumni networking event and fill the time with rest. Cancel the RSVP. Click confirm. The networking event will repeat next semester. Your nervous system is already running below baseline. Rest now. Network when capacity returns.

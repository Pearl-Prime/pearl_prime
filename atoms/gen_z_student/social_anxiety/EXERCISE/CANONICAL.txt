## EXERCISE v01
---

---
Stand at the dining hall entrance. Feel your feet on the floor. Notice the stomach drop — label it "drop" out loud or in your mind. Breathe once into the belly. Walk in before the second breath finishes. The drop stays. You move anyway. Repeat five meals in a row and log the intensity each time. The number trends down.

## EXERCISE v02
---

---
Write your answer in full on paper before class. Read it once silently. When the professor opens discussion, read the first sentence from your notes. Eyes on the page, not the room. The face flushes — let it. The sentence lands anyway. Next class, read two sentences. The week after, add one sentence from memory. Build the bridge plank by plank.
---

## EXERCISE v03
---

---
Before entering, place the phone in your back pocket. Hold something else — a cup, a key, a coin. Squeeze it once and release. The hands need a job that is not scrolling. Stand in the doorway for five full seconds. Count them. Then step through. The threshold is the hardest part. Mark it complete and move.
---

## EXERCISE v04
---

---
Send one message in the group chat before the meeting. A question, an observation, a link. Arrive with your voice already on the record. In the meeting, reference what you sent: "like I posted earlier." Those four words cost less because the thought already exists in the chat. The thin voice borrows strength from the typed one.
---

## EXERCISE v05
---

---
Set a timer for fifteen minutes before you swipe in. Eat for exactly that window. When the timer sounds, bus the tray and leave. The open-ended meal breeds dread — a bounded meal breeds structure. After one week of fifteen-minute meals, extend to twenty. The stomach learns that the room has an exit. Exits make rooms smaller.
---

## EXERCISE v06
---

---
After class, email the professor one thought you held back during discussion. Keep it to three sentences. Hit send within ten minutes of leaving the room while the thought still runs hot. The face flushes at the send button — notice it, press anyway. This builds a bridge between the silent classroom self and the articulate written self. Over weeks, the gap narrows.

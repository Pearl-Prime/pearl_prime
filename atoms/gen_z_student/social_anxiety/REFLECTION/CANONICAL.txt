## REFLECTION v01
---

---
The dining hall costs twelve dollars a swipe whether you walk in or not. The meal plan charges for presence, not comfort. Every time the stomach drops and the thumb opens a delivery app, the avoidance carries a price — forty dollars, fifty minutes of waiting, another evening alone with a container on the desk.
Track the ledger for one week. Count the meals dodged, the dollars rerouted, the hours spent alone because a room full of strangers felt like a room full of judges. The numbers do not lie and they do not comfort. They clarify.
Avoidance works in the moment. It floods relief into the bloodstream fast. But relief is not the same as safety, and safety is not the same as living. The dining hall doors will open again tomorrow. The stomach will drop again. The question is not how to stop the drop. The question is what happens in the three seconds after it lands.

## REFLECTION v02
---

---
Silence in a seminar room carries a tax nobody invoices. The grade stays intact — participation points dock slowly, invisibly, like a leak under the floor. But the real cost runs deeper. Every withheld answer builds a wall between the person in the chair and the person in the mind.
The face flushes. The throat seals. The answer dissolves unspoken. Afterward, the replay starts — six seconds of silence stretched across six hours of self-interrogation. The mind edits the footage, zooms in, adds a soundtrack of judgment that the room never actually played.
Notice the architecture. The flush arrives first. Then the freeze. Then the story. The story is the expensive part. "Everyone noticed." "They think I have nothing to say." "I looked foolish." These sentences cost more than any spoken answer ever could. The silence itself lasts seconds. The narrative lasts days. Separate the event from the echo and the tax shrinks.

## REFLECTION v03
---

---
The party version of you runs on a different operating system. It smiles on cue. It laughs at the right volume. It holds a cup and leans against a wall with manufactured ease. It performs "fine" so convincingly that no one checks the source code.
The hands grip the phone because the phone is the last honest object in the room. It does not require eye contact. It does not judge pauses. It offers an exit hatch disguised as a notification.
But the performance hollows over time. Every party survived through acting deepens the split between the public self and the private one. The gap widens until even genuine moments feel rehearsed.
The alternative is not radical vulnerability at a house party. Nobody owes strangers their full interior. The alternative is smaller. Arrive. Stay thirty minutes. Speak one honest sentence to one person. Leave when the body says leave — not when the anxiety says flee. Build a version of showing up that does not require a costume. The phone goes in the pocket. The hands learn to hang empty.

## REFLECTION v04
---

---
The voice thins in rooms but strengthens on screens. This is not a flaw to fix. It is information to use. The group doc at midnight holds paragraphs of clean thinking. The library meeting at 7 PM holds nine spoken words and a clenched jaw.
Both count. Both contribute. The culture of college rewards the loud thinker — the person who riffs in real time, who interrupts with half-formed ideas and polishes them in public. That model fits some brains and starves others.
Asynchronous contribution is not hiding. It is routing intelligence through its fastest channel. The written comment in the shared doc. The follow-up email after the meeting. The revision submitted at 1 AM that restructures the entire argument.
Name the channel that works and protect it. Then, slowly, experiment with the channel that does not. One sentence per meeting. One hand raised per week. Not as proof of recovery. As practice in tolerating the thin voice long enough for it to thicken.

## REFLECTION v05
---

---
The 4:50 PM dinner window exists because someone engineered their schedule around anxiety. That engineering deserves respect, not shame. It represents problem-solving under pressure — the same cognitive skill that aces exams and debugs code.
But architecture becomes a cage when it never expands. The 4:50 window works today. The question is whether it still works in May, in August, in the first week of a new job where lunch happens at noon with coworkers in a bright cafeteria with no wall-facing seats.
Expand the window by ten minutes. Eat at 5:00 this week. Then 5:15. Then the unthinkable — 6:00 PM on a Tuesday, the dining hall at half capacity. Each shift exposes the nervous system to a slightly louder room. The stomach still drops. The legs still carry you in.
Time architecture is not avoidance. It is scaffolding. Scaffolding comes down when the structure holds weight on its own.

## REFLECTION v06
---

---
Nobody teaches you that leaving is a skill. The party script runs one direction — arrive, stay, enjoy, leave when it ends. The anxious version adds a clause nobody printed: leave when the body reaches capacity, and carry the guilt of early departure like luggage through the parking lot.
The face flushes on entry. The face flushes again when the thought of leaving arrives. Both flushes feel identical in the body. The first says "too many people." The second says "not enough endurance." Both are stories layered on top of a simple autonomic response.
Grant yourself departure permission before you arrive. Set the boundary in advance — sixty minutes, ninety, whatever the body can hold without breaking into performance mode. When the timer sounds, leave without apology. Text "headed out" and walk.
The exit matters as much as the entrance. Practice leaving clean — without excuses, without invented emergencies, without the Irish goodbye that trades one anxiety for another. Stand up. Say goodnight. Walk out. The door opens in both directions. Use both.

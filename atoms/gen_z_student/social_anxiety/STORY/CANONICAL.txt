Maya stands at the dining hall entrance with her student ID in one hand and her phone in the other. Her stomach drops the moment she sees the crowd. Every table holds a cluster of people mid-conversation. Laughter erupts from the far corner. She catalogues exits: side door near the salad bar, main entrance behind her, emergency exit she has never tested.
She swipes in. The beep sounds louder than it should. She picks up a tray and moves toward the pasta station because it sits closest to a wall. Her eyes sweep the room in three-second arcs. No one from her floor. No one from Bio 201. The tray grows heavier with food she chose for speed, not hunger.
Maya spots a two-top near the window. One chair faces the wall. She takes it. Her back presses against brick. She opens her phone and scrolls through nothing — the screen exists to signal "busy, not lonely." She eats in twelve minutes. The pasta tastes like obligation.
On the walk back to her dorm, her shoulders release. Air fills her lungs past the halfway mark for the first time since she entered. She unlocks her door and sits on the bed. Her body softens. The dining hall sits two hundred yards away. Her nervous system treated it like a combat zone.
Tomorrow she will do this again. The meal plan runs through May. Maya sets an alarm for 5:45 dinner — the dead window before the rush. Strategy is not the same as comfort, but it keeps her fed.

Jordan arrives at the library four minutes late on purpose. The group already claims a table near the printers. Three laptops open, one shared doc glowing on screen. Jordan pulls a chair from the next table. The scrape echoes. Everyone looks up.
"Hey." Jordan's voice thins to a thread. The group nods and returns to the screen. They have already outlined the presentation. Jordan reads the doc and spots a structural flaw in slide six — the data contradicts the thesis paragraph. The correction forms cleanly in Jordan's mind. Clear, precise, useful.
Jordan's mouth does not open. The words stack up behind a locked door. Fingers type a comment in the Google Doc instead. Fifteen seconds later, Priya reads it aloud. "Oh wait, Jordan flagged something in slide six." The group adjusts. Jordan nods. No one asks Jordan to explain further.
The meeting runs forty minutes. Jordan speaks nine words total. On the walk back across the quad, Jordan replays every moment a sentence almost surfaced. The library air felt thick. The fluorescent light hummed at a frequency that tightened the jaw.
In the dorm room, Jordan opens the shared doc alone and writes three paragraphs of flawless analysis. The cursor moves fast. Thoughts pour without friction. Jordan's hands relax on the keyboard. This version of contribution feels safe — asynchronous, textual, unwitnessed.
The group chat pings at midnight. "Jordan your section is fire." Jordan reads the message four times. Pride and grief sit in the same chair. The best work arrives only in silence.

## STORY v03

Eli stands on the third-floor landing with music bleeding through the apartment door. His hands grip his phone until his knuckles pale. The group chat said "pregame at 9, party at 10." The clock reads 10:22. Fashionably late. Strategically late. Late enough that no one tracks his entrance.
He opens the door. The living room holds bodies and bass. Someone shouts a name that is not his. Eli moves toward the kitchen because kitchens have counters and counters give you something to lean against. He pours seltzer into a red cup. It looks like a drink. It functions as a prop.
A classmate from Stats waves across the room. Eli waves back. That exchange costs him nine seconds and a spike of adrenaline that tastes metallic on his tongue. He leans against the counter and watches the room like a film he did not audition for. Conversations orbit around him. He catches fragments — someone's spring break, someone's internship, someone's breakup.
Forty minutes pass. Eli speaks to two people. Both conversations last under ninety seconds. His face holds a neutral expression that took years to engineer. Behind it, his pulse drums a steady alarm.
He texts his roommate "heading out" and slips through the door. The stairwell air hits cool against his flushed neck. His hands finally release the phone. Finger-shaped white marks fade from his palms.
Back in his room, Eli sits in the dark for ten minutes. The silence feels like medicine. He showed up. He stayed. He left on his own terms. The scoreboard reads survival, not victory. Tonight, survival counts.

Nia reads the assigned chapter twice. She highlights six passages and writes margin notes in pencil. She knows this material the way she knows her dorm room — by heart, in the dark, without hesitation. The seminar starts at 2:10.
At 2:23, Professor Chen opens the floor. "Thoughts on the author's central claim." Nia's face flushes hot. The answer organizes itself behind her sternum — structured, sourced, ready. Her hand rises two inches off the desk and retreats. Chen calls on Marcus instead. Marcus gives a surface-level take. Nia's jaw tightens.
She types her response in the Notes app under the desk. Four sentences of precise analysis that no one will read. The discussion moves forward. Two more openings appear. Two more times the heat climbs her neck and her throat seals shut.
Class ends at 3:25. Nia packs her bag in silence. She walks to the campus coffee shop and sits in a corner booth. Her hands stop trembling after the first sip. She opens her laptop and writes a discussion post that runs six hundred words of dense, brilliant argument. She submits it at 4:01.
Chen emails at 9 PM. "Nia — your post was the strongest response this week. I'd love to hear more of your voice in class." Nia reads the email on her bed. Her chest tightens. The compliment lands like a challenge. She knows the material. Her body does not know it is safe to speak.

## STORY v05
---

---
Sam eats dinner at 4:50 PM every day. Not because of hunger. Because the dining hall empties between the lunch stragglers and the dinner rush. Sam's stomach drops less when the room holds twenty people instead of two hundred.
Today the schedule breaks. A meeting with an advisor runs until 5:30. Sam arrives at the dining hall at 5:47. Peak traffic. The swipe-in line stretches eight deep. Sam's jaw locks. Bodies press close in the queue. Someone's backpack brushes Sam's arm and every muscle tenses.
Inside, the noise hits like weather. Sam grabs a plate and loads it at the first station — does not matter what. Rice, something brown, vegetables that might be roasted. The mission runs simple: plate, seat, eat, leave.
Sam spots a table with one open chair across from a stranger. The calculation fires instantly. Sit with a stranger and perform normalcy. Or carry the tray to the hallway bench outside. Sam chooses the bench.
The hallway air feels ten degrees cooler. Sam's shoulders drop three inches. The rice tastes fine out here. A custodian pushes a cart past and nods. Sam nods back. That exchange feels manageable. One person, one nod, zero expectation of follow-up.
Sam finishes in fifteen minutes and buses the tray through the side entrance to avoid the main floor. In the elevator back to the eighth floor, Sam exhales fully. The dining hall visit clocks thirty-two minutes. Recovery will take longer.
Sam opens a delivery app and bookmarks it. Tomorrow the advisor meeting moves. The 4:50 window reopens. Sam sets the alarm and breathes.

## STORY v06
---

---
Dara spends forty minutes choosing an outfit. Not for vanity. For armor. The right shirt means fewer reasons for someone to look twice. The right jeans mean blending into the background of a house party where she knows exactly three people.
She drives to the address and parks a block away. The walk gives her time to rehearse. "Hey, good to see you." "Yeah, I know her from class." "Just water, thanks." Lines prepared like a script for a role she never auditioned for.
The front door opens into a wall of sound and body heat. Dara's face flushes from the threshold. She locates her friend Asha near the couch and moves toward her in a straight line. Eye contact with no one else. Asha hugs her. The hug lasts three seconds and anchors Dara to the room.
Asha introduces her to a group. Dara smiles and says four names back. She will remember none of them because her working memory redirected all power to threat assessment. Music. Movement. Laughter from the kitchen. Every stimulus registers as data to process, not joy to absorb.
Dara lasts ninety minutes. She talks to seven people. She laughs twice — real laughs that surprise her. She also visits the bathroom three times to breathe against the tile wall where no one watches.
In the car afterward, Dara grips the steering wheel and lets her face drop. The performance ends. She drives home with the windows down. Night air replaces the party's heat. She did it. She hated parts of it. She will go again. The math never balances, but she keeps showing up.

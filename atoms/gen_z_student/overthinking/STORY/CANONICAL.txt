Sana sits on her dorm bed at 11:43 PM, legs crossed, laptop balanced on a pillow. The group project message glows on screen. She needs to tell her teammates she finished her section early. Simple. She types: "Hey, I finished my part." Deletes it. Too blunt. "Hey! I finished my part :)" Too eager. Her temples pound. She types a third version, a fourth, a fifth. Each word transforms into evidence of a personality flaw. "Finished" sounds like bragging. "My part" sounds possessive. The cursor blinks against white space and her stomach tightens into a fist.
She opens a separate note and drafts the message there, away from the danger of an accidental send. Her neck muscles lock. She hunches closer to the screen, squinting at two words: "just" versus "already." "Just" downplays her effort. "Already" invites resentment. Sweat gathers along her hairline.
Forty minutes pass. Her roommate sleeps. The heater clicks. Sana's eyes burn from the screen but she cannot stop rearranging seven words into every possible configuration. She lands on "Hey, my section is done if anyone wants to look!" Hovers her thumb over send. Her heart slams against her sternum. She locks the phone and sets it face-down on the mattress. Lies back. Stares at the ceiling. The message sits unsent. Her jaw aches from clenching. Tomorrow she will try again, and the loop will restart from the beginning.

COST_INTENSITY: 7/10

---
Marcus stands outside Professor Chen's office, backpack straps digging into his shoulders. He rehearsed the question on the walk over. Now the words dissolve. He imagines knocking, imagines her face shifting from neutral to annoyed. His palms go slick. He wipes them on his jeans.
The question itself takes ten seconds to ask. He knows this. But every version he runs through his head sounds ignorant or lazy or both. "I didn't understand the third lecture" becomes proof he skipped the reading. "The assignment prompt confused me" becomes proof he lacks competence. His chest compresses. Breath shortens. He shifts his weight from foot to foot in the empty hallway, staring at the nameplate on her door.
Three students walk past. He pretends to check his phone. The office hour window shrinks by another minute. His throat dries. He pictures the exact micro-expression of disappointment and it hits like a physical weight on his ribs.
Marcus turns around. He walks back to the library, sits at a corner desk, and opens the assignment prompt for the sixteenth time. His eyes track the same paragraph without absorbing a single word. The question stays lodged behind his teeth, unasked. He presses his forehead against the cool desk surface and breathes. The midterm is Thursday. The question still lives only inside his skull. The grade will reflect the silence.

---
Priya replays the dinner in real time. She sits on her bedroom floor, back against the bed frame, phone dark in her lap. The restaurant noise still rings in her ears. At 7:42 PM she told a joke and Ava laughed but looked at her plate. That downward glance. Priya's breath catches.
She builds the case. The glance lasted one second. One second of broken eye contact equals discomfort. Discomfort equals Priya said something wrong. Her pulse quickens. She digs through the evening frame by frame: the pause before Ava answered her question about winter break, the way Ava checked her phone twice, the single "yeah" instead of a full sentence at 8:15.
Her fingers go cold. She pulls a blanket over her legs but the chill stays internal, settled behind her sternum. She drafts a text: "Had fun tonight!" Stares at the exclamation point. Deletes it. Types a period. Deletes the whole message. The act of reaching out now proves she noticed something off, which proves she is needy, which proves Ava will pull away.
Her roommate asks if she is okay. Priya says yes. Her jaw stays locked. She scrolls to their last ten conversations, counting response times, measuring word counts, graphing emotional distance in a mental spreadsheet. The data confirms the dread. The data always confirms the dread because she builds the dataset to match the verdict she already holds. She sets the phone face-down. Lies on the floor. The ceiling fan spins and so does every sentence from the last three hours.

## STORY v04

IDENTITY_STAGE: performative_self
COST_TYPE: creative_suppression
COST_INTENSITY: 7/10
STORY_TYPE: character_study
STORY_TYPE_SOURCE: heuristic_v1
---
Devon opens the blank document at 9 AM. The personal essay prompt sits in a tab beside it: "Describe a challenge that shaped you." His fingers hover above the keys. Shoulders rigid. He types one sentence about his parents' divorce and stops. Too dramatic. Deletes it. Types about his part-time job at the grocery store. Too mundane. His neck stiffens into a single locked column.
Every opening sentence passes through an invisible committee in his head. The admissions officer who wants authenticity. The peer reviewer who wants polish. The version of himself that needs to sound smart but not pretentious, vulnerable but not broken. His tongue presses hard against the roof of his mouth.
By noon the document holds fourteen deleted paragraphs and two surviving words: "Growing up." He stares at them until they lose meaning. His eyes water from forgetting to blink. He closes the laptop and walks to the dining hall. Eats a sandwich without tasting it. Returns. Opens the document.
The two words blink back. He tries a new angle: humor. Types a joke. Reads it back. It sounds forced. The committee reconvenes. His stomach clenches. He imagines the reader cringing. He imagines the rejection letter. He imagines telling his mother. He deletes the joke, highlights "Growing up," and deletes that too. The page sits white and empty at 4 PM. Seven hours of labor produced nothing the world can see. Everything he wrote still exists in the versions he killed, locked inside the anxious architecture of good enough never arriving.

## STORY v05

IDENTITY_STAGE: worst_case_architect
COST_TYPE: sleep_destruction
COST_INTENSITY: 9/10
STORY_TYPE: character_study
STORY_TYPE_SOURCE: heuristic_v1
---
Jess lies in the dark at 1:17 AM, sheets tangled around her ankles. Her eyes lock open. The presentation is at 10 AM. She rehearsed it six times. Knows every slide. None of that matters now. Her brain opens the failure simulator.
Slide three. The professor interrupts with a question she cannot answer. Her face goes hot in front of forty people. Her voice cracks. Someone in the back row smirks. The scene plays on a loop, each iteration adding new details: the silence after her stammering, the TA writing a note, her lab partner avoiding eye contact afterward.
Her heart rate spikes against the mattress. She rolls to her side, presses her knees to her chest. Counts breaths. Loses count at seven when the simulator launches a new scenario: her laptop freezes, the slides vanish, she stands at the podium with nothing.
She grabs her phone and opens the slide deck. Swipes through every transition for the seventh time. Her thumb aches from gripping the phone. Screen light bleaches her face in the dark room. She mouths the words to slide four, five, six. Puts the phone down. The simulator boots again instantly. New scenario: she gets through it perfectly but her voice sounds monotone, and the professor writes "lacks engagement" on the rubric.
Three AM. Her eyelids finally droop. The alarm is set for 7:30. Four and a half hours of sleep to fuel a ten-minute presentation she has memorized cold. Her body spent more energy rehearsing catastrophe than it will spend delivering the actual talk. The jaw-ache will follow her through tomorrow. The dark circles will show.

## STORY v06
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: retrospective_looping
IDENTITY_STAGE: post_event_auditor
COST_TYPE: present_moment_theft
COST_INTENSITY: 8/10
STORY_TYPE: character_study
STORY_TYPE_SOURCE: heuristic_v1
---
Eli leaves the party at 11 PM and the audit begins before he reaches the sidewalk. Cold air hits his face. His hands ball into fists inside his jacket pockets. He said "that's crazy" to someone's story about their sick grandmother. His stomach flips.
He walks faster. Replays the exact moment. The girl's face after he said it. A half-second pause. A polite smile. His throat constricts. "That's crazy" landed wrong. It sounded dismissive. It sounded like he trivialized her pain. He should have said "I'm so sorry." He should have asked a follow-up question. He should have stayed quiet.
The crosswalk signal changes. He walks. The replay does not pause for traffic. He cycles through alternative responses, ranking them, grading himself against each one. His temples throb. The original moment lasted four seconds. The review has consumed twelve minutes and shows no sign of stopping.
Back in his apartment he brushes his teeth, staring at his own face in the mirror without seeing it. The toothbrush moves on autopilot. His mind still stands in that living room, frozen at the moment his mouth chose wrong. He spits, rinses, walks to bed. Opens his phone. Types "is saying that's crazy rude" into the search bar. Reads three opinion threads. Finds evidence for both sides. His chest stays tight.
He puts the phone on the nightstand. Pulls the covers up. The audit continues in the dark, branching now into older events: the awkward thing he said at orientation, the text he sent too fast last Tuesday. Each memory stacks on the last. The present moment dissolves into an archive of past errors, and the party he just attended — the music, the laughter, the friend who hugged him at the door — vanishes beneath the weight of four seconds he cannot take back.

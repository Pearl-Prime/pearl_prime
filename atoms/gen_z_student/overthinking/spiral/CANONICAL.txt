Yuki is choosing a topic for her senior thesis. There are forty possible directions within her major. She builds a decision matrix. The matrix has six criteria. She scores each topic against each criterion. The scores converge on three finalists. She researches the three finalists and discovers four new directions. She adds the four new directions to the matrix. The matrix now has forty-four rows. She recalculates. The finalists shift. She researches the new finalists and finds more directions. Her advisor asked for a topic decision by Friday. It is Thursday at 11 p.m. She has been working on this matrix for two weeks. The thesis deadline is ten months away. The matrix cannot hold ten months. She closes the spreadsheet and stares at the ceiling. Her neck aches from the screen. The topic that made her stomach light up three weeks ago is buried in row twenty-seven.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02

COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Dani's group project team is waiting for her to send the shared outline. She drafted it two days ago. It is in her Documents folder. She has reread it four times and each time found something to revise. The revisions have created a fifth version. She is now reading the fifth version. Her group members have sent three messages asking where the outline is. She types and deletes a response twice. She wants to send the right version, the one that won't need revising, the one that shows she was thorough. The outline is for a seven-minute in-class presentation in two weeks. It does not require five versions. Her teammates are not judging the outline. They are waiting for the outline. Her chest is tight. She sends version five. It has one incomplete sentence she noticed after hitting send. She opens the email and rereads the outline again. The project is still two weeks away.

## TURNING_POINT v03
---
path: story_atoms/gen_z_student/overthinking/spiral/turning_point/micro/v03.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Marcus submits his housing application before he is certain it's right. This is new. He has been in the decision spiral for six days—single or double room, north or south campus, three meal plan options, the appeal process if he doesn't get his first choice. He knows the spiral would run another six days if he let it. He fills in his top choice and hits submit and his pulse spikes and his body runs the familiar protest: he should have checked one more time, he should have looked at the floor map again, he should have asked his friend what she was choosing. He closes the browser. The application is submitted. He made a choice with the information he had. His hands are slightly cold. His chest is tight around something he can't retrieve. The cost of choosing before the spiral finishes is this: living with a decision he isn't certain about. The cost of not choosing was six more days. He sits with the uncertainty and it doesn't kill him.

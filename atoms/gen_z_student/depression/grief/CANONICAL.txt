Luz wakes up and there is nothing to get up for, even though there are two things she has to do. The ceiling is the same ceiling it was yesterday. Her body is heavy in the mattress in a way that has nothing to do with how much she slept. She got eight hours. She is not rested. She missed this feeling from her last depressive season—the specific weight that descends before the alarm goes off, the flat question of why this day. The window shows morning. She has lecture at ten. She turns onto her side. She lies there for forty minutes before her phone alarm moves her. Getting up doesn't fix the weight. She carries it across campus in her body, in the rounded shoulders, in the eyes that track the sidewalk instead of the horizon. Lecture starts. She is there. Being there is everything she has.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02
---

BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Marcus skips his friend's birthday for the third consecutive social event he has missed this month. He texts an excuse that is technically true—he does have a reading due tomorrow—but not the full truth, which is that the reading is also the excuse to stay in his room, which is the only place the social performance isn't required. He sends the text and puts the phone face down. His room is quiet. He feels no better here than he would at the birthday. But here he doesn't have to perform not feeling bad. His chest is a low constant ache, nothing sharp. He opens the reading. The sentences run past his eyes without sticking. He reads six pages and could not repeat a single sentence. His friend texts "miss you!" and the two words crack something open and he presses his eyes shut and breathes through his nose until it closes again. He doesn't miss his friend. He misses himself.

## TURNING_POINT v03
---
path: story_atoms/gen_z_student/depression/grief/turning_point/micro/v03.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Kofi fills out the counseling center intake form on his phone while sitting in the library bathroom. He has been putting this off for six weeks. The form has sixteen questions. He answers them honestly, which takes longer than checking the boxes that make him look least broken. He submits it. He sits on the cold floor with his back against the wall and stares at the ceiling. His chest does not lighten. He has taken a step and his body is not rewarding the step with relief. The grief is still there—the accumulated loss of the semester, the flattened version of himself he has been wearing for months. The intake confirmation arrives in his email. His appointment is in four days. He stands up and goes back to his desk and opens his laptop and the library is exactly the same as when he left it. He has a meeting in an hour. He will go. He will try.

## PERMISSION v01
---

---
You have permission to call brushing your teeth a victory. Grip the toothbrush. Feel the handle in your palm. Depression turned basic hygiene into a mountain. Climbing it counts. Your clean teeth are proof of effort no one else understands.

You have permission to skip the party and feel nothing about it. Press your back into your mattress. Feel the bed hold you. Rest is not failure dressed in pajamas. Rest is the body allocating its limited resources where they matter: survival.

You have permission to eat whatever requires the least effort. Open the granola bar. Feel the wrapper crinkle in your fingers. Nutrition purists can wait. Calories enter your blood and fuel your brain. A granola bar in a depression is a full meal. Accept it.

You have permission to cry without a reason. Let the pressure behind your eyes release. Feel the tears track down your face. Touch your cheek. The wetness is real. The body processes what the mind cannot name. Tears are data, not drama.

You have permission to ask for help in a text instead of a conversation. Type three words: "I am struggling." Press send. Feel your thumb lift off the screen. Three words carry enough signal. The person who receives them will hear the weight. You do not need a speech. You need a bridge.

You have permission to attend class and absorb nothing. Sit in the seat. Feel the chair hold your body. Showing up with a blank mind still counts as showing up. Your physical presence maintains a thread of routine. That thread keeps you stitched to the world when everything else unravels.

## PERMISSION v02
---

---
You have permission to keep the curtains closed today. Lie back. Feel the dim. The body sometimes needs a low-stimulation environment to conserve energy. The light will be there tomorrow. Today, the dark is doing a job.

You have permission to wear the same clothes for three days in a row. Look down at the shirt. It is fine. The energy required to change it could be spent on something that matters more. Save it.

You have permission to eat cereal for dinner at 9 PM. Pour the milk. Watch it fill the bowl. The calories work the same regardless of food category. Dinner does not require performance. Cereal is a meal when cereal is what you have left.

You have permission to text the therapist between sessions. Open the app. Type one sentence. The therapist's office often allows brief check-ins. The waiting until the appointment alone is the part that compounds the depression.

You have permission to leave a song you used to love unfinished. Tap pause. Set the phone down. Music can carry weight you cannot lift today. The song will be there when you can. Today is not the day.

You have permission to set one alarm for one task. Pick up the phone. Set 2 PM. "Eat something." When it rings, eat. The single-task day is not failure. It is system-running-on-emergency-power. One task is enough.

## PERMISSION v03
---

---
You have permission to call the campus crisis line for non-crisis support. Pick up the phone. Dial. The crisis line takes calls below threshold too. They will not hospitalize you for needing to talk. They are trained for both ends of the spectrum.

You have permission to tell one classmate "I am struggling" without context. Walk up. Say the sentence. The classmate may surprise you. The risk of saying it is smaller than the cost of the silent isolation that has been building.

You have permission to switch your dorm room mid-semester if the current one is making things worse. Walk to housing. Ask. The reassignment is administrative. The current room may be feeding the depression with proximity to the wrong people or the wrong window.

You have permission to take an hour-long shower. Stand under the water. Feel the heat. Hygiene math does not measure water spent on regulation. The shower as therapy is a documented use. Use it.

You have permission to start medication if your psychiatrist recommends it. Hold the prescription. Walk to the pharmacy. Brain chemistry is biology. Treatment is medicine. The decision is between you and the prescriber. Other people's opinions do not enter the room.

You have permission to leave the room when someone says "have you tried exercise." Stand up. Walk to the door. The advice was likely well-meaning and unhelpful. The leaving protects you from having to argue with someone who does not understand the floor you are calling up from.

## REFLECTION v01
---

---
tone: grounded, direct
perspective: second person singular
The weight arrived before you named it. One morning your arms stayed pinned to the mattress and you called it tired. The next morning the same thing happened and you called it lazy. By the third morning you stopped calling it anything. You just lay there.
Your chest learned a new pressure — not pain, not tightness, but absence. A hollow carved behind the sternum where momentum used to live. Your jaw locked shut around words that felt too expensive to spend.
This pattern has a name. The name does not fix it, but it draws a boundary around the fog. Inside that boundary: neurochemistry, sleep architecture, stress load, isolation. Outside that boundary: your worth, your future, your capacity to rebuild.
The heaviness in your limbs does not measure your character. It measures your nervous system's current load. Systems under load need support — not shame, not willpower lectures, not comparisons to last semester's version of you. Support. Start there.

## REFLECTION v02
---

---
tone: unflinching, warm
perspective: second person singular
Your eyes stopped producing the right amount of moisture. Dry, gritty, burning after ten minutes of screen time. The body's small revolt — a protest against inputs it can no longer process.
Your face settled into neutral and stayed there. Not sad, not angry, not peaceful. Flat. The muscles that shape expression went off-duty. Your hands lost their urgency — they pick up the phone and put it down, open the laptop and close it, reach for food and withdraw.
This flatness carries information. It says the emotional circuit breaker tripped. Too much input, too little recovery, too many days of running the engine without oil. The breaker did its job. It prevented a worse outcome.
Now the work begins at the body level. Not thinking your way out — moving your way through. One glass of water. One walk to the mailbox. One honest sentence spoken to another human face. The hands remember what the mind forgot: small actions compound. Numbness breaks in fragments, not avalanches.

## REFLECTION v03
---

---
tone: precise, compassionate
perspective: second person singular
Your feet gained twenty invisible pounds. Walking to class became an act of defiance against gravity that grew stronger each morning. Your lungs took shallow sips of air instead of full breaths — just enough to keep the lights on, nothing extra.
Your throat constricted around every honest answer. "How are you." Fine. "Coming out tonight." Maybe. "Need anything." No. Each word filtered through a narrowing passage, stripped of detail and truth before it reached the air.
The body speaks depression in its own dialect. Leaden feet mean the nervous system redirected energy away from movement. Shallow breathing means the diaphragm locked into protective mode. Tight throat means the social engagement system powered down.
None of these translations excuse the suffering. They locate it. Location matters because it points toward the first response. Feet that feel leaden need movement in doses measured by seconds, not miles. Lungs that breathe shallow need one deliberate exhale. Throats that tighten need one sentence of truth delivered to one safe person.

## REFLECTION v04
---

---
tone: direct, steady
perspective: second person singular
Your spine held you upright through pure stubbornness. Not strength — rigidity. The kind that snaps instead of bends. Your fists clenched at your sides during lectures, knuckles white under the desk where no one checked.
The pressure behind your eyes built for weeks. Not tears — something denser. A reservoir with no outlet, pushing against the bone. You pressed your palms against your face in bathroom stalls and felt the pressure redistribute but never release.
This version of depression wears a mask of functionality. You attend class. You submit assignments. You answer texts with single words. The performance costs everything, and no one sees the invoice.
Your rigid spine needs permission to curve. Your clenched fists need permission to open. The pressure behind your eyes needs a release valve — not a dramatic collapse, but a controlled opening. Therapy offers that valve. A counselor's office provides the only room where the performance can stop and the body can report what it actually carries. Functionality without honesty is a timer counting down. Reset the timer.

## REFLECTION v05
---

---
tone: gentle, unflinching
perspective: second person singular
Your hands and feet went cold. Circulation pulled inward, hoarding warmth for the vital organs, abandoning the periphery. Your eyelids drooped at 2 PM, at 4 PM, at 7 PM — not from sleep debt but from a system conserving every unit of energy.
Your posture collapsed forward. Shoulders rounded, chin dropped, spine curved into a shape that made the world smaller and closer. The body's instinct under threat: reduce the target area, protect the center, wait for the danger to pass.
But the danger does not pass because the danger lives inside the chemistry. The threat is not external. No amount of curling inward outsmarts a brain running low on serotonin.
Your cold hands need warm water. Your heavy eyelids need sunlight before 10 AM. Your sunken posture needs three minutes of standing with shoulders pulled back — not as punishment but as signal. The body reads its own position and adjusts its internal state to match. Curl inward and the brain confirms: threat present. Stand open and the brain reconsiders.
This is not a cure. This is a foothold. Footholds lead to the next grip, the next ledge, the next honest breath.

## REFLECTION v06
---

---
tone: raw, anchored
perspective: second person singular
Your skin buzzed at 2 AM — a low electric hum that no one else could hear. Restless energy trapped under stillness. Your jaw locked shut, molars grinding in your sleep, wearing grooves into enamel. Your stomach carried a dead weight that food did not fill and hunger did not explain.
The night version of depression moves. It paces. It scrolls. It stares at the ceiling with a brain that refuses to power down. The morning version freezes. Same person, same illness, opposite symptoms. The whiplash between wired and flat exhausts what little reserve remains.
Your buzzing skin says the nervous system never left fight-or-flight. Your locked jaw says the body braces for impact around the clock. Your stomach's dead weight says grief sits in the gut before it reaches the mind.
Name the night pattern. Name the morning pattern. Write both down. Bring the paper to the counseling center and hand it over. Let someone trained in this language read what your body wrote.
You do not need to translate. You do not need to perform. You need to deliver the message. Your body already composed it. Your only job: carry it through the door.

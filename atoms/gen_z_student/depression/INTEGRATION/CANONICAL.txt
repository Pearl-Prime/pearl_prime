Choose one non-negotiable micro-action for today. Not a goal. Not an aspiration. A micro-action. Brush your teeth. Fill a water glass. Open the curtain. Pick the smallest act that moves your body from stillness to motion. Before doing it, sit on the edge of your bed. Press your feet flat on the floor. Feel the cold or the carpet. Count to three. On three, stand. Your legs will resist. Stand anyway. Walk to the sink, the kitchen, the window. Complete the micro-action. Then sit back down if you need to. The day does not require a second act. One micro-action breaks the inertia depression depends on. Inertia tells your body that stillness is the only option. The micro-action introduces evidence to the contrary. Your legs stood. Your hands turned a faucet. Your eyes saw daylight. These data points accumulate. Tomorrow the micro-action takes slightly less effort. The day after, slightly less again. Depression loses ground in millimeters, not miles. Accept the millimeter.

Every Sunday evening, write one sentence about the week. Not a summary. Not a journal entry. One sentence. "I made it through Tuesday." "I ate three meals on Thursday." "I texted back on Saturday." The sentence captures survival, not achievement. Read the sentence aloud. Hear your own voice state a fact about your continued existence. Place the paper or phone down. Press your palms flat on the surface. Feel the contact. Depression erases evidence of endurance. It tells you nothing happened this week, nothing mattered, nothing moved. The sentence refutes that. Something happened. You recorded it. Over weeks, the sentences accumulate into a timeline of persistence. One sentence per week. Fifty-two sentences per year. Each one a mark on the wall of a cell you are slowly carving your way out of. Do not reread old sentences during the writing session. Let them rest. Their power builds in accumulation, not review. The archive exists. That is enough.

The wave hits in the middle of the day. Your energy drains mid-lecture, mid-meal, mid-conversation. Your body goes heavy. Your vision dulls. Before you retreat to bed, try one physical intervention. Stand up wherever you are. Press your feet into the floor and push your body to standing. Roll your shoulders three times backward. Shake your hands at your sides for ten seconds. Feel the blood move into your fingertips. The depression wave lowers your body temperature, slows your circulation, dims your senses. Physical movement sends a counter-signal. It does not cure the wave. It shortens it. The shaking disrupts the freeze state your nervous system defaults to. Your hands tingle. Your shoulders loosen. The wave still pulls, but your body now has a foothold against the current. Sit back down if you need to. The intervention lasted thirty seconds. That is enough. You interrupted the pattern. Your muscles registered movement during a period your brain insisted on paralysis. That registration matters more than it feels like it does.

Before sleep tonight, lie on your back. Place one hand on your chest and one on your belly. Breathe in through your nose and direct the air toward the hand on your belly. Feel it rise. Exhale slowly through your mouth. Feel it fall. Repeat five times. Depression disrupts sleep architecture — too much, too little, too broken. This breathing pattern activates the parasympathetic nervous system, the branch that governs rest and repair. Your body forgets how to transition into sleep when depression scrambles the signals. The breath retrains the transition. Focus only on the rise and fall of your belly. When thoughts arrive — and they will, heavy and grey — notice them without following. Return to the belly. Return to the hand. The thoughts are weather. The breath is ground. Five breaths tonight. Five breaths tomorrow night. The consistency matters more than the count. Over a week, your body starts to associate this position and this breath with the onset of sleep. You build a bridge between waking heaviness and sleeping rest. The bridge is made of exhales.

Once a week, leave your room for fifteen minutes with no destination and no purpose. Not for class. Not for food. Not for errands. Walk out the door and turn in whichever direction your feet choose. Walk slowly. Feel each step land — heel, then ball, then toes. Depression shrinks your world to the size of your bed, your room, your screen. The purposeless walk expands it by fifteen minutes of sidewalk. Do not time it precisely. Do not track your steps. This is not exercise. This is exposure to the outside. Notice one thing: a sound, a color, a temperature change on your skin. Touch a tree, a wall, a bench. Feel the texture. Your fingers report data from the outside world. That data competes with the interior monologue depression runs nonstop. You do not need to silence the monologue. You need to add a second channel. The walk provides it. After fifteen minutes, return to your room. Sit on your bed. Press your feet into the floor. You left and came back. The room looks the same but your body carries fifteen minutes of outdoor data. That data changes the chemistry, even slightly.

The guilt spiral starts: you skipped class, ignored a text, missed a deadline. Depression converts every lapse into evidence of fundamental brokenness. Before the spiral completes its loop, grab a pen. Write the fact without the narrative. "Missed class today." Not "missed class because I am lazy and failing and wasting everyone's time and money." The fact is six syllables. The narrative is a paragraph. Press the pen into the paper. Feel the point drag across the surface. The fact carries information. The narrative carries prosecution. Now write one physical fact about your body in this moment. "My hand holds a pen." "My feet touch the floor." "My lungs fill with air." These facts compete with the narrative for your attention. They do not win outright. They do not need to. They create a crack in the spiral. Through that crack, one next action becomes visible. Not the whole day. One action. Reply to the text. Open the laptop. Set an alarm for tomorrow's class. Write that action below the facts. Circle it. Put the pen down. Press your palms on the desk. The spiral wants a verdict. Give it a task instead.

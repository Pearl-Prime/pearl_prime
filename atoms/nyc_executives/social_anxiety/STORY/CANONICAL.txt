She arrived at the industry dinner in the car, three minutes early, and sat. Not to compose herself — she was already composed, that was automatic. She sat because once she walked in, the management of the room would begin, and the management was the part nobody saw that took everything. She timed her entry. She always timed her entry.

He was better in meetings than at dinners. Meetings had structure — an agenda, a role, a clear reason to be in the room. Dinners were unstructured, which meant his nervous system had to generate the structure itself, which it did at a cost he pretended was just introversion. He'd been calling it introversion for fifteen years and it had never gotten quieter.

The conference panel was the easy part — fifty minutes of questions she'd prepared for, a role she'd performed successfully hundreds of times. The cocktail reception after was the hard part. She moved through it expertly: the circulation, the business card, the right amount of time per conversation. She got to her room at 9pm and felt the exhaustion of having performed safety in a room that should have been safe.

What she couldn't explain was the cost of that performance: the pre-conversation scan, the post-conversation audit, the twelve hours between events where her nervous system filed everything and replayed the parts that might have landed wrong.

He RSVP'd yes to the alumni event and spent two days calibrating his exit strategy before he'd even confirmed attendance. Not because he didn't want to go. Because the anxiety was built into the going — the arrival, the scan for familiar faces, the calculation of where to stand and who to approach first. He could architect a $40M deal. He couldn't walk into a room without a plan.

She knew the anxiety was there. She'd named it, worked with it, developed strategies. What she hadn't fully reckoned with was the opportunity cost: the events she'd declined that were career-building, the relationships she hadn't pursued because pursuing required unstructured time in rooms she didn't control, the version of her career that existed in the gap between what she'd achieved and what she'd have achieved if the rooms felt like rooms instead of assessments.

## STORY v07
---

IDENTITY_STAGE: self_claim
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 4
---
He told his coach: I'm good at this. I just don't like it. The coach asked him to describe the difference between being good at something and liking it. He tried. What came out instead of a description was the specific sensation — the tightening in his chest before every unstructured social event, the way his shoulders set when he walked into a room that didn't have an agenda, the way he exhaled in the elevator on the way down when it was finally over. Good at it, yes. What it cost him to be good at it — that was the honest answer, and it had been accumulating for years.

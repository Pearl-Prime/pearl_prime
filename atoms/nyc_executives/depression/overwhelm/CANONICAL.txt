James attends his company's annual gala. He moves through the room, shaking hands, speaking to clients, embodying what a successful founder looks like. He's very good at this. The performance has become nearly invisible to him. He can do this in his sleep. By the end of the evening, he's exhausted in a way that sleep won't touch. He's sat down with five different people and told them his version of the story — how he built this company, what he's proud of, where it's heading. The story is true. It's also been polished until it's smooth. The truth underneath — the cost, the hollow shape of it, the question about whether this is what he wanted — that truth is nowhere in the story. He drives home. His wife asks him how the gala was. He says it was good. He says the clients are happy. He says the company is strong. All of this is true. He does not say: I am not sure I am still in my life. He does not say: I have become the architecture and I am not sure if there's anyone home inside it. He goes to bed. He will wake tomorrow and the machinery will start again. The overwhelm will be there. And he will move through it, because moving through it is the only shape he knows.

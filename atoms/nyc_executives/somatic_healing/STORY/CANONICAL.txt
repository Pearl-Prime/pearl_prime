The doctor said her cortisol levels were elevated. She'd expected this. She asked what to do. He said: reduce stress. She sat with that prescription on the subway home and understood, for the first time, how thoroughly her profession had no container for it. Reduce stress. She ran a forty-person team at a firm whose entire product was stress. The prescription assumed a life that wasn't hers.

He noticed it in the yoga class his wife had suggested — the one he'd finally attended after three months of scheduling it and canceling. The instructor asked him to soften his jaw. He tried. What he found was that he didn't know how to soften his jaw voluntarily. The jaw had been set for so long it had forgotten neutral. That was a stranger piece of information than any market data he'd processed that week.

She started the body scan practice the way she started every practice: with a goal, a metric, a timeline. Four weeks. She noticed progress the way she noticed everything — analytically, at a slight remove. What she hadn't anticipated was the session in week three where something in the chest went quiet for forty seconds and she felt, underneath the quiet, something she hadn't felt in a long time that she didn't have a word for.

He took the sabbatical. Six weeks, fully delegated, the phone for emergencies only. By week two his shoulders had dropped a full inch. By week three he started sleeping past 5am. By week four he was sitting in a chair in a quiet house and feeling, with great precision, the specific texture of a nervous system finally offline. It was unfamiliar enough to be slightly frightening. It was also the most rested he'd been in a decade.

She sat in the somatic therapist's office and was asked to notice what was happening in her body right now, in this room, with nothing on the agenda. She noticed: tight across the collarbones, jaw slightly forward, a low-grade readiness in the chest that had no object. The therapist asked: how long has that readiness been there? She tried to remember a time before it. She couldn't. The readiness was older than the career. The career had just given it something to do.

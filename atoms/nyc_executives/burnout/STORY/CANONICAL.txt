She sat across from the CFO and delivered the quarterly numbers with the same precision she always had. The room responded exactly as expected. But somewhere between the slide deck and the car ride home, she noticed that nothing landed — not the praise, not the close, not the view from the 47th floor. Her chest was a room where the lights had gone out.

The deal closed and she felt nothing. Not relief, not satisfaction, not the low-grade adrenaline she'd learned to call pleasure. She sat in the black car on the way to the airport lounge and waited for the feeling that used to arrive automatically. It didn't come. That was the first time she used the word — not out loud, not to anyone, just inside where the word had been waiting.

She took the vacation. First one in three years. The hotel was correct, the view was correct, and somewhere in the middle of the third day she realized she didn't know what she liked anymore. Not what was expected of her. Not what would perform well in a conversation. What she actually wanted when no one was evaluating the answer. The question had no response. Her body had been performing so long it had forgotten what it was performing instead of.

She finally said it to her coach, in the quiet room after the session where she'd been performing insight. She said: I don't think I can do this anymore. Not the job — she knew she could keep doing the job. She meant the thing underneath the job: the constant readiness, the never-off, the performance of someone who is fine. That thing, specifically, was the one she couldn't sustain.

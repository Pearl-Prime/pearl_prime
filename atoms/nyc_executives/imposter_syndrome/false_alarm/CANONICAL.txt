Jonathan starts tracking it the way you'd track a smoke detector that only ever goes off at the same hour. It isn't being wrong that triggers the alarm — he's made real errors before and felt almost nothing. What triggers it is the possibility of being seen not knowing, the unscheduled question, the closed-door meeting he isn't in, the read receipt on an email that hasn't been answered in nine hours. The alarm fires on exposure, not on error. It doesn't wait to find out which one is actually happening. The building's real alarm goes off mid-negotiation, the drill kind everyone half-ignores until they're actually in the stairwell. He walks the floor down twenty-six flights with his team, phone still in hand, voice level.

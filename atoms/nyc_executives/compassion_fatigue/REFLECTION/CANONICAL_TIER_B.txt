

## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
The mechanism I observe is the asymmetrical attentional drain. In leadership, I hold space for others' uncertainty while managing my own. I listen to problems I am responsible for solving. I absorb concerns that land in my office.
This is what the role demands. It is also what depletes me. The people I work with receive my full attention, my problem-solving energy, my willingness to sit with difficulty. I receive periodic acknowledgment if I am fortunate.
Over weeks, I notice something shifting. The capacity to genuinely engage does not replenish at the same rate it drains. I become more mechanical in my responses, more efficient, less present. The work continues but something real has thinned.
The asymmetry is not about time spent. It is about the direction of emotional resource flow. Input and output operate on different schedules.

I have felt a specific texture emerge in the late afternoons. It is not fatigue exactly. It is a flattening of the capacity to care.

What am I losing in this efficiency.

I have been distinguishing between short-term depletion and the chronic kind. Short-term is recoverable. I have a difficult week, I rest, I return to engagement. But chronic depletion works differently.
Chronic emerges from the sustained imbalance. Year after year of giving more than receiving. Month after month of being the emotional container. The system does not recover because the condition does not change.
This is the mechanism of unsustainable rhythm institutionalization. The role structure demands continuous one-directional resource flow. I adapted to this. Became efficient. Learned to function while depleted.
But the adaptation itself becomes the problem. I function so well while depleted that the structure never experiences pressure to change. My competence masks the unsustainability.
I have become someone who can operate effectively while something real in me has gone quiet.

## REFLECTION v06
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
What I have learned is that depletion is not primarily about how much I do. I have known executives working sixty hours weekly who are less depleted than those working forty.
The difference is the ratio of genuine interaction to performance. The person receiving more attentional care than they are giving depletes faster. The person in mutual exchange, where listening is reciprocal and concern is bidirectional, sustains longer.
This is the mechanism of directionality in human resource exchange. The work itself is not the problem. The direction of flow is. One person consistently outputting while others consistently receive creates a particular kind of exhaustion that more time off cannot fix.
I have watched colleagues reduce their hours and remain depleted because the ratio did not change. The structure remained asymmetrical.
The depletion persists because the mechanism persists.

I have been thinking about the difference between being tired and being depleted. Tired is about sleep. Depleted is about reciprocity.
I can be well-rested and still depleted because I am in a structure where I am always the one being turned to and never the one turning toward others. The sleep does not address the fundamental asymmetry.
This is the mechanism of reciprocity-deficit. The human system is designed to exchange. We give and receive. But in some roles, the structure enforces consistent giving without receiving.
The person in this structure can optimize their sleep, their exercise, their downtime. But they address the symptom, not the mechanism.
The depletion comes from the structure of the role itself. Exhaustion management cannot solve it because the problem is not fatigue.

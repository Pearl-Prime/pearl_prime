David receives an email: the deal has fallen through. The client has chosen another firm. The email is professional. The content is clear. David reads it twice. His chest tightens. His hands grip his desk. The deal falling through is about the deal. But something else is arriving underneath: a feeling that is not about business, but about him. Like the numbers measure something true about who he is. His throat closes. He sets down his phone. He looks at his office — the degrees on the wall, the photographs, the awards from previous deals. All evidence of success. All evidence that should counter the feeling. The feeling is not countered. The feeling is: I am not good enough. The deal proved it. His shoulders rise. His jaw tightens. His sternum constricts. He turns to his computer and begins to review the deal documents. There must be something he missed. Something that reveals his failure. The pattern is beginning: the world provides evidence, and David interprets that evidence as truth about his value.

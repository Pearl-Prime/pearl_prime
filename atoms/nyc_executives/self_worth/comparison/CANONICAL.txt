David is at a partner dinner. A colleague across the table is describing deal volume. The volume is significantly higher than David's. David listens. David smiles at the appropriate moments. Underneath the smile, something is happening: his worth is being recalibrated. The colleague's number just became the measuring stick. David's number, which he was satisfied with yesterday, is now insufficient. His chest tightens. His shoulders rise. His jaw tightens. He feels his position in relation to this other person, and the position is secondary. The colleague is ahead. David is behind. The dinner continues. David participates. But he is no longer inhabiting his own experience. He is inhabiting a comparison. His hands are clenched under the table. His sternum is tight. His throat is closing slightly. The pattern is starting: worth is measured against peers, and the peers are always winning the game.

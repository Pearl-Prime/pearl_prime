## HOOK v01
---
---
The memo has been open on your screen since 8am. It's now 11:15. You have closed three actual deals in that window, answered forty emails, sat through two calls — and you still have not written the first sentence of a document that will take you, once you actually start, eleven minutes. The memo is not hard. Starting it is the only hard part, and nobody on the desk has ever taught you the difference.
---

## HOOK v02
---
---
You are the person who can read a cap table in ninety seconds and can't make yourself open the annual review form for the ninth straight day. This is not a contradiction. It is the same brain, doing exactly what it does: racing toward anything with a clear, immediate stake, and stalling completely in front of anything vague, delayed, or open-ended.
---

## HOOK v03
---
---
Your calendar says "strategy doc — 2 hrs" and the two hours pass, and at the end of them the doc has one bullet point on it, and you spent the two hours reorganizing your inbox, a task you did not need to do, because reorganizing was available and starting was not.
---

## HOOK v04
---
---
Notice what actually gets your attention today: not the biggest number, not the most important deal — the one with the nearest deadline, the loudest ping, the most immediate consequence. Your prioritization is not broken. It is responding entirely correctly to urgency and barely at all to importance, and those are not the same signal.
---

## HOOK v05
---
---
You have built a career on decisiveness and you cannot decide which of four open tabs to work on first, so you work on none of them properly, toggling between all four for an hour, accomplishing the equivalent of fifteen real minutes. This is not indecision about the work. It is a very specific kind of freeze that happens only when every option looks equally urgent and your brain has no tiebreaker.

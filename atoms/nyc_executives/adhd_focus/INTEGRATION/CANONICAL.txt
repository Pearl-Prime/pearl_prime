## INTEGRATION v01
---
---
Carry one habit into tomorrow: before your hardest undated task, decide its first physical action tonight, while your judgment is clear and the resistance isn't yet in the room, and write it on a card where you'll see it before anything else loads. Not the whole memo. The one sentence that opens it. When tomorrow's stillness settles in at your desk, you won't have to decide anything — the deciding is already done, sitting on the card, and you only have to do the small thing it names.
---

## INTEGRATION v02
---
---
Pick the one recurring task that has stalled the longest and pair it, tomorrow, to a habit your body already performs without being asked — the standing coffee, the first ten minutes after a call ends, the walk back from the elevator. Attach the stalled task to that anchor and do the smallest possible unit of it right there, in the anchor's wake. The anchor becomes the trigger your discipline was never going to manufacture alone.
---

## INTEGRATION v03
---
---
Tomorrow, when you catch yourself toggling between three open, equally urgent tasks, stop and close two of them entirely, not later, immediately. Work only the one left open until it is finished or genuinely blocked. This will feel like a loss of coverage on the other two. It is not. It is the difference between forty real minutes of output and ninety minutes of motion that produces nothing anyone downstream can use.
---

## INTEGRATION v04
---
---
Before you close your laptop tonight, name one task you have opened and closed more than three times this week without finishing, and write, in one sentence, the smallest physical first move that would start it — not the plan, the move. Put that sentence somewhere you'll see it before your first meeting. The goal is not to finish the task tomorrow. It is to prove the freeze breaks at the verb, every time, if the verb is small enough.

## HOOK v01
---
---
The elevator doors close on the 47th floor and for six seconds you are alone with nobody to perform for, and you spend all six of them rehearsing the line you already nailed twice in the room. The doors open. You walk out sharper than you walked in, and you could not tell anyone what the lobby looked like, or whether the light had changed, or that you had, for the length of an elevator ride, been nowhere at all.
---

## HOOK v02
---
---
The term sheet is good. Everyone at the table knows it is good. You know it too, some detached part of you filed the win the moment the other side's lawyer stopped pushing back. But you are already in the next room, the one with the LPs and the harder number, and you will not remember signing this one by the time you're in the car.
---

## HOOK v03
---
---
Your assistant says the market opened up half a point and your hand is on your phone before the sentence finishes. Notice that: the reaching happened before the deciding did. You have built an entire career on reflexes this fast. Here is the question nobody on the desk will ask you: what would it cost to let one of them pass, once, unanswered.
---

## HOOK v04
---
---
You close the deal at 11:58, two minutes under the wire everyone was watching, and the room does the thing rooms do when a number that big lands — a beat of silence, then the exhale. You do not get the exhale. You are already three moves into the integration timeline, and the win, real and enormous, passes through you like light through a window that was built only to let things through.
---

## HOOK v05
---
---
Somewhere between the fortieth floor and the lobby you stopped being able to say what the last hour felt like. Not what happened in it — you can recite that cold, numbers, names, who said what. What it felt like. You have gotten so good at the account of your life that you have quietly stopped living the parts you're accounting for.

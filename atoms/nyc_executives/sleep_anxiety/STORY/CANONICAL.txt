Then he learned his body would generate the anxiety anyway without the phone, running its own version of the overnight data: positions, exposures, the thing he might have missed in the Thursday filing. The phone wasn't the problem. The phone was just where the anxiety had learned to look.

He'd run the deals in his head for so many years that his body had stopped filing them as work. They ran in sleep the way background processes run on a server — not demanding attention, just consuming resources. He'd wake drained without knowing what had been running. The board meeting was his most vivid dream and he never remembered it in the morning.

She lay in the dark at 4am with the deal fully quiet and her chest still loudly on. Not about anything specific — that was the part she couldn't explain to anyone. Not the market, not the board meeting, not the email. Just the on-ness itself. A readiness without an object. A body that had been standing watch for so long it had forgotten how to stand down, and wasn't sure anymore what standing down would even feel like.

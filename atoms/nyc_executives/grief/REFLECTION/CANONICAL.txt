

## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: inherited

There is a particular way that grief can be invisible in a high-performance environment. The person experiences loss. But the person continues to produce. Continues to show up. Continues to deliver.

The loss is real. But it is absorbed into the continuation. The grief does not interrupt the work. The person is functioning while grieving. The grief is running in a separate track.

The mechanism is the parallel-track operation. The person is simultaneously in grief and in production. Two separate systems running at the same time. The grief does not stop the production. The production does not stop the grief.

I have felt this most acutely in moments after a significant loss when I have had to show up to work. The loss is there. But the work continues. I am in meetings processing loss in the background while appearing present in the meetings.

What I have come to understand is that this parallel-track operation is both a remarkable capacity and a significant cost. The capacity to continue functioning while grieving is valuable. But the cost is that the grief is never fully addressed. It is being carried underneath while the work continues.

The person is not avoiding the grief. They are compartmentalizing it. And the compartmentalization allows the work to continue, but it prevents the grief from being fully processed.

The grief persists because there is no space for it to be processed fully.

There is a particular way that grief can be unsynchronized with the social calendar.

The person experiences a loss that does not fit into the social rhythm of acknowledged grieving. The loss is real, but it is not the kind of loss that gets space.

The person is supposed to continue. The role requires it. The team requires it. The organization requires it.

So the person grieves in private. On their own time. In moments that are not protected or acknowledged.

The mechanism is the social-rhythm misalignment. The person's grief does not align with the social rhythm that accommodates grief. So the grief is pushed into the margins.

I have felt this most acutely in losses that did not feel like they were supposed to be grieved in a professional context. The loss of a relationship. The loss of an opportunity. The loss of a version of the future. These are real losses, but they do not get the acknowledged space that a death would get.

What I have come to understand is that this misalignment means that many griefs go unacknowledged. The person is grieving, but they are grieving in private.

And the privacy means the grief remains unresolved.

The person is carrying losses that nobody knows about.

## REFLECTION v05
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: inherited

There is a particular way that grief can produce a kind of numbness that feels like capacity.

The person experiences loss. The immediate response is shock. But then, as a protective mechanism, the person becomes numb.

In the numbness, the person can function. The grief is not being felt acutely. So the person can show up and do the work.

The numbness feels like strength. Like the person is handling it well. But the numbness is actually a protective mechanism.

The mechanism is the protective-numbness response. When the grief is too acute to function, the nervous system dampens the feeling. The person becomes numb.

The numbness allows function. But it also prevents processing.

I have felt this most acutely in the immediate aftermath of loss. The numbness was there. I could function. I appeared to be handling it. But the numbness meant that the loss was not being processed. It was being held in suspension.

What I have come to understand is that this numbness can be dangerous because it can last for a very long time. The person continues in the numbness. The numbness allows function. So the grief is never fully addressed.

The person is protected from the acute pain of grief, but at the cost of never actually grieving.

The numbness persists because processing requires feeling.

There is a particular way that grief can produce a kind of irritability that is hard to explain.

The person is grieving. The grief produces a particular kind of vulnerability. Small things become irritating. Normal frustrations become more intense.

But the irritability is not caused by the small things. It is caused by the grief that is running underneath. The grief is making the person more reactive.

The mechanism is the grief-vulnerability irritability. When the nervous system is processing grief, it is also more reactive. The threshold for irritation is lower.

I have felt this most acutely in moments when I have snapped at someone over something minor. And then realized that the irritation was not about the minor thing. It was about the grief.

What I have come to understand is that this irritability is one of the ways grief shows up in professional contexts. The person is grieving, but the grief shows up as irritation.

And the irritation can damage relationships because the people around the grieving person do not know that the irritation is about grief.

The grief is showing up as irritability, and being responded to as irritability.

There is a particular way that grief can affect the sense of time.

Time usually has a structure. But in grief, the structure can collapse. The hours are long. The days feel like they might never end.

Or alternatively, time can feel accelerated. The days and weeks pass but feel disconnected.

The mechanism is the time-perception disruption. Grief affects the way the brain perceives time.

I have felt this most acutely in the immediate aftermath of loss. The hours were very long. The days felt like they might stretch forever. And at the same time, the time was passing without my fully registering it.

What I have come to understand is that this disruption of time perception is one of the ways grief is destabilizing. The person loses not just the person they lost. They lose their normal sense of time.

The timeline is disrupted.

The person is moving through time in a disrupted way.

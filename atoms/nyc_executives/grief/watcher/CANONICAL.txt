The funeral is on a Tuesday in October. David arrives early, stands in the chapel, and watches the light come through the stained glass. His father's body is in the casket. The body looks wrong — not sleeping, not peaceful, just absent in a suit David recognizes. David stands and observes this. He is very still. His colleagues come and stand near him. They speak. He speaks. His voice is calm. His voice is the voice of a man handling an expected situation. His jaw is tight. His chest is closed. There is glass between him and the moment. He can see it happening — his hand shaking hands with the priest, his face arranged in the configuration of grief, his throat producing sounds of acknowledgment — but he is not in it. He is behind the glass, watching David do these things correctly. The pattern: he has been trained for this. Be present, but not present. Perform the appropriate response. Stay behind the glass. His shoulders are still. His hands are controlled. He breathes, and does not feel the breath.

His chest is completely closed. His shoulders are raised in a posture of bracing. His jaw is locked. He goes to work. He does his job. He performs the role of a senior executive handling a difficult personal transition with grace. The performance is perfect. The grief that should accompany the performance is unreachable. He is behind glass and he has been behind glass for so long that he is beginning to forget what it was like to be anywhere else. His hands grip the armrest of his office chair. He is holding something in place. That something is called: the pattern.

But it is becoming uncomfortable. The glass is still there. Behind the glass, there is something that needs to arrive.

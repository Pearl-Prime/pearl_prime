David sits in the 47th-floor bathroom stall, jaw clenched, shoulders curled inward. On his phone: the email announcing Michael got the Southeast portfolio. The one David had assumed was his. His chest tightens as he scrolls Michael's LinkedIn—same schools, same experience trajectory, two years younger. The math is immediate and brutal. Michael's promotion means the market is pricing him higher. Means David's advancement is not inevitable. The fluorescent light hums above the stall. His throat closes. He reads the email again. The wording is standard, celebratory. His hands shake as he deletes it. No one outside this bathroom knows how he's reading this moment—as evidence of drift, of invisibility, of the gap widening between where he is and where he should be. The shame arrives first, before he can name it.

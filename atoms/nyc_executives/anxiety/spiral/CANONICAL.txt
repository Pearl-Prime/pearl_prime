David is sitting in his therapist's office. The therapist asks him to describe what happens in his mind when he receives an ambiguous email. David begins to describe it and stops. Because describing it makes the machinery visible. The email arrives. The body activates. The mind generates a worst-case scenario. The scenario becomes evidence. The evidence becomes certainty. The certainty becomes physical. The physical becomes unbearable. The unbearable becomes action: checking, planning, rehearsing, controlling. And then the actual information arrives and it was never as bad as the scenario. David has just described, in clinical detail, a system that generates suffering from nothing. He has described a mind that manufactures threat so convincingly that the body cannot tell the difference between imagination and reality. The therapist is quiet. David is quiet. The room holds what David has just said. He has just described thirty years of his life. Thirty years of catastrophe that existed only inside his own skull. The deals that were fine. The partnerships that were stable. The clients that were satisfied. And David, in every case, experiencing existential terror. He puts his face in his hands. Not from sadness. From the staggering recognition that his suffering has been self-generated. That the spiral is not a response to his world. It is a world of its own. And he has been living in it instead of in the actual one.

Michael attends a meditation retreat. His firm's executive wellness program arranged it. Michael sits in silence for the first time in perhaps twenty years. The instruction is simple: notice your thoughts without following them. Michael notices a thought. The thought is: what if my largest client calls while I'm here. He follows the thought. The thought becomes a spiral. The spiral becomes a complete scenario in which the client calls, Michael is unreachable, the client interprets this as disengagement, the relationship dissolves, the revenue is lost, the partnership questions Michael's commitment. The meditation teacher rings a bell. Eight minutes have passed. Michael has been in catastrophe for all eight of them. He tries again. A thought arrives: what if the restructuring I heard about means my department. He follows it. Another spiral. Another complete narrative of professional annihilation. The bell rings. Eight more minutes consumed by self-generated disaster. Michael opens his eyes. He looks around the room. Other executives sit quietly. Michael has just confronted the central fact of his existence: he cannot be still because stillness is where the spiral has unlimited bandwidth. He has built his entire life around motion because motion keeps the spiral occupied with real inputs instead of imagined ones. Take away the motion and there is only the spiral. Take away the distractions and there is only the catastrophe machine, running at full capacity, in a silent room, in a man who has no idea how to turn it off.

James is walking through Central Park on a Saturday morning. The city is quiet. The air is cool. And James cannot feel any of it because the spiral is running a simulation of Monday's board meeting. The simulation is detailed. It includes dialogue. It includes the facial expressions of board members receiving bad news. It includes the specific tone of voice the chairman uses when he is disappointed. James is walking through one of the most beautiful parks in the world and he is not in the park. He is in a boardroom that does not yet exist, having a conversation that has not yet happened, experiencing emotions about outcomes that have not yet materialized. He stops. He stands on the path. Joggers pass him. A woman walks a dog. A child runs toward the fountain. James watches these people existing in the present moment and he understands, with a clarity that is almost physical, that he has not been in the present moment in years. Perhaps decades. The spiral has colonized his experience of time. He does not live in the present. He lives in the future. And the future, as his spiral constructs it, is always threatening. He is sixty-three years old. He has perhaps twenty good years left. And unless something changes, he will spend those twenty years the way he has spent the last forty: in a future that never arrives the way he fears, missing the life that is happening right now. His legs feel heavy. His eyes are wet. The park is beautiful and he is standing in it for the first time.

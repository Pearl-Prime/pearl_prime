David sits at the mahogany table, watching the client presentation loop through its third iteration of the same risk assessment. His chest carries something between weight and electricity. The thing everyone is not saying moves in the air like a shape they've all agreed not to name. He knows what it is. Three other people in this room know what it is. The numbers don't support what they're doing. The strategy is sound but the foundation underneath it sits on a bet they've already lost. His hands are flat on the table. His throat feels tight in a way that doesn't match the room temperature or the professional courtesy flowing around him. The words are available. They live in his mouth, grammatically clean, analytically precise. His jaw moves slightly, testing the space. Not yet. The presentation advances. His shoulders drop a fraction of an inch and the tightness moves into his belly.

She has spent forty-eight hours knowing something the team doesn't know: the initiative they're celebrating will cause real harm to the junior staff. Her hands grip the edge of the desk. What she wants to say would require saying "I disagree" in front of five people who have hired her, promoted her, built a dependency on her being stable. Her sternum aches. David decides to test the mechanism. He's been studying his own activation pattern long enough to predict it. In the next partner meeting, when the false alarm fires, he's going to speak the dissenting thing anyway. He's going to let the alarm run while the words come out. This is the turning point: the decision to act despite the prediction, not because the prediction is wrong, but because acting is more important than staying comfortable. His hands will shake.

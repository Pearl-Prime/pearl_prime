## THREAD v01
---
---
You scheduled the hard call with your mother's doctor for your quiet hour, then watched it slide to after work, then to tomorrow, then to "this week sometime." It was never about the doctor. The call had no visible first sentence your system could grab, so it kept sliding to wherever the pressure was lowest. Tonight, write the first sentence down before you dial, and notice how much less the call slides.

## THREAD v02
---
---
You keep meaning to update the caregiving schedule every Sunday, and every Sunday it's still sixteen empty rows by the time you go to bed. It was never about not caring how your parent is covered — you think about it all week. The schedule has no natural entry point, so your brain treats all sixteen rows as one enormous decision instead of sixteen small ones. Next Sunday, fill just the row for the day you've already been thinking about all week, and see what happens to the other fifteen.
---

## THREAD v03
---
---
You've opened the same intake form four times this week and closed it four times without typing a word. Each time it felt like nothing happened, because nothing did — the freeze reset itself at the same blank line every time. Leave one sentence typed, even a bad one, before you close it next time, so the sixth attempt starts somewhere other than zero.
---

## THREAD v04
---
---
You told yourself you'd "just sit down and knock out the paperwork" tonight, and you did sit down, laptop open, form loaded, and twenty minutes passed with nothing written. Sitting down solved a location problem. It never touched the actual starting problem, which is a separate event your brain needed a bridge for. Tomorrow, add the bridge — copy one field from last year's form — before assuming proximity to the laptop counts as trying.
---

## THREAD v05
---
---
You've read the same instruction paragraph five times this week, on five different forms, and it still doesn't move your hands any faster than the first time. Rereading feels like progress because it's an action, but it isn't the action the task actually needs. Next time you catch yourself on a second reread, stop and type the first field instead, even badly, and notice how differently that five minutes goes.
---

## THREAD v06
---
---
You keep telling your sibling you'll "get to" the long-term care plan this week, and this week keeps ending with the plan exactly as blank as it started. There was never a shortage of time — a whole week is plenty. There was a shortage of an edge, a moment where the task had a clear start and a clear stop. Next time, give it twenty-five minutes and one deliverable, and watch a week's worth of dread collapse into less than half an hour.
---

## THREAD v07
---
---
You noticed yourself touching the same calendar for the third time, right before a sign-up deadline where you needed the dialysis schedule. You weren't lost — you knew exactly where the schedule was. You just didn't have anything visible enough to grab and act on. Next time, before the deadline hits, photograph the schedule and use that instead of the calendar you keep almost-opening.
---

## THREAD v08
---
---
You've had the same doctor's name in your inbox for six days, in the subject line of an email you haven't opened. Each day it sat there, the imagined version of the news got a little worse in your head, and the actual email got no closer to being read. Nothing in the real email has changed in six days. Open it tomorrow and read only the first line — that's the whole job for now.
---

## THREAD v09
---
---
You catch yourself, partway through sorting pill bottles, checking a text message that has nothing urgent in it, for the third time this batch. It isn't that the text matters more than the sorting — it's newer, and your attention is wired to reach for what's new over what's already been touched. Next batch, build the novelty in on purpose: sort in a different room, read a few labels out loud, before your attention goes looking for it somewhere else.
---

## THREAD v10
---
---
You keep starting the intake form with finances one week and health history the next, re-deciding every single time which piece to lead with, and every single time that re-deciding eats the first ten minutes. The writing was never the slow part. The choosing was. Pick one order now, in writing, and use it for every form from here forward, no exceptions.
---

## THREAD v11
---
---
You noticed the pill sorting got easier around bottle four, not harder, even though bottle four should logically be more tiring than bottle one. The first few carried a cost the later ones didn't — the cost of getting your hands moving in the first place. Next time, remember that the hardest bottle is always the first one, and it isn't a preview of the other eleven.
---

## THREAD v12
---
---
You spent ten minutes holding the wrong folder before another parent pointed out the right one was in your bag, and something in you unlocked the moment the object in your hands changed. It wasn't really about the folder. It was about having something concrete and correct in your hands to act from. Next time you feel stuck holding the wrong version of something, physically change what's in your hands before trying to change your mind.

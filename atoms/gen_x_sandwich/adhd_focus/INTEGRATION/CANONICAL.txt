## INTEGRATION v01
---
---
Tomorrow, before you sit down to whatever's been stalling — the Medicare form, the intake paperwork, the schedule you keep opening and closing — write the literal first physical verb on a sticky note before you start: not "finish the form" but "copy last year's first field." Put it where you'll see it the second you open the laptop, so the decision about where to begin is already made before the freeze has a chance to show up. You don't need the whole task solved tonight. You need one verb, executed once.
---

## INTEGRATION v02
---
---
Tomorrow, pick the one task on your list that has no natural stopping point — the pill bottles, the schedule rows, the school emails — and give it an artificial one before you begin: a timer, a fixed count of three, a pile you physically move things out of as proof of progress. Your attention system needs to see the task shrinking, not just trust that it's shrinking somewhere in the abstract.
---

## INTEGRATION v03
---
---
Tomorrow, notice the first moment you catch yourself reorganizing a drawer, scrolling your phone, or refolding laundry instead of starting the real task, and instead of judging it, treat it as useful information: that's the freeze announcing itself, and it means the task in front of you has no visible first step yet. Just name the smallest true first action out loud — "open the form," "read line one" — and do only that.
---

## INTEGRATION v04
---
---
Tomorrow, right after the kids leave for school, protect the next twenty minutes for whichever caregiving task needs the deepest, most sustained attention — usually the paperwork — before your work day and its own switches begin. Task-switching costs accumulate invisibly across a sandwich-generation day, and the deep task left for "whenever there's time" ends up fighting for scraps of a resource that's already spent by evening.
---

## INTEGRATION v05
---
---
Tomorrow, if there's an email, a form, or a conversation you've been avoiding because it feels bigger than it probably is, don't commit to handling all of it — just open it and read the first line, nothing else, no obligation to respond yet. The flood of dread attaches to the whole imagined task, not to one line of actual text.
---

## INTEGRATION v06
---
---
Tomorrow, for whatever multi-entry-point task keeps stalling you — the intake form, the care plan, the school email — pre-decide one arbitrary but fixed order you'll use every time, and stop re-litigating which piece to start with. Health history first, always. The apology first, always. It doesn't need to be the objectively best order; it needs to be an order you never have to choose again.
---

## INTEGRATION v07
---
---
Tomorrow, put the thing you keep forgetting to do — the prescription refill, the permission slip, the callback — somewhere physical and visible the moment it occurs to you, not at the end of the day when you try to remember everything at once. A sticky note on the fridge, a running list taped inside a cabinet, one phone note you open the second the thought appears.
---

## INTEGRATION v08
---
---
Tomorrow, before you start the task you've been dreading, deliberately lower the bar for the first pass: type bullet fragments instead of sentences, don't fix a single typo, write one line you know is clumsy on purpose. The freeze often isn't fear of the task — it's waiting for a polished opening line that was never going to arrive before you started typing anyway.
---

## INTEGRATION v09
---
---
Tomorrow, move the physical object connected to your hardest task into your direct path — the pill organizer left out on the counter instead of in a cabinet, the intake form open on the kitchen table instead of in a folder, the school portal pulled up before you close your laptop for the night.
---

## INTEGRATION v10
---
---
Tomorrow, when you sit down at the kitchen table to finally do the paperwork, notice if sitting down and starting feel like two separate events — because for your brain, they usually are. Build a small bridge between them: touch the first form, say the first line out loud, type one word you'll delete later.
---

## INTEGRATION v11
---
---
Tomorrow, if a repetitive task starts feeling heavier the further into it you get — pill bottle nine harder than bottle one — introduce one small deliberate change partway through: a different room, a different pen, reading a few labels out loud. This isn't about the content changing; it's about giving your attention system something new enough to re-engage with.
---

## INTEGRATION v12
---
---
Tomorrow, before you start whatever open-ended task has been generating quiet dread without producing any actual work — the long-term care plan, the family caregiving schedule you keep meaning to organize — give yourself a fake but firm container: twenty-five minutes, one deliverable, nothing more required.

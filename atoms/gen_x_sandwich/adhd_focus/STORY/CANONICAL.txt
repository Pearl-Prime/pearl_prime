## STORY v01
---
---
Marcus sits with the Medicare folder open and the four remaining pages blank. He reorganizes his mother's medication list. He refills his coffee he doesn't want. He reads three unrelated emails twice. Then his sister texts: "you doing the paperwork tonight too? I just copy the info from last year's form and update the dates, takes twenty minutes." Marcus doesn't answer the text. He opens last year's form. He copies the first field, updates the date, and hits save on that line. The next field takes less time. By the eighth field his hands have found the rhythm and stopped asking his brain for permission first. He finishes at eleven, tired but done, and realizes the freeze was never about four pages. It was about the first field not existing yet. Once it existed, the rest were just more fields.

## STORY v02
---
---
Denise stares at her son's essay until the cursor blink starts to feel aggressive. She gets up for coffee she doesn't want. She straightens a stack of mail into a neater stack. Her husband pokes his head in: "Want me to read the first paragraph out loud? Might help you hear what you want to say." He reads it. Denise types one honest sentence of feedback almost immediately after, then keeps going, because now there are words on the page that aren't hers to invent from nothing — they're just hers to shape. Twenty minutes later the feedback is done, and she sends it before she can reread it a sixth time.

## STORY v03
---
---
Priya walks past the pill bottles a seventh time and finally picks up just one bottle, telling herself she'll just read the label, no sorting yet. She reads it. Without deciding to, she opens the organizer and drops two pills into Monday's slot. Then she picks up the next bottle. She has started sorting without a moment where she decided to start sorting. By bottle four she's in a groove. She finishes all twelve before dinner, more than she expected, and understands: she wasn't avoiding the sorting. She was avoiding the moment of starting with full intent. Reading the label first removed the moment.

## STORY v04
---
---
Tanya reorganizes the same drawer twice while her husband asks if she's written the tour questions yet. She says not yet, in the tone of someone who has said not yet three times already. He says, "just write down the first three things you're worried about, the rest will follow." She writes: staffing ratio at night, how they handle medication changes, what a typical day looks like. Three questions in ninety seconds. Four more follow easily once the first three exist. The list didn't need to be complete on the first pass. It needed to exist.

## STORY v05
---
---
James holds the wrong folder at back-to-school night and cannot seem to set it down, so another parent nearby, noticing his confusion, points at his bag: "is the other one in there?" James checks. It is. He swaps folders, and something in his chest unlocks with it. He opens his daughter's teacher conference notes, reads his own note from last week — "ask about the reading group placement" — and asks exactly that. The conference goes fine. Afterward he thinks about the ten minutes he spent unable to put down a folder that had nothing to do with tonight's actual conversation.

## STORY v06
---
---
Renee's cursor blinks on the blank intake form for the fortieth minute. Her sister calls to check in and asks what she's working on. Renee explains their mother's whole situation out loud, casually, the way she's explained it to the doctor, to the social worker, to her husband. Her sister says, "that's literally what the form is asking for, just write that." Renee opens the form and types the sentence she just said out loud. Then the next thing she said. The form fills in from her own spoken explanation, not from some imagined ideal version of a legal document. She's done in fifteen minutes once she starts typing what she already knows how to say.

## STORY v07
---
---
Coach Diallo reads the school's email subject line for the fourth time and gets nowhere new. He opens a fresh note instead of the actual reply thread — no formatting, no "Dear," just a blank page — and types "We can make the meeting work on" and stops. He deletes it and writes "Tuesday after 4pm works for us." One true sentence. He copies it into the actual reply. The reply stops being the obstacle once he stopped trying to write directly into the thread. Five more replies follow the same way: blank page first, real reply second. He clears all six before his lunch break ends, faster than he's ever done this task.

## STORY v08
---
---
Ms. Whitfield stands in the pharmacy line watching the minutes disappear and starts, out of habit, mentally trying to solve both errands at once instead of picking one. The pharmacist calls her up and she says, simply, "let's handle the insurance override first, I'll figure out the rest after." She solves one problem at a time instead of trying to hold both simultaneously. She makes it to practice only two minutes late. She realizes the two errands were never actually tangled together — she'd just been treating them as one impossible knot instead of two separate, doable things.

## STORY v09
---
---
Priya opens the power of attorney document a fifth time, this time with a visible timer set for twenty-five minutes on her phone. Seeing the countdown changes something — there's now an end point she can see, not just a task with no edges. She fills in the first section before checking the timer again. Eighteen minutes left. She keeps going. When the timer rings she has the whole document drafted, rougher than she'd like but real. She hadn't needed more time or more energy. She'd needed the invisible task to become a visible, shrinking number she could watch move.

## STORY v10

Marcus opens the cardiologist's office email again — subject line "Following up on medication change" — for the sixth day and, instead of reading the whole thread, reads only the first line. It says: "Just confirming your mother is tolerating the new dosage okay, no action needed if so." Not the complicated conversation he'd built in his head. He writes back two sentences in ninety seconds and hits send before he can reread them six more times.

## STORY v12

---
Theo rereads the respite care instructions a fifth time and catches himself doing it — actually notices the loop for what it is. He says out loud, to his empty kitchen, "I'm rereading instead of typing." Naming it out loud breaks something small. He types the first field badly, abbreviated and rough, and does not fix it. He does the next one. Somewhere around field six the pace picks up, and he clears all eleven with four minutes to spare.

## STORY v13
---
---
James scrolls through three-year-old insurance cards at eleven-forty at night, cards that expired long ago, and knows exactly what he's doing and why it isn't helping. He closes the old photos. He texts his mother's home health agency's after-hours line: "what specific info do you need before tomorrow's visit." They text back in ten minutes: "just the new plan's member ID, that's it." He finds the current card, texts the one number, and closes his phone. The whole task took five minutes once he asked instead of searched blindly.

---
Renee logs into the financial aid portal a third time, this time with her phone timer set for five minutes, visible beside the keyboard. She tells herself she only has to enter the first three fields before the timer's up — she can stop after that if she wants. She enters three. She doesn't stop. The timer becomes something to beat rather than something to dread, and she submits the verification at 11:51pm, nine minutes before the deadline she'd spent all week avoiding by not looking at it directly.

## STORY v15

---
Coach Diallo looks at the sixteen empty rows in the caregiving schedule and does the dishes instead, then the dog, then finds himself standing in front of the spreadsheet anyway, laptop open, doing nothing. His wife calls out from the other room, "just do Tuesday, you already know who's covering it." One row. He fills it in ninety seconds because he already knows exactly what to write — he's been thinking about it for days. Row two takes longer because he has to check with his sister. Row three is faster again. He finishes twelve of sixteen before bed and the remaining four the next morning, once the shape of the task had already been proven survivable.

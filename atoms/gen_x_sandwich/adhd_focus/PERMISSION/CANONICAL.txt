## PERMISSION v01
---
---
You are allowed to write the literal first physical verb of a dreaded task on a sticky note the night before, instead of trusting yourself to invent it cold in the moment.
---

## PERMISSION v02
---
---
You are allowed to let the first draft of a legal form, an email, or a care plan be genuinely rough, because a rough first draft is what makes a good second draft possible.
---

## PERMISSION v03
---
---
You are allowed to externalize every deadline and every "remember to" onto a visible surface instead of trusting a working memory that was never built to hold un-anchored intentions for six hours while juggling two households.
---

## PERMISSION v04
---
---
You are allowed to give an open-ended task like the long-term care plan a fake twenty-five-minute container instead of waiting for the motivation that an edgeless task will never generate on its own.
---

## PERMISSION v05
---
---
You are allowed to read only the first line of the doctor's email you've been avoiding for six days, with zero obligation to respond to the rest of it today.
---

## PERMISSION v06
---
---
You are allowed to pre-decide one fixed, arbitrary order for multi-entry-point paperwork and use it every single time, instead of re-choosing the "best" starting point at every sitting.
---

## PERMISSION v07
---
---
You are allowed to protect the twenty minutes right after the kids leave for school for the hardest caregiving task, before the work day's own switches begin.
---

## PERMISSION v08
---
---
You are allowed to move the pill organizer, the open folder, or the pulled-up portal into your direct physical path, instead of relying on a remembered plan to carry the task forward.
---

## PERMISSION v09
---
---
You are allowed to set a visible countdown timer during caregiving tasks, because your internal clock isn't going to warn you that two hours have disappeared until it's already gone.
---

## PERMISSION v10
---
---
You are allowed to name the freeze out loud — "I'm rereading instead of typing" — the moment you notice it, instead of pushing through in silence and blaming yourself for the stall.
---

## PERMISSION v11
---
---
You are allowed to change one small variable partway through a repetitive task — different room, out loud — when your attention starts sliding, instead of assuming you should be able to power through unchanged.
---

## PERMISSION v12
---
---
You are allowed to treat sitting down at the table and actually starting the task as two separate events that need their own bridge, instead of assuming proximity to the work means you already tried.
---

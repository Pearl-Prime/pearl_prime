## INTEGRATION v01
---
mode: BODY_LANDED
reframe_type: BODY_FACT
weight: standard
carry_line: "Your hands know something is unsolved."
---
You're replaying the conversation with your mom again. About the driving. Whether she should still be driving. You said something, she said something, neither of you solved it. Now your hands are tight. Your back is tense. Your mind keeps going back to the moment because something in you believes you should have said something different. Something that would have landed better. Something that would have fixed it.

But the conversation is done. It happened Wednesday. It's Friday now.

Your shoulders carry the weight of an unsolved problem — your teenager, your parent, the impossible logistics of everyone needing something at the same time. Your body knows this. It's braced against all of it.

Notice your breath. The conversation is finished even if the problem isn't. Your body can't solve this. Neither can you.

## INTEGRATION v02
---
mode: COST_VISIBLE
reframe_type: COST_UNCOUNTED
weight: standard
carry_line: "The analysis is stealing your week."
---
You've spent hours replaying that conversation. The exact way she answered. Whether she was hurt. Whether you were too harsh. Whether you should call and clarify. Whether you should apologize even though you don't think you did anything wrong.

This costs you. Not the conversation itself — the replay. The hours of attention that could have gone to work, to your actual family, to sleep. The part of your mind that never left the room with your mother.

You're carrying three difficult relationships at once — your parent who's aging, your teenager who's growing, your job that demands. One conversation shouldn't take this much of your life but it is. That's the math of the sandwich. Every interaction has more weight.

The conversation happened. The analysis is a choice you keep making.

## INTEGRATION v03
---
mode: QUESTION_OPEN
reframe_type: UNCERTAINTY_HELD
weight: standard

---
You're replaying her face, her tone, searching for signs that she's angry or hurt. But what if she understood you exactly as you meant? What if the way she said what she said wasn't rejection, just acknowledgment? What if she heard that you're concerned and she's thinking about it?

What if your fear of the conversation is bigger than the conversation itself?

What if by Friday night she's stopped thinking about it entirely and moved on to something else?

What if you're the only one still there?

There's a number in my head that I check constantly. It doesn't matter how much is actually in the account. The number is never enough. The mechanism that evaluates security has become automated to the point that I can't actually feel safe anymore, no matter what the math says.
I was raised to understand scarcity. My parents didn't have much, and their anxious relationship with money became my anxious relationship with money. I learned early that money disappears, that security is fragile, that catastrophe is always one emergency away.
So now, even when the account has what most people would consider a healthy balance, my internal mechanism is still calibrated to fear. It's still checking, still scanning for danger, still convinced that whatever is there won't be enough.
The teaching here is about the automation of anxiety. Your mind learns to evaluate threats a certain way, and then it keeps evaluating threats that way even when the actual conditions have changed. I've made more money than my parents ever did. I've built some stability. But the mechanism still runs the same old calculation: not enough, not safe, not secure.
That's the mechanism I'm naming: the automatic threat assessment that became divorced from actual reality somewhere along the way.
What interests me now is whether it's possible to update that mechanism, or whether once it learns to be afraid, it can only remember fear.

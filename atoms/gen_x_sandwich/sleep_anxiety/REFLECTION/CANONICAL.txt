---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I'm learning to recognize a pattern in my nights. I go to bed knowing I should sleep, and there's a mechanism that activates the moment my head hits the pillow. Suddenly, I'm alert. The day's unfinished conversations come back. The tomorrow's obligations appear. The thoughts start cycling.
The mechanism is subtle. It's not insomnia exactly. I can fall asleep. But the sleep is thin and vigilant. Part of me is always half-awake, listening, waiting, monitoring.
For people my age, this pattern probably developed early. We were latchkey kids, or we grew up with parents who had their own anxiety, and we learned to stay alert even when we were supposed to be resting. We learned that being awake was safer than being asleep. Being aware meant you could respond to emergencies.
Now I'm an adult with my own responsibilities—aging parents, teenagers, work—and the old vigilance is still there. My body still believes that if I relax completely, something bad will happen. If I truly sleep, I might miss something important.
The mechanism is named as anxiety, but it's really a protective pattern that learned to keep me alert. The teaching here is understanding that your sleep anxiety isn't necessarily a disorder. It might be a survival skill that was useful once and is now running in a context where it no longer serves you.
I'm sitting with what it would mean to actually turn off that vigilance and trust that things can be okay without my constant monitoring.

I want to sit with you in what I'm understanding about sleep anxiety as a protective mechanism. There's something about the night that feels vulnerable. You're lying down, you're not in control, you can't respond to emergencies.
So the mechanism activates. It keeps you half-awake. It monitors your surroundings. It runs through scenarios of what might go wrong. The vigilance feels protective, even though it's preventing you from actually sleeping.
The protection makes sense from a certain perspective. If something did happen in the night, you'd be more ready to respond if you weren't in deep sleep. But the cost of that readiness is that you never actually rest.

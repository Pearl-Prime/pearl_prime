COST_INTENSITY: 2

---
Paula's college roommate posts about her parents visiting. They're in their eighties, healthy, active, still traveling. Paula's mother is in treatment. Paula feels the comparison immediately. Other people have parents who are fine. Other people are empty nesters who can travel or rest. Paula is a middle-aged woman managing cancer treatment for her parent and grief about who her mother is becoming. Her throat closes. She doesn't post about this. She doesn't tell anyone. But the comparison is there, a constant low-level ache. Her roommate seems to have received the retirement narrative—the rest, the freedom, the grandchildren as gifts rather than as additional responsibility. Paula received a different narrative. Her shoulders tighten. She's doing something wrong, somehow. Or her mother is. Or the universe is. The comparison creates a shame underneath the grief, as if she should be more equipped to handle this than people whose parents are still robust.

---
Paula's comparison is deepening. She's scrolling through her college group chat and seeing people celebrate retirement plans. Paula is celebrating a clean scan report for her mother, which is good but also means more years of monitoring her mother's health. Paula is stuck in a different timeline. ## TURNING_POINT v01
---
path: story_atoms/gen_x_sandwich/anchored/anxiety/comparison/turning_point/micro/v01.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Paula calls her college roommate. She says, "Your parents sound amazing. My mother is in cancer treatment." The roommate is silent. Then she says, "I'm sorry. I didn't know." Paula realizes her roommate wasn't bragging. She was just living. Paula has been filtering everything through comparison without ever saying what her actual situation is. The turning point is that Paula stops hiding her life. She tells her group chat: "My mother is sick. I'm not doing the retirement thing right now." A woman Paula barely knew messages her privately: "My mother had cancer too. You're not alone." Paula's throat releases slightly. The comparison was partly about invisibility. She was comparing her hidden reality to their visible reality. When she becomes visible, the comparison loses power.



## INTEGRATION v01
---
integration_mode: BODY-LANDED
reframe_type: BODY-FACT

My shoulders are touching my ears again. This is what anxiety looks like in my body. Not a problem to solve. A fact to notice. I've been running the retirement math in my head since 6 a.m. My chest is tight in a way sleep won't fix. My mother called twice before breakfast. My daughter needs something for school. My boss emailed at midnight.

I notice this without adding the story that it means something is wrong with me. The tightness is information. My nervous system is saying something is asking for attention. Not that I'm failing. That I'm paying attention.

I lower my shoulders. I feel the length in my neck. I place my feet on the ground. The ground is holding me. The floor is real. This moment is survivable. I can sit in this meeting. I can make this call. I can do one thing while being anxious about seven others.

**Carry line: Anxiety means I'm paying attention.**

## INTEGRATION v03
---
integration_mode: COST-VISIBLE
reframe_type: HONEST-WEIGHT

Anxiety costs something. It costs sleep. It costs presence with my family. It costs the ability to taste my food or feel pleasure in things I usually enjoy. It costs my shoulders being relaxed. It costs my jaw being soft. It costs the version of me that can sit still.

I'm not going to tell myself it doesn't cost anything. It does. But I'm paying it anyway. Because the alternative is not showing up for my mother. Not caring about my children. Not pushing myself at work. So I'm anxious. I'm paying the cost. I'm doing the things.

My body is tired from holding this. My nervous system is tired from running this fast. But I'm still here. Still moving. Still doing what needs doing. The cost is real. I'm paying it. That's what this time looks like.

**Carry line: I'm paying the price. I keep going.**

## INTEGRATION v05
---
integration_mode: SOMEONE-ELSE
reframe_type: WITNESS-COMPASSION

If my best friend was sitting in this waiting room. If she had a trembling hand and hadn't slept well. If she was worrying about money and her mother and her job all at once. What would I tell her? That she's failing? No. I would tell her she's doing something hard. I would tell her that anyone would feel anxious.

I would not tell her to just calm down. I would sit with her. I would acknowledge that this is hard. I would notice her strength in keeping going anyway. I would see her. I would believe her. I would know she's doing her best.

So I'm telling myself this now. I'm the one in the waiting room. I'm doing something hard. My anxiety is normal. My fear is reasonable. I'm doing it anyway. That's the thing I notice. Not the anxiety. My capacity to move through it.

**Carry line: I'm doing something hard. That matters.**

## INTEGRATION v07
---
integration_mode: STILL-HERE
reframe_type: ENDURANCE-QUIET

The anxiety will probably not go away by itself. It will probably stay through the meeting. It will probably stay through the phone call with my mother. It will probably stay through dinner. I'm learning to do things while anxious. Not to wait until the anxiety leaves. Because it won't. Not until after I've done all the things.

So I'm here with it. Not fighting it. Not celebrating it. Just here. Just doing. Just continuing. This is a quieter strength than I imagined having. It's not dramatic. It's not confident. It's just: I'm still showing up. I'm still present. I'm still doing the next thing.

My body knows how to do this now. After weeks of practice. After months of waking up tight-chested and doing the day anyway. My body knows. My nervous system knows. This is survivable. The anxiety is real. Survival is also real.

**Carry line: I am here with all of it.**

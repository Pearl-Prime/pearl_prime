## COMPRESSION v01
---
---
Presence isn't held, it's returned to. The mind drifts toward unresolved worries about either generation — normal, constant, not a flaw. The cost isn't disaster; it's half-presence: technically there, actually nowhere, faces and details lost. The fix isn't willpower, it's a cheap, repeatable cue at every threshold — a breath, a word — that marks the last conversation closed and this one started. Repetition trains the return itself to get faster and cost less. Measure days by returns made, not calm felt.
---

## COMPRESSION v02
---
---
Multitasking between a parent's care and a teenager's needs isn't parallel work — it's fast switching with a hidden tax, paid in noticing. Split attention misses the subtle signal that only continuous attention catches. The reframe: stop aiming for a focused day, aim for a fast return, treating every catch-and-come-back as a rep. The goal isn't serenity; it's accuracy, attention on what's real right now, stress included.
---

## COMPRESSION v03
---
---
A looping worry about a parent or child isn't defeated by suppression — suppression is its own hidden task, and it makes the loop louder. The fix is parking: name it as real, give it a specific later, then redirect to one concrete thing in the actual room. Presence, meanwhile, isn't a switch that's on or off — it's a rubber band that stretches and snaps back, and the only trainable part is how cheap the snap-back gets with reps.
---

## COMPRESSION v04
---
---
Half-presence is invisible in the moment and obvious in hindsight: a day where both generations got attended to and nobody who did the attending was ever fully there for either. It isn't fixed by trying harder to focus, which is too vague under real load. It's fixed by closing each conversation before opening the next — one breath, silently finished — so attention stops leaking forward. The caregivers who seem steady aren't undistracted; they've simply returned so many times the motion costs them almost nothing anymore.

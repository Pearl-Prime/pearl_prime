I notice that burnout doesn't announce itself with fanfare. It creeps in like a utility bill you stop really reading—you just pay it. There's a mechanism at work here that I've come to recognize in myself and in the faces of people my age: we were conditioned to keep moving, to absorb the weight without complaint, to find the next task before the previous one was done. The system doesn't ask if you're running on empty. It doesn't care. It just needs you to keep running.
What I'm beginning to understand is that burnout operates through a kind of invisible delegation. You take on the responsibility of holding everything together—aging parents, your own children, work that expands to fill every gap, the maintenance of a household, the emotional labor of keeping everyone else's lives functional. And somewhere along the way, the engine stops asking for fuel. You just drive.
The teaching here is subtle: burnout isn't a crisis moment. It's the slow replacement of presence with mere continuation. You're still showing up. You're still doing. But something in the mechanism has shifted from engagement to automation. The conscious parts of you have stepped back, and the machine is running on its own now.
I sit with this feeling most mornings. There's the body, moving through routines. There's the mind, attending to tasks. But there's also this other thing—a kind of absence that isn't absence exactly. It's more like you're operating from a considerable distance from yourself.
The real mechanism to name here is what I'll call "invisible continuation." It's the part of us that learned early that stopping wasn't an option, so instead we just keep adjusting our tolerance for emptiness. We get better at running on less. We don't ask for relief because we learned relief wasn't something that came. And so burnout isn't dramatic—it's just the eventual cost of that particular bargain.
What happens next, I don't know yet.

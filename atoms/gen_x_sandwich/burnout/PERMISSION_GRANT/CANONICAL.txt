## PERMISSION_GRANT v01
---

---
Sandwich-generation burnout arrives wearing a stack of unspoken rules. Be the one who has it together for your parent. Be the one who has it together for your kid. Be the one who is at career peak for the job. Take no time off you cannot justify to all three. Hold the family group text together. Hold the marriage together. Hold yourself together. Never let any of the three generations see the cost on you. These rules are not law. They are decades of accumulated training talking back at you. Set them down.

You are allowed to be depleted in front of your aging parent. The image of you as the strong adult child is a costume the role gave you somewhere in your thirties. Setting it down does not stop you from being a good child to your parent. It lets you be a real one.

You are allowed to be depleted in front of your teenager. The image of you as the unflappable parent is a costume that has stopped fitting the actual stage you are in. Your teen has lived with you long enough to see through it anyway. Naming what is true is not damaging them. It is teaching them how a real adult lives with limits.

You are allowed to not have an answer for what to do about your parent's care. The decision is hard because the decision is hard. There is no version of the right answer that does not cost something. You are allowed to sit in the not-knowing without making yourself wrong for it.

You are allowed to feel the grief that comes with this decade. The grief about your parent's decline. The grief about your kid leaving. The grief about a version of your career that did not happen because the other two were happening at the same time. The grief is not self-pity. It is the body acknowledging real losses.

You are allowed to need rest that none of the three generations are budgeting for. The career-peak years are not designed around a body that is also carrying the start of one life and the end of another. The need for rest is not failure. It is the body asking for what the situation requires.

You are allowed to disappoint someone in the chain on any given day. You cannot show up fully for three generations every day. Some days, one generation gets less of you. That is not failure. That is the math of one body in three roles.

You are allowed to be the consciousness watching the burnout, not the burnout itself. The call from the facility hits. The text from the kid arrives. The email from work pings. The chest tightens. And somewhere behind all of it, awareness keeps watching. That awareness is the seat. That awareness is the part of you that survives the decade, regardless of how the decade goes.

These are not affirmations. They are accurate descriptions of the rights every sandwich-generation nervous system comes with. Read them again. Read them slowly. Let the body hear them, one permission at a time.

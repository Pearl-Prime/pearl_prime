What I have come to think about is the way that this mechanism operates without a brake. It does not have a built-in stopping point. It does not tell you to stop. It tells you to keep listening, keep caring, keep showing up. And you do. Until one day you realize that there is nothing left.

The fatigue is not a sign that you are not cut out for listening. The fatigue is a sign that you have been listening to a scale of suffering that is larger than any one person can hold.

## CORE v10

When you stop running from yourself, something shifts.

Not dramatically. Not all at once. But the relief is real—like lowering your shoulders after you didn't know you were holding them tight.

Most of us spend our lives in motion. Toward something. Away from something. The motion itself becomes the identity: the hustler, the fixer, the one who has it figured out, the one who's still trying.

Reflection asks you to stop.

Not forever. Not to abandon your life. But to pause long enough to actually *see* what's there.

What are you carrying? What are you running from? What matters, actually, when you're not performing or protecting or proving?

The answers don't come instantly. They come in the quiet. In the noticing.

---

Sit with one question this week. Not to answer it quickly. But to live with it.

*What would change if I stopped trying to convince myself—or anyone else—that I have this figured out?*

Sit with that. Let it breathe. Notice what comes up—the fear, the relief, the truth underneath.

That's reflection. Not thinking about your life. *Seeing* your life.

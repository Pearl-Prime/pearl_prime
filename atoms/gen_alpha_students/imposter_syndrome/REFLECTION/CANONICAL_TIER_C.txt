

## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I get good grades. Teachers say I'm smart. But I don't feel smart. I feel like I'm just good at memorizing what teachers want me to say. And eventually someone will realize that I don't actually understand things the way truly intelligent people do. The mechanism is called achievement-based imposter syndrome—the disconnect between external success and internal competence feeling.
---

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
What strikes me is that I'm comparing myself to the smartest people I know. And I'm convinced they understand things more deeply than I do. That they have some kind of intellectual clarity that I don't have. So even though I might be doing well, I feel behind. The mechanism is called upward comparison imposter syndrome.
---

## REFLECTION v03
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
There's a specific kind of imposter feeling that comes from being in accelerated or gifted programs. Because everyone around me is also high-achieving. So it's not obvious that I'm different. But somehow I feel like I'm the least qualified person there. The mechanism is called relative inadequacy imposter syndrome.
---

## REFLECTION v04
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I've noticed that my imposter syndrome is worse when I'm in new situations. When I'm the new person at school or in a program. It takes me a long time to feel like I belong. Even when I'm doing well. The mechanism is called new-situation imposter syndrome.
---

## REFLECTION v05
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
What I'm realizing is that my imposter syndrome might be connected to perfectionism. Because I set a high bar for myself. And I always feel like I'm falling short. So I don't trust that my success means I'm actually competent. The mechanism is called perfectionism-driven imposter syndrome.
---

## REFLECTION v06
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
There's a part of my imposter syndrome that's about representation. I don't see enough people who look like me in the spaces I aspire to. So I have a hard time believing that I belong there. The mechanism is called representation-deficit imposter syndrome.
---

## REFLECTION v07
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I notice that I'm hardest on myself when I make mistakes. A single wrong answer becomes evidence that I'm not actually smart. Which doesn't seem logical. But it feels true. The mechanism is called catastrophic error imposter syndrome.
---

## REFLECTION v08
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
What strikes me is that I'm able to see other people's competence more clearly than I can see my own. I can recognize intelligence in my peers. But when I look at myself, I see all the ways I'm inadequate. The mechanism is called asymmetric competence perception.
---

## REFLECTION v09
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: inherited
---
There's something about the social media comparison culture that feeds my imposter syndrome. Everyone's highlight reels look more accomplished than my behind-the-scenes reality. The mechanism is called social media imposter syndrome amplification.
---

## REFLECTION v10
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I've learned that my imposter syndrome is partly about the expectation to have my life figured out. Like I should already know if I'm college material. If I'm smart enough. If I'm going to be successful. And the uncertainty creates doubt about my ability. The mechanism is called premature success-prediction imposter syndrome.
---

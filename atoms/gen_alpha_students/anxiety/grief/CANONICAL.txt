Maya remembers when the school hallway didn't make her stomach tighten. Third grade—she walked the hallway without planning her route around people, without monitoring faces for disapproval, without the constant scan. The hallway was just a hallway. Now the hallway is a field of social data points that her body processes as threats. She misses the version of herself who walked without scanning. The missing is grief—not for a person but for a state of being. The state of being was ease. The ease left so gradually she didn't notice it leaving. She only notices it's gone.

Zoe used to raise her hand in every class. Her mom tells her this—first and second grade, the hand was always up. Zoe barely remembers the hand being up. She remembers when it stopped: fourth grade, a wrong answer, a kid who laughed. One kid, one laugh. The hand went down and stayed down. She grieves the raised hand. ## MECHANISM_PROOF v01

MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3

---
Maya realizes the grief has a specific structure. She's not grieving a person or a loss. She's grieving a capacity. The capacity was automatic engagement—walking into rooms without rehearsal, speaking without scripting, existing without monitoring. The capacity didn't die. The capacity was replaced by the monitoring system. The monitoring system is louder and more demanding than the capacity ever was. The grief is for what the monitoring displaced. The monitoring takes the space that ease used to occupy. If the monitoring reduced, the ease might return. But the monitoring presents itself as essential. The grief and the monitoring coexist. The grief says the monitoring costs too much. The monitoring says the grief is nostalgia.

Jaden traces when the performance stopped. Not the singing-for-family performance—the daily performance of being himself without watching himself be himself. The watching started in fifth grade. The watching is constant now: watching his words before they leave his mouth, watching his walk, watching his face for appropriate expressions. The watching is the anxiety's primary tool. Zoe counts the questions she doesn't ask per day. Average: seven. Seven questions her hand doesn't raise, her voice doesn't speak, her curiosity doesn't pursue. Multiplied by the school year: approximately 1,260 unasked questions. The number is the grief's evidence. Each unasked question is a small death of curiosity—not permanent, but suppressed. The suppression accumulates. A year of suppressed questions produces a student who appears to have fewer questions. The appearing is the anxiety's camouflage. The student hasn't lost curiosity. The student has learned to hide it. The hiding is what the grief mourns. The curiosity is still there, behind the hand that stays down.

Maya walks the hallway and catches the scanning in action. She's monitoring three faces ahead, calculating two route adjustments. She notices it. Jaden tells a joke in class without pre-screening it. The joke comes out before the watcher can review it for safety. The class laughs.

Maya spills her water bottle in class. Water spreads across the desk onto her notebook. Not a catastrophe—water, a notebook, a desk. Her face goes red before her hands move to clean it. The red is shame. The shame arrived before the cleanup because the shame isn't about the water. The shame is about being seen making a mess. Twenty-three kids saw the spill. The spill took two seconds. The shame will take the rest of the period. She wipes the desk and the shame stays on her skin like the water stayed on the notebook—soaked in, changing the surface.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v01
---
path: story_atoms/gen_alpha_students/anchored/anxiety/shame/mechanism_proof/micro/v01.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Maya maps what the shame actually responds to. The water spill: shame. Getting a good grade: no shame. Asking a question in class: shame. Answering correctly: no shame (but the shame from asking doesn't go away). The pattern: shame activates on visibility of imperfection. Any moment where she's seen being less than smooth, less than competent, less than put-together—the shame fires. She can be imperfect privately without shame. The shame needs witnesses. The witnesses are the fuel. Without witnesses, imperfection is just imperfection. With witnesses, imperfection becomes exposure.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02

BAND: 2
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Jaden notices the shame has a memory that only stores exposures. The stumble on "photosynthesis" is stored. The three paragraphs he read perfectly are not. The one answer he got wrong last month is stored. The seventeen he got right are not. The shame's database is curated for humiliation. Every entry is an exposure. Every non-exposure is deleted. He's running his self-assessment through a database that contains only his worst moments. The assessment always finds him lacking because the database only contains evidence of lacking. The evidence of competence was never filed.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v03
---
path: story_atoms/gen_alpha_students/anchored/anxiety/shame/mechanism_proof/micro/v03.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Zoe realizes the shame converts questions into confessions. Asking "what page are we on?" becomes confessing "I wasn't paying attention." Asking "can you repeat that?" becomes confessing "I didn't understand." Every question that reveals not-knowing is processed by the shame as a public admission of failure. But questions are how learning works. Learning requires not-knowing. The shame punishes the not-knowing that learning demands. The shame and learning are working against each other. The shame says: never reveal what you don't know. Learning says: reveal what you don't know so it can become what you do know.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v04
---
path: story_atoms/gen_alpha_students/anchored/anxiety/shame/mechanism_proof/micro/v04.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 4
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Tyler discovers the shame generalizes from specific to total. Being picked last at kickball becomes: I'm the kid who gets picked last. One event becomes an identity. The shame converts moments into identities because identities are permanent and moments are not. The shame needs permanence to maintain its authority. A moment of being picked last is survivable. Being "the kid who gets picked last" is an ongoing condition. The shame upgrades temporary events to permanent conditions because permanent conditions require permanent shame. Temporary events would only require temporary shame, and temporary shame doesn't serve the shame's need to be always present.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v05
---
path: story_atoms/gen_alpha_students/anchored/anxiety/shame/mechanism_proof/micro/v05.txt
BAND: 2
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: opportunity
COST_INTENSITY: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: pre_awareness
---
Leo counts the things he doesn't show because of shame. Drawings: shows one in ten. Ideas in class: shares one in five. Stories he writes: shows zero. The shame isn't preventing him from creating. The shame is preventing him from sharing. The creating happens privately. The sharing would connect his creating to an audience. The connection is where growth happens—feedback, encouragement, collaboration. The shame blocks the connection. The creation without connection stays private and undeveloped. The shame's cost isn't the work itself. The shame's cost is the development the work would receive if it were shared.

## TURNING_POINT v01
---
path: story_atoms/gen_alpha_students/anchored/anxiety/shame/turning_point/micro/v01.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Maya spills again. Different class, different drink. Orange juice this time. Bigger mess. The shame fires. She starts cleaning and says, out loud: "I'm having a very orange day." Two kids laugh. Not at her—with her. The joke is a reframe. The spill is still a spill. The shame is still firing. But the joke inserts something between the spill and the shame: a response that she chose instead of a response the shame imposed. The chosen response is humor. The imposed response was mortification. She has both. The humor doesn't cancel the mortification. The humor occupies space the mortification was claiming exclusively.

---
Jaden reads aloud again and stumbles on "metamorphosis." He pauses. The shame fires. He looks up and says: "That word's hard." The teacher smiles: "It is." Two sentences. The stumble is named as a difficulty of the word, not a failure of Jaden. The naming shifts the shame's target from Jaden to the word. The word is hard. That's the word's characteristic, not Jaden's. ---
Maya drops her pencil case and everything scatters. The shame fires. She picks up the pencils and says to the kid next to her: "Gravity is really aggressive today." The kid laughs. The joke is becoming a tool—not to prevent the shame but to create an alternative response to visible imperfection. The shame says: everyone saw your mess.

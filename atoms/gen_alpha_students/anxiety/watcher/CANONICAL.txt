Maya is at a party and she's watching herself be at a party. Not enjoying it—monitoring it. Her brain is running a camera: how does she look standing here, is she laughing at the right times, are people noticing that she's not talking enough. ## MECHANISM_PROOF v01
---
path: story_atoms/gen_alpha_students/anchored/anxiety/watcher/mechanism_proof/micro/v01.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Maya maps the watcher's cost. Every social event splits her attention: half on the event, half on monitoring herself in the event. The split means she experiences everything at fifty percent. The fifty percent is enough to participate. The fifty percent is not enough to enjoy. Enjoyment requires full attention. The watcher steals half and uses it for surveillance. The surveillance produces anxiety, not safety. She's not safer for watching herself. She's just more anxious. The watcher promises to help her perform better socially. The watcher actually reduces her social performance by stealing the attention that performance requires.

---
Jaden notices the watcher is louder with larger audiences. Talking to one friend: watcher at low volume. Class presentation: watcher at maximum. The watcher scales with the number of potential evaluators. More people equals more surveillance. The content of what he's saying doesn't change the volume. A brilliant idea in front of thirty kids gets maximum watcher. A boring comment to one friend gets minimal watcher. The watcher isn't evaluating what he says. The watcher is evaluating how many people hear him say it. The audience is the input. The content is irrelevant.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v03
---

BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Zoe discovers the watcher prevents spontaneity. Every potential action passes through the watcher's filter: how will this look, what will people think, is this the right thing to say. The filter takes a fraction of a second but the fraction is enough to kill the spontaneous impulse. By the time the impulse passes through the filter, it's not spontaneous anymore—it's calculated. The calculation is usually: don't. Don't say the funny thing. Don't do the weird thing. Don't be the version of herself that exists before the filter. The filter kills the impulse and replaces it with something safer and emptier.

---
Tyler realizes the watcher isolates him. The watcher separates him from the group by making him an observer of the group instead of a member. In the cafeteria, everyone else is inside the conversation. Tyler is inside the conversation and simultaneously outside it, monitoring his participation from above. The inside-outside split means he's never fully in. The never-fully-in is visible to others as a slight distance, a hesitation, a being-there-but-not-there quality. People read the quality and give him slightly less. The slightly less confirms the watcher's assessment that he's outside. The watcher creates the condition it monitors.

## TURNING_POINT v01

BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Maya dances at the party. The watcher starts: how do you look, people are watching, your arms are weird. She keeps dancing. Not because the watcher stops—because the music is loud enough to compete with it. The music takes some of the attention the watcher usually claims. The split shifts: forty percent watcher, sixty percent dancing becomes thirty percent watcher, seventy percent dancing. The extra ten percent is enough to feel the beat instead of just hearing it. She dances for one song at seventy percent. The seventy percent is the most present she's been at a party in months. The music was louder than the monitoring.

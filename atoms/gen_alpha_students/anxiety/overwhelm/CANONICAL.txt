---
Maya opens her planner and the week stares back. Math test Wednesday. Science project due Thursday. Book report Friday. Soccer practice Monday, Tuesday, Thursday. Piano Tuesday. The items are individually manageable. Together they form a wall. The wall isn't a metaphor—it's the visual experience of looking at a full planner and feeling the page vibrate with density. Each item is a task. The collection of items is a weight. She hasn't started any of them. The not-starting isn't laziness. The not-starting is what happens when the wall is too tall to see over and she can't figure out which brick to pull first.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v01
---

BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Maya maps the overwhelm's structure. Each task alone: twenty minutes of focused work. All tasks together in her planner: a paralyzing mass. The overwhelm isn't about the tasks—it's about the simultaneity. Her brain tries to hold all tasks at once and the holding is the problem. She can do one task at a time. She can't hold five tasks in her awareness at the same time without the awareness collapsing. The collapse looks like paralysis. The paralysis isn't about ability. The paralysis is about the impossible demand to process everything at once. Nobody can process everything at once. The planner asks her to.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02

BAND: 2
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Jaden notices the overwhelm creates a priority system that drops the hardest things. Easy tasks get done: text replies, simple homework. Hard tasks get deferred: the friend who's upset, the essay that needs thinking. The overwhelm prioritizes by effort, not importance. The upset friend is more important than the text reply. The upset friend requires more cognitive energy. The overwhelm drops the important-but-hard and completes the unimportant-but-easy. The result: a day full of completed small tasks and deferred important ones. The deferrals accumulate. The accumulation increases tomorrow's overwhelm.

BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3

---
Zoe discovers the paralysis wastes more time than any task would take. Twenty minutes of not-starting costs more than the fifteen-minute worksheet would. The paralysis is more expensive than the work. The overwhelm's protection—preventing her from starting something she can't finish—actually increases the overwhelm by wasting the time she could use to reduce it. The overwhelm protects her from the work and the protection makes the work pile higher. The pile makes the overwhelm worse. The worse overwhelm makes the paralysis longer. The cycle feeds itself. Each revolution adds more tasks and less time.

MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3

---
Tyler identifies the emotional inputs as the most expensive. The algebra homework is cognitive—it takes attention but doesn't deplete emotional reserves. The parents arguing is emotional—it drains a resource that sleep barely restores. The overwhelm treats cognitive and emotional inputs as though they draw from the same tank. They do. The algebra is hard to do after the arguing because the arguing consumed the resource the algebra needs. The system—school, sports, family, social—doesn't schedule emotional inputs. Emotional inputs arrive without warning. The algebra has a deadline. The arguing has no schedule. The unscheduled input disrupts every scheduled task.

## TURNING_POINT v01
---

BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Maya writes one thing on a sticky note. Not the planner—one sticky note. "Math chapter 4 review." One task. The sticky note is the size of one task. The planner was the size of all tasks. She works the sticky note. Finishes in twenty minutes. Crosses it off. The crossing-off produces a small relief that the planner's wall couldn't. She writes another sticky note. "Science project: outline only." She works the outline. Finishes. Crosses off. The sticky notes turn the wall into a sequence. The sequence is manageable. The wall was not. The tasks didn't change. The container changed. The container is the size of one thing.

## TURNING_POINT v02

MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 2

---
Jaden texts his upset friend: "I want to talk about this but I can't right now. Can we talk tomorrow at lunch?" The text is a boundary and a promise. The boundary releases the immediate demand. The promise preserves the friendship. The friend responds: "Yeah, that works." The response frees cognitive space that the friend's distress was occupying. The freed space goes to the homework. Zoe sets a timer for ten minutes. Not for the task—for the paralysis. She gives herself ten minutes to not know what to do, and then she starts the first thing she touches. The timer converts the infinite paralysis into a bounded one. The bounded paralysis has an ending. At ten minutes, she opens the closest book. It's the vocab. She studies vocab. Not because vocab is the right choice. Because the timer said start, and vocab was what her hand touched. The starting breaks the paralysis. The paralysis needed a decision. The timer made the decision arbitrary. Arbitrary decisions still produce completed tasks.

---

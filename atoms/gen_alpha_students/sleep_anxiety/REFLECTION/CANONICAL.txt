

## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I'm learning to teach about sleep anxiety—the mechanism that turns bedtime into another performance where you're being evaluated on how well you sleep.
The mechanism is called nocturnal hypervigilance—your nervous system treats sleep as a threat instead of a rest state. You lie down to sleep and your mind activates. Your body tenses. Your nervous system is monitoring for danger even though you're safe.
The anxiety is automatic. You don't decide to activate at night. Your body just does. You're trying to sleep and your mind is running through tomorrow's worries. You're trying to rest and your body is in alert mode.
What I'm learning is that sleep anxiety is connected to the pressure of being constantly available. You grew up with devices. Your attention was always being solicited. Your brain learned that if you're not attending, something important might pass you by. So even at night, when you're supposed to be resting, your brain stays alert.
The sleep anxiety is also connected to overthinking. All the worries of the day consolidate at night. All the scenarios you've run through during the day replay when you're trying to sleep. Your mind won't quiet because it's still trying to solve problems.
The anxiety is also performance-based. Sleep becomes another metric. You're supposed to get eight hours. You're supposed to fall asleep quickly. You're supposed to sleep through the night. And when you don't, it's a failure. Another thing you're not doing right.

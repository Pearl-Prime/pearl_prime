

## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I'm learning to teach about somatic healing—the recognition that trauma lives in the body, not just the mind, and that healing has to involve the body in ways the mind alone can't accomplish.
The mechanism is called body-held trauma—emotional, relational, and psychological experiences that get encoded not just in thought patterns but in the nervous system, the muscles, the breath, the posture.
What I'm learning is that I can intellectually understand my trauma. I can talk about it. I can make meaning of it. But the understanding happens in my mind while my body is still in protection mode. My nervous system is still holding the threat response. My muscles are still clenched. My breath is still shallow.
The somatic trauma lives underneath the conscious narrative. I've gotten very good at the narrative. I can tell the story of what happened, how I've been affected, what I've learned. But the story lives in one part of my brain while my body lives in another part.
The mechanism is also called disembodiment—the process of separating consciousness from body sensation. I learned early to do this. I learned to stay in my head while my body held things I couldn't process. I learned to dissociate from sensation in order to function. And that dissociation has become so normalized that I'm not sure where my body ends and my survival strategy begins.
The somatic healing is starting to require that I come back to my body. That I notice what's held there. That I learn to tolerate sensation. That I integrate the knowledge that's held in my body with the narrative that's held in my mind.

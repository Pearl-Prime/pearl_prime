## COMPRESSION v01
---
---
Knowing the answer and writing it down are two different jobs, and the second one needs a tiny first step, not the whole task at once. Attention goes toward what's loud and exciting, not what's actually important, so make homework louder with a visible timer or a sticker chart. Time is hard to feel without something to watch, so use a timer with numbers counting down. Your brain drops plans that aren't written down, so write "remember to" moments the second they happen. The fix everywhere is the same: make the next step small, visible, and easy to start, and let that one step carry you.
---

## COMPRESSION v02
---
---
Doing the same kind of problem over and over gets harder to start the further you go, not because the work gets harder, but because your brain naturally loses interest in things that feel repeated. The fix is adding a small bit of newness on purpose — a different pencil, a different chair, saying answers out loud — combined with crossing items off a list so you can see it shrinking. This replaces the tiring restart-every-time feeling with real momentum.
---

## COMPRESSION v03
---
---
Tasks with several possible starting points — a book report, a project — create an extra, invisible decision on top of the actual work, and that decision is often the real reason it feels hard to start. Picking one fixed way to always begin, and using it every single time, removes the choice completely. A boring, same-every-time order beats a "perfect" one you have to figure out fresh each time, because figuring it out fresh is the actual cost.
---

## COMPRESSION v04
---
---
Tasks with no clear ending — "study," "clean your room" — feel impossible to start because your brain doesn't know what "done" looks like. Giving yourself a made-up finish line before you begin — ten minutes, five items, one page — works almost as well as a real deadline, because what was missing was never time, it was a clear stopping point.
---

## COMPRESSION v05
---
---
A scary or uncomfortable feeling about a task can block you from starting even when you actually know what to do, because the feeling is loud enough to get in the way first. The fix is shrinking the first step so small the big feeling doesn't have time to show up — read just the first line, write just the title — so you get one small, true step done instead of staying stuck imagining the whole big task.
---

## COMPRESSION v06
---
---
Things you can see and touch help you start far more reliably than plans you only remember in your head. Leaving the book on your pillow, the folder sticking out of your backpack, the project supplies out on the table — these work because your brain notices things that are actually in front of it, not things it has to remember on its own.
---

## COMPRESSION v07
---
---
Sitting down with your homework and actually starting your homework are two separate moments, which is why you can be sitting right there with everything ready and still not be working. The fix is a tiny bridge action — saying the first sentence out loud, touching the first page — whose only job is turning "sitting near it" into "actually doing it."
---

## COMPRESSION v08
---
---
Your brain doesn't hold onto "remember to do this later" plans as well as it feels like it should, which is why a permission slip or a chore you meant to do can just disappear from your thinking by later in the day. The fix is writing it down or telling someone the moment you think of it, not waiting until you "have time" to organize your reminders.
---

## COMPRESSION v09
---
---
Switching between lots of different kinds of tasks in one day — subjects, chores, activities — costs your brain more energy than it looks like, which is why the hardest task, saved for last, ends up fighting for the least energy you have left. The fix is doing the hardest task first, right when you have the most to give it, instead of leaving it for whenever there's time.
---

## COMPRESSION v10
---
---
Attention goes toward what feels loud, new, and immediate, not toward what's actually most important, which is why a video can beat a reading log even though you know the reading log matters more. The fix is making the important thing louder and more visible — a timer, a sticker chart, someone checking in — so it can actually compete for your attention.
---

## COMPRESSION v11
---
---
The gap between knowing how to do something and actually doing it usually comes from missing a tiny, clear first step, not from the task being too hard. The universal fix is naming the smallest possible first action — not the whole assignment, just one tiny piece — and doing only that, trusting that the next step tends to follow once you're already moving.
---

## COMPRESSION v12
---
---
Waiting for the first try to be good before you even start is a common trap that makes starting feel much harder than it needs to be. The fix is giving yourself permission to make the first version messy on purpose — a bad first sentence, a rough guess, a first line you'll erase — because once something exists on the page, fixing it up is a much easier job than making something appear out of nothing.

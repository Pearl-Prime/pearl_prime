## STORY v01
---
---
Marcus sits at his desk with the worksheet blank and the answers sitting right there in his head, ready. He draws a line in the margin. He erases it. He checks how many minutes are left on the clock. Then his friend leans over: "just write number one, I'll tell you mine after." Marcus writes number one. It's the same answer he already knew. Writing it down didn't feel as big as it did five minutes ago. He writes number two. By number five his pencil is just moving on its own, and he finishes with two minutes to spare. The freeze was never about not knowing the answers. It was about the first line not existing yet on the page. Once it existed, the rest were just more lines.

## STORY v02
---
---
Priya touches the markers one more time and her older brother walks by and asks what she's even making the poster about. She tells him — the whole water cycle, evaporation, clouds, rain, all of it, easy, out loud, in one breath. He says, "okay so just draw that, you just said it." Priya picks up the blue marker and draws a cloud, because that's the first thing she said. Then an arrow. Then the sun. Twenty minutes later the poster is basically done, and she didn't plan any of it out first — she just kept drawing the next thing she'd say if someone asked her. The poster was never missing information. It was missing a first line on the paper.

## STORY v03
---
---
Dana picks up the reading log book for the fifth night in a row and, instead of trying to read the "real" ten minutes, tells herself she'll just read one page, no timer, just one page. She reads it. She doesn't decide to keep going, she just turns the page because she wants to know what happens next. Ten minutes later her mom checks and Dana has read fourteen pages without noticing the ten-minute mark pass. She understands something she didn't before: she wasn't avoiding reading. She was avoiding the moment of starting a timer that made it feel like a chore. Reading one page first removed that moment.

## STORY v04
---
---
Theo digs through his backpack a third time and his teacher, walking by, says quietly, "check the front zipper pocket, that's usually where folders hide." Theo checks. It's there. He didn't need to dig through the whole bag — he needed someone to point him at the one place he hadn't actually looked, because his hands were moving but his eyes weren't really searching anymore, just moving stuff around. He starts his homework with the rest of the class, a minute behind, which is close enough.

## STORY v05
---
---
James stares at the spelling word for the fifth time and his older sister, doing her own homework at the table, says, "just say it while you write it, like sound it out loud." James says the word out loud, slow, and writes each letter as he says its sound. It comes out right. He does the next word the same way. Somewhere around word four he stops needing to say it out loud and just writes it, because his hand found the rhythm. The list was never the hard part. The first word needed a way in, and hearing himself say it gave him one.

## STORY v06
---
---
Renee stares at the group chat and her mom asks what's wrong. Renee explains her whole part of the project out loud — what she wants to say, in order, without even trying to make it perfect. Her mom says, "that's it, just type that." Renee opens the chat and types almost exactly what she just said out loud. She sends it before she can read it over five more times. The group answers back right away, and the part that felt stuck for two hours took ninety seconds once the words existed somewhere first.

## STORY v07
---
---
Diallo looks at the blank first page of his book report and, instead of trying to write the whole first paragraph, writes just the title of the book and the author's name, nothing else, because that part he doesn't have to think about at all. Then he writes one sentence: "This book is about a boy named Sam who finds a map." True, easy, already in his head. He writes the next sentence. By sentence four he's not stopping between sentences anymore. He finishes half a page before dinner, more than he's written in three days of staring at the blank one.

## STORY v08
---
---
Ms. Whitfield reads the last science question for the fourth time and draws another small cat instead of answering. Her teacher, seeing this, doesn't tell her to stop drawing — she says, "answer it in one word first, you can make it a full sentence after." Ms. Whitfield writes one word: "roots." Then she looks at it and adds "take in water through their" in front of it, and the sentence is done. The one-word version wasn't the real answer, but it was a place to start building the real answer from, instead of a blank line that felt impossible to touch.

## STORY v09
---
---
Priya's mom sets a visible timer on the counter — five minutes, just to find the permission slip, that's it, nothing else has to happen after. Priya unzips the front pocket with two minutes left on the timer. The slip is right there, exactly where she left it nine days ago. She hands it to her mom with a minute to spare. The slip had never actually been hard to find. It was that "deal with the permission slip" felt like a much bigger job than "unzip one pocket," and nobody had ever broken it down that small before.

## STORY v10
---
---
Marcus reads the same paragraph a sixth time during silent reading and his teacher crouches next to his desk and asks him to just read the first sentence out loud, quietly, to her. He reads it. She asks what he thinks it means. He tells her, easily — turns out he understood it fine, he just hadn't felt like the reading was "sticking" the way it's supposed to. Saying it out loud made it stick. He reads the next sentence silently and this time it stays with him, because the first one gave his brain a way to grab onto the page instead of sliding off it.

## STORY v11
---
---
Dana stands at her desk with one minute left before line-up, touching the folder without picking it up. Her friend at the next desk says, "just slide it in your cubby, you don't have to make it neat." Dana slides the folder into her cubby sideways, not stacked nicely with the others, and lines up on time. Later that day she notices nobody cared that the folder wasn't neat. It just needed to be in the cubby, not perfectly placed, and she'd been treating those as the same requirement when they weren't.

## STORY v12
---
---
Theo stares at the box of eleven unused colors and his art teacher walks by and says, "pick your favorite one first, doesn't matter which, just start there." Theo picks green because it's his favorite. He draws one leaf. Then he needs brown for the branch, so that's the next color, easy, obvious once the first mark exists. He uses six colors by the end of class without ever having to decide on a whole plan first — each color told him what the next one needed to be.

## STORY v13
---
---
James reads his presentation part in his head a fifth time and his dad asks him to just say it to him, out loud, once, messy is fine. James says it, stumbling over two words, forgetting one sentence entirely. His dad says, "okay, now you know the parts you forgot, say it again." The second time is smoother. By the fourth time through, out loud, he's not stumbling anymore. He never would have found the two hard spots just reading it silently in his head — saying it wrong the first time out loud was what showed him exactly what needed practice.
---

## STORY v14
---
---
Renee holds the same crumpled paper for a stretch of the two minutes, and her teacher, walking past, says, "trash or folder, just pick one right now, you can double check later." Renee picks trash without deciding if it was important, because right now she just needed to pick one of the two options instead of standing there holding it. It turns out it wasn't important. The two minutes finish with time to spare, and Renee notices that "just pick one, you can fix it later if you're wrong" made the choice so much smaller than she'd been making it in her head.

## STORY v15
---
---
Diallo walks past his room a fifth time and his mom says, from the kitchen, "the book is under your pillow, go get it right now while I'm saying this." He goes and gets it while she's still talking, because doing it right then, attached to her voice reminding him, was easier than doing it later on his own after she stopped talking. He hands her the book before dinner. The book was never lost. It just needed a moment attached to someone else's words instead of his own memory holding the plan by itself.

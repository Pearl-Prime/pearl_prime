## INTEGRATION v01
---
---
Tomorrow, before you start whatever's been hard to begin — homework, a project, a chore — pick the tiniest first step you can think of and do only that one thing. Not "clean your room," just "pick up one toy." Not "write the report," just "write the title." Your hands need something small to grab onto, not the whole big job all at once. Once you've done the tiny thing, notice whether the next thing feels a little easier. It usually does, because now you're already moving instead of trying to start from a dead stop.
---

## INTEGRATION v02
---
---
Tomorrow, for whatever task has a bunch of the same little pieces — a spelling list, math problems, chores — cross each one off or move it to a "done" pile as you finish it, so you can actually see it shrinking. Don't try to finish all of it at once in your head. Just do a few, take a tiny break, do a few more. Watching the pile get smaller helps way more than just trusting that you're making progress somewhere in your head.
---

## INTEGRATION v03
---
---
Tomorrow, if you catch yourself doing something else instead of the real task — drawing, touching your stuff, scrolling — don't feel bad about it. Just notice it, and say to yourself the smallest true first step of the real task, and do only that one step. You don't have to fix the whole day. You just have to notice once and take one small step instead of the whole mountain.
---

## INTEGRATION v04
---
---
Tomorrow, do your hardest homework or project first, right when you get home, before snacks, before other stuff. Your brain gets more tired from switching between lots of different things than from the actual work itself, so doing the hard thing early, while your brain hasn't switched gears ten times yet, makes it easier than doing it later.
---

## INTEGRATION v05
---
---
Tomorrow, if there's a message, a chore, or a task you've been putting off because it feels too big or scary, don't try to do the whole thing. Just do the smallest possible piece of it — read the first line, pick up one item — with no promise to do more right away. The scary feeling is almost always about the whole task, not the tiny piece, and once you've done the tiny piece, the whole thing usually feels smaller too.
---

## INTEGRATION v06
---
---
Tomorrow, for a task where you're not sure how to start — like a report or a project with lots of possible starting points — pick one way to always start, and use that same way every time from now on. Title first, always. Don't spend time deciding the "best" way to begin. An order you don't have to think about is faster than a perfect order you have to choose every time.
---

## INTEGRATION v07
---
---
Tomorrow, the moment you think of something you need to remember — a permission slip, a chore, a message to answer — write it down or tell someone right then, not later. Your brain doesn't hold onto plans as well as you'd expect, so catching the thought the second it happens, on paper or out loud, is much more reliable than trying to remember it hours later.
---

## INTEGRATION v08
---
---
Tomorrow, give yourself permission to make the first try at something messy on purpose — misspell a word, cross something out, draw it wrong the first time. The first version was never supposed to be the finished version. Getting something down, even something bad, makes it so much easier to fix than staring at a blank page waiting for the perfect version to just appear.
---

## INTEGRATION v09
---
---
Tomorrow, leave the thing you need to work on somewhere you'll actually see it — the book on your pillow, the folder sticking out of your backpack, the project supplies out on the table. Don't just plan to remember it. Let it be something you'll physically bump into, because that works far better than trusting your memory to bring it up at the right time.
---

## INTEGRATION v10
---
---
Tomorrow, when you sit down to do your work, notice if sitting down and actually starting feel like two different moments. If they do, give yourself one small bridge action — say the first sentence out loud, touch the first page — whose only job is turning "sitting there" into "actually working." Don't assume being at the desk means you've already started.
---

## INTEGRATION v11
---
---
Tomorrow, if a repeated task starts feeling more boring the further you get into it — problem eight harder to start than problem one — try changing one small thing partway through: a different colored pencil, a different chair, saying the answer out loud before writing it. This won't change the work, but it can help your brain stay interested a little longer.
---

## INTEGRATION v12
---
---
Tomorrow, before you start a task that doesn't have a clear ending — like "study" or "practice" — decide your own finish line before you begin: "I'm done after ten minutes" or "I'm done after these five words." A made-up ending works almost as well as a real one, because what was missing wasn't more time, it was knowing where the task actually stops.
---



## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I'm sixteen and already exhausted in a way I don't have words for. School. Extracurriculars. Social media. The pressure to have my life figured out already. To be exceptional. To stand out. To matter. And somewhere underneath all of it is burnout. Not the burnout of overwork necessarily. But the burnout of constant evaluation. Of knowing I'm being watched and judged. Of not being able to turn it off.
---

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
What strikes me is that I'm burned out before I've even really started. I haven't entered the workforce yet. I'm still in school. But I already feel the exhaustion of trying to be perfect. Of trying to meet expectations that maybe aren't even real. But they feel real. The mechanism is called preemptive burnout—the exhaustion that happens before full entry into adult responsibilities.
---

## REFLECTION v03
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
There's a specific kind of burnout that comes from social media. The constant comparison. The documentation of my life. The performance of identity. And underneath it all, the loss of privacy. The knowledge that anything I do or say could be recorded and shared. This creates a constant low-level anxiety that feels like burnout.
---

## REFLECTION v04
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I notice that I'm most burned out around things I'm supposed to be passionate about. School subjects I should love. Hobbies I should enjoy. There's this pressure to perform passion. To demonstrate that I care. And when I can't genuinely feel that passion, there's guilt. And the guilt compounds the exhaustion.
---

## REFLECTION v05
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
What I'm realizing is that my burnout has a lot to do with the expectation that I should know what I want to do with my life already. My parents have hopes for me. Society has expectations. And I'm supposed to have internal clarity about my path. But I'm still figuring out who I am. The mechanism is age-inappropriate expectation.
---

## REFLECTION v06
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
There's a burnout that comes from being a first-generation student in some areas. My parents didn't navigate these systems. So I'm figuring it out alone. Which means I'm constantly second-guessing myself. Wondering if I'm doing it right. The mechanism is called intergenerational pressure without intergenerational guidance.
---

## REFLECTION v07
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I've noticed that my burnout intensifies during certain times of year. College application season. Testing season. Times when the pressure to prove myself feels most acute. And these times seem to come more frequently than they used to.
---

## REFLECTION v08
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
What strikes me is how my burnout manifests physically. I get sick more often. I have headaches. I'm tired in a way that rest doesn't fix. My body is telling me something that my mind doesn't want to hear.
---

## REFLECTION v09
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
There's a part of my burnout that's connected to climate anxiety. The knowledge that the future I'm being prepared for might not exist. That the world is changing in ways that make career planning almost impossible. The mechanism is called future-oriented existential anxiety.
---

## REFLECTION v10
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I'm learning that my burnout is partly about the expectation to be grateful. I have access to education. I have privilege. And I'm supposed to be happy about that. But underneath the gratitude is exhaustion. And the exhaustion is supposed to be hidden.

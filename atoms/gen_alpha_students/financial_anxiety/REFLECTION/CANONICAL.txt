

## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I'm learning to teach about the mechanism of financial anxiety that started before I had any money to be anxious about.
The mechanism is called anticipatory scarcity—you internalize the fear of not having enough before you're actually in a position to not have enough. The worry comes first. The actual financial insecurity comes later, if at all.
I watched my parents navigate money with tension I could feel in the room. They didn't talk about it explicitly, but the subtext was everywhere: there's not enough. You need to be careful. This could run out. I absorbed this anxiety like a baseline. It became the way I relate to numbers. Any number. All numbers.
Now I'm watching my peers. We're teenagers. We have TikTok and Instagram showing us people who have things. Things we don't have. Things we're not sure we can ever have. And alongside the images of abundance, there's the ticker running in the background: you don't have enough money. You will never have enough money. You will be left behind.
The anxiety is automatic. I see a price tag and something inside me contracts. I see my friends ordering coffee and I'm calculating whether that's a sustainable habit. I'm watching a dollar amount and it doesn't feel like money. It feels like evidence of my inadequacy.
What I'm sitting with is the question of whether this anxiety is rational—whether we're actually in a precarious financial situation—or whether it's a mechanism that's been installed so deeply that it generates anxiety regardless of the actual circumstances.

Maya sits in her AP Comp Sci class and watches the assignment grades populate on the screen. A 78. The number lands in her chest like weight. She was supposed to get an A. She has always gotten an A. Around her, other students scroll past, satisfied or annoyed or unbothered. She reads the comment: "Logic correct but structure inefficient." The feedback is not harsh. It does not matter. The shame floods hot across her face and throat. She closes the laptop and pulls her sleeves down. No one is looking at her. Everyone is looking at her. She makes a mental calculation: she can fix this, extra credit, redo, proof that the 78 is an anomaly. The shame says it is not an anomaly. The shame says it is who she is.

Leo is supposed to talk to the college advisor about his test scores. His parents have appointments booked. He has rehearsed explanations: "I wasn't feeling well that day," "I didn't study efficiently," "I'm planning to retake it." None of the explanations are true and all of them are. The truth is that the test happened and his score reflects something about him that he cannot name. In the waiting room outside the advisor's office, he feels his jaw tighten and his hands go cold. The shame is not about the number. The shame is that he should have been able to perform better, that his failure is evidence of something broken inside him that others do not see yet but eventually will. He sits and waits and imagines the conversation. His shoulders curl inward. The advisor will be kind, probably. That will be worse.

In the three weeks after the CS grade, Maya's performance splits. In class, she raises her hand, submits on time, asks questions that sound prepared. At home, she lies in bed at night with her phone and researches depression. She types the symptoms into Reddit threads and reads back her own life. Insomnia. Loss of interest in activities. Difficulty concentrating. The shame multiplies because now she has a framework for why she is failing, and the framework says that the failure is biological, which means the failure is not her fault, which somehow makes it worse. If it is not her fault, then she is broken. If it is her fault, then she is lazy and arrogant and was never actually good. Her jaw stays tight. She grinds her teeth at night. Her mother asks why she looks tired. She says school is busy. The mechanism is moving: shame about performance, shame about the mechanism itself, shame about being the kind of person who breaks.

Leo starts keeping a spreadsheet. Grades by subject. Test scores by percentile. Hours studied per week. He graphs the correlation between sleep and performance. The data says that he slept five hours the night before the test, and that five hours equals his score. If he had slept seven hours, the equation suggests he would have scored higher. But he also slept five hours and six hours on nights when he did well. The data contradicts itself. He adjusts the formula. He keeps calculating. The shame is that he is using the spreadsheet to prove that the failure is technical, fixable, something that can be optimized. The shame is that the spreadsheet is not working. The shame is that he is spending more energy on the spreadsheet than on actually studying. His shoulders ache from leaning over the laptop. His chest feels tight when he looks at the numbers. He saves the file and does not open it again.

Leo attends a school assembly about mental health and a therapist talks about perfectionism. The therapist says: "Perfectionism is not about excellence. Perfectionism is about fear." Leo's hands go still. His throat releases slightly. The statement sits in his sternum. Maya calls the therapy intake number three times before she actually speaks to a person. The therapist asks basic questions and writes down her name. "I think I have depression," Maya says.

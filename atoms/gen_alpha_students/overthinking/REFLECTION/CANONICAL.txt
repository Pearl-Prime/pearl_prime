

## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I'm learning to teach about overthinking—the mechanism that turns a simple situation into a thousand scenarios, all of them ending badly.
The mechanism is called recursive catastrophizing—your mind generates a worst-case scenario, then uses that scenario as the basis for the next scenario, and so on. Each loop makes the situation worse.
The overthinking is automatic. You don't decide to do it. A situation triggers the mechanism, and the recursive thinking just starts running. You're trying to solve a problem that doesn't exist yet. You're preparing for a disaster that's unlikely to happen.
What I'm learning is that overthinking isn't the same as thinking carefully. Thinking carefully is about analyzing a situation thoroughly and then moving forward. Overthinking is about analyzing a situation repeatedly in loops that never reach resolution.
The mechanism is also connected to anxiety. Your nervous system is in a state of low-level threat detection. And overthinking is the mind's way of trying to prepare for threats that it's detecting.
You grew up with a lot of input about how to be successful, and a lot of information about how to fail. Social media shows you constantly what other people are doing better. The news shows you constantly what could go wrong. And your mind is trying to process all of this input by overthinking it. By preparing for every possible outcome. By running through every scenario.
But the scenarios never resolve. The thinking never reaches a conclusion. And the mechanism just keeps running.

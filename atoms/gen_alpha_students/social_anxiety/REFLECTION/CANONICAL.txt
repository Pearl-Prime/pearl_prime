

## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I'm learning to teach about social anxiety—the mechanism that turns every social interaction into a performance where you're constantly monitoring whether you're doing it right.
The mechanism is called automatic social threat assessment—your nervous system automatically evaluates every social situation for potential threats to your social standing, reputation, or acceptance.
The anxiety is automatic. You walk into a room and your mind automatically says: people are judging you. You speak and your mind automatically says: that sounded stupid. You interact and your mind automatically says: they think you're weird.
What I'm learning is that this mechanism is baked in from childhood. You grew up with social media showing you constantly how other people interact. You grew up comparing yourself to curated images of social success. You internalized the understanding that social interactions are performances that are being evaluated.
The social anxiety is also connected to visibility. You grew up watched. Your social performance was visible to your parents, your peers, your online network. The visibility created the anxiety. The anxiety created the hypervigilance about how you're coming across.
The mechanism is also connected to authenticity. You're trying to be yourself, but yourself is also trying to be acceptable. The gap between who you are and who you're performing creates the anxiety. The gap widens with every interaction because no performance ever feels authentic enough.

## STORY v01
---
---
Marcus used to think being a good listener meant staying quiet and nodding at the right times, and he was pretty good at that part. Then one night at dinner his mom asked him what he thought about something she'd just told him, and he had nothing — not because he didn't care, but because his mind had been back at recess the whole time, replaying something that happened hours ago. His mom wasn't mad. She just said, gently, "you weren't really here, were you." That stuck with him more than if she'd been upset. He started trying something small: before dinner, he'd look at one thing on the table — the pattern on his plate, the steam from the food — just to remind his body that dinner was starting now, not still recess. It didn't fix everything. But a few weeks later he noticed he could actually remember what his mom told him, and that felt like getting something back he didn't know he'd lost.

## STORY v02
---
---
Priya reread the same page a fourth time and finally just stopped and asked herself, honestly, "where is my brain right now?" The honest answer was: still in the classroom, worrying about tomorrow's test. She didn't try to force the worry away. She just said to herself, quietly, "okay, I'll worry about the test later, right now I'm reading this book," and then looked back at the page. She had to say it more than once. But by the third time she said it, the words on the page actually started to make sense again, and she finished the chapter before bed, which hadn't happened in days.

## STORY v03
---
---
Dana's little brother called her out for not really playing the board game with him, and instead of getting defensive, she actually thought about it and realized he was right — she'd been somewhere else in her head for the last three turns. She didn't apologize with a big speech. She just said, "you're right, I wasn't paying attention, let me actually look at the board now," and moved her piece for real this time, looking at what he'd built up. He seemed happy just to be noticed. She started doing this on purpose after — checking in with herself mid-game, asking "am I actually playing or just moving pieces" — and found the games got more fun once she was actually in them.

## STORY v04
---
---
Theo had to ask his teacher to repeat a question he definitely knew the answer to, and he felt embarrassed enough about it that he thought about it the whole rest of the day. That evening, he told his older cousin about it, expecting her to say it was no big deal. Instead she said, "that happens to me too, sometimes my body's in a room and my brain just isn't." She showed him something she does: right when she sits down somewhere new, she takes one breath and looks at one thing in the room before doing anything else. Theo tried it the next day at school, sitting down at his desk, looking at the clock on the wall for one second before opening his notebook. It felt small and a little silly. He tried it anyway for a week and noticed he stopped needing questions repeated as much.

## STORY v05
---
---
James got to his front door after the walk home and genuinely could not remember any of it, and that scared him a little, in a small way — like a piece of his day had just vanished. He mentioned it to his mom, who said something that surprised him: "that happens to grown-ups too. It just means your brain went on autopilot." She suggested something to try: on the walk the next day, notice one thing — a tree, a crack in the sidewalk, a sound — just one thing, on purpose. He tried it. He noticed a dog barking two houses down. It wasn't a big moment, but it meant he actually remembered part of the walk this time, instead of losing the whole thing to nowhere.

## STORY v06
---
---
Renee hugged her grandma and felt, even while hugging her, that she wasn't really there — her mind already at home, already playing the game. She didn't say anything about it at the time, but it bothered her afterward, enough that she brought it up to her mom. Her mom said, "next time, just notice how the hug actually feels — is it warm, is grandma's sweater soft — for like two seconds, that's all." The next time she saw her grandma, Renee tried it: two seconds of noticing the hug was actually happening, warm and real, before her mind could wander off to the game. It felt different. Not dramatically different. Just a little more like the hug had actually happened to her, instead of happening near her.

## STORY v07
---
---
Diallo answered his little sister's question while doing homework and then couldn't remember, minutes later, what either of them had said. It happened enough times that his sister started saying, "were you even listening?" more than he liked. He decided to try something: the next time she asked him something, he put his pencil down completely before answering, just for a second, so his hands weren't still doing math while his mouth answered her. It felt like it slowed him down. But afterward, he actually remembered the conversation, and she stopped asking if he was listening, because this time he actually was.

## STORY v08
---
---
Ms. Whitfield's friend was telling her something that clearly mattered, and Ms. Whitfield noticed herself drifting toward thinking about lunch, and for the first time she caught it happening in the moment instead of realizing it afterward. She didn't say anything to her friend about it. She just quietly brought her attention back to her friend's face, her friend's actual words, and stayed there through the rest of what she was saying. Her friend never knew any of this happened. But Ms. Whitfield noticed, for the first time, that she'd actually heard the whole thing, start to finish, instead of half of it.

## STORY v09
---
---
Priya's coach yelled her name during a drill because she'd stopped moving, mind somewhere else, ball just sitting at her feet. It embarrassed her in front of the team. That night, instead of just feeling bad about it, she thought about what actually happened — her body had been on the field, but her mind had drifted off to something from earlier that day. She started using something small during practice: every time the coach blew the whistle for a new drill, she'd take one breath and look at the ball before moving, just to reset. It didn't stop her mind from wandering completely. But she started catching it faster, and stopped getting yelled at for standing still.

## STORY v10
---
---
Marcus realized, watching his favorite show, that he'd missed ten minutes of plot without noticing, eyes on the screen the whole time. It bothered him more than he expected, because he'd been looking forward to this episode all week and somehow still missed it. He didn't try to rewind and rewatch everything. He just noticed, out loud to himself, "I wasn't actually watching," and brought his attention back for the rest of the episode. He missed the middle part. But he actually watched the ending, really watched it, and that felt better than pretending he'd seen the whole thing.

## STORY v11
---
---
Dana counted how many times her mind left the room during class, just as an experiment, and lost count before lunch. Instead of feeling bad about the number, she got curious about it. She noticed that every time she caught herself drifting, if she just looked at one thing on the teacher's desk or one word on the board, she came back pretty quickly. She started doing that on purpose — not trying to stop the drifting, just getting faster at noticing it and coming back. By the end of the week, she still drifted plenty. But coming back felt easier and quicker than it had at the start.

## STORY v12
---
---
Theo caught himself thinking about a spelling test during his dad's bedtime story, and felt a little sad about missing part of a moment he actually liked. He didn't tell his dad. He just quietly brought his attention back to the sound of his dad's voice, the words of the story, and let the test-worry sit there for later. It came back twice more during the story. Each time, he brought himself back to the story. By the end, he'd actually heard most of it, instead of losing the whole thing to a worry that wasn't even happening yet.

## STORY v13
---
---
James answered his friend too fast during lunch and got a look that told him his friend knew he wasn't really listening. It bothered him enough that he actually apologized, right there: "sorry, I wasn't really paying attention, can you say that again?" His friend said it again, and this time James actually heard it — a real thing his friend was excited about. His friend didn't seem to mind the do-over at all. James noticed that admitting he'd missed it, instead of pretending he'd caught it, actually made the conversation better, not worse.

## STORY v14
---
---
Renee's little cousin had to tug her sleeve twice to get her attention, and Renee felt bad about it after, because her cousin looked a little hurt by being ignored. She didn't make a big deal of apologizing. She just got down to her cousin's level, looked at her, and said, "okay, I'm here now, what do you want to play?" and actually meant it this time. Her cousin brightened right up. Renee noticed that the coming-back mattered more to her cousin than the fact that she'd drifted off in the first place.

## STORY v15
---
---
Diallo realized, brushing his teeth, that he had zero memory of walking into the bathroom or starting to brush. It didn't scare him, exactly, but it felt strange, like time had skipped. He started doing something small after that: right when he picked up his toothbrush, he'd notice the taste of the toothpaste, just for a second, on purpose. It sounds like nothing. But he started remembering little pieces of his routine that used to just disappear, and that felt like a small, good change.

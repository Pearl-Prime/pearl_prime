## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: protective

I have noticed that boundaries are often presented as something you should have, as if they are a choice. In reality, the capacity to set a boundary is something that develops through having had boundaries modeled, reinforced, and respected. You cannot choose to have boundaries if you have never experienced them.

There is a mechanism that learns what is negotiable and what is not negotiable through experience. When someone violates your space and nothing happens to them, the mechanism learns that space is negotiable. When someone demands something of you and you are punished for refusing, the mechanism learns that refusal is not safe. When someone respects your no, the mechanism learns that no is possible.

What I have come to understand is that many students did not grow up with modeling of healthy boundaries. There was no one who said no to the parents. There was no one who said no to the school. The adults in the environment were managing their own boundarylessness, not teaching boundaries. So the student learns the architecture of boundarylessness instead.

This creates a particular kind of vulnerability. You do not know which requests are reasonable to deny. You do not know that you are allowed to have preferences that differ from what other people want. You do not have the mechanism in place that says: this is mine, this is not mine. You have the mechanism that says: everything is available. Everything is negotiable. Everything can be demanded.

The absence of boundaries is not the same as being generous or kind. But because you do not have the experience of boundaries being enforced without consequence, you cannot distinguish between the two. So you interpret boundarylessness as the price of being acceptable. If you have a boundary, you will be rejected.

What I have come to think about is that the capacity to set a boundary without fear, without guilt, without the expectation of punishment, is not a character trait you are born with. It is something you learn through experience. And if you do not have that experience, you have to learn it in a different way. And learning it is slow and often painful.

But the mechanism has been trained in a different model. And updating it requires evidence that the other model is real.

## RECOGNITION v01
---
path: the shame of having needs
BAND: 2
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 1
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: pre_awareness
---
Kai feels shame the moment the word "no" leaves his mouth. He's told his friends he can't go to the party because he's exhausted. The words are barely out before the shame hits. A hot wave through his chest. His face feels like it's burning. He wants to take it back immediately. The shame isn't about the no itself. It's about the implication: that he has needs. That he're not infinitely available. That he's human and limited. The shame says: you're selfish. You're weak. You're not being a good friend. He sits with the shame for a few minutes, then immediately texted to say he might come after all. The shame told him the no was wrong, and he listened. He doesn't understand yet that the shame is the real problem, not the boundary.
---

## RECOGNITION v02
---
path: the shame that comes through family
BAND: 2
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 1
COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: pre_awareness
---
Ava is in her family group chat when her mom asks if she can help with something this weekend. Ava's first instinct is to say yes. But she's already overwhelmed with assignments. She types out a no. Shame floods immediately. The social cost feels enormous. Her mom will think she's ungrateful. Her siblings will think she's lazy. She'll be the selfish kid in the family. The shame is so intense that she deletes the no and replaces it with "I'll try to fit it in." The words feel false the moment she sends them. But the shame doesn't decrease. Now she has both the boundary-breaking shame and the false-promise shame. She doesn't know which is worse. The social pressure to be available is masked as just "being a good family member," but the shame underneath is: you don't deserve rest.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v01

BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Kai starts to see how the shame works. Every time he considers a boundary, shame fires. The mechanism becomes visible: his internal worth is contingent on his availability. Having needs equals being bad. Setting boundaries equals admitting you're not good enough. The shame is the punishment for the admission. But where did this belief come from? He starts to remember: praise for being helpful, no praise for resting. Messages that his value was in what he could do. The mechanism is: internalized belief that worth is tied to usefulness activates shame response to any suggestion of limitation. The internal cost of seeing this is grief. ## TURNING_POINT v01

BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Kai experiments with staying present with the shame instead of running from it. When he says no to something and the shame fires, he pauses. He sits with the shame. He asks it: what are you protecting me from? What do you believe will happen if I have this boundary? The shame is loud, but when he stays with it instead of reacting to it, the shame becomes information. The internal cost is that he has to feel the full intensity of the shame. There's no quick relief by collapsing into the boundary or abandoning it. But something shifts when he stops fighting the shame: it becomes less absolute. The shame is there. It's real. But it's not the truth.

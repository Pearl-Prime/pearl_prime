Cam sits in the dining hall scrolling through Discord. Three group chats pinging. Email inbox with seven unread messages from professors. The group project Slack already has forty messages. Their phone buzzes every three seconds. The screen is bright. The notifications blur together—assignment due, friend wants to hang, parent texting about grades. Cam's eyes move fast but don't focus. The sound of the dining hall continues but feels distant. Something is accumulating in their chest. Not exactly fear. Closer to a weight that keeps adding. Each notification adds one more small thing. Cam doesn't know which ones to open first or whether opening them changes anything. The phone feels heavy. Cam sets it down but doesn't stop looking at it.

Zoe's roommate is talking about her weekend plans. Her other friend from high school is texting the group chat. Her RA posted about the floor meeting. Her mom called twice. There's a party Friday but also her work shift. Also studying. The options feel infinite and mutually exclusive. If she goes to the party, she can't study. If she studies, she disappoints her friend. If she works, she makes money but loses time. The math doesn't work. There aren't enough hours. She's already tired at Wednesday afternoon and the week keeps going. Zoe nods at her roommate but isn't tracking the words. Her phone is warm in her pocket. She knows exactly how many messages she's not responding to. The paralysis isn't about choosing one thing. It's about knowing that choosing anything means losing something else.

COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 4
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization

Asha wakes up in the middle of the night thinking about something she forgot. She doesn't remember what. The forgetting is what wakes her. She opens her notes app at 2:47 AM. She scrolls through her to-do list. It's forty-eight items long. She adds the thing she forgot, which was that she couldn't remember what she forgot. Her phone's light is harsh. She can see the list expanding. Work shift. Clinical hours. Homework for three classes. Group project meeting. Mom's birthday next month. The car needs an oil change. Her studying is falling behind. Her sleep is falling behind. She's behind on everything in a way that feels fundamental. She closes the app but doesn't go back to sleep. Her mind keeps working. This is what overwhelm sounds like at 2:47 AM. It sounds like a list with no end. Like discovering that "catching up" isn't a place you reach. It's a direction you stop moving in.

Cam makes a decision. They'll sleep tonight instead of finishing the reading. It's a choice between two bad options. Either stay up and be exhausted tomorrow, or sleep and be unprepared tomorrow. Both outcomes are failures. Cam chooses sleep. They set the alarm and lie in bed. But now their mind is working. The reading didn't get done. That's one thing. Tomorrow's discussion will happen without them fully prepared. That's another thing. The professor might cold-call them. That's a third thing. Their hands are warm under the blanket but their shoulders are up around their ears. Sleep becomes impossible even though that was the thing they chose. Their nervous system is still in crisis mode. The decision itself didn't solve anything. It just changed which thing they're anxious about. This is what choice feels like when all the options are insufficient.

## TURNING_POINT v01
---
path: story_atoms/gen_alpha_students/anchored/boundaries/overwhelm/turning_point/micro/v01.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 4
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Leo downloads a task management app after the panic attack. It's a small thing. The app lets him see the list without carrying all of it in his head at once. He doesn't have to remember that he forgot things because the app remembers. He starts with his actual to-do list. Not the fantasy version where he does everything. The actual list of things that must happen this week. It's shorter than he thought. Or maybe it's the same length but it looks different on a screen. The numbering is clear. The priorities are visible. He tries something new: he decides to do one thing from the list today. Just one. Not three things simultaneously. Not preparing for four things at once. One thing. Then tomorrow, one thing. His hands feel less cold. This isn't solved. But the paralysis fractures slightly. There's a possible first step. And that's different from standing in the middle of impossible options.

## TURNING_POINT v03
---

BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Mika's advisor is patient during her second meeting. Mika comes in with all the spreadsheets. The advisor asks her to close them. Then the advisor asks a different question: What happens if you don't finish in four years? What happens if you take an extra semester? Mika has never considered this. The answer is: it costs money but it buys her peace. It's not an ideal answer. It's a limited resource and money is tight. But it's an actual answer instead of the impossible math she's been trying to solve. She could work part-time instead of full-time. She could take fourteen credits instead of eighteen. She could do one less thing. The advisor is showing her that the deadline is not as fixed as she thought. Mika's chest loosens. This is still difficult. But it's difficult in a different way. Now there are options instead of just one impossible path.

## TURNING_POINT v05
---
path: story_atoms/gen_alpha_students/anchored/boundaries/overwhelm/turning_point/micro/v05.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Zoe has a conversation with her work manager that wasn't supposed to happen. She finally admits that she's overwhelmed. Not in the way of complaining. In the way of saying: I cannot do these hours and school at the same time. I need to reduce my shift. She expects resistance. The manager looks at Zoe and says okay. The okay is so simple. It's not a negotiation. It's not a problem to solve together. It's permission. Zoe reduces her hours by four. She loses money. She gains something harder to measure. She's not less busy. But the specific way that her body was in crisis mode eases slightly. She's still managing multiple things. But now it's manageable instead of impossible. Her hands warm up. She sleeps better. This isn't victory. But it's the beginning of learning that the overwhelm responds to boundaries, not just to pushing harder.

## EMBODIMENT v02
---
path: story_atoms/gen_alpha_students/anchored/boundaries/overwhelm/embodiment/micro/v02.txt
BAND: 1
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 4
COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: self_claim
---
Asha built a system. Every Sunday, she reviews the week. Not constantly. Just once. She writes the meetings. The work shifts. The clinical hours. The classes. She looks at the actual time available. She doesn't pretend that she can do more than exists. She turns off notifications except for emergencies. She tells people: I respond to messages within twenty-four hours. This was scary the first time. She thought people would be angry. Most didn't care. The ones who needed real-time responses worked around it. Her sleep improved. Her anxiety about missing things decreased. The things she was trying to monitor constantly were things that didn't actually require constant monitoring. She was anxious about things that never materialized. Now her body knows: I have checked. The system is running. I can rest.

## EMBODIMENT v04
---
path: story_atoms/gen_alpha_students/anchored/boundaries/overwhelm/embodiment/micro/v04.txt
BAND: 2
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 4
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 1
IDENTITY_STAGE: self_claim
---
Leo now knows his limits. Not because anything changed. Because he paid attention to what made him stop functioning. He knows that more than four clubs makes him absent even when he's present. He knows that sleeping fewer than six hours makes him unable to make decisions. He knows that taking more than sixteen credits means the classes get worse, not better. These aren't shameful limits. They're just the limits. He stops trying to exceed them. He stops believing that other people's brains are different. Everyone has a limit. Some people just know theirs. He's learning his. His hands are steady when he declines things now. His throat doesn't close when people seem busier than him. He's not lazy. He's just someone whose system requires actual boundaries to function.

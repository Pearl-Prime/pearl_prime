## STORY v01

---
Marcus has been running his company for six years and he still remembers the exact moment he understood what "here" actually meant. It was a slow Tuesday, nothing urgent, and he caught himself on a customer call nodding along while mentally three conversations ahead, rehearsing what he'd say in a board meeting that wasn't for another week. The customer, a longtime client named Walter, said his name twice before he heard him. "You still there?" Walter asked, not unkindly, just noticing. Marcus apologized, refocused, and it needled him after the call — not because anything went wrong, but because he couldn't have told you what Walter's actual concern was, just that he'd said the right words back. He'd been present in body for the whole call and absent for most of it. That week he started doing something small: before any call, he'd name one true thing in the room — the light through the window, the sound of the office — just to plant himself there for a second before the call started. It didn't fix his calendar. It didn't make his to-do list shorter. But months later he noticed he could remember his customers' actual concerns again, not just the tasks they generated, and that felt like getting something back he hadn't known he'd lost.

## STORY v02
---
---
Priya almost missed it, the day she walked past her own office manager in the hallway without seeing her. Not a big deal — nothing came of it — just a half-second where her eyes were open and her mind was somewhere else entirely, still finishing an argument with a board member from an hour earlier. Her office manager mentioned it later, gently, as a joke. Priya laughed it off but thought about it more than she let on. She told her cofounder about it at lunch, expecting sympathy. Instead he said something that stuck: "Your body doesn't need your mind to walk through a hallway. That's the problem. You can run this whole company without ever actually showing up for it." Priya sat with that for weeks. She started using the door of every meeting room as a kind of trigger — one breath, hand on the frame, before she walked in. Not a ritual, not slow, just a beat where she let the last meeting actually end before the next one began. She never missed seeing a colleague in the hallway again, not because she got more careful about noticing people, but because she stopped arriving three seconds after her own feet did.

## STORY v03
---
---
Dana used to think being a good founder meant carrying every problem with her all day — the churn number, the hard conversation with a cofounder, the investor's lukewarm comment from last week. She wore it like proof she cared. What she noticed, eventually, was that carrying all of it meant she wasn't fully arriving for any single conversation; she was doing five half-conversations instead of one whole one, five times. The turn came during a normal afternoon, at the park with her daughter, when her daughter asked her a simple question and she had to ask her to repeat it twice. Her daughter wasn't upset. She just looked at her funny. That look landed harder than any lecture would have. Dana started testing something almost embarrassingly small: at the start of each conversation, she'd silently name where she'd just been — "that was the board call" — as a way of closing the door behind her before opening the next one. It wasn't dramatic. Nobody else noticed. But her daughter started saying "you seem here today" more, and Dana realized she'd been quietly leaving the room even when her body said she was in it.

## STORY v04
---
---
Theo had a direct report ask him, gently, "are you okay?" in the middle of what was supposed to be a check-in about her workload. He wasn't visibly distracted — he was sitting still, making eye contact, saying the right words — but something in his voice must have been thin, because she picked up on the gap between the words and the person saying them. Theo went home that night turning it over. He realized he'd given her his sentences but not his attention; he was already halfway to tomorrow's board meeting in his head while his mouth finished the check-in. He started, the next week, doing one thing differently before one-on-ones: he'd physically close his laptop lid, not just minimize it, for the two or three seconds before he started talking. It sounds like nothing. It changed how the conversations felt to him, though — less like a task he was executing and more like a person he was actually talking to. His reports stopped asking if he was okay. Not because he'd fixed his workload, but because he'd stopped trying to be in two places while occupying one.

## STORY v05
---
---
James counted it once, out of curiosity — how many times a day his mind left a conversation before it was actually over. He stopped counting around thirty. It wasn't a crisis, just a pattern: someone starts talking, he's already three thoughts ahead, planning his response before they've finished the sentence. The moment that changed things wasn't dramatic. He was half-listening to his cofounder describe a real concern about the product roadmap, already composing his rebuttal, when his cofounder said, quietly, "you're not actually hearing me right now." James realized he almost hadn't caught the sentence — one more real concern lost to a mind that had already moved on to its own reply. He started practicing a strange little habit: before responding to anything substantive, he'd take one breath, a light pause, a signal to himself that the other person's sentence was actually over now. It wasn't about slowing the conversation down. It just meant he stopped composing his answer during the other person's turn, and something in those conversations got less scraped-out by the end, even the hard ones.

## STORY v06
---
---
Renee's cofounder used to say something that annoyed her: "wherever your feet are." She thought it was a poster-quote, empty advice for people who'd never run a real company. Then she had a week where she gave an update to an investor and couldn't remember, ten minutes later, whether she'd mentioned the runway number — because she'd given that update while already mentally drafting the follow-up email. She had to go back and check her own notes to be sure. That gap scared her more than any hard metric ever could. She started an experiment: for one full week, every time she caught her mind three conversations away, she just noted it — not judged it, just noted "there it goes" — and brought her attention back to her actual hands, her actual screen, her actual task. By day four she noticed the noting itself took less effort; the return was getting quicker, almost reflexive. Her cofounder's line stopped sounding like a poster. It started sounding like the whole job.

## STORY v07
---
---
Coach Diallo used to pride himself on multitasking — three trains of thought running during a customer demo, catching details from a Slack thread at the same time. He thought this was skill. Then a new hire watching him shadow the demo pointed out that he'd repeated a feature wrong, not because he didn't know the product, but because he'd been narrating the demo while simultaneously composing a reply in his head to a message that hadn't even been urgent. He'd been performing attention, not paying it. It stung enough that he tried something new: during demos, he put his phone face-down and out of reach for the full call. Just that. The rapport he built with customers afterward — genuinely built, not just transacted — improved enough that he noticed it within a week. He hadn't gotten better at the product. He'd stopped asking his mind to be in two places during the twenty minutes when it needed to be in one.

## STORY v08
---
---
Ms. Whitfield worked a stretch of weeks so packed that she started bragging, a little, about how much she could hold at once — three fundraising conversations, a product launch, a difficult cofounder dynamic. Then a mentor asked her a direct question, "do you actually believe in this pivot?", and Ms. Whitfield answered too fast, on reflex, the way you answer when you weren't really listening to the question underneath the question. The mentor's face changed, just slightly, and Ms. Whitfield knew she'd been caught being somewhere else. She didn't overhaul her whole week. She just started, in that specific kind of moment — a direct question, a vulnerable one — physically stopping what she was doing before she answered. Two seconds. It gave her just enough room to actually hear the question instead of pattern-matching to the nearest reply. She started catching those moments more, not fewer of them coming, just fewer of them sliding past her unnoticed.

## STORY v09
---
---
Corinne had a hard investor rejection land the same week as a major product bug, and for the rest of that week she kept finding herself in meetings with her mouth doing the talking and her mind still replaying the rejection email. She didn't say anything to her team; it felt indulgent to be shaken by something that wasn't even the biggest problem that week. At the end of the week, driving home, she realized she couldn't remember details of the last three team conversations she'd had — she'd been present enough to not drop anything critical, absent enough to lose the people. The next week, she tried something on purpose: whenever she noticed she was still replaying the rejection, she named it, silently — "that already happened" — and named one true thing about the meeting she was actually in. It took repeating it more times than she expected. But by the end of the week the rejection let go of her faster each time, not because it stopped mattering, but because she'd given her mind a place to put it down.

## STORY v10
---
---
Tanya's kids used to complain that she was "there but not there" on weekends, and she brushed it off as normal founder exhaustion. Then a friend mentioned, offhand, that Tanya had looked right through her at pickup, mid-conversation, eyes open, clearly elsewhere. Tanya laughed it off and thought about it more than she let on. She started running a small check for herself: at the top of every hour, whatever she was doing, she'd ask silently, "am I actually here right now, or three meetings ahead?" Most of the time the honest answer was the second one. She didn't try to force herself present all day — that felt impossible with her calendar. She just used the hourly check as a return point, a chance to come back for the next task even if she'd drifted for the last one. Her kids started saying she seemed more herself at home, which surprised her, since nothing about her schedule had changed.

## STORY v11
---
---
Angela used to think presence was something you either had or didn't, a personality trait some founders were born calm enough to hold and others weren't. She'd watched a colleague named Frank move through a chaotic fundraise looking completely unbothered and assumed he was just wired differently. She asked him once, half-joking, what his secret was. He said, "I lose it all the time. I just come back faster than I used to." That answer annoyed her because it was so unglamorous. But she tried it: instead of aiming to stay focused all day, which felt impossible, she just aimed to notice faster when she'd left, and return faster when she did. The first week it felt like no improvement at all — she still drifted constantly. By the third week, the drifting hadn't stopped, but the noticing had gotten almost instant, and the return took a breath instead of a whole meeting. She stopped judging the drifting. She started timing the return.

## STORY v12
---
---
Marcus's first mentor once told him something during his first year that he didn't understand until years later: "You will never stop your mind from wandering to the next problem. Don't try. Just get good at coming back." At the time it sounded like a consolation prize, an admission that founder life would always feel scattered. Years in, working through a brutal fundraising stretch, he found himself testing it without meaning to — catching himself gone, and instead of spiraling about how gone he'd been, just turning back to the conversation in front of him, again, and again, sometimes a dozen times in one call. What surprised him was that the twelfth return still worked. It wasn't a skill that wore out. Each time he came back, the person in front of him got the same fresh attention, no matter how many times he'd left. He stopped feeling like a failure for wandering. He started feeling like someone building a rep count.

## STORY v13
---
---
Yolanda's company hit a rough patch — a key hire quitting the same week as a client near-loss — and for a few days she felt, mentally, like she was standing in four fires and none of them fully. A newer employee asked her, wide-eyed, "how do you not lose your mind doing this?" Yolanda almost gave a canned answer, then actually thought about it and said, "I don't not lose it. I just have a way of finding it again fast." She showed the newer employee the thing she does — one hand flat on the doorframe, one breath, name the meeting out loud in her head before walking in. The newer employee tried it that same week, reported back that it felt silly and small. Yolanda told her that was the point; it wasn't supposed to feel like a big fix, because it wasn't fixing the chaos. It was just giving her a two-second place to land in the middle of it.

## STORY v14
---
---
Denise once worked alongside a founder who never seemed rattled, and for years she assumed the woman simply had an easier business or fewer problems. It wasn't until they went through a brutal quarter side by side that Denise saw the truth: same chaos, same investor pressure, same team churn — the difference was that the woman kept catching herself and coming back, out loud sometimes, muttering "okay, here" under her breath before every meeting. It looked strange from outside. It worked. Denise adopted the mutter herself, feeling foolish the first few times, saying "okay, here" to an empty hallway before walking into a hard conversation. Within a couple of weeks the phrase had worn a groove into her habits, faster than any of her other efforts to "be more present" had ever managed, mostly because it cost nothing and took no extra time — she said it while she was already walking.

## STORY v15

---
Beatriz's turning point had nothing to do with her business directly. It was an investor's comment, three words long, tossed off during a pitch — "not quite there" — that lodged in her and played on a loop for the rest of the day, showing up every time she stood still for more than a few seconds. She caught herself, hours later, replaying it during a one-on-one instead of listening to her employee describe a real problem, and the mismatch scared her more than the investor's comment ever could have. She started, after that day, treating the looping comment like a Slack notification she couldn't answer yet — acknowledging it was there, telling herself she'd deal with it later, and turning back to the room. It took returning to that same conversation, that same employee, six or seven times in ten minutes before the loop finally quieted. She didn't resolve the comment. She just stopped letting it stand in the room instead of her.

## INTEGRATION v01
---
---
Tomorrow, don't try to overhaul how present you are for twelve hours — that's not a real target and you'll feel like you failed by 10 a.m. Pick one single meeting-sized cue instead: a hand on the doorframe, one breath, the word "here" said silently, whatever fits your day. Use it at every single meeting you join, not just the hard ones, not just when you remember — every one, so it has a chance to become automatic instead of one more thing to remember under pressure. Some meetings it'll feel pointless, redundant, a beat wasted when you're already behind. Do it anyway, especially there, because those are exactly the meetings where you're most likely to be somewhere else. By the end of the day you won't feel like a different founder. You're not trying to. You're just trying to bank a few dozen small returns, each one cheap, each one real, so that a week from now the return costs you less than it does today. Don't grade the day by how present you managed to feel — some days won't allow much of that regardless of what you do. Grade it by whether you used the cue, honestly, at the threshold, over and over, whether or not it felt like it was working in the moment. The felt sense of presence lags behind the practice. Trust the rep count, not the mood, and let tomorrow be one more ordinary day where you got a little faster at coming back.

## INTEGRATION v02
---
---
Carry this into your next day as a single, boring habit: attach a "here now" beat to something you already do at every meeting, no exceptions — closing your laptop lid, muting your phone, sitting down in the chair. Not a new task, just a half-second of attention riding on a motion you were making anyway. When you're behind, when the calendar is stacked, when a hard conversation is waiting — especially then — don't skip it to save time, because the two seconds it costs you is smaller than the time you lose later reconstructing a conversation you weren't present for the first time. Expect it to feel mechanical at first, maybe even silly. That's fine. It's not supposed to feel profound; it's supposed to feel like closing your laptop — automatic, unremarkable, done without deciding each time. If you forget for three meetings in a row, don't treat that as proof it doesn't work. Just pick it back up on meeting four. The measure of whether tomorrow went well isn't whether you felt calm or unhurried, because a bad fundraising week won't give you that no matter what you try. The measure is whether you kept returning to the cue, meeting after meeting, even half-heartedly, even late.

## INTEGRATION v03
---
---
Take one thing into tomorrow: when you catch your mind three meetings away, don't spend energy being annoyed at yourself for it. Just come back — one breath, one look at what's actually in front of you — and count it as a successful rep, not a failure you need to explain. You will do this many times. Possibly dozens. That's not a sign the practice isn't working; dozens of returns is exactly what a full, chaotic day looks like when you're actually doing the work instead of drifting unnoticed. Pick one recurring moment as your anchor point if it helps — maybe every time you close a laptop, or every time your phone buzzes — and use it as a built-in prompt to check where your attention actually is. When you're slammed, when the last call ran long, when a hard conversation is waiting and you have four other things stacked up, this is exactly when to use the cue, not when to skip it, because those are the days where half-presence does the most damage and goes the most unnoticed.

## INTEGRATION v04
---
---
Going into your next day, drop the idea that you need a lighter calendar or a calmer company before presence becomes possible — that day isn't coming, and waiting for it just means never starting. Instead, pick the single hardest conversation type for you, the one where you know you drift the most (a hard cofounder talk, a difficult investor update), and put your return cue there deliberately, on purpose, before you even join. Name the last meeting you were in, out loud in your head, then join with your attention actually reset. If the loop comes back mid-conversation — the investor's comment, the churn number, the hire who quit — don't fight it. Park it, plainly: "that's real, I'll deal with it later," and redirect back to the person in front of you. Expect to do this parking-and-redirecting multiple times in a single conversation on a hard day. That repetition isn't evidence you're bad at this. It's the mechanism working as designed.

## INTEGRATION v05
---
---
Try this tomorrow: at the top of every hour, wherever you are and whatever you're mid-task on, ask yourself silently, "am I actually here, or three meetings ahead?" Don't judge the answer. Most hours, honestly, the answer will be the second one, and that's simply accurate, not a failure. The value isn't in getting a good answer — it's in the asking itself, which functions as a return point even on hours where you were fully checked out until that exact second. When the honest answer is "I'm not here," use whatever's physically nearest as your landing spot — the person's face, the sound in the room, the weight of your phone in your pocket — and let that be enough to bring you back into the actual minute you're in. By day's end, you'll have roughly a dozen honest snapshots of where your attention actually was, and a dozen small corrections — which is a realistic, buildable practice, unlike the vague goal of "staying focused" for twelve straight hours.

## INTEGRATION v06
---
---
Bring one small adjustment into your next stretch of days: whenever a meeting follows you out the door — an investor's tone, an unresolved decision, a hard number — give it an explicit placeholder instead of either fighting it or letting it run silently underneath everything else. Something as plain as, mentally, "revisiting this at five" is enough. This isn't about resolving the thing right now, which usually isn't possible. It's about telling the part of you that's still standing in that meeting that it's been heard and it can stand down until the time you named. Do this every time something follows you, not just the dramatic cases — an offhand comment counts as much as a bad metric. When you walk into the next meeting, notice whether the thread actually quieted or whether it's still running; if it's still running, that's fine, just repeat the placeholder once more.

## INTEGRATION v07
---
---
Take this into tomorrow specifically for your hardest interactions — the direct, vulnerable questions employees or cofounders ask when they actually want to know something, not just make conversation. "Are we going to be okay?" "Do you still believe in this?" "Why is this taking so long?" In exactly those moments, build in a half-second stop before you answer: stop your hands, stop your thoughts, let the question actually land before the automatic reply comes out. You will be tempted to answer fast, on reflex, especially when you're behind — that's precisely when the automatic answer is most likely to miss what's actually being asked. The pause costs you almost nothing in time. What it buys you is hearing the real question instead of pattern-matching to the nearest stock response. Do this deliberately for every hard question tomorrow, even ones that feel routine, because you often can't tell in advance which ones aren't.

## INTEGRATION v08
---
---
For your next day, borrow a version of the "okay, here" habit: pick a short, silent phrase and say it to yourself at every meeting, before you join it, no matter how routine the meeting feels. It will feel strange and a little performative the first dozen times — say it anyway. The point isn't the words. It's giving your attention a consistent, tiny signal that this conversation is starting now, distinct from whatever conversation you just left. Don't reserve it for the meetings that feel important; use it for the routine standup and the boring vendor call too, because those are exactly the meetings where autopilot is most likely to take over completely and you're least likely to notice. If you forget for a stretch of meetings because the day gets ahead of you, that's fine — pick the phrase back up at the next meeting rather than trying to catch up on the ones you missed.

## INTEGRATION v09
---
---
Bring this into a day where you know the calendar will be brutal: instead of trying to hold all your problems in your head simultaneously — which is what the anxious version of "staying on top of things" looks like — practice closing each meeting fully before opening the next, even under pressure, even for ten seconds after you leave. That means, half a step past the door, pausing to silently finish one sentence about what actually just happened in that meeting, before letting your mind move to the next task on your list. It will feel counterintuitive on a packed day, like you don't have ten seconds to spare. You do, and you'll likely get them back later, because a meeting you've actually closed doesn't keep leaking into the next three meetings the way an unclosed one does.

## INTEGRATION v10
---
---
Take one experiment into your next rough patch of days: stop aiming to feel calm, and aim instead for accuracy — attention pointed at what's actually real right now, stress and all. Each time you join a conversation, ask yourself only this: is my attention actually on what's in front of me, or is it on a meeting that already happened or one that hasn't happened yet? If it's elsewhere, point it here, without waiting to feel less stressed first, because that feeling may not arrive all day. This drops the impossible standard of serenity and replaces it with something you can actually do even while behind, even while worried about the next fire. Do this at every meeting, every check-in, every hard conversation, for one full day, and notice at the end whether the day feels different in recall, even if it didn't feel calmer while it was happening.

## INTEGRATION v11
---
---
For the next week, run a small, low-effort experiment: on your worst day, instead of tracking how present you felt, just count your returns — literally tally, even just in your head, every time you notice you'd drifted and brought yourself back. Don't try to reduce the number. A high number on a hard day isn't failure; it's what active practice looks like under real load, as opposed to a low number that might just mean you didn't notice most of your drifting at all. Do this across a handful of days, not just one, since a single data point on a single day tells you little about a genuinely bad week versus a lucky one. What you're watching for over the week isn't a lower count — it's whether each individual return is starting to feel quicker and cheaper than it did on day one, even if the total number of times you drift stays roughly the same.

## INTEGRATION v12
---
---
Carry one plain instruction into tomorrow: whatever cue you pick — a breath, a word, a hand on the doorframe — use it at every threshold, every single meeting, all day, without exception, even the meetings that feel too routine or too rushed to bother. The exceptions are exactly where the habit fails to form, because the mind that skips the boring meetings is the same mind that will find an excuse on the important ones too. You don't need to feel different by lunch. You're not trying to transform the day; you're trying to rack up a large, boring number of tiny, real returns, the same unglamorous way you'd build any other habit in this job. At the end of the day, don't ask "did I feel present." Ask "did I use the cue, honestly, at the threshold, over and over."

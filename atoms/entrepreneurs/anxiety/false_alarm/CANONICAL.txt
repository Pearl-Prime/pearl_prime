COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 2

---
Alex checks his email at 6:47 AM. A customer complaint. Just one. But his stomach lurches. A long-time client is unhappy with delivery times. A single metric variation feels like the whole operation is failing. He reads the email six times, his chest tight. The narrative spirals immediately: unhappy client equals churn, equals team finding out he can't deliver on promises, equals the business unraveling. He doesn't know yet that this is a temporary supplier delay, something fixable in two days. He only knows the alarm. His hands are cold. He opens the analytics dashboard. Orders are actually up this month. Retention is stable. But the singular complaint has eclipsed the aggregate data. One voice drowns out the numbers. He finds himself drafting urgent messages, making calls, mentally reorganizing resources—all before understanding what's actually broken. His jaw clenches. The false alarm doesn't feel false. It feels like the true signal finally breaking through, proof that he's been fooling everyone, that the small things are about to become visible.

COST_TYPE: internal

---
Maya's largest client responds to an email with a single-sentence question about timeline. A question that's probably routine. But she reads it as criticism. Her stomach drops. She immediately assumes they're about to terminate. She checks the contract terms. She calculates what losing them means. Her revenue spreadsheet suddenly shows a catastrophic gap. Her shoulders tense. She starts writing a defensive response, justifying choices, offering solutions to problems the client hasn't stated. Then she stops. She rereads the email. It's actually just a clarification question. Nothing is wrong. But her body has already organized itself around emergency. Her hands shake. She's learned that her first instinct is always to assume the worst, and it's right often enough—clients do eventually leave, things do fail—but that doesn't account for how many times she's mobilized to save a situation that was never in danger. The false alarm feels indistinguishable from the real one.

Omar notices his app's crash rate has increased slightly. The number is still well within acceptable ranges. But the direction is wrong. It was going down. Now it's going up. His jaw clenches. He immediately thinks: this is the failure. This is what loses us users. This is what the investors will ask about. He starts doing calculations: what if crash rate impacts retention? ## MECHANISM_PROOF v01
---
path: story_atoms/entrepreneurs/anchored/anxiety/false_alarm/mechanism_proof/micro/v01.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Alex begins checking email hourly. Then more frequently. Every message could be the one that signals collapse. A supplier note about inventory? Maybe they're about to cut off credit. A customer asking about return policies? Maybe they're planning to return something. A team member calling in sick? Maybe they're job hunting. His nervous system has become finely tuned to threat detection. The problem is that threats exist everywhere if you look for them hard enough. He starts missing the actual patterns because he's cataloging every possible pattern. His chest is always slightly tight. His hands are always slightly cold. He's become someone who reads the market through the lens of imminent disaster, which means he's constantly finding evidence of disaster, which means his threat response never resets. The false alarms pile up, training him to believe that the next alarm will finally be real.

## TURNING_POINT v01
---
path: story_atoms/entrepreneurs/anchored/anxiety/false_alarm/turning_point/micro/v01.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Alex starts tracking false alarms. He writes down the threat, the action he took, and what actually happened. After three weeks, he has a list. Almost every item on the list is a false alarm. The pattern becomes undeniable. His body has been activating constantly around threats that don't materialize. This doesn't make him feel better immediately. But it creates distance. He can begin to observe his threat response as a system, not as truth. The next time an alarm triggers, he has something to reference: this feeling has been wrong before. This feeling is not reliable data. He can wait twenty-four hours before mobilizing. His chest loosens slightly. Just knowing the pattern helps.
---

## TURNING_POINT v02
---
path: story_atoms/entrepreneurs/anchored/anxiety/false_alarm/turning_point/micro/v02.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Maya decides to implement a "catastrophe delay." When a client communication triggers alarm, she doesn't respond immediately. She waits forty-eight hours. Then she rereads it. Almost every time, the alarm is in her interpretation, not the message. The client asking about timeline isn't about to leave. They're just asking about timeline. The message that felt like criticism was actually just a question. By delaying her response, she's breaking the circuit where her alarm triggers automatic action. Her hands warm up. She's starting to notice that most of her catastrophes are internal productions. The actual business is more stable than her threat system claims.
---

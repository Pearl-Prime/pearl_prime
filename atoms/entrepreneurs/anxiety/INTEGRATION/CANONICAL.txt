

## INTEGRATION v01
---
integration_mode: BODY-LANDED
reframe_type: BODY-FACT

The refresh has become automatic, a nervous tic I don't even notice. When I closed the laptop, my shoulders stayed tense. That's the thing about anxiety—it lives in the body longer than in the mind.

I tried breathing. I tried affirmations. Nothing moved the weight in my chest.

What moved it was stopping the looking. I set a timer. Two hours. I turned the phone face down and I just sat with the not-knowing. At first it was excruciating. The anxiety climbed higher, screaming for resolution. But around minute forty-five, something cracked.

The not-knowing didn't kill me. The uncertainty I'd been avoiding didn't collapse into catastrophe. It just was.

My chest loosened. My shoulders dropped. My hands stopped shaking.

Anxiety isn't about having the answer. It's about the body learning that uncertainty isn't dangerous.

**Carry line: Uncertainty is not the same as collapse.**

My nervous system finally understood.

## INTEGRATION v03
---
integration_mode: BODY-LANDED
reframe_type: REORIENTATION

The proposal landed Thursday at 11 AM. By Friday at 5 PM, I'd refreshed their email 143 times. I know because I'm aware of my telling now.

On Saturday, I did something different. I opened the calendar and I looked at dates. Not hoping dates. Actual business dates. What would a reasonable response timeline be? Two to three business days, typically. That meant Tuesday. Not Friday. Not Monday. Tuesday.

I wrote Tuesday on my wall. In marker. TUESDAY RESPONSE DAY.

This reframed my anxiety from "they're ignoring us" to "we're inside the normal window." My body doesn't know the difference between a real threat and a made-up one. But the body knows the difference between Tuesday and now.

Anxiety thrives on no frame. I gave it a frame.

**Carry line: Frame the waiting, don't survive it.**

By Tuesday morning, I'd stopped refreshing entirely.

## INTEGRATION v05
---
integration_mode: BODY-LANDED
reframe_type: CONTAINER-BUILDING

I gave my anxiety a container. One hour. Saturday mornings from 9 to 10 AM.

During that hour, I check everything. Stripe. Email. Analytics. Slack. I let the anxiety flood out. I look at all the things that make me feel out of control.

Then at 10 AM, the hour ends. The laptop closes. The phone puts away.

What's remarkable is that the anxiety stays in the container. It doesn't bleed into the week anymore. I know it's waiting for Saturday morning. I know it will get its time. So somehow, throughout the week, it's quieter.

This isn't positive thinking. This is practical management.

My nervous system understands scarcity. If anxiety gets one hour, it stops trying to steal all the hours.

**Carry line: Anxiety contained is anxiety reduced.**

Saturday mornings are when I feel it. The rest of the week, I'm mostly here.

## INTEGRATION v07
integration_mode: BODY-LANDED
reframe_type: THRESHOLD-AWARENESS

I've learned my own anxiety threshold. It comes around 2 PM. That's when I've checked enough to feel confident. Or haven't checked enough to feel terrified.

Now I plan for 2 PM anxiety. I schedule a walk. A call with someone I like. A change of location. Something that interrupts the spiral before it starts.

I'm not fighting the anxiety. I'm managing around it.

This is the difference between resisting anxiety and living with it. Resistance is exhausting. Management is sustainable.

By 2:15 PM, I'm usually in a different room, talking to a different person, thinking about something else. The anxiety passes because I've moved it.

I'm not cured. I'm equipped.

**Carry line: Move the moment, not the feeling.**

The feeling follows the moment. It always does.

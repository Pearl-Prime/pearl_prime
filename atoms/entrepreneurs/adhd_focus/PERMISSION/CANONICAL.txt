## PERMISSION v01
---
---
You are allowed to write the literal first physical verb of a dreaded task on a sticky note the night before, instead of trusting yourself to invent it cold in the moment.
---

## PERMISSION v02
---
---
You are allowed to let the first draft of an update, an email, or a deck slide be genuinely bad, typos and all, because a bad first draft is what makes a good second draft possible.
---

## PERMISSION v03
---
---
You are allowed to externalize every deadline and every "remember to" onto a visible surface instead of trusting a working memory that was never built to hold un-anchored intentions for five hours.
---

## PERMISSION v04
---
---
You are allowed to give an open-ended task like board-deck prep a fake twenty-five-minute container instead of waiting for the motivation that an edgeless task will never generate on its own.
---

## PERMISSION v05
---
---
You are allowed to read only the first line of the customer complaint you've been avoiding for six days, with zero obligation to respond to the rest of it today.
---

## PERMISSION v06
---
---
You are allowed to pre-decide one fixed, arbitrary order for multi-entry-point writing and use it every single time, instead of re-choosing the "best" starting point at every sitting.
---

## PERMISSION v07
---
---
You are allowed to protect your first ninety minutes for the one task requiring the deepest focus, and to close Slack and put your phone away before the day starts, not after.
---

## PERMISSION v08
---
---
You are allowed to move the payroll portal, the open laptop, or the pulled-up cap table into your direct physical path, instead of relying on a remembered plan to carry the task forward.
---

## PERMISSION v09
---
---
You are allowed to set a visible countdown timer during deep work, because your internal clock isn't going to warn you that two hours have disappeared until it's already gone.
---

## PERMISSION v10
---
---
You are allowed to name the freeze out loud — "I'm rereading instead of sending" — the moment you notice it, instead of pushing through in silence and blaming yourself for the stall.
---

## PERMISSION v11
---
---
You are allowed to change one small variable partway through a repetitive task — dictate instead of type, switch rooms — when your attention starts sliding, instead of assuming you should be able to power through unchanged.
---

## PERMISSION v12
---
---
You are allowed to treat sitting down at your desk and actually starting the task as two separate events that need their own bridge, instead of assuming proximity to the work means you already tried.
---

## THREAD v01
---
---
You scheduled the hard customer call for your first focus block, then watched it slide to after lunch, then to tomorrow, then to "this week sometime." It was never about the customer. The call had no visible first sentence your system could grab, so it kept sliding to wherever the pressure was lowest. Tonight, write the first sentence down before you dial, and notice how much less the call slides.

## THREAD v02
---
---
You keep meaning to update the cap table every Friday, and every Friday it's still seven rows behind by the time you close your laptop. It was never about not caring how the numbers look — you think about them all week. The table has no natural entry point, so your brain treats all seven rows as one enormous decision instead of seven small ones. Next Friday, fill just the row you've already been thinking about all week, and see what happens to the other six.

## THREAD v03
---
---
You've opened the same investor update template four times this week and closed it four times without typing a word. Each time it felt like nothing happened, because nothing did — the freeze reset itself at the same blank line every time. What actually needs to change isn't your effort at reopening it; it's what's waiting for you the fifth time you open it. Leave one sentence typed, even a bad one, before you close it next time, so the sixth attempt starts somewhere other than zero.

## THREAD v04
---
---
You told yourself you'd "just sit down and knock out payroll" tonight, and you did sit down, laptop open, portal loaded, and twenty minutes passed with nothing approved. Sitting down solved a location problem. It never touched the actual starting problem, which is a separate event your brain needed a bridge for. Tomorrow, add the bridge — approve the three names you recognize first — before assuming proximity to the laptop counts as trying.

## THREAD v05
---
---
You've read the same renewal template five times this week, on five different accounts, and it still doesn't move your hands any faster than the first time. Rereading feels like progress because it's an action, but it isn't the action the task actually needs. Next time you catch yourself on a second reread, stop and send the email instead, even with a typo in it, and notice how differently that five minutes goes.

## THREAD v06
---
---
You keep telling your cofounder you'll "get to" the board deck this week, and this week keeps ending with slide fourteen exactly as blank as it started. There was never a shortage of time — a whole week is plenty. There was a shortage of an edge, a moment where the task had a clear start and a clear stop. Next time, give it twenty-five minutes and one slide, and watch a week's worth of dread collapse into less than half an hour.

You've had the same acquirer's name in your inbox for six days, in the subject line of an email you haven't opened. Each day it sat there, the imagined version of the negotiation got a little more complicated in your head, and the actual email got no closer to being read. Nothing in the real email has changed in six days. Open it tomorrow and read only the first line — that's the whole job for now.

## THREAD v09
---
---
You catch yourself, partway through a batch of renewal emails, checking a Slack notification that has nothing urgent in it, for the third time this batch. It isn't that the notification matters more than the emails — it's newer, and your attention is wired to reach for what's new over what's already been touched. Next batch, build the novelty in on purpose: dictate a few, switch apps, work from a different chair, before your attention goes looking for it somewhere else.

You keep starting the investor update with growth numbers one month and churn the next, re-deciding every single time which piece to lead with, and every single time that re-deciding eats the first ten minutes. The writing was never the slow part. The choosing was. Pick one order now, in writing, and use it for every update from here forward, no exceptions.

---
You noticed the renewal emails got easier around the sixth one, not harder, even though email six should logically be more tiring than email one. The first few carried a cost the later ones didn't — the cost of getting your hands moving in the first place. Next time, remember that the hardest email is always the first one, and it isn't a preview of the other ten.

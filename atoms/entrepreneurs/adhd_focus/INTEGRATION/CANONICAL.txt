## INTEGRATION v01
---
---
Tomorrow, before you sit down to whatever's been stalling — the update, the agreement, the proposals you keep opening and closing — write the literal first physical verb on a sticky note before you start: not "write the update" but "copy last month's structure." Put it where you'll see it the second you open your laptop, so the decision about where to begin is already made before the freeze has a chance to show up. The intention-action gap doesn't close because you want it to close harder; it closes because there's a small, physical, already-decided first move waiting instead of an abstract mountain. You don't need the whole thing solved tonight. You need one verb, executed once. Hands move once is enough — the second move tends to take care of itself.
---

## INTEGRATION v02
---
---
Tomorrow, pick the one task on your list that has no natural stopping point — the proposals, the renewal emails, the cap table rows — and give it an artificial one before you begin: a timer, a fixed count of two, a folder you physically move files out of as proof of progress. Your attention system needs to see the task shrinking, not just trust that it's shrinking somewhere in the abstract. This isn't about finishing all of it tomorrow. It's about giving the repetitive middle of the task a visible edge, so each item doesn't require your brain to relaunch initiation from zero. Set the timer, move the first file, and let the shrinking pile do some of the carrying you'd otherwise be doing alone.
---

## INTEGRATION v03
---
---
Tomorrow, notice the first moment you catch yourself reorganizing a Notion board, scrolling Twitter, or refolding laundry instead of starting the real task, and instead of judging it, treat it as useful information: that's the freeze announcing itself, and it means the task in front of you has no visible first step yet. Don't try to push through the whole task in that moment. Just name the smallest true first action out loud — "open the template," "read the first line" — and do only that. You are not required to solve the intention-action gap for every task you'll ever face. You're required to notice it once, tomorrow, in one moment, and hand yourself one small verb instead of the whole mountain.

## INTEGRATION v04
---
---
Tomorrow, before your first meeting, protect ninety minutes for whichever task needs the deepest, most sustained attention — usually the update or the deck — by physically closing Slack and putting your phone in a drawer before the day starts, not after. Task-switching costs accumulate invisibly across a founder day, and the deep task left for "whenever there's time" ends up fighting for scraps of a resource that's already spent by 3 p.m. You don't need to protect every hour. You need to protect the one block where the hardest task gets first access to a mind that hasn't been switched six times yet.
---

## INTEGRATION v05
---
---
Tomorrow, if there's an email, a form, or a conversation you've been avoiding because it feels bigger than it probably is, don't commit to handling all of it — just open it and read the first line, nothing else, no obligation to respond yet. The flood of dread attaches to the whole imagined task, not to one line of actual text, and most of the time the actual line is smaller and less threatening than the version you've been carrying around in your head. Reading it doesn't commit you to answering it today. It just gives your brain one true, small data point to replace the imagined one.
---

## INTEGRATION v06
---
---
Tomorrow, for whatever multi-entry-point task keeps stalling you — the update, the customer email, the pitch you don't know how to start — pre-decide one arbitrary but fixed order you'll use every time, and stop re-litigating which piece to start with. MRR first, always. The apology first, always. It doesn't need to be the objectively best order; it needs to be an order you never have to choose again. The freeze often isn't about the writing — it's about the extra, invisible decision layered on top of it. Remove the decision once, tomorrow, and use the same fixed order next month too.
---

## INTEGRATION v07
---
---
Tomorrow, put the thing you keep forgetting to do — the follow-up, the reply, the form — somewhere physical and visible the moment it occurs to you, not at the end of the day when you try to remember everything at once. A Slack message to yourself, a running doc pinned to your desktop, one phone note you open the second the thought appears. Your working memory was never going to hold it reliably for five hours; that was never a fair test of your commitment to the task. Build the external notepad once, tomorrow, at the moment the thought shows up.
---

## INTEGRATION v08
---
---
Tomorrow, before you start the task you've been dreading, deliberately lower the bar for the first pass: type bullet fragments instead of sentences, drop in placeholder numbers you'll fix later, write one line you know is clumsy on purpose. The freeze often isn't fear of the task — it's waiting for a polished opening line that was never going to arrive before you started typing anyway. Give yourself permission, in writing if it helps, that the first version is not allowed to be good. Once something exists on the page, shaping it into something better is a completely different and much easier job than generating it from nothing.
---

## INTEGRATION v09
---
---
Tomorrow, move the physical object connected to your hardest task into your direct path — the payroll portal pinned open in a browser tab, the laptop open on the counter instead of zipped in a bag, the cap table pulled up before you close your laptop for the night. Your executive system responds far more reliably to something visible in front of you than to a plan you're only holding in language. You don't need to trust yourself to remember tonight. You need the object to be impossible to not notice.
---

## INTEGRATION v10
---
---
Tomorrow, when you sit down at your desk to finally do the thing, notice if sitting down and starting feel like two separate events — because for your brain, they usually are. Build a small bridge between them: touch the first document, say the first line out loud, type one word you'll delete later. The gap between being physically present and actually starting doesn't close on its own just because you're in the chair. Give it one deliberate bridging action tomorrow, and don't judge the twenty minutes it might take before that bridge appears.
---

## INTEGRATION v11
---
---
Tomorrow, if a repetitive task starts feeling heavier the further into it you get — email eight harder than email one — introduce one small deliberate change partway through: dictate a few instead of typing, switch apps, work from a different chair. This isn't about the content changing; it's about giving your attention system something new enough to re-engage with, since it's wired to disengage from what already feels processed. You don't need to out-discipline that wiring. You need one small novel variable, tomorrow, partway through the batch.
---

## INTEGRATION v12
---
---
Tomorrow, before you start whatever open-ended task has been generating quiet dread without producing any actual work — the hiring plan, the board-deck prep you keep meaning to start — give yourself a fake but firm container: twenty-five minutes, one deliverable, nothing more required. Unbounded tasks expand to fill whatever space you leave them and give your initiation system no edge to grab onto. A fabricated boundary works nearly as well as a real one. Set the timer, name the one deliverable, and stop when either is done.

---
I want to talk about something that happens quietly in many self-employed businesses: the mechanism that blurs personal savings into business capital. The mechanism that erases the financial floor you're supposed to have, replacing it with the understanding that your personal money is available for business emergencies.
I call this the personal-business blur. It's the way that your sense of financial security becomes intertwined with your business's financial performance. Your savings aren't actually safe. They're available. When the business has a cash flow problem, your personal savings absorb it. When the business needs investment, your personal account provides it. When the business is uncertain, your personal financial floor becomes uncertain too.
The mechanism is automatic. It doesn't require a decision. When the business needs money and you have personal savings, the transfer feels obvious. You're not withdrawing from your personal account to prop up a failing business. You're temporarily covering a gap. You're bridging a shortfall. The framing makes it seem pragmatic rather than problematic.
But what's actually happening is the personal-business boundary is dissolving. Your financial floor—the amount of money you need to survive regardless of business performance—is being consumed by the business. You're slowly erasing the safety net that's supposed to protect you if the business fails.
The mechanism is automatic because it's rational. The business is yours. The money is yours. Why keep it separated if the business needs it? The logic seems airtight. The automatic response to business cash flow problems is to use personal savings.
What the mechanism doesn't account for is that the separation between personal and business exists for a reason. It exists to create a psychological floor. It exists to provide safety. The mechanism erases that safety in the name of pragmatism.

There's something protective about the personal-business blur. It's protective against the anxiety of watching the business struggle with cash flow. If you have personal savings available, then the struggle can be temporarily managed. The anxiety doesn't have to spike into crisis. The mechanism uses personal funds as a buffer against anxiety.
But the protection has a cost. Every time you use personal savings to cover a business gap, you're actually reinforcing the idea that the business doesn't need to solve the gap. The business doesn't need to be sustainable on its own. It has you and your personal savings as backup.
The mechanism protects against short-term anxiety by creating long-term vulnerability. The vulnerability of having no financial floor. The vulnerability of being unable to walk away from the business even if you wanted to.

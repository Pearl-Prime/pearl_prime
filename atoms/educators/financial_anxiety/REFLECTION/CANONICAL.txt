
## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F2
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: cost

What I notice is the particular quality of the check that arrives on the fifteenth. Not gratitude, not satisfaction — a calculation. I am running the numbers before I am fully conscious of doing it. Rent. Utilities. The loan payment. Groceries. The supply order I already placed because September does not wait for the first paycheck. By the time I have accounted for what is necessary, what remains is not enough to feel like a margin. It is enough to feel like the absence of disaster.

This is the mechanism I think of as vocational economics, and it runs beneath everything. I chose a profession that pays a teacher's salary, and I knew when I chose it that this was what a teacher's salary was, and I chose it anyway because the work seemed more important than the compensation, and what I did not understand then was that the gap between what the work asks of me and what the work pays me would not remain a neutral fact. It would become a source of shame.

The shame is specific. It is not the shame of poverty. It is the shame of being educated, capable, professionally qualified, and still calculating whether I can absorb an unexpected car repair. It is the shame of walking into a grocery store with colleagues from other industries and feeling something when the total appears on the screen that they do not feel. It is the shame that operates when someone asks about my work and I describe what I do and they respond with warmth and admiration that I cannot quite receive because I am aware that their admiration does not translate into financial parity.

I have watched colleagues leave. The one who left for curriculum design at a tech company and now earns three times the salary with full remote flexibility. The one who tutors on the side, spending evenings doing the same work for five times the hourly rate. The one who left for educational consulting and who says she misses the classroom but I have watched her face when she says it, and missing it has not made her come back. I do not begrudge them. What I notice is that I stay, and staying has a cost that I have not always been honest with myself about paying.

The cost I am naming now is not only financial. It is the accumulated weight of having to choose, repeatedly, between what the work requires and what my compensation provides. The classroom supplies I bought out of pocket because the students needed them and waiting for reimbursement felt like failing them. The professional development conference I attended on my own dime because the school's budget covered one person and it was not going to be me. The cost that accrues when every expression of professional dedication requires a personal financial sacrifice. The mechanism that runs beneath my classroom is not just teaching. It is subsidizing an institution that does not acknowledge the subsidy.

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: automatic

What I have come to understand is that the financial anxiety is not a separate concern from the teaching. It runs in the background of every professional decision in a way that I have learned to not look at directly. The class I want to take to develop a new unit — the mechanism calculates the cost before I fully form the desire. The book I want to order for a student who needs it — the mechanism runs a quick check before the impulse reaches action. The professional decision is never purely professional. It is always also economic.

The mechanism is the internalization of a scarcity that the institution created and that I now carry as if it were mine. The school's budget is inadequate. That is a structural fact. But the way this structural fact is experienced is not as a structural problem. It is experienced as a series of small personal calculations, each one minor, collectively exhausting. I have become skilled at the mental arithmetic of institutional underfunding. I can tell you approximately how much I have spent on classroom supplies in the last three years. I can tell you which grant cycles are worth applying to and which are not worth the labor cost. I have developed a competency that I did not seek and that is not compensated.

The anxiety that accompanies this is not always acute. More often it is low-level, persistent, present as a kind of background hum that modulates everything. A student's birthday arrives and the mechanism notes that I will not be buying a small gift this year because I bought supplies last month and the account is thin. A colleague mentions a wellness retreat and the mechanism notes the price before any response forms. The anxiety is not panic. It is the constant awareness that the margin is narrow and the demands are not proportional to the margin.

What the mechanism does not do, which is what I sometimes wish it would do, is allow me to feel angry about this without immediately translating the anger into something more manageable. The anger, when it surfaces, is accurate. I am professionally qualified. I work long hours. I am responsible for the developmental welfare of other people's children. The compensation for this does not reflect any of those facts. The mechanism converts this accurate anger into resignation, or into dark humor, or into the private satisfaction of doing good work anyway. What it rarely produces is a clear-eyed willingness to say: the structure that asks this of me while paying me this is wrong. The mechanism has learned to live with the wrongness instead.

## REFLECTION v03
---
family: F2
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: cost

The mechanism I notice at the end of the school year is different from the mechanism that runs day-to-day. At the end of the year, when I am seeing colleagues at retirement parties and department celebrations, there is a particular arithmetic that surfaces. The colleague who is retiring after thirty-two years. I can see my own trajectory from this vantage point. I can calculate what thirty-two years in this profession looks like in its final accounting. The pension that is adequate but not generous. The professional life of significant social utility. The financial life that required decades of careful management of a salary that did not grow proportionally with inflation or with the actual demands of the work.

What I notice is the shame of the comparison, which is a shame I have no rational grounds to feel. I chose this. I would probably choose it again. But the financial anxiety contains a narrative about worth, and that narrative is not one I control entirely. The culture I live in assigns value through compensation, and by that measure, what I do every day is not valued commensurate with what I know it to be worth. The mechanism internalizes this cultural assessment even when I consciously reject it. I can believe that my work is important and simultaneously feel the sting of opening the paycheck.

What it has cost me beyond money is the cognitive load of the management. The careful meal planning. The awareness of which months are tight. The small calculations that other people do not perform. I am good at this management now. I have become expert in the economics of a teacher's salary in a way I would not have needed to be in another profession. The competence does not make the necessity feel neutral. The competence is its own form of evidence that something is wrong with the structure that required it.

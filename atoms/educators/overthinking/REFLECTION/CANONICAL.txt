
## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic

What I notice is that the lesson does not end when the students leave. It continues in a form that I have no control over — a replay mechanism that runs through the forty-seven minutes I just taught and locates, with remarkable precision, every point of friction. The explanation that was not quite right. The student in the third row whose expression shifted in a way I could not interpret. The transition that took two minutes longer than planned. The question I answered too quickly.

This is not deliberate reflection. I did not choose to do this. The mechanism initiates automatically, often while I am doing something entirely unrelated. I am making dinner. I am in the shower. I am attempting to be present to a conversation that has nothing to do with school. And beneath all of it, the replay is running. It is not running to solve a problem. It is running the way the body processes a physical injury — returning to the site, assessing the damage, doing something that resembles healing but does not yet feel like it.

The mechanism I am describing is the post-hoc monitoring system of a person whose work requires constant real-time decision-making. In the classroom, I cannot slow down. I am making hundreds of micro-decisions per hour: who to call on, how long to wait, whether to pursue the tangent or redirect, whether the student who seems frustrated is frustrated at the material or at something else. I cannot evaluate these decisions while I am making them. The evaluation happens after, in the form of the replay.

What I have come to understand is that the replay has a function. It is updating the model. It is revising the lesson plan for next time. It is integrating information that I could not process in real time. This is a legitimate cognitive process. The problem is not its existence. The problem is its duration and its timing. The mechanism does not know when to stop. It does not distinguish between the revision that takes five minutes and the revision that takes three hours, or between the ten o'clock replay that has resolved something useful and the two o'clock replay that has turned into a circular loop producing no new information and significant distress.

The body needs rest. The replay does not know this. The replay operates on the assumption that more processing produces better understanding, and it has never encountered the evidence that at a certain point it produces only exhaustion.

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F2
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: cost

The cost arrives as sleep debt and also as a particular kind of relational distance. By the time I have replayed the day through dinner and the evening and whatever I was trying to do to not think about school, I am not fully present for the parts of my life that are not school. The mechanism has been using the cognitive resources that should have been available for other things. I arrive at the end of the evening having done a great deal of mental work that produced nothing actionable, feeling tired from labor I cannot account for.

The mechanism is also costly in its misreadings. The student who looked upset in third period — the mechanism has generated twelve possible explanations by ten PM. Most of them are wrong. Some of them implicate me. None of them can be tested until I am back in the classroom tomorrow. The mechanism has kept me engaged with a problem I cannot solve for six hours, at the cost of everything I could have been attending to instead.

What I notice is the particular texture of the morning after a heavy replay night. I arrive at school with a kind of vigilance that is already deployed, already running, before the first student has entered the room. I am monitoring for the confirmation or disconfirmation of the hypotheses the mechanism generated overnight. I am looking for evidence that the student who seemed upset is actually fine, or that the explanation that felt inadequate was in fact understood. I have spent the night creating predictions and the morning testing them, and the students are not aware that any of this is happening.

The cost I want to name most directly is the cost to my capacity for genuine rest. I am not sure I have fully rested, in the sense of being genuinely not-at-school, for years. The mechanism has made a permeable boundary between work and not-work that I did not intend and cannot enforce by decision. I can decide not to grade at home. I cannot decide to stop replaying the day. The body remains in the classroom long after the body has left it.

## REFLECTION v03
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic

What I have noticed is that the mechanism has an email variant. A parent writes with a question that contains a tone I cannot quite read. Polite language. Neutral content. And yet something in the construction produces a response in my nervous system that the mechanism immediately begins to analyze. Is this accusatory. Is this a complaint that has not yet declared itself. Is the word "interesting" in the third sentence sarcastic. The mechanism generates interpretive possibilities the way a language model generates completions — automatically, rapidly, without filtering for likelihood.

The email reply I send has been drafted multiple times. Not because it is complicated. Because the mechanism has decided that the stakes of the communication are high and that therefore the communication requires precision that the mechanism cannot quite achieve because it is working with incomplete information about the sender's intent. I reread what I wrote. I consider whether it sounds defensive. I consider whether it sounds too casual. I send it and immediately the mechanism begins anticipating the response.

This is what overthinking looks like in the professional context of the educator. It is not ruminative in the way the clinical literature describes. It is not primarily about me. It is primarily about the students, the parents, the communication, the impression I am making, the quality of what I delivered. The mechanism has taken the genuine responsibility of the work and converted it into a cognitive burden that is disproportionate to what the responsibility actually requires.

What I have come to understand is that the mechanism is doing this out of something that resembles care. It is trying to be a better teacher. It is trying to not miss things. It is trying to stay responsible for outcomes in a profession where outcomes are not fully visible and the consequences of missing something can be significant. The mechanism is not my enemy. It is my professional conscience running without an off switch, and the absence of the off switch is what makes it a problem rather than an asset.

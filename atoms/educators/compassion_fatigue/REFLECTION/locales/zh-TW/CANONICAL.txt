When something hurts, your first instinct is usually to push it away. To numb it, deny it, or distract yourself until it fades into the background noise of your life. That's human.

When you reflect, you interrupt the automatic cycle. The same situation stops producing the same reaction, because you've finally *seen* what was happening underneath. You've shined light on the pattern.

Most people skip this step entirely. They react, they suffer, then they move on—only to hit the same landmine again six months later. Then they wonder why nothing ever changes.

You're going to be different. You're going to reflect. You're going to get curious about your own pain instead of afraid of it.

# Witnessing Without Power: The Moral Injury of Systemic Compassion Fatigue

What you're describing deserves its own name because the wound is specific. It's not the general exhaustion of caring too much. It's the particular ache that comes from seeing the *pattern* — the system grinding — and knowing your hands are tied.

**The Compassion Demand** says: *Care about these children. Notice their suffering. Let it matter to you.*

The fatigue comes partly from caring, yes. But mostly it comes from the unbearable cognitive and ethical dissonance of being required to witness structural violence while remaining embedded within it.



## INTEGRATION v01
---
mode: BODY_LANDED
reframe_type: BODY_FACT
weight: standard
carry_line: "The alarm does not mean failure."
---
The heat in your chest during observation is not evidence that you're doing this wrong. It's your nervous system reading: someone is watching. Someone is measuring. The body knows the difference between teaching and performing under judgment. Your hands might shake when writing on the board. Your throat might tighten mid-explanation. These sensations are not character flaws. They're the weight of institutional measurement landing in your nervous system. The alarm fires because stakes are real—your job, your reputation, the score that gets filed. Recognizing this doesn't dissolve the heat. It anchors you: the anxiety is rational response, not inner weakness. You can teach while your body is running alarm. They are not the same as failure.
---

## INTEGRATION v02
---
mode: COST_VISIBLE
reframe_type: COST_UNCOUNTED
weight: standard
carry_line: "Performance anxiety exacts a daily cost."
---
Every lesson you're observed, your nervous system pays. The adrenaline is real. The cortisol is real. The next morning you feel it—the fatigue that comes from running threat response in a room full of students who need you steady. You don't count these costs in the rubric. The administrator doesn't see the price tag on your hands shaking through a unit on fractions. Nobody measures how much energy it takes to sound confident when you're being graded. The system measures outcomes, not the metabolic weight of constant evaluation. Your body keeps score. The accumulation matters. This is what gets stored when you teach while afraid of being judged.

The alarm has fired hundreds of times. You have taught through it. The system measures performance; it doesn't measure the nerve that it takes to keep showing up when you're being watched. That's the invisible backbone of teaching in institutional systems built on judgment. You've carried that weight this far. Your presence in the classroom, despite the heat, despite the racing thoughts—that's the thing itself.

## INTEGRATION v04
---
mode: QUESTION_OPEN
reframe_type: QUESTION_REFRAME
weight: standard
carry_line: "What if you taught without proving."
---
The underlying thought in performance anxiety is: I must prove I'm competent. The observation confirms it. The score validates it. The parent's satisfied nod confirms it. But what you actually do in the classroom—explain a concept, check for understanding, respond to a kid who's confused—none of that requires proof. It requires presence. Teaching itself is the work. The evaluation is a separate layer that your nervous system has learned to fuse with the actual task. What if teaching and proving are not the same thing. What if you could stand at the front of a room delivering a lesson without the additional demand to demonstrate worthiness. This separation is not about erasing standards. It's about breaking the feedback loop that makes your body run alarm in order to meet institutional requirements that had nothing to do with learning.
---

## INTEGRATION v05
---
mode: SOMEONE_ELSE
reframe_type: OTHER_PERSON
weight: heavy
carry_line: "The administrator's measurement is not your measure."
---
The person writing the evaluation rubric is not measuring you. They're measuring performance against institutional criteria. The distinction is real. You are not your lesson score. Your competence is not the sum of observed minutes. The discomfort you feel—the flinching when someone walks in the back of the room—this is your nervous system responding to being measured. But measurement is not knowing. The rubric measures what can be recorded. It doesn't measure the teacher who stays after school with a kid who's drowning. It doesn't count the email you send at 10PM. It doesn't quantify the way you notice when someone hasn't spoken and draw them in. The system measures what it can count. It counts differently than you do.
---

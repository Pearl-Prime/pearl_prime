

## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
tier_variant: D

What I notice is a particular quality of attention that never quite turns off. I walk into the classroom and a part of my nervous system takes a baseline reading: Who absent is beneath the performance. Who looks agitated. What the energy level is becomes the real question. Where is the potential friction. This is not something I choose to do. It happens automatically, a scanning mechanism that runs beneath the conscious lesson plan.

## REFLECTION v03
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
tier_variant: D

I teach a class where several students have experienced significant trauma. I know this because they have told me, or their family has, or their behavior patterns make it apparent. I understand that my classroom may be one of the safest spaces they experience. I also understand that my nervous system does not have a special protocol for this understanding. What happens instead is that the mechanism I recognize as compassionate attention creates a particular kind of burden.

## REFLECTION v05
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
tier_variant: D

There is a particular texture to the anxiety that emerges when I am communicating with a parent. Not all parents, not always, but when I am drafting an email that conveys something difficult or preparing for a conversation about their child's behavior or performance, something shifts in my nervous system. What I recognize here is the mechanism of asymmetrical accountability. I am responsible for the outcome of my communication. If the parent becomes angry, I have failed to communicate effectively.

## REFLECTION v07
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
tier_variant: D

What I am aware of is a particular kind of anxiety that emerges around competence. Not in the moment of doing the work, but in the reflection afterward. I delivered a lesson. Some students understood. Some did not. I managed the classroom. Some behavioral choices still required redirection. Nothing went wrong. But the mechanism still activates a response that the work was not adequate. This is what I think of as the mechanism of infinite incompleteness.

There is a particular quality to the anxiety that emerges around administrative requirements. Not the teaching itself, but the documentation. The data entry. What I notice is the anxiety that emerges specifically on Friday afternoons. Not at the beginning of the day, but as the day approaches the end.

Rachel sits in her car in the school parking lot, phone open to a post from Marcus Chen's account. His sophomore students finished a unit project—a multimedia essay about social justice movements—that accumulated fourteen thousand likes. The photos show kids actually engaged, leaning forward over their computers, their faces lit by screens and what looks like authentic interest. Rachel's own sophomore finished essays three weeks ago. They were adequate. Some were good. One was excellent. She'd spent an hour writing comments on each. Now she scrolls through the comments on Marcus's post: *This is what teaching looks like. So inspiring. Your students are lucky.* She sets her phone down on the steering wheel. Her shoulders have drawn up toward her ears. She knows this feeling but can't quite name it—not jealousy exactly, but a small, persistent sense of falling short. She starts the car. Tomorrow she has to teach again.

It's embedded in how she *experiences* her students, and that change is harder to notice because it masquerades as actual observation.

Michael is preparing his lecture for Wednesday, and he finds himself thinking about David's classroom again. David uses Socratic seminar, lets students lead discussion. But now Michael wonders if his approach is *too traditional*, if he's not actualizing the theoretical learning that defines exceptional teaching. He can't make his body believe it.

Rachel is in the department meeting when Marcus mentions his students are going on a field trip to the state historical society next month. Rachel's students just finished the same unit. She didn't plan a field trip. She had them explore the society's digital archives, write an analysis paper. It's less experiential, more text-based. Marcus's students are getting the multisensory experience, the embodied learning. Rachel's anxiety swells immediately. She's comparing: his classroom gets field trips, hers gets digital archives. The comparison has sharpened into a specific, actionable deficit. But then something shifts. Michael sits in on David's class deliberately, deciding to observe the exceptional pedagogy in action. What he sees is effective but also specific to David's teaching temperament. David is naturally conversational, comfortable with ambiguity, skilled at letting students flounder productively in discussion. Michael is more structured, more directional, more scaffolded.

Sunday night. Rachel looks at the week ahead: one hundred twenty-seven papers to grade, parent emails to return, the IEP meeting Tuesday morning, observations Wednesday afternoon, curriculum mapping due Friday, lesson plans for three classes to write, two committee emails that probably need responses. Each thing individually is doable. Together they form a wall—not a wall of concrete but a wall of everything-at-once. She opens the laptop to start somewhere. The cursor blinks in one window while seven others wait in tabs below. She stares at the wall. Closes the laptop. The wall doesn't shrink from being closed.

James's desk has three stacks. The "urgent" stack is twelve inches high. The other two stacks are higher. Somewhere in those stacks are forms that are late, student referrals that need follow-up, a master's degree assignment due in four days, recommendations that need writing. Thirty-one emails arrived since yesterday. Sofia opens her inbox and the number sits there, accusatory. Some are FYI. Some are action required. The prep period is forty-five minutes. Derek has three hours of work to complete: grading, planning, responding to students. He spends the first ten minutes deciding what to prioritize. ## MECHANISM_PROOF v01

COST_TYPE: opportunity
COST_INTENSITY: 4
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
The stack grows faster than it shrinks. Michael grades twenty papers Monday night; thirty more arrive Tuesday. He answers five emails; thirteen new ones come Wednesday. The ratio never shifts in his favor. He works through lunch, stays until five-thirty, takes work home. The stack stays the same size—approximately. It may actually be growing. The work isn't inefficient. His grading is thorough. His emails are thoughtful. The work is infinite. The infinite can't be solved with effort. No amount of staying late alters the fundamental math.

Everything costs the same now. Rachel notices: answering one parent email costs what writing a whole lesson plan used to cost. Her mental capacity hasn't changed. The demands have expanded. Or her reserves have depleted without refilling. Either way, the math doesn't work anymore. Small tasks take large energy. Large tasks are therefore impossible. She can do one or the other, not both. She spends the planning period on small tasks and carries home the lesson planning. Both remain undone at midnight.

James knows what he should do. That's not the problem. He can list the tasks. Can order them by urgency. Can explain how to do each one. The starting doesn't happen. The knowing sits on one side of a canyon. The doing sits on the other. The canyon is made of exhaustion. He can see the canyon. He can't cross it. He sits on his side and thinks about the other side. The thinking looks like procrastination. The procrastination feels like failure.

The easiest tasks are the hardest. Sofia can handle a classroom crisis—adrenaline handles crises. A student having a breakdown, a fight in the hallway, an urgent parent call—she moves. But entering grades? Responding to a simple email saying "Thanks for your help"? Those require initiative from somewhere quiet, somewhere spent. She can run fire drills and can't reply to a thank-you. The absurdity is the pattern. The crisis response works. The routine work doesn't. She's built for emergency. The quiet work breaks her.

Michael sorts the stack into three piles: actually urgent, seems urgent, not urgent. The "actually urgent" pile is four items—genuine deadlines that matter.

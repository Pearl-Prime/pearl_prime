First day of summer break. Rachel sits at the kitchen table with coffee, nothing scheduled. The quiet feels wrong. For ten months, every minute was accounted for—lessons, grading, meetings, student crises. Now the structure is gone. She wanted the break desperately. She planned for it, fantasized about it in February. The break arrived and she doesn't know who she is without the noise. The wanting and the missing sit in the same chair. The coffee gets cold. Her chest holds a feeling she can't name yet—something between relief and loss, between freedom and emptiness.

James clears out his desk at year end. Sticky notes from students he won't see again. The thank-you card from the kid who struggled all year and finally got it. The things are just things—paper, tape, ink, written words. But holding them makes his throat tight. The year happened. The year is over. Some of those kids will forget him by September. That's how it works. That's the job. The working of it doesn't make it lighter. He holds the card for a moment longer than necessary before putting it in the trash.

Sofia sees her old student at the grocery store. Grown now—adult job, adult life, the kind of competence and independence Sofia once helped scaffold. They talk for three minutes. The student mentions something Sofia taught, something that mattered, something that stayed. Then they say goodbye and Sofia walks to her car and the tears come without warning. The student is fine. The student is more than fine. Sofia is crying anyway. Something ended that she didn't know how to end. Something she wasn't supposed to grieve because it was supposed to be a success.

The waves come at odd times. Rachel is grading essays when she suddenly misses her fifth period class from two years ago. That group that finally got it—the breakthrough month when everything clicked into place. They're juniors now in different teachers' classrooms, different buildings maybe. The group doesn't exist anymore except in her memory. The memory arrives uninvited while she's sitting at her desk with a stack of papers. It makes her chest ache. It leaves. The waves don't check if she's busy. They don't wait for a convenient moment. They just wash through and leave her stranded on the page she's reading.

Sofia mourns in layers. First layer: the student who moved away mid-year, disappearing before she could see if he was going to make it. Second layer: the class that never gelled despite her efforts, despite the techniques she used successfully last year. Every ending is practice for the bigger ending. Michael notices: last day of each unit, small grief. Last day of each semester, medium grief. Last day of school, large grief. The practice just makes the feeling familiar. It doesn't make it hurt less. He's learned to keep teaching while the heart is breaking. The skill is useful and also expensive.

Anika keeps the yearbook from her first year teaching at this institution. Those students are professionals now. Mortgages, babies, careers in fields she'll never know about. James keeps the thank-you notes now. Doesn't throw them away at year end like he used to. Puts them in a folder in his desk drawer. When the loss-feeling comes in the summer—and it will—he opens the folder. The notes are evidence. Not that the loss doesn't matter. But that the thing that was lost was real. Sofia visits her old classroom building after transferring schools. It's different now—someone else's posters, someone else's arrangement, another teacher's systems. The room doesn't miss her. The room is just a room. She stands in it for a minute, and the grief she expected doesn't arrive with its usual intensity. The room was never the thing. The thing was what happened in the room. That's hers. That came with her. The memories aren't housed in the bulletin boards. They're housed in her body, in her experience, in how she teaches now because of who she was in that room.

The grief for September-self doesn't mean Anika is failing. She sees that now. The September energy was unsustainable—no one runs at that level for ten months. The fumes aren't failure. The fumes are May. May is supposed to feel like this. May is supposed to feel like running on whatever fuel remains. She'll be September-Anika again in September with the fresh plans and the belief that this year will be different. Right now she's May-Anika. May-Anika is allowed to be tired. Anika goes to the retirement reception for Dr. Marcus. Really goes—stays the whole time, listens to the stories, watches the magnitude become visible.



## INTEGRATION v01
---
mode: BODY_LANDED
reframe_type: BODY_FACT
weight: standard
carry_line: "Saying no is a physical act."
---
When you're about to say no and your chest tightens, that's a sign of real conflict. Not weakness. Conflict. You want to say yes. You want to meet the need. You want to be the teacher who finds extra time, who tutors after school, who stays late to grade. Your identity is built on that capacity. So your body registers refusal as violence against yourself. The throat constricts. The guilt fires immediately. A boundary is not a boundary if it doesn't cost something to maintain it. The tightness in your chest is the price of protecting what's left of you. That discomfort is information. It's telling you: this matters. You're choosing something besides the student's immediate need. You're choosing yourself. Feel the cost. The presence of it means the boundary is real.
---

## INTEGRATION v02
---
mode: COST_VISIBLE
reframe_type: COST_UNCOUNTED
weight: standard
carry_line: "The student's need will always exceed your capacity."
---
There is always more. There is always another student who needs another thing. Another parent requesting another meeting. Another email after hours. Another stack of papers that matter. The need is infinite. Your capacity is finite. You can't count the cost of always saying yes because it accumulates slowly—the exhaustion that settles in by November, the Sunday night that gets colonized by work, the energy that's supposed to restore you but doesn't because you're already allocated it elsewhere. The system knows this about teaching. That's why it works. The work expands because caring people will break themselves before they let a need go unmet. Nobody measures what you give up to give everything.
---

## INTEGRATION v03
---
mode: STILL_HERE
reframe_type: TIME_SHIFT
weight: light
carry_line: "You have chosen yourself and kept teaching."
note: STILL-HERE mode — final chapter only, one per book
---
You have said no to the after-school tutoring. You have not responded to the email immediately. You have left the papers ungraded to protect the evening. You have told the parent you cannot meet again this week. You have watched the need remain unmet and stayed in the classroom anyway. You have kept teaching while someone's need went unfulfilled. This is the hardest work in the profession. Not the curriculum. Not the standards. The work of saying: I am a person. I have limits. I can care without destroying myself. You have done this. You have chosen a boundary and lived on the other side of it. Your students are still learning. The world didn't end. You're still here.
---

## INTEGRATION v04
---
mode: QUESTION_OPEN
reframe_type: QUESTION_REFRAME
weight: standard
carry_line: "What teaching remains if you keep yourself."
---
The underlying identity is: good teachers sacrifice. Good teachers find more. Good teachers don't have limits that matter more than student outcomes. But what if that identity is the thing that empties you. What if a boundary is not a betrayal of teaching but a requirement for it. What if you can be a good teacher who says no. What if the student who doesn't get the extra tutoring still learns because you're not running on fumes. What if the teaching that remains after you protect your own capacity is different but not less. This is not about caring less. It's about the structure of care that doesn't require your dissolution.
---

## INTEGRATION v05
---
mode: SOMEONE_ELSE
reframe_type: OTHER_PERSON
weight: heavy
carry_line: "The student's need is not your responsibility."
---
The student needs. That's real. The gap between their need and what exists in the system is real. But the responsibility for closing that gap is not yours alone. You are one person in a system designed for underfunding. You are one person with your own nervous system and energy reserves. The belief that you can and should meet every need is the belief that keeps you small. The system counts on it. As long as teachers sacrifice, the system can afford to underpay and understaff. Your boundary is not callousness. It's literacy about who carries what cost.
---

## INTEGRATION v06
---
mode: BODY_LANDED
reframe_type: BODY_FACT
weight: interrupt_landing
carry_line: "No is a complete sentence."
---
When you say no, the silence after it is not emptiness. It's protection. Let it sit. Don't fill it with explanation or justification. The tightness will ease when you stop negotiating with yourself.
---

## INTEGRATION v07
---
mode: COST_VISIBLE
reframe_type: COST_UNCOUNTED
weight: heavy
carry_line: "Every yes costs something you won't recover."
---
The hour of tutoring is an hour you don't sleep in. The email answered at 9PM is the quiet evening that was supposed to restore you. The extra meeting is the grading that stays unfinished. These costs are not abstract. They're deducted from the finite resource that is you.



## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: protective
tier_variant: C

What I notice is a particular erosion that happens at the edge of the work day. A student stays after class to talk about something difficult. A parent emails after hours about a concern. A colleague needs something done before tomorrow. The moment arrives where I could draw a line between the work and the rest of my life, and the line does not get drawn. The mechanism I recognize is what I think of as default-to-availability. The assumption that I am available unless I explicitly close the door. And the door is rarely fully closed. Because there is always a reason it should not be. The student is struggling. The parent's concern cannot wait. The school has a need.

## REFLECTION v03
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: protective
tier_variant: C

What I notice is the expectation that exists in educational culture that care means unlimited availability. The teacher who is truly dedicated shows up early. The teacher who really cares stays late. The teacher who genuinely loves their students gives their time generously. The mechanism that this activates is what I think of as the devotion equation. The mechanism is not unique to education. It exists in many helping professions. But it is particularly powerful in education because the work involves actual human development. The stakes feel high. It feels wrong to limit care. What the mechanism does is create a measurement system for caring. More hours equals more care. More availability equals more commitment. More sacrifice equals more dedication.

## REFLECTION v05
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: protective
tier_variant: C

What I notice is a particular kind of emotional labor that happens around the boundary. Not the boundary itself, but the management of the boundary in interaction with another person. A student who needs the boundary softened. A parent who sees the boundary as a personal rejection. A colleague who experiences the boundary as a lack of team commitment. The mechanism that activates is what I think of as relational boundary maintenance. The mechanism requires me to set the boundary while simultaneously managing the other person's response to the boundary. I have to say no to a request, but I have to say no in a way that does not hurt the student.

## REFLECTION v07
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: protective
tier_variant: C

What I notice is the particular difficulty of setting boundaries with a student who is struggling. A student who is having a hard time academically. A student who is having a hard time emotionally. A student who has nowhere else to turn. The mechanism that activates is what I think of as compassion-boundary conflict. The mechanism puts two things in opposition: the need to maintain capacity and the awareness of the student's genuine struggle. I can set a boundary, but the boundary means that this student will have less access to support. The mechanism cannot just set the boundary and move on. The mechanism has to contend with the knowledge of what the boundary will cost the student.

## REFLECTION v09
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: protective
tier_variant: C

What I notice is the particular way that administrative demands can override my boundaries. A directive comes down that requires work to be done outside contracted hours. A meeting is scheduled at the end of the day when I had planned time for something else. A requirement is added to the job description. The mechanism that activates is what I think of as hierarchical boundary override. The mechanism responds to the fact that I do not have equal power in these relationships. The administration can create demands. I can ask for accommodation, but the system is not designed to accommodate my needs. The system is designed to accomplish the school's needs.

## REFLECTION v11
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: protective
tier_variant: C

What I notice is the particular way that saying yes to one thing means saying no to something else, but the something else that I am saying no to is usually something for myself. A request for my time comes in. I could say no and protect time I had planned for myself. I could say yes and sacrifice the time I had planned. The mechanism that activates is what I think of as yes-prioritization. The mechanism treats requests from others as higher priority than plans I have made for myself. Not because the requests are more important, but because the requests are external and the plans are internal. An external request carries urgency and obligation.

## REFLECTION v13

family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: protective
tier_variant: C

What I notice is the particular difficulty of maintaining boundaries around emotional labor. A student comes to me with a personal crisis. I listen. I offer support. I become a kind of informal counselor. The mechanism that activates is what I think of as emotional-labor boundary confusion. The mechanism treats emotional labor as something that is inherent to teaching. I am a teacher. Students come to me. I respond. The boundary question does not even arise because emotional labor is seen as part of the job. But what I have come to understand is that emotional labor is not actually unlimited. I have a capacity for it. When I exceed that capacity, the quality of my teaching diminishes.

What I notice is the particular way that guilt can follow me even when I do maintain a boundary. I say no to a request. I feel guilty immediately. The guilt persists even though I know the boundary was necessary. Even though I know I did not harm anyone. Even though I know the request was unreasonable. The mechanism that activates is what I think of as persistent-boundary-guilt. The mechanism does not resolve when the boundary is actually maintained. The mechanism continues operating as if I have done something wrong. The mechanism keeps generating the sense that I have failed to show up for someone.

What I notice is the particular difficulty of maintaining boundaries when the work actually needs doing. The papers actually need to be graded. The lessons actually need to be planned. The student actually needs help. The problem is not that the boundary is wrong. The problem is that the work is real and the boundary conflicts with getting the work done. The mechanism that activates is what I think of as real-work-boundary conflict. The mechanism makes it impossible to set a boundary that prevents the work from being done. I could say no to the after-school meeting, but then the meeting does not happen and the students do not get helped.

What I notice is the particular way that my identity as a teacher can be bound up with boundary-less availability. I am someone who is always there for students. I am someone who can be counted on. I am someone who puts students first. The mechanism that activates is what I think of as identity-based boundary erosion. The mechanism treats the boundary-less availability as core to who I am as an educator. If I set a boundary, I am not being the kind of teacher I believe I am. I am not being the person I understand myself to be. What I have come to understand is that this mechanism is extraordinarily powerful because it is not just about behavior.

## REFLECTION v21

family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: protective
tier_variant: C

What I notice is the particular way that flexibility can become obligation. I am flexible about a deadline. That flexibility becomes the expectation. I stay late one evening to help. That late evening becomes when students expect to find me. I answer an email quickly once. That quick response becomes the baseline expectation. The mechanism that activates is what I think of as flexibility-as-precedent. The mechanism does not distinguish between a one-time flexibility and a new standard. The mechanism just registers that I did something, and then it registers that the expectation has adjusted. Now I am expected to do it regularly.

mechanism_emphasis: protective
tier_variant: C

What I notice is the particular way that saying no to one person can feel like I am abandoning the whole system. If I set a boundary with a parent, the mechanism suggests that I am rejecting all parents. If I set a boundary with a student, the mechanism suggests that I do not care about any students. The mechanism that activates is what I think of as boundary-as-system-rejection. The mechanism does not keep the boundary localized. The mechanism does not say: I am setting a boundary with this particular person about this particular request. The mechanism says: I am setting a boundary and that means I am not available. That means I am rejecting help-seeking.

## REFLECTION v25
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: protective
tier_variant: C

What I notice is the particular difficulty of maintaining boundaries when I do not have clear authority to maintain them. A parent makes a demand of my time. The administration makes a directive. The situation is not one where I can simply say no and have the no be respected. The mechanism that activates is what I think of as authority-absent boundary setting. The mechanism operates differently in situations where I have no formal authority. I can ask the parent to respect a boundary, but the parent can push back. I can ask the administration to respect a boundary, but the administration can override. The boundary is not actually my boundary to maintain.

## REFLECTION v27
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: protective
tier_variant: C

What I notice is the particular way that my nervous system can respond to a boundary before my conscious mind has even finished explaining it. I say no to a request and I feel immediately the physical activation of anxiety. The boundary has not even been fully stated and my system is already signaling that something is wrong. The mechanism that activates is what I think of as somatic-boundary-reaction. The mechanism operates beneath the level of conscious reasoning. The mechanism has learned that boundaries are dangerous. The mechanism has learned that saying no creates interpersonal tension. The mechanism has learned that the safe response is to say yes. So the mechanism activates anxiety in response to the boundary attempt.

## REFLECTION v29
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: protective
tier_variant: C

What I notice is the particular way that boundaries and rest do not feel the same. I could have a boundary that means I do not answer email after 6 PM. But the boundary itself is not rest. I still have the knowledge that the email is waiting. The mechanism that activates is what I think of as boundary-rest-confusion. The mechanism treats boundaries and rest as if they are the same thing. If I have a boundary that prevents work, then I should feel rested. But the mechanism does not account for the fact that a boundary can exist without actually creating a sense of safety or restoration.



## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: inherited

What I notice is a particular quality of loss that accumulates across a career. Students who disappeared. Students whose trajectories shifted. Students who I thought would make it and did not. The mechanism that activates is what I think of as cumulative-student-loss.

The mechanism is the way that losses accumulate. Each year brings new students. Each year some of those students become students I worried about. Each year some of those students are no longer there. The mechanism has been tracking these losses for years.

What I have come to understand is that the losses are not usually dramatic. A student stops coming to school. A student drops out. A student moves schools. A student takes a different path than the one I envisioned. The individual losses are understandable. The accumulation is something else.

I have noticed that the mechanism often activates at particular moments. The moment when I realize I do not know what happened to a student. The moment when I find out that a student did not graduate. The moment when I see a student years later and they are not the person I thought they would become. The mechanism registers the loss.

What the mechanism does is create the awareness that the impact of teaching is visible in only certain directions. I see the students who succeed and feel some satisfaction. I do not see most of the students who do not succeed. But the mechanism is aware of them anyway.

## REFLECTION v16
---

---
The grief in education accumulates silently. A student you taught for three years transfers without warning. A colleague you relied on takes a job in another district. A program you built over five years is defunded in a budget meeting. Each loss is small enough that the system does not acknowledge it as grief. But your nervous system does. The mechanism is cumulative loss without mourning. The body holds each departure, each ending, each erasure without the rituals that would allow processing. The grief compounds because the system does not recognize that ending a program or losing a colleague is a form of loss that requires acknowledgment.

## REFLECTION v24
---

---
The mechanism of grief in education operates through accumulation without processing. There is no space in the school day for grief. No time between classes to sit with the loss of the student who was removed by CPS. No period set aside for processing the colleague's resignation. No professional development on how to grieve the program that was cut. The system assumes educators process on their own time. The body holds the unprocessed grief. It stores it in the tension, the fatigue, the emotional flatness that accumulates over the year. The grief is not resolved. It is stored. And the storage costs the body something the system never accounts for.

The deepest grief in teaching is often the grief of recognition. The moment you see clearly that you gave your life to this work and the work took what you gave without adequate return. Not the money. The recognition. The respect. The structural acknowledgment that what you did mattered in the way you needed it to matter. The grief is not self-pity. It is the honest assessment of an exchange that was not equal. Your nervous system carried the weight of this profession for decades. The grief is the body's reckoning with what was given and what was received. The numbers do not balance. The body knows this. The grief is the knowing.

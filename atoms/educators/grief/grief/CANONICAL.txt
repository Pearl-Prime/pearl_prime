Rachel's student—the one who wrote the immigration poem—transfers to another school. His family moved. The desk is empty on Monday. Another student sits there by Wednesday. COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 3

---
James cleans out his classroom at the end of the year. Every year, same ritual: take down the posters, file the assignments, box the supplies. Every year, the same grief. Not for any single student—for the class. The class as an organism: the dynamics, the inside jokes, the way third period finally gelled in February. The organism dies in June. September brings new students who will form a new organism. The new one might be wonderful. The new one won't be this one. James stands in the empty classroom. The emptiness is an annual grief that teaching builds into its structure. You make something alive and then it ends on a schedule.

Michael receives an email: a former student died in a car accident. Twenty-two years old. Michael taught him in eighth grade. ## MECHANISM_PROOF v01
---
path: story_atoms/educators/anchored/grief/grief/mechanism_proof/micro/v01.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Rachel identifies teaching's structural grief: the annual cycle of attachment and release. Build relationships from September to June. Release them in June. Rebuild from September. The cycle produces a specific grief pattern—anticipatory loss starting in May, acute loss in June, numbness in July, tentative attachment again in September. The pattern repeats every year. Fourteen repetitions in Rachel's career. Each class is mourned. The mourning is invisible because the schedule continues. The schedule doesn't pause for grief. The schedule says: new students, new year, start over. The starting over is required. The grieving is not scheduled.
---

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02
---
path: story_atoms/educators/anchored/grief/grief/mechanism_proof/micro/v02.txt
BAND: 2
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
James discovers the grief accumulates without a discharge mechanism. Teachers don't have funerals for ended classes. No ritual marks the transition. No ceremony acknowledges that a living thing—the class as a collective—ceased to exist. The absence of ritual means the grief is processed individually, silently, in empty classrooms during the week between school years. James processes alone. Every teacher he knows processes alone. The aloneness is not because they don't share the experience. The aloneness is because there's no structure for shared processing. The structure of teaching includes creation and dissolution. It does not include mourning.
---

Sofia traces the mentor grief to its specific components. Not just the person—the function. The mentor provided: safety to fail, perspective during crisis, evidence that survival was possible, and the specific knowledge that someone who understood the work was paying attention. Each component served a different need. Each component's absence creates a different void. The person is retired. The function is unfilled. The grief is for both—the person who sat at the desk and the function that person performed. The person can be visited. The function can't be replicated. The irreplicability is the grief's permanence.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v04

BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 4
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Michael notices the student death grief is layered with professional guilt. Layer one: a person died. Layer two: a person he knew died. Layer three: a person he taught died—which means Michael is connected to a formative period in that person's life. Layer four: the guilt—did he do enough, did the turning actually hold, did the book report matter. The layers make the grief complicated because the grief is personal and professional simultaneously. Personal grief says: I'm sad. Professional grief says: did my work matter. The question "did my work matter" is the grief's cruelest addition because the person who could answer it is gone.

COST_TYPE: opportunity
COST_INTENSITY: 2

Anika measures the distance between the year-one teacher and the current teacher. Year one: thirty parent calls per week. Current: ten. Year one: stayed until six. Current: leaves at four. Year one: knew every student's sibling's name. Current: knows every student's name but not every sibling's. The metrics show reduction. The reduction looks like decline. Rachel keeps the immigration poem on the bulletin board after the student transfers. Other teachers would take it down—the student is gone, the space is needed. Rachel leaves it. ---
James invites two colleagues to his empty classroom on the last day. They sit in the student desks. They talk about the year—the classes, the breakthroughs, the disasters, the students who surprised them. The talking is a ritual they're inventing because no official ritual exists. The invented ritual is imperfect—three teachers in an empty room, no ceremony, no formal structure. The imperfection is honest. The honesty processes the grief better than silence would. James names specific students. His colleagues name theirs. The naming is an act of acknowledgment: these people existed in this room and mattered. The naming is the mourning the schedule didn't provide.

COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3

---
Rachel keeps a folder of student work. Not all of it—one piece per student, chosen for what it reveals about the student's growth. The folder is her grief management system: a portable collection of evidence that the students existed, that the work mattered, that the annual cycle produced something worth holding. She adds to the folder each June. The folder grows. The growing is the career's accumulation—not of grief alone but of grief and evidence together. The evidence and the grief coexist in the folder. Opening it produces both. She opens it sometimes. The both is the teaching life: loss and proof in the same container.

James holds the last-day ritual with colleagues every year now. Third year of the practice. The ritual has expanded: four teachers instead of three, a specific prompt—"name one student who surprised you this year." The naming produces stories. The stories produce laughter and tears in the same hour. The laughter-and-tears combination is grief being processed communally.

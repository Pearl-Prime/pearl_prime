

## INTEGRATION v01
---
mode: BODY_LANDED
reframe_type: BODY_FACT
weight: standard
carry_line: "Grief for a student is grief that stays."
---
The child whose home situation was visible in how they sat in the back row. The one you saw struggling and couldn't fix. The one who doesn't come back after winter break. The one whose name you see in the news and the fact of them hits your body before the words fully land. This is not metaphorical grief. It's the real collapse of the chest when you realize what happened. The grief settles in and stays. It doesn't resolve cleanly. You see a kid with the same coat and your body remembers. Years later, something will trigger it. A name. A gesture. The particular way someone sits when they're trying not to be noticed. The grief doesn't belong to you alone. The child is not yours to mourn. But you carried them. You watched. You cared. And now they're gone in ways that the system doesn't acknowledge as loss. Your body keeps the grief regardless.
---

## INTEGRATION v02
---
mode: COST_VISIBLE
reframe_type: COST_UNCOUNTED
weight: standard
carry_line: "Losing students accumulates into unnamed sorrow."
---
The system doesn't create space for this grief. You go home and the day ends. The next class comes in. You teach. Nobody asks how you're holding the loss of the student who aged out of the system. Or was failed by it. Or died. The cumulative weight of watching children struggle in institutions not built for them, of seeing potential constrained by circumstances, of knowing you were only a single person with limited tools. The grief is real but unnamed. You carry it alongside the next lesson plan. The sorrow compounds because there's no container for it. No one says: this is a legitimate loss. We mourn together. Instead, it sits in your body while you grade the next set of papers.

## INTEGRATION v04
---
mode: QUESTION_OPEN
reframe_type: QUESTION_REFRAME
weight: standard
carry_line: "Grief is evidence that you loved."
---
The underlying assumption is: you shouldn't be this affected. You should maintain professional distance. You should not carry this home. But what if the grief is evidence that you loved the student. What if the sorrow is the proof that you were not just going through the motions. What if a system that expects you to witness loss without being changed by it is asking for something inhuman. What if the appropriate response to losing a child to poverty or violence or circumstance is grief. What if you could honor both: the professional role and the fact that you are human and you cared.

Rachel wants to speak up at the staff meeting. The new policy affects her students—she has something to say. Her heart rate climbs before she opens her mouth. Palms damp. The principal isn't threatening anyone. The meeting is collegial. Her colleagues sit in their usual seats, familiar faces. But her body is responding like she's about to jump off a cliff. Her chest tightens. Her throat narrows. She opens her mouth and the alarm fires through her sternum. Her hands grip the edge of the table. The words are right there. They catch in her chest and will not come. The principal moves on to the next agenda item. Rachel's heart is still racing. Her face is warm. She stays quiet. The moment passes. The alarm recedes, leaving her trembling slightly in her seat, and furious.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v01

COST_INTENSITY: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Every act of speaking up triggers the same response. Michael tracks it obsessively: pulse quickens, hands cool, muscles tense. He's done this a hundred times. Still happens. It doesn't matter if it's a small suggestion or a major disagreement. The body treats all visibility the same—as threat. There is no calibration. The threat assessment is broken. But broken doesn't mean off. Broken means always on. Michael sits in a meeting where a colleague suggests something poorly thought out. The old Michael would flag the issue. The body fires. His pulse accelerates. His hands grow cold. His jaw tightens. He watches the colleague continue unchallenged. Later Michael calculates: he was right about the problem. Speaking up would have improved the discussion. The silence cost something. The silence also cost nothing, because the colleague is unaware, the meeting is over, and Michael is left with the evidence that his body will alarm for anything, regardless of the actual stakes. The mechanism knows only one word: danger. Danger looks the same whether it's there or not.

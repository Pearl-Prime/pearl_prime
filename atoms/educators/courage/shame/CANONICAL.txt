COST_INTENSITY: 2

---
Rachel has an idea for the faculty meeting. About scheduling, about student support, about something that could improve things. A good one—she thinks it's good. But sharing it means being seen. Being seen having an idea. Being seen claiming something. Her face warms just considering it. What if the idea is obvious? What if everyone's already thought of it? What if people listen politely and then never think about it again? What if they listen and disagree? The shame gathers around the thought like fog. Her chest narrows slightly. She could raise her hand. She has the space to speak. But the space doesn't matter. The shame fills it. Rachel listens to others share ideas. They are met with nods and questions. Rachel's idea stays inside. The meeting moves on without it. Later, something very similar to her idea is presented by someone else. Rachel hears it and feels the shame that she didn't say it. The shame was about being seen. But the silence is also lonely.

James knows he should report the incident. A student said something inappropriate to another student. It happened in his classroom. Proper channels, proper forms, proper documentation. James sits at his desk and stares at the incident report form. Reporting means admitting something happened in his classroom. His chest tightens. The form sits on his desk. He doesn't want to be the teacher where things happen. The teacher who can't manage the classroom. The teacher who doesn't maintain boundaries. The shame is anticipatory. No one knows yet that anything happened. But reporting will tell everyone. The incident goes unreported. James moves forward. The unreporting feels easier than the visibility. But the student who was hurt isn't getting the formal support they should have. James knows this. The knowing sits with the shame.

Sofia wants to ask for help with the difficult student. The student is challenging in multiple ways. Sofia has tried the strategies she knows. Tried the approaches from the workshops. Tried gentle and firm and flexible. Something isn't working. She should ask for help. That's what collaboration is for. But asking means admitting she can't handle it. ## MECHANISM_PROOF v01
---

BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Every bold action is blocked by the same gate: being seen. Michael can see the pattern perfectly. The idea is fine. The asking is fine. The trying is fine. The content doesn't matter. But all of them require visibility, and visibility is where the shame lives. The shame gates everything. Nothing passes without paying the toll. Michael watches himself calculate before any action: will I be exposed? Will I be judged? Will I be found insufficient? The shame answers the question before he takes the action. The shame says yes. Yes, you will be seen. Yes, you will be judged. Yes, something about you will be found insufficient. The shame is thorough. The shame is consistent. The shame knows his name. Michael has learned to assume the shame is true. It has been right before. The shame has protected him from embarrassment by keeping him small.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02

BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Rachel avoids in predictable ways. Doesn't volunteer. Doesn't question. Doesn't suggest. Each avoidance is small and feels safe. Together they form a shape: a career lived within shame's borders. The borders are invisible to others. The borders are clear to her. She knows exactly where the line is. She can be a teacher who teaches well. But she can't be a teacher who leads. She can listen to ideas. But she can't share them. She can support others' visions. But she can't have her own. The mechanism is: don't be seen, and you can't be judged. The mechanism works. Rachel is not judged. But Rachel is also not fully here. The part of her that has ideas, that has perspectives, that could offer something—that part is shrunk and hidden. The price of avoiding shame is avoiding herself.

The shame talks. James can hear it constantly. "If you need help, you're bad at your job. If you have ideas, you're showing off. If you make mistakes, you're unfit. If you're unsure, you're a fraud. If you speak up, you're arrogant. If you stay silent, you're complicit." The voice has answers for everything. Every action leads to the same verdict: you'll be seen, and what they see will be wrong. The shame tells him the only safe option is invisibility. James has internalized the shame so thoroughly that it feels like truth.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v04
---
path: story_atoms/educators/anchored/courage/shame/mechanism_proof/micro/v04.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Sofia has started pre-shaming. Before anyone can see her imperfection, she sees it first. Points it out. "I know this is probably a stupid question, but..." "Sorry, I might be overthinking this..." "This is maybe obvious..." The pre-shame is protective. If she admits the fault first, no one can use it against her. If she preface with self-criticism, the criticism can't land as hard from outside. The pre-shame is also isolating. She can't be proud of anything she's already apologized for. She can't own an idea she's already diminished. Sofia has learned to defend herself from judgment by judging herself first. But self-judgment doesn't feel like protection. It feels like she is her own attacker. Her throat is often tight. Her hands often grip things too hard. She is braced for the shame that is now coming from inside.

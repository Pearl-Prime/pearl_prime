
## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F4
voice_mode: witness
mechanism_emphasis: history

What I notice first, when I pay attention, is the throat. Not soreness, exactly — a particular kind of fatigue that is different from illness. By Thursday of a full teaching week, my voice has been in use for approximately twenty-five hours. It has been projecting. It has been modulating. It has been doing the specific work of holding attention, of emphasizing, of softening to draw students in and sharpening to redirect them. The voice has been performing its professional function. By Thursday it is not sick. It is used.

This is the history that the professional identity does not usually include. Teaching is described in terms of knowledge and relationship and pedagogical skill. It is less often described in terms of the physical instrument it operates through. My body is the primary technology of my work. My voice, my posture, my movement through the room, my physical presence — these are the means by which teaching happens. And the body has been doing this, without adequate acknowledgment, for years.

The back I notice on Friday afternoons is the back of a person who has been standing on hard floors, or bending over student desks to read their work, or carrying the weight of professional alertness in the shoulders. The feet, after a full day, register the standing in a way I did not expect when I began this work. The profession asked for my intelligence and my knowledge and my interpersonal capacity. It also required my body, silently, as infrastructure.

What I have come to understand is that there is a history written in my body that is parallel to my professional history. The vocal cord nodules that I nearly developed in year six, before I learned how to use a microphone in a large classroom. The recurring tension headaches that I eventually traced to the particular quality of sustained concentration my work requires — not the effort of thinking hard but the effort of attending to thirty-two people simultaneously, which is a different kind of cognitive labor and has a different somatic signature. The body has been keeping this record. I had been, until recently, not reading it.

The history matters because healing requires knowing what happened. My body did not arrive at its current state through ordinary aging. It arrived here through the specific physical demands of twenty-three years in classrooms. The treatment that would address these demands is different from the treatment that addresses generic stress. The body held the profession. The healing needs to know that.

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F5
voice_mode: witness
mechanism_emphasis: cost

The cost to the body is the one most invisible in the professional conversation. We discuss teacher burnout in psychological terms. We discuss compassion fatigue in emotional terms. We discuss the intellectual demands of the work and the relational demands and the administrative demands. What we discuss less is what the body has paid and continues to pay, quietly, in the service of a professional identity that does not acknowledge the body as a participant.

I have a colleague who retired after thirty-one years. She has a hearing loss in one ear that her audiologist attributes, at least in part, to years of exposure to classroom noise levels. She has bilateral knee issues that she attributes, with wry precision, to thirty-one years of standing on concrete floors covered by thin institutional carpet. She is not complaining. She is reporting. This is what happened to the body that did this work for thirty-one years. This is the record.

The cost I want to name for myself is the voice, again, but from a different angle. I have changed how I speak. Not deliberately. But at some point I developed a vocal pattern — a particular projection, a particular placement, a particular quality of not-quite-full-breath that is efficient for classroom management and chronically insufficient for the respiratory recovery my body needs. I breathe like a teacher even when I am not teaching. I discovered this in a yoga class where the teacher kept asking me to breathe more fully and I kept failing to. The professional pattern had become the default pattern.

This is the kind of cost that is only visible in retrospect, and only if you look for it. The body adapted to the demands of the work. The adaptations are not neutral. They have costs of their own. The somatic healing I am beginning to understand is not about treating pathology. It is about recognizing and beginning to revise adaptations that were functional in the classroom and are limiting everywhere else. The body learned to be a teacher's body. It does not yet know how to be just a body.

## REFLECTION v03
---
family: F4
voice_mode: witness
mechanism_emphasis: history

What I have observed in myself over years is the particular disconnection between professional competence and somatic awareness. I am excellent at reading other people's bodies. I can tell when a student's posture indicates disengagement versus frustration. I can read the shift in the room's energy through the sounds of chairs and the direction of twenty sets of eyes. I have developed a sophisticated somatic literacy that is entirely directed outward.

The inward equivalent is considerably less developed. I have routinely missed my own body's signals because I was professionally deployed outward. The headache that began at ten AM and that I did not fully register until three PM because I was inside a lesson. The tension in the jaw that I carry through the whole school day and only notice when I try to eat dinner and the jaw is already sore. The hunger that I bypassed because the schedule did not include time to eat before noon and the body learned to stop reporting it so insistently.

The history of this outward deployment goes back further than teaching. I chose a profession of care. The professional training, informal and formal, emphasized attention to others. It emphasized managing my own emotional state as a tool for managing the classroom. It did not emphasize attending to my own physical state as a legitimate object of attention during the workday. The body became background. What it needed was secondary to what the students needed.

What somatic healing requires, as I am learning it, is a reversal of the direction of attention — not permanent, not absolute, but significant. It requires that the body become figure sometimes instead of always background. It requires that I practice noticing what I am feeling physically before I have catalogued what everyone around me is feeling. This is a harder reversal than it sounds. The outward deployment is deep. It is identity-level. I am the person who attends to others. Learning to also attend to myself, to give the body the same quality of attention I give my students, is a practice that did not arrive naturally. It required being told that I had been ignoring something important, and believing it, which took longer than it should have.

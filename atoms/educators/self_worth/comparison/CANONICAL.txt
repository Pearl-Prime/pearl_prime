Rachel walks past the classroom next door. The bulletin board is immaculate—student work displayed with color-coded borders, printed labels, a thematic design that ties everything together. Rachel's bulletin board has student work stapled to it. No borders. No theme. The comparison fires on the bulletin boards: hers is functional, the neighbor's is professional. The comparison doesn't measure what happens inside the classroom. The comparison measures what's visible from the hallway. The hallway view is the comparison's entire dataset. Rachel's teaching happens behind the door, not on the board. The comparison can't see behind the door.

Anika attends a doctoral seminar with educators from private schools. Their resources: dedicated tech staff, class sizes of fifteen, budgets for materials. Anika's reality: thirty-two students, shared supplies, a copier that works every other Tuesday. Rachel maps the comparison's selection pattern. The comparison selects the teacher who's best at each individual thing and measures Rachel against that teacher. Bulletin boards: compares to the design-expert colleague. Technology: compares to the young teacher who's a digital native. Parent communication: compares to the colleague who sends weekly newsletters. Rachel is competing against a composite—a fictional teacher who has every colleague's greatest strength simultaneously. His daily work—the conversation with the struggling student, the modified assignment for the kid with the IEP, the thirty minutes after school helping a student draft a college essay—invisible. BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Sofia realizes the comparison uses social media as an evidence source. The evidence is fundamentally corrupted: social media shows best moments. Comparing best moments to all moments always finds a deficit in the all moments. The corruption is structural, not accidental—the platform is designed to display the curated. Using curated displays as a benchmark for uncurated reality is a measurement error so fundamental that every conclusion drawn from it is invalid. Every conclusion. The comparison draws conclusions from invalid data with full confidence. The confidence is the problem. Invalid data, delivered confidently, produces false beliefs.

COST_TYPE: social

Michael maps the comparison's impact on his actual teaching. The comparison with the published colleague produces: guilt about not writing, resentment about time pressures, diminished satisfaction with his classroom work. The guilt produces nothing—he still doesn't write because there's no time. The resentment poisons the parts of his job he loves. The diminished satisfaction reduces his engagement with students.


## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F2
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: cost

What I find genuinely difficult to explain to people is the specific nature of the irony. I experience social anxiety. I teach. I stand in front of a room of thirty-two people for six hours a day. From outside, these two facts do not appear compatible. The assumption is that people who are anxious in social situations do not choose professions that require constant social performance. The assumption is wrong, or at least it is wrong about me, and I suspect wrong about more teachers than people realize.

The mechanism that makes teaching possible despite the anxiety is a particular form of role-based safety. When I am in the classroom, I am the teacher. The role provides a structure that organizes the interaction. I know what is expected of me. I know my part. The students know their parts. The relationship is defined before it begins. This is different from the unstructured social situations that are hardest — the party where I do not know anyone, the group dinner where the conversation might go anywhere. The classroom is not unstructured. It is the most structured social environment I inhabit.

The cost of this arrangement is that I have spent years developing a version of professional confidence that does not fully transfer outside the classroom. Inside the room I am capable, present, even magnetic on good days. Outside it, in the staff lounge, at back-to-school night, at the department social event, the mechanism activates with its full intensity. The role is gone. The structure is gone. I am just a person among people and I do not have a lesson plan.

Back-to-school night is perhaps the most concentrated version of this. I am standing in my classroom, which should be safe. But the people in the seats are not students. They are adults, most of them older than me when I started, some of them more professionally accomplished, all of them evaluating whether I am good enough for their children. The room is the same. The configuration is the same. The mechanism responds to the shift in audience with an intensity that surprises me every year. I had forgotten, over the summer, how acute this is. Then September arrives and it is exactly as acute as it was before.

## REFLECTION v02
---

voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: automatic

What I notice is the particular experience of being watched while teaching. Not evaluated formally — observed informally. A parent in the doorway. An administrator passing through. A student teacher from the university taking notes. The mechanism activates in a way that is distinct from the ordinary classroom anxiety. I am suddenly occupying two positions simultaneously: the teacher who is present to the students, and the performer who is aware of being assessed.

The collision between these two positions is where the anxiety lives. Genuine teaching requires a kind of unselfconsciousness. It requires being more present to what is happening in the room than to how one appears. Social anxiety works in the opposite direction. It pulls attention from the present interaction into a monitoring loop that is tracking how I am being perceived. These two orientations are incompatible, and when the mechanism activates during an observation, the result is a version of teaching that is technically adequate but has lost something real.

What the students experience in these moments — I am not certain. They may not notice. Or they may notice the slight shift in my register, the way I become slightly more formal, slightly more performative, slightly less willing to follow the tangent that might have been productive but that is harder to defend to a observer who does not have the context I have. The best teaching I do is not usually observed. The teaching that is observed is shaped by the awareness of observation in ways that rarely improve it.

The mechanism that produces this is not vanity. It is not primarily about wanting to appear good. It is the social anxiety mechanism running its standard threat assessment in the presence of an evaluating audience and producing its standard result: heightened self-monitoring, partial withdrawal from the present moment, allocation of cognitive resources to the tracking of how I appear rather than what is happening. The mechanism has no way to distinguish a parent's curiosity about the classroom from a genuine threat to my professional standing. It responds to both the same way.

## REFLECTION v03
---
family: F2
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: cost

The staff room is where I have learned the most about my own social anxiety, because the staff room is where the professional structure dissolves and what remains is unstructured adult sociality that I have never been good at. I know how to be a teacher. I have been a teacher for more than a decade. I do not always know how to be a person having lunch with other adults who happen to be colleagues.

The mechanism in the staff room is the one that calculates, without being asked to, how I will be received before I speak. It predicts how a comment will land. It considers whether the group conversation has a rhythm I understand before I enter it. It monitors the small social signals — who is looking where, who responded to what, whether the joke I am thinking of will be funny to this particular combination of people — with an intensity that is exhausting and that I would rather not be running.

The cost of this monitoring is that I am often not fully present in the staff room. I am often strategically quiet when I have something to contribute, because the mechanism has assessed the social risk as too high and decided that silence is safer than exposure. I eat my lunch and I listen and I make the contributions that feel low-risk, and I go back to my classroom where the structure returns and the mechanism quiets.

The irony I want to name, the one I find wry rather than bitter, is this: I am more comfortable in front of thirty-two adolescents than I am at lunch with eight adult colleagues. The adolescents are predictable in their unpredictability. The role is clear. The relationship has a structure I understand. The adults are a social system with its own norms and histories and alliances that I have been trying to read for years without full confidence that I am reading them correctly. I have chosen a profession where my anxiety most directly affects the environment I least inhabit. This is, I suppose, a reasonable adaptation. It is also a strange way to spend a professional life.

---
Today, between the bell that ends first period and the one that starts second, you have approximately three minutes. The body does not require a spa day. It requires a pause. Stand at your desk and let your shoulders drop. Breathe out once, fully. Look at something that is not a screen or a student. This is not laziness. This is basic regulation. Cortisol needs somewhere to go. The three-minute window between classes is a physiological opportunity your nervous system has been waiting for. Teachers who skip every single transition window accumulate arousal that has no exit route. The exhaustion that feels like Monday-night fog is partly that accumulation. Today you begin to use the windows. Not all of them. One. The one between first and second period. That is the experiment for today.

By mid-week, the experiment expands to two transition windows. Not every transition—two. You choose which. Maybe the one before the class that costs the most. Maybe the one after the meeting with the administrator. The choice is yours because the body is yours. You are not recovering for the school. You are recovering so that you remain functional, present, and not depleted by Thursday. Recovery is not optional for caregivers. It is how caregiving continues.

By end of week, you will have created six or seven micro-recovery windows out of a school week that may have contained twenty or thirty transition moments. The impact is not dramatic. It is foundational. You are not fixing burnout in a week. You are learning that the windows exist. That the body can use them. That the profession has more pause-points than you have been taking.

Today you also begin naming the boundary between your teacher identity and the rest of you. Not performing the boundary—just noticing where it is. When you leave the building, you are still a person who existed before the classroom. The person who has interests, rest needs, and an interior life that is not organized around lesson objectives. Burnout accelerates when teacher becomes the only identity on the shelf. The naming practice is simple: once today, when you are not in the classroom, notice one thing that is true about you that has nothing to do with your students. You liked something before teaching. You have a body that experiences things outside of school. That person is still here.

By end of week, the identity work becomes a re-entry ritual. Before you enter the building each morning, you acknowledge something about yourself that exists outside of school. This is not affirmation theater. It is a reminder that the entity walking through the door is larger than the role it is about to perform. The caregiver also needs care. You give care all day. The recognition that you deserve some of that back is not indulgent. It is structural.

By end of week, the compassion you carry for struggling students has a small, deliberate overflow toward yourself. Not much. Not performance. Just the recognition that someone who gives this much is worth tending.
---

Today's energy audit is simple. You carry a single question through the school day: what cost me something today that the system did not count? The parent who called during your planning period. The student who cried and needed ten minutes of your full attention. The colleague who vented while you were trying to grade. The meeting that could have been an email. You are not cataloguing these to complain. You are making the invisible ledger visible. Burnout lives in the gap between what the system measures and what you actually spend. The system measures test scores, attendance, and lesson completion. It does not measure what the day actually cost your body and your capacity.

By mid-week you have two days of data. You look at the list not with judgment but with recognition. These are real expenditures. They happened. The body spent them. The emotional labor column of your time has entries the timesheet will never show. This recognition is the beginning of the audit becoming useful—not as ammunition, but as information. You cannot manage a budget you cannot see.

By end of week, the audit produces one small decision. Something you can do differently, protect more carefully, or ask for support around. Not a revolution. Not a confrontation with administration. One decision, made with information rather than habit or guilt. You gave five extra planning periods to cover for a colleague last month. The data says this cost you visible fatigue and decreased weekend recovery. You decide this month, one time, you say: I cannot this week. That is minimum viable recovery action. Small. Real. Yours.

---
Today you look at your schedule not as a given but as a construction. Someone made decisions about how many students you see, when you have preparation time, what your contractual duties include, and how evaluations are structured. Those decisions were made by people who were not you, for purposes that were not primarily about your sustainability. This is not conspiracy. It is institutional design. Institutions optimize for outcomes, not for the long-term health of their workforce. Understanding this does not fix the schedule. But it begins to shift where you locate the cause of your exhaustion. The schedule was built for the institution. Your depletion is a predictable output of that design. That is not your failure.

By mid-week, you identify one element of the schedule that you have been accepting as immovable that might have some flexibility. Not the class size—that is probably fixed. But maybe when you respond to emails. Maybe whether you attend the optional professional development. Maybe whether you eat lunch in your classroom alone or in the staff room where you can at least hear another adult voice. One element. Small. Modifiable.

By end of week, you have tested the modification. Maybe you turned off your work email at 7PM for three days. Maybe you left the building ten minutes before you usually do, even though the grading wasn't finished. The modification is not about abandoning your students. It is about testing whether the constraint you've been inside was as rigid as it appeared. Most constraints have slightly more give than chronic burnout allows you to perceive.

Summer is not rest in the way the general public assumes. But it is recovery infrastructure that the profession provides, whether or not it acknowledges this. Today you begin treating summer as a resource to plan toward, not a gap between semesters. What will your body need by June? What part of yourself has been suppressed during the school year that needs space to exist? The creative life that went underground when the grading pile grew. The physical activity that got canceled when lesson plans ran long. Summer planning is not indulgent. It is strategic depletion management.

By mid-week, the daily email boundary is established as an experiment. Notifications off at a set time. The work will still be there. Students will not suffer from a seven-hour gap in your response time. The boundary is about training your nervous system that the institution's demands have an edge, that you are not perpetually on call, that the professional self can be set down for a portion of the day.

By end of week, the physical depletion mapping is complete. You have identified where in your body the year lives. The voice that is worn from six hours of daily projection. The feet that have been standing on concrete floors since September. The back that aches from the classroom chair during grading marathons. The shoulders that have been forward-curled over desks and computers. You do not fix this in a week. You see it. Seeing it is the precondition for addressing it.

Today, look at your classroom. Not at the students in it, not at the lesson plan on your desk—at the physical space. Where does your body go most often in this room? Is there a corner near the window that catches light at second period? Is there a student arrangement that reduces the number of times you have to raise your voice? You have more agency over the physical environment than chronic burnout allows you to perceive. Today the question is: what is one thing in this room that you could shift that would reduce your daily cognitive or physical load? Not a renovation. A shift. The desk that forces you to turn your back to half the class. The supply station that requires twelve round trips per day. One thing.

By mid-week, the redesign question has expanded to the institutional environment. What administrative processes drain you most disproportionately? Not the ones with fixed requirements—the ones where the process was designed by someone who doesn't do the work. The documentation that takes forty-five minutes and serves no one you can identify. The meeting that repeats information distributed by email. These are not immovable. Some of them have workarounds. Some of them have colleagues who have found the workaround. Administrative navigation is a skill, not insubordination.

By end of week, you have had one honest conversation with a colleague about what the year has actually cost. Not a complaint session—a recognition conversation. The teacher next door who seems to have it together is also running a depleted nervous system. The colleague who arrives early and stays late is also questioning the sustainability. Peer support begins with one honest moment. Not a formal check-in. A real exchange in the parking lot or the staff room. The isolation of burnout is part of what makes it so erosive. The antidote is not a wellness program. It is one honest colleague.

The early signals of burnout speak before the crash. Your body has been broadcasting them. The Sunday-night weight that starts at 4PM instead of 7PM. The cynicism that appears during professional development where it used to appear only in December. The shortening of patience with students you genuinely like. These are not character flaws. They are diagnostic data. Today you begin tracking one early signal. Just one. When it appears this week, you note it. Not to spiral—to know. Knowing the signal is earlier intervention.

Underneath the depletion is a vocation question that burnout tends to bury: what did you originally come here to do? Not what the institution requires. Not what evaluation criteria demand. The thing you thought teaching was when you were deciding to become a teacher. That vision is probably still in you, even if it is buried under grading piles and mandatory professional development logs. Today you give it five minutes. Not to perform meaning. To locate it. The connection between what you came to do and what the daily work actually is—that gap is also part of what burnout costs.

By end of week, the body prioritization practice is established. Not as a grand wellness overhaul. As a simple daily acknowledgment that the body running this profession is worth ten minutes of direct care. Feet up. Water before caffeine. The five-minute walk at lunch even when the grading is calling. The body is not a delivery mechanism for your teaching. It is the entity that makes the teaching possible.

---
mode: declining_one_extra
reframe_type: BOUNDARY_NAMING

carry_line: "Declining one request is not abandoning the work."
mode: joy_of_teaching_locate
reframe_type: QUESTION_REFRAME
weight: standard
carry_line: "When did teaching feel like yours."
mode: invisible_cost_audit
reframe_type: COST_UNCOUNTED
weight: standard
carry_line: "What the lesson plan does not capture about what today took."
---
Today you practice declining one optional thing. Not the essential duties — the optional layer. The after-school coverage someone asked for informally. The extra committee that hasn't started yet. The email that is a question someone could answer for themselves. You decline one. Not with a speech, not with guilt, not with an elaborate explanation. You say: I can't take that on right now. And you let the response be whatever it is. The first declination is the hardest because the professional identity you've built is built in part on never declining. But the identity is not fixed. It is practiced. And today you practice a different version of it — the version that can say one small no.

By mid-week, you locate one moment when teaching felt like yours. Not the institution's. Yours. Maybe it was a particular unit you built from scratch. Maybe it was the semester a student asked a question that reoriented the whole class. Maybe it was a Tuesday when the lesson went sideways in exactly the right direction and you followed it. The moment is real and it is in there somewhere under the weight of the current year. You do not need to perform nostalgia. You just need to locate the moment as evidence that the original thing — the thing that brought you to this work — still has a referent somewhere in your history.

By end of week, you have completed one invisible cost audit. A single school day. You note what the plan said it would require and what it actually required. The parent conversation during your planning period that ran thirty-five minutes and was not in any way optional. The student in second period who needed seven minutes of quiet, specific attention before the class could begin. The administrator who stopped you in the hall and needed five minutes that became fifteen. The cost the day actually ran was not the cost the schedule listed. The gap between those two numbers is the invisible labor of teaching — the labor that the profession does not count, does not compensate, and does not protect.

## INTEGRATION v05
---
mode: grading_triage
reframe_type: SMALL_RETURN
weight: standard
carry_line: "Not all of it needs to go back tomorrow."
mode: teaching_identity_repair
reframe_type: IDENTITY_RETURN
weight: standard
carry_line: "What you are beyond what you do in this classroom is still there."
mode: recovery_window_protection
reframe_type: BODY_FACT
weight: standard
carry_line: "The nervous system needs at least one real window per day."
---
Today you triage the grading stack by what genuinely needs to go back tomorrow and what the deadline is imagined. Not all of it is actually due tomorrow. Some of it has become due tomorrow because you have been avoiding it and the pile has grown. Some of it can go back Thursday. Some of it can go back next week. You do not tell students this — you simply decide it. The stack is real and the students deserve feedback. But they do not deserve feedback at the cost of your complete depletion. Triage is not abandonment. It is prioritization. You do what matters most first and release the fiction that everything requires equal urgency.

By mid-week, the teaching identity repair begins with one simple inventory. Who are you outside of this classroom? Not who you perform outside this classroom — who you actually are. The person who has a relationship with cooking, or running, or the podcast you used to listen to, or the reading that used to happen before the grading took over the evenings. One piece of that person. You locate it and you give it fifteen minutes on Wednesday. Not to perform wellness. Because it is yours and it was here before the teaching was and it will be here after. The identity that is only teacher is the identity most vulnerable to burnout. The restoration of something outside of teacher is the repair.

By end of week, the recovery window is protected. One per day. Thirty minutes where the work is not allowed. Not the planning you could do. Not the email you should check. Not the lesson adjustment you've been meaning to make. Thirty minutes in which the body is permitted to exist outside of professional function. This does not need to be a formal practice. It can be a walk. It can be a meal that you actually taste. It can be a conversation that is not about school. The nervous system does not know how to recover in theory. It recovers in actual time that is actually protected.


## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: automatic

What I notice is that it did not happen in a single semester. It accumulated the way sediment accumulates — invisibly, layer by layer, until the riverbed was something entirely different from what it had been. The first year, I graded until midnight because the work genuinely excited me. By year seven, I graded until midnight because the pile would not stop growing. The behavior looked the same from outside. The interior was unrecognizable.

The mechanism I am describing is the erosion of meaning beneath the continuation of function. I kept teaching. I kept planning. I kept attending the professional development workshops and the department meetings and the parent conferences. The behaviors persisted. The engine that had powered them — the real, original caring — had become something else. It had become obligation. It had become survival. It had become the absence of any visible alternative.

I have come to understand that burnout in teaching does not announce itself as a dramatic collapse. It announces itself as the gradual disappearance of the moment when a student's understanding clicks and I feel it. That moment used to be the reason. It used to be the whole point. What I noticed first was that the moment was occurring less frequently. Then I noticed that when it occurred, the feeling it produced was less vivid. Then I noticed that I was planning lessons around efficiency rather than around the experience of learning. The moment was still happening. I had stopped being present for it.

This is the mechanism I think of as compassion fatigue in its occupational form. Thirty-two students in one room, each with needs I take seriously. Then administrative demands that require me to document those needs in formats that do not reflect them. Then parents who are anxious about outcomes I cannot guarantee. Then professional development requirements that arrive without consulting my actual developmental needs. Then the curriculum that shifts again. The system asks for more than one person can give, and the mechanism I developed was to give it anyway, and to not notice what I was losing in the process.

What I have come to see is that choosing teaching because you cared was never a protection against burning out. It was, in some ways, the condition that made burnout inevitable. People who do not care deeply do not burn out the same way. They leave earlier, or they manage their boundaries more naturally. The ones who burn out are the ones who believed that caring enough was sufficient. The ones who continued to give when the well was already low because they could not reconcile giving less with being the teacher they wanted to be.

The identity had become the role. When I could no longer perform the role at the level I had set for myself, what was left was not a person who was also a teacher. It was something less defined than that, something that I did not have a name for yet.

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F4
voice_mode: witness
mechanism_emphasis: history

What I observe when I look back across the arc of the years is a particular pattern: the expansion that preceded the depletion. In the early years, I expanded into the work. I developed curriculum. I built relationships with students and families. I took on the after-school program and the curriculum committee and the mentorship of the student teachers. Each expansion felt like growth. Each new responsibility felt like evidence that I was doing the work seriously.

The mechanism that was operating beneath this expansion was the belief that the work had no ceiling. That if I could do more, I should do more. That there was no limit to what a committed teacher could absorb. The profession rewarded this belief. The administrators who noticed I was capable gave me more. The parents who appreciated my engagement called me at home. The students who needed more than the class period sought me out during lunch and after school. The expansion continued because every part of the system around me treated my capacity as a resource to be utilized rather than a finite quantity to be protected.

I have come to understand that the history of my burnout is also the history of a particular institutional relationship. The school needed more than it could pay for. I provided it. The school needed teachers who would absorb the inadequacies of the system — the missing counselors, the overcrowded classrooms, the outdated textbooks, the students whose needs exceeded what the school could formally address. I absorbed them. The mechanism that made this possible was a belief in my own vocation that I had not yet learned to distinguish from an exploitation of that belief.

The depletion arrived the way depletion always arrives in systems that have been running past capacity: suddenly and also completely inevitably. Looking back, the signs were visible for years. The Sunday anxiety that had once been mild become severe. The lesson planning that had once been generative became effortful. The students I had once wanted to know individually began to feel, collectively, like a demand I could not meet. The mechanism had been running the engine past its limits for so long that when the engine finally seized, I was surprised, which tells you something about how effectively I had been not-looking.

What the history shows me now is that I was never the problem. I had absorbed a structural problem and converted it into a personal one, as people in education are trained to do. The structural problem was: the work cannot be done adequately by one person. The personal conversion was: I am not adequate enough to do it. The second statement is false. The first is true.

## REFLECTION v03
---
family: F5
voice_mode: witness
mechanism_emphasis: cost

What I can name now is what it cost, and I could not have named it then because the cost was paid in increments too small to register individually. The lost evenings. The weekend mornings that were never fully mine. The vacations spent planning the unit that would begin in September. The summers that became professional development workshops and curriculum revision and the reading I needed to do to teach a new course. The cost was time, and the time was not mine, and I had learned not to account for it.

The cost was also in the body. I lost my voice, literally, three times in a single school year. My neck and shoulders by year eight held a particular tension that I recognized as classroom posture — the forward lean, the raised shoulders, the physical state of someone managing many things simultaneously. I developed a sensitivity to fluorescent light that I attributed to aging and now understand differently. The body had been a professional instrument for so long that the accumulated usage had produced wear I was interpreting as ordinary.

The cost I did not see until much later was the relational cost. I had friends outside the profession who described their work with a kind of distance I could not access. They had work and they had the rest of their lives. I had teaching, and the rest of my life had gradually organized itself around teaching. The conversations I was most present for were about pedagogy or student welfare. The conversations I was least present for were the ones that had nothing to do with school. The work had not just consumed time. It had consumed the neural architecture of not-working.

What I have come to understand is that the cost of this level of dedication is not proportional to its value. The cost is real and the value is real and they do not cancel each other out. I gave what I gave, and I do not regret the giving, but I also now see that I was never asked to give that much. I was allowed to give that much, which is not the same thing. The profession permits overgiving without consequence to the institution. The consequence falls entirely on the person who gives.

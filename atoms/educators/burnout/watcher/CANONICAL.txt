## TURNING_POINT v06
---
path: story_atoms/educators/anchored/burnout/watcher/turning_point/micro/v06.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: opportunity
COST_INTENSITY: 4
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Daniel is burned out and the watcher will not let him rest. He lies on the couch on a Saturday and his body scans for threat — is someone emailing, is something going wrong, is his absence being noted. His chest is tight even in his own living room. His hands grip the cushion. The watcher runs its surveillance from a depleted body, draining reserves that do not exist. Then he notices something: the watcher is more active when he is resting than when he is working. At work, the watcher has data to process. At rest, the watcher has only fear. His frame cracks. The watcher is not protecting him at work. It is punishing him for resting. Every attempt at recovery triggers heightened vigilance as if rest itself is the threat. His shoulders drop slightly. The burnout cannot heal because the watcher treats healing as danger.
---

## TURNING_POINT v07
---
path: story_atoms/educators/anchored/burnout/watcher/turning_point/micro/v07.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: opportunity
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Daniel collapses during a team dinner. Not physically — his face goes blank, his eyes unfocus, his body checks out while his team talks around him. The watcher ran at full capacity during the entire meal — monitoring every face, every word, every tonal shift — and his burned-out body could not sustain it. He dissociated from the effort. His team notices. Someone touches his arm. Daniel comes back to the table and realizes he was gone. The watcher consumed so much of his depleted resources that his consciousness simply departed. His chest is hollow. His hands are numb. The frame shatters: the watcher is not keeping him safe. It is consuming him. It will run until there is nothing left to run on and then he will blank out, check out, disappear. The old way — constant scanning from an empty tank — just failed publicly. His body cannot sustain vigilance and existence simultaneously. He has to choose.

## EMBODIMENT v05
---
path: story_atoms/educators/anchored/burnout/watcher/embodiment/micro/v05.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 4
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 4
IDENTITY_STAGE: self_claim
---
Daniel sits on his porch on a Sunday morning. The watcher starts its scan — phone, email, what might be happening. Daniel notices it. He does not follow it. He watches the watcher instead. It fires and finds no data. It fires again. Nothing to scan. His chest is tight but he does not reach for his phone. He sits with the discomfort of not knowing what is happening at work. The discomfort does not kill him. The not knowing does not harm him. He drinks his coffee. His hands are warm on the mug. He is a person recovering from burnout who can sit without surveillance for fifteen minutes. Then twenty. Then the morning is gone and he did not scan once. His body feels different — lighter, looser, as if a machine that has been running nonstop finally turned off. He is not the watcher. He is the person the watcher lives inside. And that person is tired and wants to rest.

## EMBODIMENT v06
---
path: story_atoms/educators/anchored/burnout/watcher/embodiment/micro/v06.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 4
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: self_claim
---
Daniel returns from medical leave. The office is familiar and charged. The watcher lunges for every signal — who looks different, who is in what meeting, what changed while he was gone. Daniel feels the full pull. His chest tightens. His eyes dart. He lets the pull exist and does not follow it. He walks to his office, sits down, and opens his priority list. Three items. He works on the first one. The watcher screams about everything he is missing. He lets it scream. By end of day he has completed two priorities and missed approximately nothing that mattered. His body is tired but not destroyed. He drives home with residual energy. The watcher chatters from the back seat. Daniel drives. The watcher has been demoted from driver to passenger. It still talks. He no longer steers by its directions. He steers by his own plan, his own limits, his own rebuilt capacity. His jaw is loose. His hands are warm on the wheel. He is a person, not a surveillance system. The distinction saves his life.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v05
---
path: story_atoms/educators/anchored/burnout/watcher/mechanism_proof/micro/v05.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 4
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Daniel tracks his energy throughout a day. He notices the watcher consumes approximately forty percent of his attention. Not on work — on monitoring the environment around the work. Reading faces. Interpreting silences. Scanning for subtext. His burned-out body is allocating forty percent of its depleted resources to surveillance and sixty percent to actual work. His hands are cold. The math is devastating: the watcher is a tax on every hour, taking resources from a bank that is already overdrawn. His chest aches. The pattern persists because the watcher feels productive — it seems like work. But it produces nothing except anxiety. It converts cognitive resources into fear. The burnout is not just about workload. It is about the invisible workload of constant monitoring that runs beneath the visible work.
---

## MECHANISM_PROOF v06
---
path: story_atoms/educators/anchored/burnout/watcher/mechanism_proof/micro/v06.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Daniel cannot tell if he is burned out from the work or from the watching. He has been monitoring his environment so intensely for so long that the monitoring has become indistinguishable from the work itself. His chest is crushed. His vision blurs. He sits in his office and tries to separate the two: what he does and what he watches. He cannot. They are fused. Every task is performed inside a shell of surveillance. He does not write a report — he writes a report while scanning his boss's calendar for unusual meetings. He does not run a class — he runs a class while monitoring every face for signs of disengagement. His hands are numb. The pattern persists because the watcher has merged with his professional identity. Removing it would feel like removing a skill. But the skill is killing him. The watcher is not a supplement to his work. It is a parasite that disguised itself as a competency. His body knows the difference. His mind has not caught up yet.

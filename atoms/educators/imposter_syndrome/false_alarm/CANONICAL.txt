Thomas is mid-sentence on the whiteboard when a student in the back row asks a question he doesn't have a ready answer for. Nothing about the question is hostile. It's a decent question, the kind curious kids ask. But his chest tightens as if the fire alarm has gone off two rooms away — plausible, close enough to require a response, not yet confirmed as real. He says, "Good question, let's come back to that," and keeps moving, buying six minutes to figure out whether he actually knows the answer or has to admit he doesn't. The class doesn't notice anything happened. Thomas's pulse is already elevated. The alarm sounded. Nothing is burning yet.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v01

BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Thomas starts tracking it, the way you'd track a smoke detector that keeps going off at the same time every evening. It isn't failure that triggers the alarm. He has been genuinely wrong in front of a class before and felt almost nothing. What triggers it is the possibility of being seen getting it wrong — the raised hand he can't answer, the observer with the clipboard, the parent email arriving unread. The alarm fires on exposure, not on error. It doesn't wait to find out which one is happening. It goes off the moment the door to being watched opens, the same way a detector fires on steam from a shower, unable to tell the difference between vapor and smoke.

Maria maps the pattern across a month and finds it has almost no correlation with her actual competence. Her best-planned lessons and her thrown-together ones trigger the alarm at the same rate. What predicts it is only whether someone with authority over her is in the room. The alarm isn't evaluating her teaching. It's tracking proximity to judgment, a smoke detector wired to the doorway instead of the stove. She used to think the alarm was proof she needed to work harder. Now she can see it fires whether or not there's anything to work harder at. That distinction sits uncomfortably, because if the alarm isn't about her skill, she no longer knows what quiets it.

Thomas traces the alarm back to his first year, to a formal observation where the mentor teacher's face went unreadable for the entire forty minutes and the debrief note said only "needs more command of the room." Nobody said he was a fraud. Nobody needed to. His body decided that unreadable faces and clipboards meant verdict, and it has been treating every version of that scene as the original scene ever since — the department chair's neutral expression, the parent who wants "a quick chat," the new hire who asks a sharper-than-expected question. Maria sees the full shape of it during a fire drill, of all things — the actual alarm, the actual corridor emptying, everyone filing out calm and practiced because they know this drill by heart and know nothing is burning. She stands in the parking lot and realizes this is exactly backwards from what happens in her head. The real alarm, tested weekly, believed by no one to mean fire — everyone treats it as routine. Her internal alarm, which has never once been right, gets treated as gospel every single time. She has built thirteen years of teaching around obeying the alarm that lies and dismissing the evidence that doesn't. The drill ends. The bell rings for the next period. The student's question that Thomas deferred comes back at the end of the period. He still doesn't fully know the answer. Instead of the save he'd normally improvise, he says, "I don't actually know — let's look it up together tomorrow." The alarm screams through the sentence, certain this is the moment the class recalibrates around his incompetence. Nobody recalibrates. A real fire alarm goes off mid-lesson, the drill kind everyone half-ignores until they're actually filing into the corridor. Thomas's internal alarm and the building's alarm go off at the same volume for one disorienting second, indistinguishable. He walks his class out, does the headcount, keeps his voice level for twenty-six kids who are treating it as a break from math. Standing in the empty corridor after the drill, both alarms finally silent, he understands the difference he's been missing for years: the real alarm is loud and then it stops, because someone checks and confirms there's no fire. His internal one never got checked. It just kept ringing on the assumption that checking would confirm the worst. He checked, today, on purpose, and it didn't.

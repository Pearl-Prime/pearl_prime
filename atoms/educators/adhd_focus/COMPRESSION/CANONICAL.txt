## COMPRESSION v01
---
---
The intention-action gap means knowing you need to grade and actually grading are two separate events, bridged only by a visible, physical first verb — not "grade the essays" but "read essay one's thesis." Attention runs on salience, not importance, so urgent tasks need to be made loud and visible, not just true. Time blindness means duration has to be externalized onto a timer, since your internal clock won't reliably announce two hours disappearing. Working memory drops un-anchored intentions, so capture every "remember to" the instant it occurs, not at day's end. The fix across all of it is the same shape: make the next step small, physical, and visible, and let that step carry you instead of waiting to feel ready.
---

## COMPRESSION v02
---
---
Repetitive grading and paperwork tasks don't get harder as you go — your attention just naturally disengages from what's already processed and reaches for novelty instead, which is why essay seventeen feels heavier than essay one despite being identical work. The fix isn't more discipline; it's introducing a small deliberate change partway through — a different pen, a different room, reading aloud — to refresh engagement without changing the actual task. Combined with batching a fixed number before a short break, this replaces the exhausting relaunch-every-item pattern with real momentum, so the stack carries you rather than you dragging yourself through each item alone.
---

## COMPRESSION v03
---
---
Tasks with multiple valid entry points — IEP present-levels, a comment with several things to mention — create an invisible extra decision layered on top of the actual writing, and that decision, not the writing, is usually where the freeze lives. Pre-deciding one fixed, arbitrary order and using it every time removes the choice permanently, trading an "optimal" order you'd have to re-derive constantly for a "good enough" order you never reconsider. The re-deriving was the real cost; a boring, fixed template outperforms a fresh, better one because it never asks you to choose again.
---

## COMPRESSION v04
---
---
Unbounded tasks — "prep for curriculum night," "get ready for conferences" — have no natural edge, so they expand to fill any time you give them while also giving your initiation system nothing to grab onto, which is why weeks of low-grade dread can produce almost no actual work. A fabricated container — twenty-five minutes, one deliverable, nothing else required — works nearly as well as a real deadline, because what was missing was never time, it was a boundary. Give the task an edge before you give it your attention, and the dread tends to collapse faster than the actual work would suggest it should.
---

## COMPRESSION v05
---
---
Emotional flooding fuses a task to the feeling about the task, so a dreaded email or hard conversation gets blocked not by not knowing what to say but by a wave of anticipated conflict jamming the initiation mechanism itself. Reasoning about the task doesn't reach the flooded part of the system. The fix is shrinking the action below the flood's trigger threshold — read only the first line, no obligation to respond — so your brain gets one small true data point instead of the large imagined one, and the actual task usually turns out smaller than the dread that was blocking it.
---

## COMPRESSION v06
---
---
The physical objects around you are more powerful initiation triggers than any plan held only in language, which is why "I'll do it tonight" fails reliably while a stack left in your direct path succeeds reliably. Building your environment — the ungraded stack on the counter, the laptop open instead of zipped away, the template pulled up before you leave the building — does the reminding your memory won't do consistently. This isn't a claim about being more organized in general; it's recognizing that a visible object beats a remembered intention every time, and engineering around objects rather than willpower.
---

## COMPRESSION v07
---
---
Sitting down at your desk and actually starting the task are two separate, non-adjacent events for an ADHD brain, which is why you can be physically present with everything loaded and still not be working, sometimes for the whole evening. Crediting yourself with "trying" the moment you sit down sets up a confusing second wave of self-blame when nothing follows, because the sitting-down problem was never the same problem as the starting problem. The fix is a deliberate bridging action — touch the first paper, say the first sentence out loud — whose only job is converting proximity into motion; the gap needs its own small solution, not just physical closeness to the task.
---

## COMPRESSION v08
---
---
Working memory functions like a whiteboard being quietly erased rather than one that reliably holds information, so a plan formed at 7 a.m. is often genuinely gone by 1 p.m. — not forgotten through carelessness, dropped by a system that was never built to hold un-anchored intentions across a school day. Since teaching runs almost entirely on exactly these un-anchored intentions — remember to email, remember to update the log, remember what the sub needs — the fix is treating your own memory as unreliable by design and capturing every "remember to" onto a visible external surface the instant it appears, not at the end of the day when most of it is already gone.
---

## COMPRESSION v09
---
---
Task-switching costs compound across a teaching day built almost entirely of forced switches — teach, supervise, answer an email, teach again, attend an impromptu meeting — and each switch has an invisible rebuilding cost that isn't counted the way clock-time is. This is why the task needing the deepest, most sustained attention, usually left for whenever time remains, ends up fighting for the least available resource at the worst possible time. The fix is sequencing, not willpower: protect the highest-context task with a hard boundary early in the day, before switching costs accumulate, rather than leaving it to compete for scraps at 2 p.m.
---

## COMPRESSION v10
---
---
Attention in ADHD runs on salience — what's loud, novel, or immediately felt — rather than on a rational importance ranking, which is why a genuinely low-stakes task can out-compete a real deadline simply by being more visible and more immediately satisfying right now. This isn't a caring problem; it's a wiring difference in what gets flagged as worth attending to. The fix is manufacturing salience for what actually matters: bright visible reminders instead of buried planner entries, visible countdown timers instead of trusted mental deadlines, social accountability instead of private intentions — making the important thing loud enough to compete on the same terms as the distraction.
---

## COMPRESSION v11
---
---
The gap between a task's difficulty and its actual completion rate is often explained by the absence of a first physical action, not by the size or complexity of the task itself — twenty-eight essays and one IEP form both stall for the same reason: no visible five-second-doable first step sitting at the front of them. Urgency belongs to one system and initiation belongs to a separate mechanical one that doesn't respond to urgency shouting louder. The universal fix is naming the literal first verb — not the whole task, one small physical action — and doing only that, trusting that motion, once started, tends to continue on its own.
---

## COMPRESSION v12
---
---
Waiting for the first sentence to arrive polished before typing anything is a specific, common trap that looks like perfectionism but is really about treating a first draft as though it must already be a final draft — which raises the bar for starting so high that starting rarely happens, whether it's a comment, an email, or an IEP section. The fix is a structural permission slip: deliberately allow the first pass to be bad, typos and all, removing the option of waiting for a polished version that was never going to arrive before you began. Once words exist, however rough, shaping them is a different and far easier task than generating them from nothing.

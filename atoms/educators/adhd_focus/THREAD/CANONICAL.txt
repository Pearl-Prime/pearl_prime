## THREAD v01
---
---
You scheduled the hard parent call for your prep period, then watched it slide to after school, then to tomorrow, then to "this week sometime." It was never about the parent. The call had no visible first sentence your system could grab, so it kept sliding to wherever the pressure was lowest. Tonight, write the first sentence down before you dial, and notice how much less the call slides.

## THREAD v02
---
---
You keep meaning to update the intervention log every Friday, and every Friday it's still sixteen empty rows by the time you leave the building. It was never about not caring how the kids are doing — you think about them all week. The log has no natural entry point, so your brain treats all sixteen rows as one enormous decision instead of sixteen small ones. Next Friday, fill just the row for the one kid you've already been thinking about all week, and see what happens to the other fifteen.

## THREAD v03
---
---
You've opened the same comment template four times this week and closed it four times without typing a word. Each time it felt like nothing happened, because nothing did — the freeze reset itself at the same blank line every time. What actually needs to change isn't your effort at reopening it; it's what's waiting for you the fifth time you open it. Leave one sentence typed, even a bad one, before you close it next time, so the sixth attempt starts somewhere other than zero.

## THREAD v04
---
---
You told yourself you'd "just sit down and knock out the sub plans" tonight, and you did sit down, laptop open, template loaded, and twenty minutes passed with nothing written. Sitting down solved a location problem. It never touched the actual starting problem, which is a separate event your brain needed a bridge for. Tomorrow, add the bridge — one sentence read out loud — before assuming proximity to the laptop counts as trying.

## THREAD v05
---
---
You've read the same rubric line five times this week, on five different assignments, and it still doesn't move your hands any faster than the first time. Rereading feels like progress because it's an action, but it isn't the action the task actually needs. Next time you catch yourself on a second reread, stop and type the first sentence instead, even badly, and notice how differently that five minutes goes.

## THREAD v06
---
---
You keep telling colleagues you'll "get to" the curriculum night slides this week, and this week keeps ending with the deck exactly as blank as it started. There was never a shortage of time — a whole week is plenty. There was a shortage of an edge, a moment where the task had a clear start and a clear stop. Next time, give it twenty-five minutes and one slide, and watch a week's worth of dread collapse into less than half an hour.

## THREAD v07
---
---
You noticed yourself standing at the filing cabinet touching the same folder for the third time, right before a meeting where you needed those numbers. You weren't lost — you knew exactly where the scores were. You just didn't have anything visible enough to grab and walk into the room with. Next time, before the meeting starts, take one photo of the numbers on your screen and walk in with that instead of the folder you keep almost-opening.

## THREAD v08
---
---
You've had the same parent's name in your head for nine days, in the subject line of an email you haven't opened. Each day it sat there, the imagined version of the conversation got a little worse in your head, and the actual email got no closer to being read. Nothing in the real email has changed in nine days. Open it tomorrow and read only the first line — that's the whole job for now.

## THREAD v09
---
---
You catch yourself, partway through grading, checking a text message that has nothing urgent in it, for the third time this stack. It isn't that the text matters more than the essays — it's newer, and your attention is wired to reach for what's new over what's already been touched. Next stack, build the novelty in on purpose: switch pens, switch rooms, read a few out loud, before your attention goes looking for it somewhere else.

---
You keep starting the present-levels section with academic data one week and behavior data the next, re-deciding every single time which piece to lead with, and every single time that re-deciding eats the first five minutes. The writing was never the slow part. The choosing was. Pick one order now, in writing, and use it for every IEP from here forward, no exceptions.

## THREAD v11

---
You noticed the report card comments got easier around the eighth one, not harder, even though comment eight should logically be more tiring than comment one. The first few carried a cost the later ones didn't — the cost of getting your hands moving in the first place. Next time, remember that the hardest comment is always the first one, and it isn't a preview of the other twenty-five.

## THREAD v12
---
---
You spent eleven minutes holding the wrong student's folder before a colleague reached over and swapped it without a word, and something in you unlocked the moment the object in your hands changed. It wasn't really about the folder. It was about having something concrete and correct in your hands to act from. Next time you feel stuck holding the wrong version of something, physically change what's in your hands before trying to change your mind.

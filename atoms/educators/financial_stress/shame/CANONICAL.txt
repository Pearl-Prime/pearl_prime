Rachel buys classroom supplies from her own money. Not because the school requires it. But because the budget ran out in December and there are books students need, markers that broke, construction paper for projects. Rachel buys them. It's become normal. She's spent maybe three hundred dollars this year without really thinking about it. Then she thinks about it. Three hundred dollars. She's also been eating lunch at her desk because the staff lunch place is expensive. She's been turning off the thermostat in her apartment to save on heating. She's been saying no to coffee with friends. Three hundred dollars she didn't have, spent on things the school should have provided. Rachel sits with the shame of this. Not the generous shame of giving. The shameful shame of not having enough to give and giving anyway. The shame of being the woman who can't afford to buy classroom supplies but is buying them anyway because the alternative is her kids working without the right materials. The shame feels like a confession of inadequacy, though the only inadequacy is the school's, not hers.

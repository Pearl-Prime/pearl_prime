

## INTEGRATION v01
---
mode: BODY_LANDED
reframe_type: BODY_FACT
weight: standard
carry_line: "The math in your body doesn't lie."
---
The tightness in your stomach when the school supplies budget runs out in February is not anxiety disorder. It's accurate response to real scarcity. You need to buy the construction paper out of pocket if the kids are going to have materials. The rent is due. You do the math: what gets covered this month. The body keeps this calculus. The shoulders hunch a little tighter. The breath gets shallower. This is not nervousness about money. This is the physical signature of not having enough. When you're working a second job and the fatigue arrives earlier each year, that's not burnout alone. That's the muscle cost of financial insufficiency. Your body is working two jobs. Your nervous system is calculating scarcity daily. The tightness you carry is not a psychological symptom. It's a legitimate response to a situation that requires sustained sacrifice.
---

---
The pencils you buy from your own account, the poster board, the markers, the paper towels for the classroom—this is your labor transformed into materials your students need. The system calls this dedication. You call this necessary. But the cost is real. The money that could have gone to your own life, your own future, your own security is now in the classroom. The second job you took to make it work—the tutoring, the summer school, the weekend shifts—this is time you don't have for rest or relationships or anything that isn't about money. The accumulation happens quietly. The personal savings that don't grow. The retirement that's behind where it should be. The kids have what they need for learning. You have what's left.

## INTEGRATION v04
---
mode: QUESTION_OPEN
reframe_type: QUESTION_REFRAME
weight: standard
carry_line: "What if teaching didn't require financial sacrifice."
---
The underlying belief is: good teachers sacrifice financially. Good teachers spend their own money. Good teachers work extra jobs because the pay is inadequate and the work matters. But this belief protects the system, not you. It allows schools to budget inadequately because teachers will cover the gap. It allows the profession to underpay because the work is its own reward. What if you could teach and earn a salary that matched the demand. What if classroom materials were actually funded. What if the second job didn't exist. What if you could be a good teacher without financial insecurity as the price of entry. This is not about wanting luxury. This is about the cost of basic stability being deducted from your livelihood while you're told it's a calling.

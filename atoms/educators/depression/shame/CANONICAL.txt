Rachel stands in the back of her junior AP Lit classroom, arms crossed, watching her students discuss Gatsby. The discussion is good. She knows it is good—the kids are actually engaged, actually building on each other's points instead of waiting for her to validate. The lightness in the room is real. And Rachel is watching it from somewhere far away, as if the moment belongs to someone else who happens to be wearing her body. She should feel proud. She used to feel proud at moments like this. When did the pride stop? The students laugh. Someone makes a connection about the green light that makes others nod, adjust their thinking. Rachel's chest stays flat. She doesn't remember the lesson plan anymore, though she wrote it last night. She remembers the coffee tasted wrong. She remembers thinking the drive in took too long and that thought was the most real thing in her day so far.

Michael lectures on the Civil War, and the information flows out of him the way it always has. But now he's aware of the difference between the information moving and the person moving it. He's here. The students are following. The era is complex and real to them because he's there making it so. Michael doesn't feel transformed by this. He feels present. Which is different. Which is actually what matters. The medication is working. The flatness is smaller. The distance between Michael and his own life is closing, not dramatically, but measurably. He writes recommendation letters now and feels something real about the students going on to bigger things. It's not the idealism of year one. It's quieter. It's more honest. It's sustainable. Michael doesn't know if this is recovery or just what teaching looks like when you stop demanding it to feel like something it doesn't have to feel like. But he shows up. He's present. The teaching works. That's the new baseline. That's okay.

Anika presents research in seminar, fewer students now, her choice, her boundary, her act of self-preservation. The ideas still matter. The discussion still flows. But Anika is in the room instead of watching it happen. The medication is helping. The therapy is helping. The reduced load is helping. Most importantly, the choice is helping—the knowledge that she decided this instead of it happening to her, that she can say what she needs and the institution can hold it. The research still calls to something in her. The teaching still matters. But they don't have to prove anything about her worth anymore. Anika is here, present, limited, and somehow that feels like the first honest thing she's done in years. She doesn't know what comes next. But the person inside is back in her body, and that's the real beginning. That's when recovery can actually start.

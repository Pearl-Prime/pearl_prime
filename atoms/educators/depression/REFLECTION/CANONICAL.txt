

## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: inherited

What I notice is a particular quality of absence in the presence. I am teaching the lesson. I am managing the classroom. I am responding to students. But something fundamental is not there. The enthusiasm that used to animate the work has become absent. The sense of purpose that used to drive the work has become distant. The mechanism that activates is what I think of as presence-without-aliveness.

The mechanism is the capacity to show up and do the work while experiencing a kind of internal deadness. The work happens. The output is there. But the inner experience is flat. The work is happening but I am not particularly there for it.

What I have come to understand is that this mechanism can persist for a long time without being recognized. From the outside, everything appears normal. The work is being done. The students are being taught. But internally, the sense of being alive is absent.

I have noticed that the mechanism often makes the work feel like going through motions. I know what comes next. I execute it. But the execution is automatic, not engaged. The mechanism has converted teaching from an act of presence into a series of behaviors.

What the mechanism does is create a kind of invisibility about the absence. The work is visible. The absence is invisible. So for a long time, I might not recognize that something is fundamentally wrong.

What I notice is a particular disconnection from students that can happen alongside the continuing performance of teaching. I see the student. I know I should care. But the caring does not come. The mechanism that activates is what I think of as care-disconnection.

The mechanism is the way that depression can prevent the emotional response that I normally have to students. The students are still there. They still have needs. But my capacity to feel moved by their needs has become absent. The mechanism has created a distance that I cannot cross.

What I have come to understand is that this mechanism creates a particular kind of guilt. I am supposed to care about students. I am paid to care about students. But I do not feel the caring. The mechanism has made me feel like I am betraying my role.

I have noticed that the mechanism often makes the disconnection feel like a personal failure. I used to care more. I used to be more engaged. The fact that I am no longer caring feels like evidence that I have become a worse teacher.

What the mechanism does is compound the depression by adding guilt to the disconnect. The depression has removed the caring and then the system tells me that not-caring is a moral failure.

## REFLECTION v05

family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: inherited

What I notice is a particular fatigue that is not responsive to rest. I sleep and I am still tired. I have a break and I am still tired. I do something that should be restorative and the restoration does not come. The mechanism that activates is what I think of as depression-fatigue.

The mechanism is a particular kind of fatigue that is generated by the nervous system itself rather than by physical exertion. The fatigue is the default state. The mechanism has set the baseline to exhausted.

What I have come to understand is that this mechanism is distinct from the fatigue that comes from working hard. This fatigue is present even when I am doing nothing. This fatigue is the experience of the nervous system running on the default setting of depletion.

I have noticed that the mechanism often creates a feeling of heaviness. Everything is harder because everything has weight. Moving through the day is an effort not because the day is particularly demanding but because the baseline has become effortful.

What the mechanism does is make ordinary things feel like they require enormous energy. Getting out of bed requires energy. Going to work requires energy. Being in the classroom requires energy. All of it is harder because the nervous system is already operating in a depleted state.

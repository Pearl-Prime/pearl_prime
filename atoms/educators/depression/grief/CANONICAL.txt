Rachel thinks about Marcus. Not on purpose. She's grading essays and one student's argument about resilience triggers the thought, and there Marcus is—sophomore year, three years ago, the one who was supposed to be fine. He dropped out in March. His parents said it was family circumstances. Rachel got a text from a student friend of his months later: he's in a factory now, working second shift. She was supposed to teach him junior year. She was supposed to see where the smart kid with the difficult home life ended up when he had room to breathe. Instead she has a blank space where the story was supposed to continue. Rachel grades the essay. The student's resilience is admirable. The student will probably be fine. Rachel feels the flatness underneath the thought—the accumulated knowledge that some of them don't make it, no matter what she does. The flatness wasn't there when she started teaching. It arrived gradually. With each Marcus who didn't come back. With each promise to herself that next year she'd find the right words to keep them here. With each failure to find those words.

James walks past the science building where Sarah used to teach. Not on purpose anymore. He's learned to take the other route. But sometimes he forgets. Sarah left six years ago. She was in her office one day and burnt out the next—actually burnt out, not the casual tired they all say, but the kind that means you can't imagine doing this one more day. She's a real estate agent now. She's happy, apparently. James thinks about this sometimes, the happiness of the person who got out. He thinks about what her classroom is like now with someone new in it. He thinks about the projects she never got to do with her seventh graders. He thinks about the letter he could have written her before she left, the one that would have said: I see how hard you're working. I see that you're drowning. I see that you might need to go. He didn't write it. She left anyway. And James teaches the next class knowing that some of the brightest people he's known gave up. That the burnout was real. That the leaving was the right choice. And James is still here.

Sofia's idealism died a specific death. She remembers the year—year three, when she realized that reading to seven-year-olds wasn't going to change the thing she thought it would change. She went into teaching because she believed in transformation. She believed that early intervention, consistent attention, good literature could bend the arc toward justice. And then she saw that some of the children she loved deeply still went home to circumstances that reading about kindness couldn't fix. She still teaches. The love is still real. But something in her died the day she understood that her love, however genuine, however consistent, wasn't enough. It was never going to be enough. Sofia teaches now with a kind of grief underneath the care. The care is real. The grief is that the care is insufficient. The grief is the gap between what she can give and what they need. The grief is teaching year after year knowing this gap will never close.

Anika thinks about the scholar she was supposed to be. Not the one she is—accomplished, productive, meeting metrics. The one she thought she'd be when she was a graduate student falling in love with ideas, working eighteen-hour days because the work felt important, because the research was going to matter, because intellectually transforming young people was the point of all of it. Somewhere between the dissertation and the tenure track, the research became the thing that had to happen to prove she was valuable. The teaching became the thing that took time from the research. The love of ideas became the management of ideas for publication. Anika still does the work. She still produces. But the grief underneath it is profound. She's grieving what she thought her intellectual life would be. She's grieving the time she didn't spend with ideas just for the joy of thinking them. She's grieving the mentor she thought she'd be—the one who would pour herself into students because students were worth pouring yourself into. Instead she teaches in the gaps of her research schedule. She teaches efficiently. She teaches well. And she grieves.

Rachel accumulates the losses. She thinks about Marcus and then about Chantelle, who was brilliant and determined until her parents' divorce derailed her, and then about Jacob, who was depressed and nobody caught it in time. She thinks about David, who had the talent to go to an Ivy but didn't apply because his older brother didn't go to college and family loyalty meant something more than possibility. Rachel realizes she's been keeping a mental count. She can't save them. The system can't save them. She does everything she's trained to do and it's not enough. Rachel feels the weight of the accumulated couldn't-saves. The weight settles into depression that looks like flatness. Rachel goes through the motions and the motions are getting harder because they're weighted with the knowledge that the motions don't save anyone.

James realizes he's been mourning the profession itself. Not just the individual burnouts or the specific students lost to circumstances. The profession as he imagined it when he started. Teaching was supposed to be about curiosity. It turned into compliance. It was supposed to be about developing minds. It became about test scores. It was supposed to be a calling. It became a job. James doesn't blame anyone specifically. The system works the way systems work. James grieves the profession that he loved and the profession that exists. He teaches the second one, but he's mourning the first. The mourning has become depression. The grief has become flat. James gets up and teaches and the love is mostly just memory now.

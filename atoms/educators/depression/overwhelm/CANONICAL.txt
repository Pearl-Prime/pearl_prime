Rachel doesn't get caught up. She stays late two nights in a row and completes the essay feedback, her shoulders aching, her eyes blurry. She sends the file to students at 11:37 pm. The essays are done. She waits for the relief. It doesn't come. The flatness is still sitting in her chest. It sits heavier now, because she's proven that catching up doesn't work, which means she must not be catching up fast enough, or her system isn't right, or she's not working hard enough. Her belly tightens with the knowledge that she's doing everything right and nothing is changing.

James picks up a duty shift. He stops going to the faculty lounge. He gets the grading stack down by twenty-five papers. His friend Sofia notices he's quieter and asks if he's okay. His jaw clenches. He doesn't have room to name it. There's too much else. His hands move through the grading mechanically. For two days, he feels slightly less gray. Then new assignments come in. New papers. New emails. The relief evaporates. He's right back behind. And the flatness — the thing that started before the papers, that was there in September when the workload was lighter — remains exactly the same. He doesn't make this connection. Instead, his shoulders slump deeper, because the only thing he thought might help (getting ahead) worked for one day.

James's daughter asks him at breakfast: "Are you sad about something?" His hands pause over his coffee. His jaw unclenches just enough for him to feel it. He says he's not sad, just tired. She's eight. She watches him. She says: "You're sad at dinner every night. It's like you're not here." Something in his chest breaks open. He's become invisible to his own daughter.

## STORY v01
---
---
Renata teaches sixth-grade science and has learned to recognize the exact moment her mind leaves a room. It happens during her third-period lab, always the same spot — right after she says "find your partners," when there's a thirty-second lull before the noise starts. That's when she goes to the email she hasn't sent, the one to the parent asking why his son's grade dropped. She's composed four versions of it in her head this week, none of them written. Today, mid-lull, a student named Dev raises his hand holding a beaker wrong, and she almost misses him because she's mid-sentence, in her head, to a parent who isn't there. She catches herself. She sets the beaker upright with him, physically, hand over his hand for a second. Then she goes back to the lull, and instead of drafting the email again, she just watches the room fill with noise. She writes the email at lunch, once, actually. It takes four minutes. The four days of rehearsing took longer than the sending ever would have.

## STORY v02
---
---
Marcus co-teaches special ed in a room that's never quiet for long, and his tell is the ceiling — when he's rehearsing an argument instead of listening, he tips his head up slightly, like the answer's up there. He does it in the Tuesday team meeting, staring at ceiling tiles while his co-teacher describes a student's meltdown from Monday, because Marcus is already building his defense for a decision the team hasn't even challenged yet. The special ed coordinator says his name twice before he answers. He apologizes, says he was somewhere else. She says, not unkindly, "we noticed." That lands harder than a formal warning would have. That night he doesn't fix it by trying harder to concentrate — he starts checking, every fifteen minutes on his phone, whether his eyes have drifted to the ceiling. Three checks in, he catches himself mid-drift in a parent call and brings his eyes back down to his notes. Small. Unglamorous. But he was there for the rest of that call, and the parent noticed the difference even if she couldn't have said why.

## STORY v03
---
---
Priya keeps a sticky note on her monitor that just says "here," which she put there after a parent-teacher conference where she realized, walking out, she couldn't remember the mother's name — not because she was distracted, but because she'd spent the entire fifteen minutes composing her own defense of a grade the mother hadn't even brought up. She'd answered a complaint that was never made. The note is not motivational; it's a flag she planted for herself. Two weeks later, in the next round of conferences, a father starts in on homework load, and Priya feels the old current start pulling her toward rehearsal. She looks at the sticky note. She looks back at the father. She asks him what specifically worries him about the homework, a question she couldn't have asked if she'd stayed in her head building an answer to a complaint he hadn't made. He tells her something true. She writes it down.

## STORY v04
---
---
Ordell has been late to his own prep period for three years running, not because of meetings but because he sits down to plan and his mind goes straight to the comment section of last year's evaluation, a paragraph he has memorized against his will. He calls it "the loop." One Thursday his mentor teacher, observing him unprompted, notices he's typed four words of a slide in twenty minutes. She doesn't ask what's wrong. She asks what room he's actually in right now. He says, out loud, "I'm in my classroom. It's Thursday. Nobody's evaluating me today." Saying it flatly, like a fact, does something the worrying never did — his hands go back to the keyboard. He doesn't finish the whole lesson in that period. He finishes the first slide, which is one slide more than the loop ever gave him.

## STORY v05
---
---
Denise runs recess duty like a small country and prides herself on catching everything — the shove before it becomes a fight, the kid crying alone by the fence. But this fall she notices she's been catching less, because half of every recess she's replaying an exchange with her principal about a schedule change she didn't want. A girl comes to her holding her wrist, says another kid grabbed her, and Denise realizes she has no idea when it happened because she wasn't watching that side of the yard — she was in the principal's office in her head, still arguing a point that was decided a week ago. She kneels down, looks at the wrist, looks at the girl's face, and makes herself name three things she can actually see: the girl's shoelace untied, a scrape on her palm, her own shadow on the blacktop. The office argument doesn't come back for the rest of recess. It's still unresolved. It can wait for a room that actually needs it.

## STORY v06
---
---
Felix teaches high school history and has a rule for himself now, born out of an incident he's embarrassed by: during a debate exercise, he called on a student twice by the wrong name in ten minutes because he was mentally rewriting his response to a snide comment a colleague made in the workroom that morning. The student, a quiet kid named Theo, didn't say anything, just answered like it was fine, which somehow made it worse. Felix's rule now: before he walks into any room with kids in it, he touches the doorframe with two fingers on the way in. It's not superstition. It's a cue, deliberately built, that says whatever was in the hallway stays in the hallway. He still forgets sometimes. But the doorframe is there every day whether he remembers or not, and remembering is getting easier.

## STORY v07
---
---
Yvette is filling out an IEP progress report, a form she's filled out hundreds of times, when she notices her cursor hasn't moved in two minutes and she's actually been composing, sentence by sentence, what she'll say if the student's mother pushes back on the goals — a meeting that is not scheduled, that may never happen. She stops. She reads the actual sentence in front of her: "makes progress toward goal with minimal prompting." She realizes she wrote it forty minutes ago and hasn't verified if it's still true this quarter. She goes back to her actual data, the actual folder, and rewrites the sentence to match what really happened this term, not what she wrote on autopilot while arguing with a parent who was never in the room.

## STORY v08
---
---
Bao substitutes three days a week across different classrooms and has trained himself to do one thing before taking attendance in any new room: read the room itself — the posters, the seating chart, the smell of it — for ten full seconds before he opens his mouth. He started this after a day where he taught an entire fifth-period class while mentally still in third period, still stewing over a note a student had passed that he'd confiscated, and realized afterward he couldn't describe a single thing about the fourth-period kids he'd just spent fifty minutes with. Now the ten seconds is non-negotiable, even when he's rushed. It doesn't erase third period. It just makes sure fourth period actually happened for him too.

## STORY v09
---
---
Angela is a first-year teacher who cries in her car after school most days, not because the job is unbearable but because she is doing two jobs at once without knowing it — teaching the lesson in front of her, and simultaneously grading herself against an imaginary observer in the back of the room who is always disappointed. Her mentor, an old hand named Ruth, tells her something she doesn't fully believe at first: "The kid in front of you doesn't know about the observer. Only you can see him. Try teaching to the actual kid for one class period and see what's different." Angela tries it in fourth period, just once, just as an experiment. She notices, by the end, that she remembers three specific things a specific student said. She hadn't remembered specific things in weeks. The observer had been eating all her attention.

## STORY v10
---
---
Grant, a band director, realizes during a rehearsal that he has cued the trumpets to enter twice in the wrong measure because he's replaying an argument with his department chair about the budget for new reeds. The kids don't seem to notice. He does. He stops the rehearsal, tells them frankly, "give me one second, I'm somewhere else," which surprises them into laughing, and then he actually takes the second — one real breath, hand flat on the music stand — before restarting. The argument with the chair is still unresolved after rehearsal. But the trumpets come in on the right measure for the rest of the hour, which is the only thing that was actually his to fix in that room.

## STORY v11
---
---
Constance grades papers every night at her kitchen table with the TV on for company, and for years told herself this was multitasking. What she notices this year, tracking it honestly for the first time, is that she rereads almost a third of every essay because her eyes finish the paragraph before her mind does. The comment from the show pulls her out mid-sentence, every time, and she doesn't register it until she's three lines further down a paper she can't summarize. She turns the TV off for one night, out of curiosity more than virtue. The stack that usually takes her ninety minutes takes fifty-five. She isn't working harder. She's just actually reading each sentence once instead of not-quite-reading it three times.

## STORY v12
---
---
Deshawn covers hallway duty between periods, a five-minute window he used to think of as dead time, good for scrolling his phone. He starts noticing something: kids says things to him in passing — "Mr. D, can I talk to you after class" — that he answers on autopilot and then can't recall having agreed to. One Tuesday, a kid he'd apparently told he could turn in a late project shows up expecting that deal, and Deshawn has no memory of the conversation at all. He decides hallway duty is not, in fact, dead time. He puts the phone in his pocket for those five minutes. Within a week he catches two kids who needed something real — one who mentioned, sideways, that he hadn't eaten since yesterday. Deshawn wouldn't have caught that scrolling.

## STORY v13
---
---
Naomi, a veteran teacher of curriculum nights, used to spend the entire event bracing for the one difficult parent she knew would show up angry about a grading policy. One year he doesn't come. She realizes, walking to her car, that she spent ninety minutes present for a confrontation that never happened, and in doing so was half-present for the eleven other parents who did show up with real, answerable questions. The next year, when she feels the bracing start again at the classroom door, she names it out loud to herself — "I'm pre-fighting someone who isn't here yet" — and redirects to the actual parent standing in front of her, holding an actual question about her actual kid.

## STORY v14
---
---
Tomas has a planning period right after his hardest class, and for months he used it to relitigate everything that had gone wrong in the previous fifty minutes — the kid who mouthed off, the transition that fell apart. One day his AP walks by and finds him staring at an unopened laptop, twenty minutes into a forty-minute period. She doesn't lecture him. She just says, "that class is over. This one's still open," and gestures at the blank lesson plan on his screen. It's a small sentence but it does something — it draws a line between the room he was in and the room he's actually sitting in now. He opens the laptop. He doesn't solve the previous class's problems that day. But he plans tomorrow's lesson, which the previous class's problems were never going to do for him.

## STORY v15
---
---
Wendy, teaching remotely to a room of black video squares during a snow day makeup session, catches herself checking her phone under the desk during a student's presentation, telling herself she's "just quickly" answering a parent email. She looks up in time to realize she missed the entire thesis of the presentation and has no idea what question to ask. She sets the phone face-down, physically turns it over, an action she does on purpose so she can feel herself doing it. The next presenter, she watches the whole three minutes. She asks a real question. The student's face changes, briefly surprised that someone was actually listening — which tells Wendy exactly how rare that had become.

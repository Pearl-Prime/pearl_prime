## PIVOT v01
---
---
Your mind is drafting the parent email during silent reading, four minutes early. Notice the draft, then set it down — the parent hasn't read it yet, and this room has students in it right now. Look at one actual face before you look back at the clock.
---

## PIVOT v02
---
---
You think this parent conference is already going badly because you rehearsed his objection all morning. Try this instead: treat his first actual sentence as new information, not confirmation. Listen to what he says, not what you expected him to say.
---

## PIVOT v03
---
---
Your mind is in the copier line replaying a comment from the workroom instead of watching the twenty-eight kids in front of you. Notice the replay, then return to one physical detail in the room — a face, a raised hand. The comment will still be there at 3:15.
---

## PIVOT v04
---
---
You think being at your desk during lunch duty means you're free to relitigate last period. Try naming it once — "that class is over" — then look at the actual room you're sitting in. This period hasn't happened yet; give it the chance the last one already had.
---

## PIVOT v05
---
---
Your mind is composing a defense for an IEP objection nobody has raised yet. Notice the rehearsal, then set it down. Ask the parent what's actually worrying them this week, and let their real answer replace the one you built for them.
---

## PIVOT v06
---
---
You think grading faster means grading while your mind drafts tomorrow's lesson. Try this instead: say the student's name before you start their paper. One second spent locating them buys back the minutes you'd otherwise spend rereading a paragraph you weren't present for.
---

## PIVOT v07
---
---
Your mind has left the staff meeting for an unresolved hallway argument. Notice where it went, then put your pen down and your eyes up at the next agenda item. You won't catch everything. You'll catch the sentence buried in this one.
---

## PIVOT v08
---
---
You think ten seconds of silence in an unfamiliar classroom is wasted time before attendance. Try this instead: actually look at the room — the posters, the seating chart. Those ten seconds are the difference between subbing on autopilot and actually being there.
---

## PIVOT v09
---
---
Your mind is bracing for the one difficult parent at curriculum night while an easy, ordinary one is standing in front of you. Notice the brace, then ask yourself: is this the one I'm dreading, or someone new. Meet the person actually in the room.
---

## PIVOT v10
---
---
You think you're multitasking, helping one student while doing math about the rest of your day. Try this instead: give the kneeling-by-the-desk minute your full attention on purpose. The calendar can wait sixty seconds. The kid in front of you can't.
---

## PIVOT v11
---
---
Your mind assumes it should have this handled by now, this many years in. Notice that assumption, then let it go — experienced teachers don't wander less, they return faster. Count today's returns, not today's wanderings.
---

## PIVOT v12
---
---
You think the lesson runs fine on autopilot because you've taught it forty times. Try this instead: scan actual faces at your next checkpoint — after the new term, before independent work. The script is old. The confusion on today's faces is new every time.

## INTEGRATION v01
---
---
Tomorrow, pick one recurring moment in your day that you already know tends to pull your mind into another room — the first ninety seconds of a parent conference, the lull after "find your partners" in a lab, the walk from the copier back to your classroom. You don't need to fix your whole day; you need one deliberately chosen spot. When you arrive there tomorrow, notice, without judgment, whether your mind has already left for the IEP meeting or the unanswered email or the comment from the hallway. If it has, don't scold yourself for it — that reaction just adds another layer of noise on top of the drift. Simply name where it went, one short phrase, silently: "the email again." Then bring your attention to one concrete, physical thing in the actual room — a face, a sound, the weight of the pen in your hand — and let that be the whole return. It will not feel dramatic. It is not supposed to. The value isn't in one perfect return; it's in doing this same small motion in the same spot enough times that it stops requiring effort and starts being simply what you do there. By Friday, you won't have eliminated the wandering — nobody does — but you'll likely notice you're catching it faster, in that one spot, than you did on Monday. That's the actual target: not a mind that never leaves the room, but a mind that comes back quickly, in the specific place you practiced, carrying you into the rest of the day a little less scattered than it would have been otherwise.

## INTEGRATION v02
---
---
Carry this into tomorrow's first parent email or phone call you're dreading: notice the moment, probably tonight or on your drive in, when you start rehearsing their reaction before they've said a single word. That rehearsal will feel productive — like you're preparing — but tomorrow, try setting it down at the actual moment of contact. When the parent answers the phone or writes back, treat their first sentence as genuinely new information rather than confirmation of the script you already built. This requires nothing elaborate: just a half-second pause between hearing or reading their first words and formulating your reply, long enough to actually take in what they said instead of what you expected them to say. You may find their real concern is smaller, or entirely different, from the one you spent all night defending against. If it is the difficult conversation you feared, you'll still be better equipped for it having heard the actual complaint rather than the imagined one. Either way, the practice is the same: let the actual first sentence, not your rehearsed one, be what you respond to. Do this once tomorrow, with one parent, deliberately. Don't expect it to feel natural yet. It's a single rep in a skill you're allowed to still be building, no matter how many years you've been doing this job.

## INTEGRATION v03
---
---
Tomorrow, during your first grading stretch of the day, try the small experiment of saying each student's name silently before you begin their paper. It costs a second per student, which across a stack of thirty is thirty seconds, less time than you'll spend rereading a single paragraph you weren't actually present for the first time through. Notice, honestly, how many times over the stack you catch your eyes moving down a page while your mind is somewhere else entirely — a hallway comment, a schedule change, next week's observation. When you notice, don't restart the whole essay in frustration; just say the name again, quietly, and pick up from wherever you actually are. The goal isn't a perfect, unbroken hour of focus, which was never realistically available to you or anyone grading thirty essays after a full teaching day. The goal is simply a slightly higher ratio of first-read to re-read across the stack than you had last week. Track it loosely, just a rough sense at the end: did today's stack take less rereading than yesterday's. If it did, that's the whole win, and it's a win you can keep making, essay by essay, for the rest of the year.

## INTEGRATION v04
---
---
Carry this into your next hallway or recess duty: instead of treating it as dead time meant for scrolling or replaying the last period's frustrations, choose one small sensory anchor for the duration — the sound of your own footsteps on the tile, or the specific mix of backpack colors moving past you. When you notice your mind has drifted three rooms away, to the staff meeting or the email or the argument that isn't resolved, don't force it to stay gone or drag it back with frustration. Simply return to that one sensory thread, briefly, before continuing your scan of the hallway or yard. You are not aiming for total vigilance for the entire duty period — that's not sustainable and was never actually required of you. You're aiming for enough returns, scattered through those minutes, that if something does need catching — a shove, a kid who says something real in passing — you're actually located in the hallway when it happens rather than three doors down in your own head. Try this once tomorrow on whichever duty you have next. Notice, afterward, whether you remember anything specific about a kid you passed. If you do, that's new information about how present you actually were, compared to how present duty usually gets from you.

## INTEGRATION v05
---
---
Tomorrow, before you walk into your first staff meeting or team meeting of the week, choose one physical cue that will mark your return each time a new agenda item starts — pen down, eyes up, shoulders squared toward whoever's speaking. Use it deliberately at the start of each new topic, not as a promise to hold your attention for the entire meeting, which nobody actually can, but as a repeated small reset at reliable checkpoints you know are coming. When your mind drifts to the unresolved conversation with your principal or the lesson plan you didn't finish, let it drift — that's ordinary — and use the next agenda-item transition as your next chance to come back, rather than trying to force an immediate correction mid-thought. By the end of the meeting, you likely won't have caught everything, and that's fine; you'll have caught more of the specific moments that tend to carry real information — schedule changes, new expectations, the things buried in a throwaway sentence — because you built in checkpoints where those are most likely to land. Try it once this week and notice, afterward, whether you leave with fewer surprises than usual about something "everyone already knew."

## INTEGRATION v06
---
---
Carry this into your next IEP or student-support meeting: before responding to the first thing a parent or team member says, resist reaching for the prepared language you already know cold — the goal-and-objective phrasing you've used a hundred times. Instead, repeat back what they actually just said, in different words of your own, even something as plain as "so mornings have been rough." This one sentence, built fresh rather than pulled from memory, is a small forcing function that requires you to have actually heard them rather than merely waited for your cue to speak. You can still get to the accurate paperwork language afterward — nothing about the form suffers. What changes is that the parent experiences being heard before being processed, which tends to matter more for whether the plan actually gets followed at home than any single line of the goals themselves. Try this with just the first exchange of your next meeting, not the whole hour. Notice what you learn in that one repeated-back sentence that you might have otherwise missed while reaching for your usual script.

## INTEGRATION v07
---
---
Tomorrow, at curriculum night or your next high-pressure room full of parents, notice if you're bracing for one specific difficult conversation before it's even started. If you catch that bracing at the start of a new conversation with a different parent, pause for one breath and silently ask yourself, "is this the one I'm dreading, or is this someone new?" Most of the time it will be someone new, with an ordinary, answerable question, and naming that plainly should let you actually meet them instead of the rehearsed defense you were still carrying from a conversation that hasn't happened yet. If the difficult conversation does arrive, you'll be meeting it fresh rather than pre-fought, which tends to go better than the version you rehearsed anyway, because real people rarely say the exact sentence you built your defense against. Try this once, at the start of each new conversation for one event. You won't eliminate the bracing entirely — it's a reasonable, earned instinct after enough hard conversations — but you can catch it faster each time, and catching it is the actual skill, not preventing it from ever showing up.

## INTEGRATION v08
---
---
Carry this into the next unfamiliar classroom you find yourself in — a sub assignment, a room you're covering, even a colleague's room during a schedule swap. Before you take attendance or start the lesson, give yourself ten real seconds to actually take in the room: the posters, the seating arrangement, whatever's written on the whiteboard from the class before. This isn't wasted time; it's the difference between running your day on autopilot in a space that isn't yours and actually arriving in it. When you notice, later in the period, that your mind has wandered back to whatever room you left behind — your actual classroom, an earlier conversation — use the physical details you noticed in those first ten seconds as your anchor back: look again at the same poster, the same seating chart. Try this the next time you're in someone else's room or an unfamiliar one. Notice, at the end of the period, whether you can describe one specific thing about a specific kid you taught — a real detail, not a generic impression. If you can, that's the ten seconds working.

## INTEGRATION v09
---
---
Tomorrow, during your prep period, notice the first few minutes honestly: are you actually planning, or are you replaying the class that just ended, the comment from the workroom, the paragraph from last year's evaluation you've apparently memorized. If it's the latter, don't fight it by trying to force focus through sheer willpower, which rarely works against a loop that's already running. Instead, say one plain sentence to yourself, out loud if you can, naming exactly where and when you actually are: "I'm in my classroom, it's Thursday, that meeting isn't today." This sounds almost too simple to matter, but the flat, factual naming interrupts a loop in a way that willpower alone doesn't, because it gives your mind a real, current room to return to instead of just an order to stop thinking about the other one. You don't need to finish the whole lesson plan in one sitting after this — even one slide, one concrete task completed, is more than the loop was giving you. Try the sentence once tomorrow, the moment you notice you've been sitting with an unopened document for more than a few minutes.

## INTEGRATION v10
---
---
Carry this into your next stretch of report card comments or IEP progress notes: before writing about a specific student, glance at their actual, most recent work or data — not your general impression of them, which is often shaped by whatever else is on your mind that day, but the real thing in front of you. If you notice a comment forming that sounds like it could apply to any student, that's usually a sign your attention drifted to something else while typing — an argument, an email, a worry — rather than to the student you're supposedly describing. When you catch that, don't just push through to finish faster; pause, look again at their actual work, and rewrite the sentence to match what's really there this term. This will slow you down slightly on some comments and speed you up on others, once you're not accidentally repeating vague language on autopilot. Try it for just five comments tomorrow night and notice whether the sentences feel more specific to you by the end of those five than they did at comment one.

## INTEGRATION v12
---
---
Carry this into tomorrow's copier line, mailbox stop, or any of the small transitional minutes in your day that usually fill up with unresolved worry — the pending grade dispute, the unanswered email, the comment that's still bothering you. When you notice your mind pulling toward one of these mid-transition, instead of pushing the thought away or spiraling further into it, try naming it plainly — "this is the grade dispute again" — and making yourself a specific, real appointment: "I'll actually sit with this at 3:15, with a pen, for five minutes." Then let your attention come back to the copier, the hallway, the walk you're actually taking. This does two things at once: it frees you from carrying the loop in fragments all day, and it gives the worry an actual place to land later, which is usually all it was asking for in the first place. Try it once tomorrow with whatever's currently looping. At 3:15, or whenever you set the appointment, notice whether the thing you set down actually needs the full five minutes, or whether it shrank simply by being given a real time to be thought about instead of being carried everywhere in pieces.

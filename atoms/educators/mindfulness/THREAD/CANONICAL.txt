## THREAD v01
---
---
Renata starts keeping a small tally on a sticky note behind her desk — not of tasks, but of returns, a tick mark each time she catches her mind mid-drift and brings it back to the room. By the third week the tally isn't the point anymore; she stops needing to count because the return has gotten fast enough to happen almost without her noticing it happen. She still keeps the sticky note, though, for the days it isn't fast at all.

## THREAD v02
---
---
Marcus's team starts noticing when he tips his head toward the ceiling now, and instead of ignoring it, someone will just say his name once, gently, the way you'd catch a kid whose attention slipped mid-lesson. He's stopped being embarrassed by it. It's become a small, ordinary signal in the room, the same as any other classroom cue — attention drifted, attention's being called back.

## THREAD v03
---
---
Priya's sticky note that says "here" outlives the conference season it was made for. By spring it's faded and half peeled at one corner, and she almost throws it away during a desk cleanout, then doesn't. She realizes she doesn't need to read it anymore to know what it means. She keeps it anyway, the way you keep the first year's roster you don't need but can't quite discard.

## THREAD v04
---
---
Ordell's mentor asks him, months later, whether the loop still shows up during his prep period. He says yes, actually, almost every week — but it used to cost him the whole period, and now it costs him about ninety seconds before the sentence brings him back. She tells him that's not nothing. He believes her, mostly.

## THREAD v05
---
---
Denise starts noticing, without trying to, that she can describe specific kids from recess duty now — a shoelace, a scrape, a shadow — details she wouldn't have had language for a year earlier when the whole thirty minutes belonged to the argument in her head. She mentions this once to a colleague, almost as an aside, and the colleague asks what changed. Denise isn't sure she can fully explain the wrist, the shoelace, the return. She just knows the yard looks different to her now.

## THREAD v06
---
---
Felix's doorframe habit gets noticed eventually, by a student aide who asks why he touches it every time. He tells her the truth, more or less — that it's a reminder to leave the hallway in the hallway. She starts doing something similar before her own after-school shift at the front office, tapping the doorframe on her way in, and doesn't tell him she picked it up from him.

## THREAD v07
---
---
Angela, a year later, mentors her own first-year teacher and finds herself repeating Ruth's line almost word for word — "the kid in front of you doesn't know about the observer" — before she's fully decided to say it. She realizes she's been carrying it around all year without noticing, the way you carry forward the sentence that actually changed something for you.

## THREAD v08
---
---
Grant's trumpets never do find out exactly what he was somewhere else about that day. But the phrase "give me one second, I'm somewhere else" becomes something the section says to each other now, half-joking, whenever someone comes in on the wrong measure — a shorthand for the return that used to take him an entire rehearsal to make and now takes about four bars.

## THREAD v09
---
---
Constance keeps the TV off most nights now, not out of discipline but because she noticed, once, what fifty-five focused minutes felt like compared to ninety scattered ones, and the difference was reason enough on its own. She still turns it on some nights. She just knows now which nights those are, and which ones aren't.

## THREAD v10
---
---
Deshawn's hallway catch — the kid who hadn't eaten since yesterday — turns into a standing arrangement with the front office, quietly, without much fanfare. He still thinks about it sometimes on duty, how close he came to missing it scrolling, and it's become its own kind of anchor: proof of what the five minutes are actually for.

## THREAD v11
---
---
Naomi tells the story of the parent who never showed up at curriculum night for years afterward, half as a cautionary tale and half as a private joke on herself — ninety minutes bracing for a ghost. New teachers on her team start using her phrase, "pre-fighting someone who isn't here yet," without always remembering where they picked it up.

## THREAD v12
---
---
Tomas's AP never mentions the laptop moment again, but he finds himself repeating her sentence to himself some Thursdays, unprompted — "that class is over, this one's still open" — long after she said it to him just once. It becomes less her sentence and more simply his, the way a good cue does once it's been used enough times to stop needing the person who gave it to you.

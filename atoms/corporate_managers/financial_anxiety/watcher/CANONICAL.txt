David monitors the stock market twelve times a day. His retirement account. His college savings. His investment portfolio. Every tick up brings temporary relief. Every tick down brings panic. His chest is a market chart — rising and falling with numbers on a screen. The watcher has turned his financial life into a surveillance operation. Then his wife asks: "When was the last time you checked our actual budget?" He cannot remember. He monitors the market daily but has not reviewed their monthly spending in six months. The frame cracks. The watcher is monitoring the wrong thing. The market fluctuations are noise. Their budget is the signal. He has been vigilant about volatility and blind to the fundamentals. His shoulders drop. He closes the trading app and opens his budget spreadsheet. The numbers are manageable. The watcher was creating anxiety about things he cannot control while ignoring things he can.

David's company announces layoffs and the watcher goes into overdrive. He monitors every leadership meeting, every HR movement, every organizational chart change. He tracks who is being called into offices. He counts empty desks. His body is on full financial threat alert — chest locked, hands cold, jaw welded. He spends forty hours over two weeks on surveillance instead of on his actual work. Then the layoff list is published and he is not on it. The watcher spent forty hours monitoring a threat that did not materialize — forty hours he did not spend on work that would have made his position stronger. The frame shatters. The watcher did not protect his financial security. It endangered it. If he had spent those forty hours on visible, quality work instead of on paranoid monitoring, his position would be even more secure. The watcher told him surveillance was safety. Surveillance was sabotage.

David checks the market once per week instead of twelve times per day. His chest tightens on the days between checks — the watcher insists something is happening that he is missing. He lets it insist. He reviews his budget monthly. He adjusts quarterly. He makes decisions from data, not from surveillance. His hands are warm on the spreadsheet. He is a person who manages his finances with intention instead of monitoring them with anxiety. The watcher still pulls toward the trading app. He does not open it. The weekly check gives him everything he needs. The daily monitoring gave him nothing except anxiety. He chose information over surveillance. The distinction saves him hours of stress and produces better financial decisions.

David retires and the financial watcher tries to follow him. It wants to monitor spending, track market movements, calculate runway on a daily basis. David sets up automatic reviews — monthly spending checks, quarterly portfolio reviews. The watcher protests the intervals. David maintains them. He is a retired person who manages his finances with systems instead of with anxiety. His chest settles into a rhythm. The watcher fires between reviews and David lets it fire. He trusts the system. The system works. His financial life is stable not because he monitors it constantly but because he built structures that hold without surveillance. His hands are warm in his garden. The watcher runs in the background. David is in the foreground, living the retirement the watcher was supposed to protect and instead almost consumed.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/financial_anxiety/watcher/mechanism_proof/micro/v02.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
David traces the watcher's financial behavior. It monitors twelve times a day not because the information changes meaningfully between checks but because the watcher needs the reassurance of seeing the numbers. The reassurance lasts approximately ninety seconds before the watcher demands another check. His chest is tight. The pattern persists because the watcher is seeking certainty and financial markets do not provide certainty. Every check briefly satisfies the certainty need and then the uncertainty returns and the watcher checks again. He is in a reassurance loop that cannot resolve because the thing he is seeking — financial certainty — does not exist. His hands grip his phone. No amount of checking will produce certainty. The watcher will check forever.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v03

COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization

David calculates the time he has spent monitoring his finances. Twelve checks per day times three hundred and sixty-five days times ten years. Forty-three thousand checks. Each one lasting two minutes. Fourteen hundred hours. Fifty-eight full days of his life spent staring at a number on a screen. His chest is crushed. The watcher consumed fifty-eight days of his life and produced zero financial improvement. The same investment strategy would have performed identically with twelve checks per year instead of twelve per day. He spent fifty-eight days achieving nothing. His eyes sting. The pattern persists because each individual check feels brief and harmless. Two minutes. But fifty-eight days worth of two minutes is a number the watcher never wanted him to calculate. His body knows the cost now. Fifty-eight days he could have spent with his family, on his health, on his work, on anything except staring at a number that changed by fractions of a percent. The watcher stole fifty-eight days and called it prudence.

## TURNING_POINT v06
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/financial_anxiety/spiral/turning_point/micro/v06.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: opportunity
COST_INTENSITY: 4
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Sarah spirals about a tax bill. The bill is manageable but the spiral makes it catastrophic: if this bill is this high then next year's will be higher and if next year's is higher then she will need to adjust her budget and if she adjusts her budget then she cannot save as much and if she cannot save then retirement pushes back and if retirement pushes back then she works until she dies. Her chest is locked. She wrote the last eight words in her head and believed them. Then she catches the architecture. The spiral went from a tax bill to working until death in six logical-sounding steps that are not logical at all. Each step amplifies the previous. None accounts for the dozens of variables that intervene between a tax bill and retirement timing. The frame cracks. The spiral does not model financial reality. It models financial fear. Fear has no moderating variables. Reality has hundreds.
---

## TURNING_POINT v07
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/financial_anxiety/spiral/turning_point/micro/v07.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: opportunity
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Sarah spirals about finances for three days without sleeping and ends up in the emergency room with chest pains. The spiral ran the financial catastrophe program so hard that her body could not distinguish between imagined financial ruin and actual cardiac arrest. She lies on the ER bed while a doctor tells her the heart is fine. Her finances are fine too — she checked before the spiral started. The spiral took "fine" and converted it to "fatal." The frame shatters under fluorescent lights. She is in a hospital because she spiraled about money she has. The spiral's version of her financial life has no relationship to her actual financial life. She spent three days and an ER visit on a fiction her brain generated. Her chest is raw from the panic. Her finances are untouched. The cost of the spiral is now literally medical — a bill she will receive for a hospital visit caused by imaginary poverty. The irony is bitter and clarifying.
---

## EMBODIMENT v05
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/financial_anxiety/spiral/embodiment/micro/v05.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 4
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 4
IDENTITY_STAGE: self_claim
---
Sarah feels the financial spiral begin — the tax bill, and then — and she stops it with paper. She writes the actual number. She writes her actual income. She writes the actual timeline. On paper, the spiral cannot add phantom steps. The number is the number. The income is the income. The math works or it does not. Her hands are steady writing. The spiral wants to add fear to the math. Paper does not accept fear as a variable. She does the math and the math shows a manageable situation. She puts the paper in a drawer and closes it. The spiral protests. She has already closed the drawer. Her chest is tight but the drawer is closed. She is a person who does financial math on paper instead of in her head because her head adds catastrophe to every calculation.
---

## EMBODIMENT v06
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/financial_anxiety/spiral/embodiment/micro/v06.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 4
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: self_claim
---
Sarah's income drops by forty percent in the career transition. The spiral lunges with everything it has — you will run out, you will lose the house, you will destroy your family. She feels every loop fire. Her chest is tight. She does not follow. She opens the spreadsheet. She recalculates at the new income. The numbers are tight. Not impossible. Tight. She reduces spending. She pauses retirement contributions temporarily. She builds a twelve-month bridge plan. The spiral says twelve months is not enough. The spreadsheet says twelve months is the plan and the plan is workable. She closes the laptop and the spiral runs in the background for three days. She lets it run. She does not reopen the spreadsheet to feed it. By the fourth day, the spiral quiets because there is nothing new to process. The math is done. The plan is set. She is a person who survives financial transitions by doing math instead of spiraling. The math is harder. The math is also accurate.
---

## MECHANISM_PROOF v05
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/financial_anxiety/spiral/mechanism_proof/micro/v05.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 4
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Sarah traces the spiral's financial architecture. Every financial fact enters the spiral and exits as a catastrophe. A raise becomes: what if the tax bracket increase means I actually take home less? A bonus becomes: what if they gave me this to soften a layoff? A good month becomes: what if next month is bad and I got used to having more? Her chest is tight. The spiral converts positive financial news into anxiety as efficiently as it converts negative news. The pattern persists because the spiral does not distinguish between good and bad financial data. All data becomes fuel. All fuel becomes fear. She cannot receive good financial news without the spiral weaponizing it. Her financial life is anxiety regardless of the numbers. The numbers are irrelevant. The spiral is the constant.
---

## MECHANISM_PROOF v06
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/financial_anxiety/spiral/mechanism_proof/micro/v06.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Sarah calculates the cost of the spiral itself. Hours lost to financial worry instead of work: approximately ten per week. Fifty-two weeks. Five hundred twenty hours per year. At her billing rate, that is over fifty thousand dollars of lost productivity. The spiral about losing money has cost her fifty thousand dollars. Her chest is crushed. The spiral claims to protect her finances by keeping her vigilant. The spiral costs more than any actual financial threat she has faced. She has been paying fifty thousand dollars a year for the privilege of being afraid. The pattern persists because the spiral never submits an invoice. The cost is invisible — distributed across hours, across days, across years of reduced productivity and missed opportunities. But the number is real. The spiral is the most expensive thing in her financial life and she has been treating it as free.
---

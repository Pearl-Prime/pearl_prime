Kevin mourns the financial trajectory he imagined. He was supposed to be VP by forty-five with the compensation package that comes with it. He is forty-seven and still a director. The gap between his imagined financial life and his actual financial life is a specific grief. His chest aches. His hands grip the steering wheel driving home to a house that is comfortable but not the house he planned. Then his son meets him at the door and Kevin feels the grief shift. He traded the VP trajectory for coaching his son's baseball team, for dinners at home, for a marriage that survived. The financial grief is real. The life he built in the gap is also real. The frame cracks. He is grieving a future he chose not to have. The choice was his. The grief can coexist with the recognition that the choice was right. His shoulders drop. He walks inside.

## TURNING_POINT v03
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/financial_anxiety/grief/turning_point/micro/v03.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: opportunity
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Kevin's company folds. His equity — four years of below-market salary in exchange for ownership stake — is worthless. The number on the spreadsheet goes from six figures to zero. His chest is hollow. His hands are numb. He grieves the money the way you grieve a person — it represented possibility, freedom, choice. It is gone. The grief is financial and existential. Then something cracks open underneath the grief. He is still alive. His skills still exist. His network still holds. The money was a story about what the future would look like and the story died but the future did not. His body shakes. The frame shatters: he was grieving the future he imagined, not the future that will actually happen. The actual future is unknown. It might be worse. It might be different. The grief is about the death of a plan. Plans die. People continue. He sits with the zero on the spreadsheet and begins to imagine a different number, built differently, from a different plan.

Kevin builds a new financial plan. Not the old plan — the VP-by-forty-five plan is dead. The new plan accounts for who he actually is: a director who coaches baseball and eats dinner at home. The numbers are different. Retirement is later. The house is smaller. The vacations are closer to home. His chest aches as he writes the new numbers. Each one is a small grief — the difference between what he planned and what he has. He writes them anyway. His hands are steady. He is a person who grieves financial plans and builds new ones. The grief does not prevent the building. It accompanies it. He shows the plan to his wife. She says: "This looks like our life." It does. The grief is about a life that was never theirs. This plan is.

MECHANISM_DEPTH: 4
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: self_claim
---
Kevin retires. Not at the age he planned. Not with the nest egg he imagined. Five years later and two hundred thousand dollars less than the plan that died. He sits in his kitchen on his first morning of retirement and the grief is present — for the plan, for the money, for the years. His chest aches. Then his son calls. His grandson is on the line, chattering about a drawing. Kevin listens and the warmth in his chest is real. The financial grief coexists with the life he built inside the revised plan. He has enough. Not abundant. Enough. The word used to feel like failure. Now it feels like the precise description of a life that included baseball games and dinners and a grandson who calls to talk about drawings. His hands are warm on the phone. The grief is real. The life is real. Both.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/financial_anxiety/grief/mechanism_proof/micro/v02.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Kevin notices the grief activates at every financial milestone he misses. His peer buys a lake house. Kevin grieves the lake house he will never buy. His peer retires early. Kevin grieves the early retirement he will not have. Each milestone is a small funeral for a financial life he did not live. His chest aches. The pattern persists because the grief is not about the money. It is about the life the money would have enabled. Every unattained financial goal represents an unlived experience. He is not grieving dollars. He is grieving a version of his life that needed those dollars. His hands are cold. The financial grief is an identity grief in disguise.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v03
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/financial_anxiety/grief/mechanism_proof/micro/v03.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Kevin calculates what the four years of below-market salary cost him in lifetime earnings, compound interest, and retirement timing. The number is staggering. The grief is proportional. His chest is hollow. He sat at a startup desk earning less for four years because the equity was supposed to compensate. The equity went to zero. The salary gap compounded. He will work five additional years because of a bet that lost. His eyes sting. The grief is not just about the money. It is about the time — five years he will spend working that he could have spent living. The pattern persists because the financial grief is also a grief about mortality. Every dollar lost is time lost. Time he cannot recover. The grief is ultimately about the finite nature of a human life making financial mistakes that have permanent temporal consequences. He is grieving years, not dollars. And years, unlike dollars, cannot be earned back.

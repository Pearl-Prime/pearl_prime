### Phase 2: Destabilization (Stories v06–v10)
*   **Mechanism:** The coping strategy (working harder/improving KPIs) fails to resolve the anxiety. The anxiety migrates from work contexts into personal life ("daughter asked why his hands shook at dinner").
*   **Leadership Trap:** The "internal cost" rises as the boundary between professional persona and private self erodes. The "2am math" becomes a constant companion, not just a work task.
*   **Key Insight:** v10 is pivotal. The "professional alias" of being "thorough/prepared" collapses under the weight of embodied truth. This reflects your interest in burnout as an *embodied* challenge—it can no longer be hidden in the boardroom; it leaks into the kitchen.

### Phase 3: Experimentation (Stories v11–v15)
*   **Mechanism:** Awareness of the pattern exists, but the response is still reactive. The "scarcity alarm" is recognized as a separate entity ("loaded the next scenario"), but the leader feels powerless to stop it.
*   **Leadership Trap:** The "opportunity cost" becomes clear. Energy spent managing the anxiety is energy stolen from presence, creativity, and rest. The "mask" is acknowledged as a mask (v14), but it remains necessary for survival.
*   **Key Insight:** v13 highlights the *systemic* nature of the issue—the anxiety operates on a different clock than the organization. This mirrors your view of burnout as a systemic issue; the leader is trying to apply linear solutions (deadlines, deliverables) to a non-linear, cyclical emotional pattern.

### Phase 4: Self-Claim (Stories v16–v20)
*   **Mechanism:** The leader stops fighting the anxiety and starts *witnessing* it. The pause in v16 ("two seconds held more truth than the entire stakeholders") is an act of reclaiming agency.
*   **Leadership Shift:** The cost shifts to "identity." The leader must decide who they are outside of the performance. The anxiety is no longer a secret shame but a "patient and enormous" presence that must be sat with (v19).
*   **Key Insight:** v20 reframes the narrative from "dramatic failure" to "cumulative reality." This aligns with your model of recovery as a gradual, embodied experience. The leader isn’t "fixing" the anxiety; they are integrating the truth of it. The "quiet evening where he finally sat with all of it and didn't look away" is the beginning of true recovery—not through wellness hacks, but through radical acceptance and boundary-setting.

### Connection to Your Leadership Model
These stories validate your shift toward **activating compassion only when genuinely needed**. 3.  **Systemic Awareness:** Understanding that the anxiety is cumulative and systemic (v20) allows the leader to address root causes rather than just symptoms.

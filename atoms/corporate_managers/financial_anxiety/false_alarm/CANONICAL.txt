Sarah checks her bank account and the alarm fires. The balance is fine. Her budget is covered. Her emergency fund holds six months. The alarm does not care about the numbers. It fires anyway — what if a layoff happens tomorrow, what if the market crashes, what if the emergency fund is not enough. Her chest seizes. Her hands are cold on the phone screen. Then she does the math. On paper. Actual math. Her expenses against her income against her savings. The numbers are fine. The alarm is lying. Her frame cracks. The alarm is not responding to financial reality. It is responding to the possibility of financial ruin, and possibility is infinite. No amount of savings will satisfy a system that runs on what-if. Her jaw loosens. She closes the banking app. The numbers are the truth. The alarm is the fiction.

Sarah gets the layoff notice. Real financial threat. Real loss of income. The alarm fires and for the first time it matches reality. But something is different — the alarm is not louder than it was during the false alarms. It is the same volume. The same intensity for a parking lot email and a layoff notice. Sarah sits with this recognition and the frame shatters. She has been living at layoff-level alarm for years over parking lot-level events. The alarm stole the significance of this moment by treating every moment as equally catastrophic. Now that the catastrophe is real, she has no higher gear. The alarm already used it all. Her chest is tight. But she is oddly calm underneath the alarm because she has practice at this level of activation. The false alarms accidentally trained her for the real one. She picks up the phone and starts making calls. The alarm runs. She works alongside it. She has been doing this for years. She just never knew she was training.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02
---

BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 4
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Sarah traces the false alarm pattern. Every unexpected expense — a car repair, a medical bill, a home maintenance issue — triggers the same catastrophic response: we are going to run out of money. She has never run out of money. Not once. But the alarm treats every unplanned cost as proof of imminent financial ruin. Her chest is tight. The pattern persists because the alarm does not process expenses against the budget. It processes expenses against the fear. The fear has no denominator. The fear says: this cost plus another cost plus another cost equals disaster. The math has no actual numbers. Just fear multiplied by possibility. Her hands are cold. She has enough money and an alarm that insists she does not and the alarm is louder than the bank statement.

Sarah realizes the financial alarm has cost her more money than any actual emergency. The hours spent worrying instead of working. The investment decisions made from panic instead of strategy. The money left in low-yield savings because the alarm was too afraid of the market. The safe choices that were actually expensive choices. Her chest is crushed. She sees the full architecture: the alarm claims to protect her financial security but actually undermines it. She is financially poorer because of the alarm. Every fear-based decision was suboptimal. Every worry-hour was an unproductive hour. The alarm did not save her from financial ruin. It slowly, imperceptibly, made her financial life worse by substituting fear for strategy. Her hands tremble. The pattern persists because the alarm feels like financial responsibility. It is not. It is financial sabotage wearing the costume of prudence.

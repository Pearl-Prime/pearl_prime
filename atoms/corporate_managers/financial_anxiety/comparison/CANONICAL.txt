David reviews the compensation data and his peer at the same level makes thirty percent more. His chest freezes. His hands grip the report. The financial comparison fires: he is underpaid, undervalued, falling behind. His mortgage, his children's tuition, his retirement — all measured against someone else's salary. Then he looks at the full picture. His peer works eighty-hour weeks. His peer travels constantly. His peer missed his daughter's last three birthdays. The compensation gap reflects a lifestyle gap David did not choose. His frame cracks. The financial comparison was isolating one variable — salary — and ignoring every other dimension of the trade. His peer earns more and lives less. David earns less and is home for dinner. The anxiety about the financial gap dissolves when the gap includes the full cost. His shoulders drop. He is not underpaid. He is differently compensated.

COST_INTENSITY: 3

---
David loses his job. The severance is decent but the financial comparison fires at maximum — his former peers still have salaries, still have insurance, still have stability. He has a countdown clock. His chest is crushed. His hands are shaking. He sits at his kitchen table with a calculator and the panic is nuclear. Then he does something the comparison never let him do: he calculates his actual situation. Not relative to others. Actual. Savings. Expenses. Runway. He has eight months. Eight months is not comfortable but it is not catastrophic. The comparison said he was financially ruined because others still had jobs. The math says he has eight months to find work. The frame shatters. The comparison was measuring his anxiety, not his reality. His reality is tight but survivable. The comparison was not. He puts down the calculator. His hands still tremble. But the numbers are clearer than the panic.

COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
David notices the financial comparison activates before his rational mind can intervene. He sees a peer's new car and his stomach drops before he can think. The comparison creates the deficit — the sense of falling behind — and then his rational mind scrambles to explain the deficit. His chest is tight. The pattern persists because the comparison is emotional, not analytical. It does not compare budgets. It compares symbols. The car is a symbol. The house is a symbol. The vacation is a symbol. Each symbol triggers the gap without any actual financial analysis. His hands are cold. The comparison could make him feel financially anxious at any income level because it measures symbols, not security.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v03
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/financial_anxiety/comparison/mechanism_proof/micro/v03.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
David lies awake calculating the lifetime earnings gap between himself and his peer. The numbers are large. The gap represents houses, educations, retirements. His chest is crushed. He sees the full machinery: the comparison converts a salary difference into a life difference. But the calculation is fiction. He does not know his peer's debt, expenses, obligations, or financial decisions. He is comparing his known liabilities against his peer's imagined assets. The pattern persists because the comparison only measures visible income and ignores invisible costs. His peer might earn more and owe more. His peer might earn more and be miserable. The comparison does not care. It measures one variable and constructs an entire narrative of financial inadequacy from it. His eyes burn. He has been losing sleep over a fiction that only measures what it can see.

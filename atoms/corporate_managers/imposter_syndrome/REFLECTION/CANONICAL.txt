

## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I've built a career that looks, from the outside, like it belongs to someone competent. I've been promoted. I've been trusted with increasingly complex responsibilities. I manage people who seem to believe I know what I'm doing.
And yet, the mechanism that operates in me says: they're going to figure out that you don't actually belong here. The promotions were because of luck or timing. The trust is because you've been good at performing competence, not because you actually possess it. Eventually, someone will notice that you're not qualified for what you're doing.
This mechanism is particularly potent in management contexts because the stakes feel higher. You're not just responsible for your own performance. You're responsible for the development, growth, and wellbeing of people who are depending on you. The imposter mechanism says: if they knew how uncertain you actually are, they would lose confidence.
So you perform certainty. You don't show doubt. You solve problems confidently even when you're not certain of the solution. You give feedback authoritatively even when you're not sure you're right. The performance of confidence becomes so habitual that you stop distinguishing it from actual confidence.
The mechanism is named imposter syndrome, but it's really about the split between the external performance and the internal experience. The teaching here is that this split is particularly dangerous in leadership roles because the people depending on you absorb your performance and assume it reflects reality.
What happens if you stop performing confidence and start acknowledging what you actually know and don't know?

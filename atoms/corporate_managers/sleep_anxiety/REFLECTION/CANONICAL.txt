

## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I've noticed a pattern in my nights. I go to bed thinking I'll rest, and the moment I lie down, my mind activates. There are meetings tomorrow, problems that need solving, decisions that need to be made. The mechanism that keeps me strategically engaged during the day keeps running after hours.
The mechanism is named "strategic vigilance," and it operates through the belief that I need to be mentally prepared for whatever the next day brings. If I don't think through the scenarios, if I don't mentally prepare, if I don't stay alert to potential problems, I'll be caught off guard.
For managers, sleep anxiety often comes with a particular flavor: you're responsible for people, for decisions, for outcomes. The vigilance makes sense. If you're not thinking ahead, if you're not considering contingencies, something might fall through the cracks.
But the mechanism doesn't distinguish between the kind of vigilance that's useful and the kind that prevents actual sleep. It just runs continuously, calibrated for crisis management even when there is no crisis.
The teaching here is that management responsibility can become the justification for chronic sleep deprivation. You tell yourself the sleeplessness is necessary, that it's the price of the position, that real leaders don't sleep well because they're always thinking about their responsibilities.
What happens if you acknowledge that your people would actually benefit from you being well-rested more than they benefit from you being perpetually sleep-deprived?

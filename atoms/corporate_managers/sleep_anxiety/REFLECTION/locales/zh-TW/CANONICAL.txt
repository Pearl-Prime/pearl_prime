1.  **The Trigger:** The transition to rest (lying down).
2.  **The Action:** Automatic mental rehearsal and crisis simulation.

Here is a synthesis of these reflections, offered back to you through three distinct lenses, to help you decide where to intervene.

### 1. The Teacher’s Lens: The Efficiency Paradox
**Core Insight:** You are paying a high tax on your cognitive capital.
You believe that night-time analysis increases your preparedness. However, neurobiology suggests the opposite. Sleep is not merely "offline time"; it is the active process of consolidating memory, clearing metabolic waste from the brain, and regulating emotional reactivity.
*   **The Reality:** By refusing to sleep, you are deliberately impairing the very instrument (your brain) you rely on for strategic thinking.
*   **The Question:** If you viewed your brain as a critical piece of organizational infrastructure, would you allow it to run at 60% capacity due to lack of maintenance? You are currently treating your mind like a machine that can be overworked indefinitely without degradation. It cannot. The "vigilance" you feel is actually **fatigue masquerading as alertness**.

### 2. The Companion’s Lens: The Fear of Powerlessness
**Core Insight:** Sleep feels like surrender.
You mentioned that the mechanism protects you from feeling powerless. When you are awake, analyzing, and planning, you are *doing*. You are active. When you sleep, you are passive. You are trusting that the world will hold together without your conscious intervention.
*   **The Vulnerability:** For someone whose self-worth is tied to being "needed" and "urgent," rest feels like irrelevance. If you are not solving problems, who are you?
*   **The Shift:** What if rest was not an absence of leadership, but a different *mode* of leadership? Trusting your team, your systems, and your own accumulated wisdom enough to let go for eight hours is a profound act of confidence. It says, "I have built something robust enough to survive my absence." Currently, your insomnia signals a lack of trust in your own structures.

### 3. The Mirror’s Lens: The Inherited Script
**Core Insight:** You are performing a role written by others.
You see the managers before you—the ones who modeled "always-on" leadership. You absorbed the narrative that suffering (sleep deprivation) equals virtue. But look closely at those leaders. Were they truly more effective? Or were they just more visible in their struggle?
*   **The Reflection:** You are 47. You have a mortgage, children, and a career that has survived despite being underpaid. You have proven your competence repeatedly. Yet, you still feel like an imposter. Why? Because the "script" you inherited tells you that if you aren't struggling, you aren't working hard enough.
*   **The Break:** The managers you admired may have been poor models of sustainability. They may have been passing on their own anxiety, not their wisdom. You have the opportunity to break this lineage. You can define a new version of leadership—one that is rested, present, and psychologically safe—not just for your team, but for yourself.

### A Proposed Experiment: "The Handover Ritual"

Since the mechanism activates automatically upon lying down, we need a **conscious interrupt** before that moment. The goal is not to "force" sleep, but to formally close the loop of vigilance.

**Try this for three nights:**

1.  **The Brain Dump (8:00 PM):** Take 10 minutes to write down every open loop, worry, meeting, and decision point for tomorrow. Get it out of your head and onto paper.
2.  **The Strategic Plan (8:15 PM):** Next to each item, write the *very next physical action* required. Not the solution, just the next step. (e.g., "Email 莎拉 re: Q3 budget" instead of "Solve budget crisis").
3.  **The Handover Statement (9:30 PM, right before bed):** Say aloud (or write): *"I have identified the risks. I have planned the first steps. My team is capable. My systems are in place. I am now handing over the watch to my future self, who will be better equipped to handle this after rest. I am off duty."*

**Why this might work for you:**
*   It appeals to your **analytical nature** (you are still "processing" the data).
*   It addresses the **fear of being caught off guard** (you have documented the risks).
*   It reframes sleep as **preparation** (fueling the "future self") rather than abandonment of duty.

**Reflection Question for You:**
If you were to sleep well tonight, what is the specific fear that arises? Is it that you might miss something? Or is it that you might have to face the quiet truth that you are enough, even when you are not *doing*?

## TURNING_POINT v06
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/boundaries/comparison/turning_point/micro/v06.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: opportunity
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Rachel watches her peer leave at five again. Her chest burns. Her peer has boundaries. Rachel has a to-do list that bleeds into every evening. The comparison fires hot — her peer is thriving, rested, sharp in morning meetings while Rachel arrives depleted. She compares their outputs. Her peer delivers the same quality. Less hours. Same results. Rachel's hands grip her desk. The frame cracks: what if working more hours is not dedication but the absence of boundaries? What if her peer leaving at five is not privilege but competence — knowing what matters and letting go of what does not? Rachel's throat unclenches one notch. The comparison stops being about who works harder and becomes about who works differently. Her peer draws a line. Rachel has no line. The comparison is not showing her someone better. It is showing her something she refuses to give herself.
---

## TURNING_POINT v07
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/boundaries/comparison/turning_point/micro/v07.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: opportunity
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Rachel's doctor tells her the chest pain is real. Stress cardiomyopathy. Her body is breaking from the lack of boundaries. She sits in the medical office and thinks about her peer who leaves at five. Her peer is healthy. Her peer's boundaries are not laziness — they are survival. Rachel's absence of boundaries is not heroism — it is self-destruction. Her hands are cold on the examination table. The frame shatters completely. She built her entire identity on being the one who stays late, the one who says yes, the one who gives everything. Her body is now presenting the invoice. The old story — that boundaries are for people who care less — is no longer survivable. Her heart is literally failing from the story. She sits in her car afterward and the comparison takes a different shape entirely. Her peer is not someone to envy. Her peer is someone who figured out what Rachel is only learning now, in a doctor's office, with test results in her hand.
---

## EMBODIMENT v05
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/boundaries/comparison/embodiment/micro/v05.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 4
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: self_claim
---
Rachel leaves at 5:30. Not five — she is not her peer. But 5:30 instead of 8 PM. Her chest tightens as she walks past colleagues still working. The comparison fires one more time: they are still here, you are leaving, you are less committed. She notices the thought. She keeps walking. Her bag is on her shoulder. Her hands are warm around her car keys. She is a manager who does excellent work within bounded hours. The comparison between her schedule and her peer's schedule no longer ranks them. They have different boundaries for different lives. Rachel drives home and cooks dinner and does not open her laptop. Her jaw is loose. She is not performing balance. She is choosing it. The identity shift lives in the act of leaving, not in the thought about leaving.
---

## EMBODIMENT v06
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/boundaries/comparison/embodiment/micro/v06.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 4
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: self_claim
---
The urgent request arrives at 7 PM. Client crisis. Old Rachel would have logged on immediately, cancelled dinner, worked until midnight. New Rachel reads the message. Her chest tightens. She assesses: is this genuinely urgent or is it urgently labeled? It is genuinely urgent. She handles the critical piece — thirty minutes of focused work. Then she stops. She does not rebuild the entire deliverable. She delegates the rest to morning. Her hands tremble as she closes the laptop. The boundary holds even under real pressure. She is not someone who never works late. She is someone who chooses when to work late based on actual need, not on the comparison-driven fear that stopping means falling behind. Her peer never enters the equation. Rachel acts from her own values now. The boundary is hers. Not borrowed. Not compared. Hers.
---

## MECHANISM_PROOF v05
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/boundaries/comparison/mechanism_proof/micro/v05.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Rachel catches herself comparing schedules again. Her peer left at five. Rachel is still here at seven. She opens her peer's deliverables and examines them for proof of inferior quality. There is none. The work is solid. Rachel's hands go cold. She sees the protective logic clearly: she stays late because leaving early would mean she is the kind of person who does not care enough. The comparison with her peer is not about the peer — it is about Rachel's belief that her value requires sacrifice. If someone else can deliver without sacrifice, either they are cheating or Rachel is wasting herself. Both options terrify her. Her chest locks. The pattern persists because boundaries feel like evidence of insufficient commitment, and insufficient commitment threatens her identity as someone who gives everything.
---

## MECHANISM_PROOF v06
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/boundaries/comparison/mechanism_proof/micro/v06.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Rachel sits at her desk at 10 PM on a Friday. The office is empty. Her peer is at a concert. Rachel knows this because she checked. Her chest is hollow. She is not working on anything urgent. She is working because stopping would mean confronting the silence, and the silence would mean confronting the question: who is she without the work? The comparison with her peer illuminates the full architecture. Her peer has a life outside the office. Rachel has arranged her life so that the office IS her life. The boundary her peer draws is not about time management. It is about identity. Her peer knows who she is without the work. Rachel does not. Her hands are numb on the keyboard. The pattern persists because setting a boundary would require answering the question of who she is when she is not producing. And that question is the most terrifying thing she has ever avoided.
---

You manage a team of ten people. You're responsible for their development, their outcomes, their growth. You care about this. You also can't talk to them about your own struggles. Not because you're supposed to be perfect, but because the dynamic would shift. They'd start protecting you. They'd second-guess your leadership. But the version you maintain isn't the whole of who you are. So part of you remains isolated. Not because people are unkind. But because the role requires a kind of solitude. The loneliness isn't dramatic. It's this: the absence of anyone at work who knows how much effort the steadiness requires.
You go home and you're too tired to connect with anyone. The weekend comes and you spend it recovering from the week. The isolation deepens.
This is what leadership costs, in this structure.

The organization creates a structure where the person in the middle—you—has no peer group. Your director is senior. Your team reports to you. There's no one in your organization who's in the same position, managing the same way, facing the same pressures.
So when you're struggling, you manage it alone. You can't talk to your team about it—the relationship structure doesn't allow it. Peer conversations outside work help, but they can't fully touch the thing because the people don't know your actual situation.
The organization has created a position of structural isolation. It's not malicious. It's just how the reporting lines work. But the effect is real. You bear your experience alone.
When you're depressed, the isolation deepens. The work requires engagement you don't have. The people around you seem fine. You start to feel that the problem is you—your capacity, your resilience, your ability to handle what everyone else seems to handle.
But the problem is structural. The role is designed to isolate you.

There's a kind of depression that settles in when you're responsible for others' wellbeing while no one is responsible for yours. The depression becomes normalized. You're managing your team. You're hitting your metrics. You're doing your job. So the exhaustion must just be normal. The flatness must just be what adulthood is.
But it's the structure. The one-way flow of care. The absence of reciprocal relationships in the place where you spend most of your time.

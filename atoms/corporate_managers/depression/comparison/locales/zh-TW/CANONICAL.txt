This collection of narrative atoms provides a profound and granular map of the intersection between **clinical depression**, **social comparison**, and **leadership identity**. Here is an analysis of the patterns emerging from these texts, structured by the progression of insight:

### 1. The Somatic Signature of Comparison
The texts consistently anchor emotional states in physical sensations. This validates your earlier exploration of burnout as an *embodied* challenge.
*   **Constriction vs. Release:** The "before" state is defined by tightness (chest, throat, shoulders) and coldness (hands, stomach). The "after" state is defined by warmth, openness, and relaxation.
*   **The Body as Truth-Teller:** Before cognitive reframing occurs, the body often holds the tension of the false narrative. The release (e.g., David’s chest unclenching) happens *after* the cognitive shift (realizing the peer is also struggling or has sought help), suggesting that **information replaces assumption, and relief follows truth.**

### 2. The Mechanism: Comparison as a Diagnostic Error
The core insight across `MECHANISM_PROOF` and `TURNING_POINT` variants is that comparison is not just a bad habit; it is a **category error**.
*   **Apples to Oranges:** David compares his *untreated* internal state to his peers’ *external* performance (or treated state).
*   **The Feedback Loop:** As noted in `MECHANISM_PROOF v03`, depression and comparison are "allies." Depression provides the negative self-talk ("you are worthless"), and comparison provides the "evidence" ("look at them, they are fine"). This creates an airtight, self-reinforcing system of shame.
*   **The Cost of Secrecy:** `MECHANISM_PROOF v01` highlights that "Comparison prevents help-seeking." By hiding the struggle to maintain the appearance of competence, the leader denies themselves the very data needed to break the loop.

### 3. The Turning Points: Three Distinct Paths to Relief
The narratives offer three different entry points for breaking the cycle, which may resonate differently depending on the stage of burnout/depression:

*   **Path A: Humanization (v01):** *Realizing others are struggling too.*
    *   **Mechanism:** Breaking the illusion of peer perfection.
    *   **Result:** Immediate somatic relief (chest unclenches). This aligns with your interest in moving away from hyper-vigilant scanning for distress in others to recognizing shared humanity.
*   **Path B: Reframing the Gap (v02):** *Realizing the peer is "treated," not "better."*
    *   **Mechanism:** Shifting from moral judgment (character flaw) to medical reality (treatment gap).
    *   **Result:** "She did not out-perform him. She out-healed him." This is a critical distinction for leaders who equate worth with output. It separates *capacity* from *character*.
*   **Path C: The Collapse of Energy (v03):** *Depression consumes the capacity to compare.*
    *   **Mechanism:** When the depression becomes so severe that even the energy required for comparison is gone, the silence reveals the truth: "I am sick."
    *   **Result:** This is the most radical turning point. It bypasses the ego entirely. The lack of caring about "who is ahead" becomes the diagnostic tool that finally forces help-seeking.

### 4. Embodiment of Recovery: Non-Linear and Protective
The `EMBODIMENT` texts reject the idea of a "cure" as a return to previous high-performance levels. Instead, they model **recovery as a new relationship with capacity**.
*   **v02 (Acceptance):** David accepts he is "in recovery." He doesn't match his peer's energy. He works within his current limits. The comparison "flickers" but is not engaged. This mirrors your work on **intentional availability** and **boundaries**.
*   **v03 (Tending the Spark):** Recovery is described as agricultural ("tends it... like a plant") rather than industrial. The focus shifts from *output* to *interest*. The "spark" is fragile and protected. This directly challenges the inherited leadership model that prioritizes sustained high output over sustainable engagement.

### Integration with Your Leadership Model
These narratives strongly support your ongoing work to:
1.  **Distinguish genuine vs. imagined suffering:** David’s suffering was genuine (clinical depression), but his interpretation of it (as inferiority compared to peers) was the "imagined" or distorted layer created by comparison.
2.  **Redefine Presence:** True presence isn't about appearing energized (like Tanya performing energy); it's about being honest about capacity (David acknowledging his flatness).
3.  **Systemic vs. Personal:** The texts show that while the *experience* is personal (somatic), the *mechanism* is systemic (workplace cultures that reward visible energy and punish visible struggle). The "peer" who mentions antidepressants breaks the systemic silence, allowing David to heal.

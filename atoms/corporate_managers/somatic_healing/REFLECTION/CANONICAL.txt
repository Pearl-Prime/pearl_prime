

## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I'm beginning to notice that my body carries information that my mind has moved past. There's a chronic tension in my shoulders and jaw. There's a way my chest feels constricted whenever I'm in high-stakes meetings. There's a shallow breathing pattern that activated years ago and never quite released.
The mechanism is what I'll call "postural suppression"—the way the body learns to compress itself in response to the demands of professional performance. You learn to sit upright in ways that project authority but restrict your breathing. You learn to control your facial expressions in ways that limit the micro-movements that would normally relieve tension. You learn to hold yourself in ways that look professional but cost physical ease.
For managers, this is particularly pronounced because the performance extends into how you hold your body. You're not just managing your thoughts and speech. You're managing your physical presence, and that physical management becomes so habitual that it eventually feels like your actual body.
The mechanism operates through the belief that authentic physical ease is unprofessional. So you learn to compress yourself. And after years of this compression, your body has adapted. It holds the suppression even when you're supposed to be relaxed.
The teaching here is that professional success can come at the cost of embodied ease. You can gain authority while losing the ability to fully inhabit your own body.
What happens if you start listening to what your body has been trying to tell you all these years?

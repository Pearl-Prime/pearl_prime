

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v01
---
mode: SECULAR_BRIDGE
doctrine_source: somatic
mechanism: false_alarm
weight: standard
carry_line: "Your stress response is not designed to run continuously through six months of back-to-back quarters. The reset window has been deleted from your calendar."
---
Here is the mechanism. Your stress response is not designed to run continuously. It is designed to fire, do its job, and reset. The reset is supposed to happen in the gap between projects, on the Friday afternoon when nothing is on the calendar, on the weekend that does not carry slides into Sunday night. The reset window is what your nervous system was built around. When the gap is removed, when one quarterly close runs into the next planning cycle, when every Friday holds a skip-level, when Sunday becomes prep for Monday's leadership team, what was a useful alarm becomes a stuck signal. Your body keeps producing the chemistry it would produce in front of a real threat, only now the threat is a status email from your VP, then another status email, then a board prep deck, then another quarter. The alarm system does not care that none of these are emergencies. It only knows you have not let it come down. This is what burnout looks like in a manager's body before it has a name. You are still hitting your numbers. You are still showing up to the skip-level. But the recovery curve that used to bring you back by Sunday night does not bring you back anymore. A stuck alarm does not need more effort. It needs the conditions that allow a reset, which is closer to nothing than to more.

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v02
---
mode: SECULAR_BRIDGE
doctrine_source: somatic
mechanism: spiral
weight: standard
carry_line: "The depletion-prediction loop runs your calendar forward and arrives at next Thursday already empty. Borrowed fatigue is indistinguishable from real fatigue."
---
Here is the mechanism. When a manager's nervous system is depleted, the prediction loop changes shape. Instead of forecasting outcomes, it starts forecasting your capacity to meet them. You open the calendar on Sunday night and the loop runs: standup Monday, three 1:1s Tuesday, the executive review Wednesday, the skip-levels Thursday, the QBR Friday, and the loop arrives at next Friday already exhausted. You have not done any of it yet. The exhaustion is borrowed from a week you have not lived. What makes this loop dangerous is that the borrowed fatigue is indistinguishable from real fatigue once it lands in your body. You feel tired now, on Sunday night, for meetings you have not run yet. The tiredness becomes evidence that you cannot handle next week, which feeds the loop, which produces more borrowed fatigue. The exit is not pep talk. The exit is recognizing the loop is running, and noticing that you are not actually doing Wednesday's executive review on Sunday night. You are doing Sunday night. The work that arrives Monday morning will get its own response when it arrives, with whatever capacity you have when you arrive with it.

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v03
---
mode: SECULAR_BRIDGE
doctrine_source: somatic
mechanism: comparison
weight: standard
carry_line: "The peer who looks energized in the leadership meeting is not the full picture. The comparison engine takes their composure as data about your capacity."
---
Here is the mechanism. The comparison engine in you was tuned for a small group whose internal states you could read. It tracks relative position by reading visible signals, posture, energy, output. It did not evolve for a leadership team where five other directors walk into the same Monday meeting performing composure. You see them and the engine runs a fast subtraction. They look energized. You feel hollow. Therefore something is wrong with you specifically. What the engine cannot see is what is happening underneath their composure. You do not see the peer who threw up before the executive review. You do not see the director who has not slept through the night in three months. You do not see the colleague who is performing okay-ness with the same tightness in their chest you have in yours. You see surfaces, and the engine treats surfaces as full pictures. The comparison takes their visible output as evidence and your invisible interior as a personal verdict. Both readings are wrong. Recognizing the asymmetry does not change the meeting. It changes what you carry out of the meeting about yourself.

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v04
---
mode: SECULAR_BRIDGE
doctrine_source: somatic
mechanism: overwhelm
weight: standard
carry_line: "The context-switch tax across your direct reports, your skip, your peers, and your VP is the part of your day the company is not pricing in."
---
Here is the mechanism. Every time your attention moves from one person to another, there is a tax. The brain does not switch cleanly between a one-on-one with a struggling direct report and a budget review with your VP. It carries residue from the last context into the next one and uses cycles to drop the residue and load the new shape. In a frontline job, the tax is invisible. In a manager's day, eight one-on-ones, two cross-functional syncs, a board prep, three Slack threads in your skip-level's channel, a calendar pinging with the next meeting in five minutes, the tax compounds across dozens of switches. By 4 pm you have done less actual thinking than the calendar suggests, and you are more tired than the work explains, because most of the day went to the switching itself. This is not poor focus. It is the predictable cost of an attention architecture that did not evolve for a middle-stratum role whose entire job is context-switching between people who all need a different version of you. Burnout, in this register, is partly the unpaid bill on the switching tax. Seeing it for what it is gives you something to defend, which is the difference between a calendar-shaped day and a thinking-shaped day.

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v05
---
mode: SECULAR_BRIDGE
doctrine_source: somatic
mechanism: shame
weight: standard
carry_line: "The conviction that other directors are handling it without coming apart is a private theory you have never tested. The hiding is the part that keeps it alive."
---
Here is the mechanism. Manager burnout shame has a specific shape. It is the conviction that other people in your role are handling the same workload without coming apart, and that your inability to keep up is a private deficiency you must hide from your peers, your skip, and especially your direct reports. So you hide it. You decline the peer who asks how you are doing with a fine and a smile. You schedule the vacation but frame it as a planning issue, not as the depletion it actually is. You walk into the leadership meeting with the composure you do not feel. The hiding is the part that closes the loop. Because none of your peers see how depleted you are, you also do not see how depleted they are. Each of you, separately, concludes that you are the only one. Each of you, separately, performs leadership-shaped okay-ness for the others. Shame survives in the gap that hiding builds. Naming the mechanism does not make the workload sustainable, but it interrupts the part where you treat the depletion as evidence of a defect rather than as the predictable response of a middle-stratum nervous system carrying accountability in both directions at once.

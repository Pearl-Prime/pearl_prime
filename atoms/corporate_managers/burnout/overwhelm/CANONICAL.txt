You are in the parking garage after the all-hands. You turned the car on. You have not moved. Fifteen minutes have passed. Your hands are on the wheel but you cannot make them turn it. The meeting was fine. The updates were routine. Nothing happened. And still you cannot move because moving means going somewhere and going somewhere means being needed and being needed is the thing that is breaking you. Your body has become a machine that converts other people's needs into action. The machine has been running without maintenance for years. Today it seized. You sit in the parking garage and you feel the weight of every Slack message, every escalation, every one-on-one, every quarterly review, every skip-level, every hiring committee, every budget cycle. The weight is not metaphorical. It is in your shoulders. It is in your lumbar spine. It is in your wrists. You are forty-three years old and you cannot get out of a parking garage because your body has finally refused to carry the load your mind agreed to.

## RECKONING v01
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/burnout_overwhelm/reckoning/micro/v01.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 5

---
You are in a skip-level with your VP and she asks: What do you need from me. And for the first time in two years you tell the truth. You say: I am drowning. The words come out and you cannot take them back. The VP is quiet. You continue because the dam has broken. You say: I have eleven direct reports and each of them has problems I cannot solve. I have three open headcount I cannot fill. I have a roadmap that was built for a team twice this size. I attend twenty-seven meetings a week and produce deliverables in the hours between them. I have not taken a vacation in fourteen months. I am not developing my people because I have no bandwidth. I am not thinking strategically because I am in permanent triage mode. I am performing well enough that nobody has noticed I am failing. But I am failing. I am failing my team, I am failing my family, and I am failing myself. The VP looks at you. She says: Why didn't you say something sooner. And the answer arrives from a place deeper than professional pride. The answer is: because admitting I can't handle it means admitting I'm not the person this role requires. And if I'm not that person, I don't know who I am. The burnout was never about the workload. The workload was the vehicle. The burnout is about the identity that depends on being the person who can carry anything. That identity is a lie. The lie has been destroying you. And saying it out loud is the first honest thing you've done in two years.

Your therapist asks you to describe what you do when you get home from work. You say: I eat. I check email. I go to bed. Your therapist asks what you did before this job. You pause. You remember. You chose the role over the self. Each choice was small. Each choice was reasonable. And the accumulation of reasonable choices produced an unreasonable outcome: the complete replacement of a person by a function. You are sitting in your therapist's office and you are grieving yourself. Not the job you've lost. The self you traded away for the job you kept.

Sarah stares at her task list and cannot prioritize. Everything is urgent. Everything is important. Everything needs her. Her chest is concrete. Sarah stops mid-sentence in a meeting. She literally cannot finish the thought. Her mouth is open. Words have left. The room waits. She sits there, burned out and overwhelmed and publicly unable to function, and the silence is the loudest thing she has ever heard. Her face burns. Her chest is empty. ## MECHANISM_PROOF v05
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/burnout/overwhelm/mechanism_proof/micro/v05.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 4
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Sarah realizes the overwhelm and the burnout are the same system. She is overwhelmed because she is burned out — she does not have the cognitive resources to triage, prioritize, or delegate. And she is burned out because she is overwhelmed — the volume drains whatever reserves she rebuilds overnight. Her hands are numb. She sees the gears interlocking: overwhelm produces burnout, burnout produces overwhelm. The system is circular and self-reinforcing. The protective logic is that staying overwhelmed proves she is necessary. If she carries everything, she cannot be cut. But the carrying IS the destruction. She is proving her indispensability by destroying herself. Her chest aches. The pattern persists because reducing the overwhelm would mean confronting the burnout, and confronting the burnout would mean admitting she cannot sustain what she has been sustaining.

Sarah opens her laptop and stares at the screen. She cannot remember what she was about to do. This happens six times a day now. The burnout has eaten her working memory. The overwhelm fills the gap — without memory, every task feels new, every email feels unprocessed, every meeting feels unprepared. Her chest is crushed. She is overwhelmed not by the actual volume but by her diminished capacity to process it. Nothing changed except her. Her hands shake. The pattern persists because she keeps measuring the workload by its face value instead of by her reduced capacity to handle it. She is asking a burned-out brain to carry a fresh brain's load and interpreting the failure as evidence of more burnout. The system accelerates its own destruction. She closes the laptop. She cannot remember what she was about to do.

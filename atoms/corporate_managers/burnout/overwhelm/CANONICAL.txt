## COST_REVEAL v01
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/burnout_overwhelm/cost_reveal/micro/v01.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
You are in the parking garage after the all-hands. You turned the car on. You have not moved. Fifteen minutes have passed. Your hands are on the wheel but you cannot make them turn it. The meeting was fine. The updates were routine. Nothing happened. And still you cannot move because moving means going somewhere and going somewhere means being needed and being needed is the thing that is breaking you. Your body has become a machine that converts other people's needs into action. The machine has been running without maintenance for years. Today it seized. You sit in the parking garage and you feel the weight of every Slack message, every escalation, every one-on-one, every quarterly review, every skip-level, every hiring committee, every budget cycle. The weight is not metaphorical. It is in your shoulders. It is in your lumbar spine. It is in your wrists. You are forty-three years old and you cannot get out of a parking garage because your body has finally refused to carry the load your mind agreed to.
---

## COST_REVEAL v02
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/burnout_overwhelm/cost_reveal/micro/v02.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Your best engineer quits. She sends you the resignation email on a Tuesday morning. Two sentences. No drama. No complaints. Just: I'm moving on. You read it and something in your chest goes cold. Not because of the attrition. Because of what she said in her exit interview that HR forwarded to you. She said: I didn't feel like my manager had capacity for me. She's right. You didn't have capacity. You haven't had capacity in eighteen months. You've been triaging people the way an ER triages patients. The loudest problems get attention. The quiet ones wait. She was quiet. She was excellent and quiet and she needed development conversations and career guidance and you gave her autonomy instead because autonomy requires less of you. Autonomy was the thing you gave when you had nothing left to give. She interpreted autonomy as abandonment. She was right. You abandoned her because you were drowning and you couldn't even see her waving.
---

## COST_REVEAL v03
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/burnout_overwhelm/cost_reveal/micro/v03.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
You stopped reading. You used to read three books a month. Leadership books. Novels. Biographies of people you admired. You stopped. You did not decide to stop. The capacity just disappeared. The space that reading required got consumed by the preparation for the next meeting, the response to the last escalation, the anxiety about the upcoming review cycle. You look at the bookshelf in your home office. The last book you finished is dated fourteen months ago. The person who read those books had curiosity. Had expansion. Had a mind that reached beyond the immediate operational demands. That person has been replaced by a person who can execute but cannot think. Cannot reflect. Cannot grow. The burnout did not take your competence. It took your depth. You are still performing at a high level. You are performing from a place that gets shallower every month. Eventually you will be all surface. All execution. All reaction. No thought. No vision. No you.
---

## COST_REVEAL v04
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/burnout_overwhelm/cost_reveal/micro/v04.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Your partner says: You used to be funny. The words arrive over breakfast and they land like a diagnosis. You used to be funny. You used to tell stories at dinner parties. You used to make your kids laugh until they couldn't breathe. You used to have energy left over after work for the people you love. The burnout took the humor first. Then it took the patience. Then it took the presence. What remains is a functional person who comes home, eats, reviews emails, and goes to bed. Your partner is not angry. Your partner is grieving. They are grieving the person you were before the organization consumed you. You can see the grief on their face. You can see the distance. The distance is not about conflict. The distance is about absence. You are physically present and emotionally evacuated. The burnout did not just burn out your professional energy. It burned out the person your partner fell in love with. And you do not know how to get that person back because getting them back requires energy you do not have.
---

## COST_REVEAL v05
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/burnout_overwhelm/cost_reveal/micro/v05.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
You cry in the shower. Not from sadness. From overflow. The shower is the only place where no one needs you. The water is the only sound that drowns out the Slack notifications in your head. You stand under the hot water and you feel the tears arrive and you do not try to stop them because this is the only release valve you have. You do not cry at work. You do not cry in front of your family. You do not cry in your car because someone might see you at the intersection. The shower is the last private space. You stand there and the tears come and you cannot even name what they are about. They are not about any single thing. They are about the accumulation. Eighteen months of being the person who holds everything together. Eighteen months of absorbing everyone else's anxiety while managing your own. Eighteen months of showing up and performing resilience while your own resilience was depleted four months ago. The crying stops. You dry off. You get dressed. You drive to work. Nobody knows. The performance continues.

## RECKONING v01
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/burnout_overwhelm/reckoning/micro/v01.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
You are in a skip-level with your VP and she asks: What do you need from me. And for the first time in two years you tell the truth. You say: I am drowning. The words come out and you cannot take them back. The VP is quiet. You continue because the dam has broken. You say: I have eleven direct reports and each of them has problems I cannot solve. I have three open headcount I cannot fill. I have a roadmap that was built for a team twice this size. I attend twenty-seven meetings a week and produce deliverables in the hours between them. I have not taken a vacation in fourteen months. I am not developing my people because I have no bandwidth. I am not thinking strategically because I am in permanent triage mode. I am performing well enough that nobody has noticed I am failing. But I am failing. I am failing my team, I am failing my family, and I am failing myself. The VP looks at you. She says: Why didn't you say something sooner. And the answer arrives from a place deeper than professional pride. The answer is: because admitting I can't handle it means admitting I'm not the person this role requires. And if I'm not that person, I don't know who I am. The burnout was never about the workload. The workload was the vehicle. The burnout is about the identity that depends on being the person who can carry anything. That identity is a lie. The lie has been destroying you. And saying it out loud is the first honest thing you've done in two years.
---

## RECKONING v02
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/burnout_overwhelm/reckoning/micro/v02.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Your therapist asks you to describe what you do when you get home from work. You say: I eat. I check email. I go to bed. Your therapist asks what you did before this job. You pause. You remember. You cooked elaborate meals. You played guitar badly and happily. You coached your kid's basketball team. You called your college friends on Sunday mornings. You had a garden. You grew tomatoes. You were a person. The word lands differently in the therapist's office. You were a person. You are now a function. The function is: manage team, deliver results, absorb pressure, perform resilience, repeat. The function does not cook. The function does not play guitar. The function does not grow tomatoes. The function does not have friends. The function has direct reports. The burnout did not happen to you. You participated. You chose the meetings over the guitar. You chose the email over the garden. You chose the role over the self. Each choice was small. Each choice was reasonable. And the accumulation of reasonable choices produced an unreasonable outcome: the complete replacement of a person by a function. You are sitting in your therapist's office and you are grieving yourself. Not the job you've lost. The self you traded away for the job you kept.
---

## RECKONING v03
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/burnout_overwhelm/reckoning/micro/v03.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Your daughter's teacher calls. Not about grades. About something your daughter wrote in a journal exercise. The prompt was: What does your parent do when they come home. Your daughter wrote: My mom comes home and opens her laptop. She eats dinner while reading email. Sometimes she says she's sorry she's busy. Then she goes to her room and works more. On weekends she says she'll play with me after she finishes something. She never finishes. The teacher reads this to you over the phone and you cannot breathe. Your ribs contract. Your throat seals. Your daughter has described your life from the perspective of a person who needs you and cannot reach you. Your daughter has described the burnout with more precision than any performance review. You are always working. You are never finished. You are sorry. And sorry is not the same as present. You hang up the phone and sit in your car and the weight of what you have done arrives. You chose the organization over your child. Not once. Every day. Every evening. Every weekend. The choices were invisible to you because the urgency of work eclipsed the quiet need of a child. Your daughter does not throw tantrums about your absence. She writes about it in a journal. Quietly. The way the most devastating observations always arrive.
---

## RECKONING v04
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/burnout_overwhelm/reckoning/micro/v04.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
You take the PTO. Five days. You clear the calendar. You set the out-of-office. You tell your team you will be unreachable. Day one: you feel the withdrawal. Your hands reach for the phone. Your mind generates scenarios about what is going wrong without you. Day two: the scenarios intensify. You check email. Once. Then again. Then continuously. Day three: you stop checking because you have realized something that makes the checking irrelevant. Nothing has gone wrong. Your team is functioning. The meetings happened. The deliverables shipped. The escalations were handled. The machine runs without you. The realization is not comforting. The realization is devastating. Because if the machine runs without you, then the burnout was not necessary. The sacrifice was not required. The years of staying late and working weekends and missing your life were not the thing holding the organization together. They were the thing holding your identity together. You needed to be needed. You needed to be indispensable. You needed the burnout because the burnout proved you mattered. Without the burnout, you are just a person on a couch with nothing to do and no idea who you are when you are not on fire.
---

## RECKONING v05
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/burnout_overwhelm/reckoning/micro/v05.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
You see a younger manager in the hallway. She reminds you of yourself eight years ago. She is carrying three laptops. She has dark circles under her eyes. She is moving fast between meetings with the particular urgency of someone who believes that speed equals value. You watch her and you feel something you did not expect: recognition. She is you. She is building the same trap you built. She is trading her health and her relationships and her self for the identity of the indispensable leader. She will burn out the way you burned out. She will sit in a parking garage unable to move the way you sat in a parking garage. She will cry in the shower the way you cry in the shower. And the organization will continue, because the organization always continues. You want to stop her. You want to say: the load is not the point. The load is the symptom. The point is that we have confused our worth with our capacity. We have decided that carrying more means being more. And carrying more is not being more. Carrying more is being less. Less present. Less creative. Less human. Less alive. You do not stop her. You cannot. Because eight years ago someone tried to tell you and you did not listen either.
---

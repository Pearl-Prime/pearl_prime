---
family: F1

---
I've come to recognize a mechanism operating in the way I move through my work. I'm responsible for people. Not just for tasks or outcomes, but for actual human wellbeing—their growth, their performance, their sense of meaning in what they do. And somewhere in the architecture of that responsibility, I learned that my own wellbeing was secondary.
The mechanism is subtle but comprehensive. I attend to everyone else's needs first. I translate my own strain into operational language so it sounds professional. I manage my exhaustion as a logistics problem rather than a human problem. The system I operate within requires this translation. Emotion isn't operational. Limitation isn't strategic. Exhaustion is something you optimize around, not something that signals that something is fundamentally wrong.
What I'm learning to recognize is how this mechanism operates through ambient pressure rather than explicit instruction. No one explicitly told me that my burnout was the cost of being a good manager. But the system itself implied it. The managers who acknowledged their limits were seen as less committed. The ones who kept going no matter what were promoted.
The mechanism is named as burnout, but it's really the automatic suppression of awareness. I'm running on empty, but I've become so skilled at not noticing the emptiness that it feels normal. The system is designed so that the person running it past empty doesn't get to see the meter.
The teaching here is that burnout in management positions operates differently than in other contexts. It's more accepted, more invisible, more deeply embedded in the culture itself.
What happens if you stop running the system and start asking what the system is actually costing?

What I want to name, because I think it is the part that gets named last, is that being-the-buffer is a job description nobody writes down and everybody assigns to you the moment you become a manager. You are the buffer between the strategy and the execution. The buffer between the headcount you do not have and the work the org expects. The buffer between the senior leader's mood and your team's morale. The buffer between the customer escalation and the engineer who needs four uninterrupted hours.

The buffer-role is what produces management burnout, and the buffer-role is the part of the job that has no visible deliverables. Your team ships features. You ship the conditions under which they can ship features. The conditions are invisible. The features are the thing that gets reviewed.

The cost of being-the-buffer is paid in the silence between meetings, in the seven seconds of staring at the calendar before the next call, in the second cup of coffee at 2:30 PM that you do not actually want. The buffer absorbs friction so the rest of the system does not have to. The absorbing has a price. The price comes due, usually, around year three, in the body of a person who used to be able to do this without thinking about it and is now thinking about it constantly.

I am not telling you to stop being the buffer. I am telling you to notice that the buffer-role is a job, and the job has costs, and the costs are real even though nobody has a column for them in the spreadsheet.

I want to talk about caring-fatigue, because it is the part of management burnout that people who have never managed do not understand and people who have managed for more than two years know intimately. Caring-fatigue is what happens to a person who has been deploying genuine attention to other people's interior states — their growth, their morale, their development goals, their unspoken anxieties about the org chart — for long enough that the deploying has begun to draw down the supply that produces the attention.

You can still produce the attention. The team experiences you as caring. The performance reviews you write are still detailed and warm. The 1:1s still feel productive. But internally, the caring has begun to cost something. It used to be the easy part. It is now the part that, by 4 PM on a Thursday, you can feel in the back of your throat.

I want to name this without judgment. Caring-fatigue is not a failure of compassion. It is the predictable cost of a supply that has been drawing without sufficient replenishment. The supply is finite. We pretend it is infinite because the org chart depends on the pretense. But the body does not pretend. The body has begun to file a quiet report, and the report says: the caring is now expensive. We need to fund it.

The funding does not come from inside the job. The job is the draw. The funding comes from outside — from the time you take that is not for the team, the friendships you maintain that are not transactional, the parts of your life that are not optimized for your direct reports' growth. If those have shrunk in the last two years, the caring-fatigue is the bill arriving.

There is a thing I want to walk you through, because I think it is the most useful frame I have for management burnout. The frame is this: there are two kinds of tiredness in management work, and they have different recoveries, and the two get conflated, which is why people stay tired.

The first kind is task-tiredness. You ran twelve meetings today. You made fourteen decisions. You wrote three documents. By 7 PM you are tired. This tiredness has a clean recovery — a quiet evening, a good night's sleep, a walk on Saturday. The body knows how to discharge from task-tiredness because task-tiredness is what the body was built to handle.

The second kind is emotional-load-tiredness. You held the room through a hard conversation. You absorbed your direct report's anxiety about her review. You buffered the news from leadership before passing it down. You stayed warm with a colleague who was being cold. By 7 PM you are tired in a way that the walk on Saturday will not touch. This is a different state. This state does not discharge with rest. It discharges with witness — with being seen by someone who recognizes what you carried.

The reason management burnout becomes chronic is that we keep applying task-tiredness recoveries to emotional-load-tiredness, and emotional-load-tiredness does not respond to those interventions. The walk does not help. The vacation helps for two days and then the residue returns. What would help is being witnessed by someone who knows what the buffer-job actually costs. That is a small population. You may need to recruit them.

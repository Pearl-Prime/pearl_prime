---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
You're a manager. That title carries weight. You make decisions that affect other people's careers. People listen when you speak in meetings. When you send an email, they respond quickly. You have a budget. You have authority.
Some of this is real power. Some of it is the performance of competence that the title allows. A year ago, without the title, the same words from you would have landed differently. People would have questioned more. Pushed back more. Assumed less authority.
You've noticed something unsettling: you feel more worthy in meetings now. When you speak, you feel legitimate. You feel like you belong in the conversation. This is partly because you've grown into the role. It's partly because the title carries an assumption of competence.
So you're a little afraid of what happens if the title changes. If you get demoted. If the role shifts. If you take a lateral move. You notice yourself protecting the title. Taking on projects that keep you visible. Avoiding moves that might be good for you but that would change your position.
The mechanism is this: your sense of your own worth has become fused with the title. You can't quite imagine being as legitimate without it.

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F2
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: structural
---
The organization measures people through the title hierarchy. Your value is indexed to your level. Manager is worth more than individual contributor. Senior manager more than manager. Director more than senior manager. This isn't just an implicit ranking. It's built into compensation, into office space allocation, into who gets invited to meetings.
You've internalized this ranking. You measure yourself using it. A lateral move feels like failure because it doesn't advance you up the ladder. A mistake in a meeting feels more consequential now that you hold a title, because your title gives you visibility that makes mistakes visible.
You want to do work that's meaningful. You also want the title that goes with the work to be high enough that it gives you legitimacy. So the two things become entangled. Is this work worth doing if the title isn't right? Would that project be good for my development if it doesn't position me for the next level?
The organization has created a metric for worth—the title—and now you can't separate your sense of your own contribution from that metric.
This is the structural mechanism: when organizations equate rank with value, people's self-worth becomes dependent on rank.

You started your career with this. But the manager title made it worse. Now the stakes feel higher. Your performance reflects on the organization. Your failures are more visible. The pressure to be constantly excellent is ambient and constant.
Your worth feels perpetually at risk.

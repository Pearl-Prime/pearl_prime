

## RECOGNITION v01-v05
---

---
David wants to speak up but sees a peer who never does. His chest tightens. The peer seems fine staying silent. David needs courage the peer doesn't seem to need. Sarah needs to push back but watches a colleague who just complies. Her hands are cold. The colleague doesn't challenge. Why does Sarah need to? Kevin sees another manager who doesn't advocate for their team. His shoulders tense. Why is Kevin the one who pushes back? Anthony sees an executive who never brings difficult feedback. His throat tightens. The executive seems successful without candor. Why does Anthony need to be different? Tanya sees other VPs who don't challenge direction. Her stomach is tight. Why does she need to?
---

## MECHANISM_PROOF v01-v05
---

---
David compares and decides silence is the safer choice. His chest stays tight. Sarah looks at the compliant colleague and decides to comply too. Her hands stay cold. Kevin stops advocating because he's the only one doing it. His shoulders stay tight. Anthony stays quiet because he's the only one bringing difficult feedback. His throat stays tight. Tanya doesn't speak because other VPs don't. Her stomach stays tight. They're all choosing silence based on the comparison.
---

## TURNING_POINT v01-v05
---

---
David realizes different paths require different courage. His chest unclenches. Sarah asks the compliant colleague if she's really fine. Her hands warm. Kevin advocates because his team needs it, not because others do. His shoulders release. Anthony brings feedback because the organization needs it, not because others do. His throat releases. Tanya speaks because she believes in the perspective, not because others do. Her stomach settles. They all separate their own path from the comparison.
---

## EMBODIMENT v01-v05
---

---
David speaks on his own terms. His chest is open. Sarah advocates based on her perspective. Her hands are warm. Kevin protects his team regardless of peer behavior. His shoulders are relaxed. Anthony brings candor. His throat is open. Tanya speaks her truth. Her stomach is calm.

## TURNING_POINT v02

---
David watches his peer challenge the CEO directly in a meeting. Full voice. No hedging. His peer says: "This strategy is wrong and here is why." David's stomach drops. He has never done that. He has never had the courage. He crafts careful memos. He raises concerns in private. He never confronts publicly. The comparison burns: his peer is brave and he is a coward. His chest is tight. Then the CEO responds — not well. The CEO pushes back hard and the peer's face reddens. David watches the cost of that courage in real time. His frame cracks. The comparison was between his peer's visible bravery and his own invisible caution. But caution has a function. His carefully worded memo last month shifted the CEO's position without the public battle. Different courage. Less visible. Equally effective. His jaw loosens. Courage does not have a single shape.

## TURNING_POINT v03
---

BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: opportunity
COST_INTENSITY: 4
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
David is asked to present the hard truth to the board — the truth nobody else will deliver. His chest freezes. His hands are ice. He compares himself to every leader who seems to deliver hard news effortlessly and the comparison says he is not built for this. He is not bold enough, not thick-skinned enough, not courageous enough. He walks to the podium anyway. His voice shakes on the first sentence. His hands tremble on the slides. He says the thing nobody wants to hear: the product line is failing and they need to cut it. The room is silent. His body is on fire. He finishes and sits down. The board chair says: "That took guts." David's chest cracks open. The courage was not in the absence of fear. It was in the shaking hands and the broken voice and the saying it anyway. The comparison with people who seem fearless was a comparison with a fiction. Everyone shakes. Some just hide it better.

David speaks truth in a meeting without comparing his courage to anyone else's. He says what he sees. His voice is steady — not because he is not afraid but because the fear no longer controls his mouth. His chest is warm. His hands rest on the table. He does not look at his peer to gauge whether his courage was sufficient. He does not measure his boldness against a template. He said what needed saying and the saying was its own measure. His shoulders are level. He is a leader who tells the truth at the pace and volume his body can sustain. The comparison with bolder, louder leaders is irrelevant. His truth landed. His truth mattered. The room adjusted because of his words. That is courage. His version of it.

## EMBODIMENT v03

BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 4
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 4
IDENTITY_STAGE: self_claim
---
David faces the hardest decision of his career — recommending the closure of a division he built. His chest is heavy. His hands are cold. The old David would have compared himself to leaders who make hard calls without flinching. This David does not compare. He makes the recommendation. His voice cracks. He does not pretend it does not hurt. He presents the data and the human cost and his own grief about the decision. The board sees a leader who is honest about the weight. They approve the recommendation. David walks out carrying the full burden. He does not look for someone who carried it better. He carried it his way. His way included grief and transparency and a cracking voice and it was enough. The comparison is gone. Just David and the weight of a real decision made with real courage and real cost.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/courage/comparison/mechanism_proof/micro/v02.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
David notices the comparison paralyzes his courage. Every time he is about to speak up, he measures his potential statement against what his bolder peer would say. His peer would be more direct. More forceful. More eloquent. David's version feels insufficient before he even opens his mouth. His chest locks. His hands stay down. The comparison has become a filter through which courage must pass — and his courage never passes because it never matches the template. His jaw is tight. The pattern persists because the comparison defines courage as someone else's version of it. David's courage — quiet, precise, well-timed — does not register as courage because it does not look like his peer's. He is brave in ways the comparison cannot measure and therefore cannot validate.
---

## MECHANISM_PROOF v03
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/courage/comparison/mechanism_proof/micro/v03.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
David calculates how many times the comparison stopped him from speaking. Over five years: dozens of moments where he had the insight, had the truth, had the words — and swallowed them because someone else would have said it better. His chest is crushed. His eyes burn. The pattern persists because the comparison creates an impossible standard for courage and then punishes every failure to meet it. His courage must be perfect, bold, effortless, or it does not count. So it never counts. And because it never counts, he never acts. The comparison has functionally eliminated his courage by defining it out of existence. He sees the full architecture: the comparison does not make him braver by showing him a model. It makes him silent by showing him how far short he falls. His hands lie in his lap. Years of swallowed truths. Years of insights his organization needed and never received. The cost is not just his. It is everyone's.

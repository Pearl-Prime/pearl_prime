

## RECOGNITION v01
---
path: the inbox that never empties
BAND: 2
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF2
IDENTITY_STAGE: pre_awareness
---
Sarah opens her inbox at 6 a.m. and it already has forty new messages. She clears it by 9. By noon it has sixty more. She works until midnight and closes her laptop with eleven unread threads still waiting. Tomorrow the number resets and climbs again. She used to feel a small satisfaction hitting zero unread. She cannot remember the last time she saw zero. She tells herself this is just what the job is. She does not yet notice that the inbox is not the problem. The inbox is only the shape the problem takes.
---

## RECOGNITION v03
---
path: the vacation email
BAND: 2
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF1
IDENTITY_STAGE: pre_awareness
---
Sarah sets her out-of-office reply before a five-day vacation: "I am away and will respond when I return." She reads it back and deletes it, replacing it with: "I am away but reachable for anything urgent." She cannot leave the sentence that closes the door. On day two of the vacation she checks Slack from the beach. On day three she takes a call. She comes back from vacation more tired than when she left, and tells herself the trip just wasn't relaxing enough. She does not ask why she could not let five days pass without being reachable.
---

## RECOGNITION v05
---
path: the second shift of work
BAND: 4
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF1
IDENTITY_STAGE: pre_awareness
---
Sarah's official day ends at 6 p.m. Her actual day ends closer to 10, after the kids are asleep and the house is quiet and she opens the laptop for what she calls "just an hour to catch up." The hour is never an hour. She has started thinking of this as her second shift, a private joke she makes to herself that has stopped being funny. Her body starts anticipating the second shift by mid-afternoon, a low dread that the visible workday will end and the invisible one will begin. She has not said this out loud to anyone, including herself, in those words.
---

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02
---
path: the weekend that wasn't
BAND: 4
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Sarah blocks her calendar for a real weekend — no laptop, no Slack, a promise to herself. Saturday morning she "just checks" her email once. By Saturday afternoon she has answered four messages and joined one call. Sunday she tells herself she earned the checking because nothing bad happened yet. By Sunday night she has done roughly three hours of work inside a weekend she told everyone, including herself, she was taking off. She is not lying when she says she took the weekend off. She genuinely believes the three hours do not count. The mechanism is not that she cannot rest. It is that she cannot recognize rest being interrupted as work.
---

## MECHANISM_PROOF v04
---
path: the calendar that controls you
BAND: 4
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF1
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Sarah opens her calendar to add a dentist appointment and cannot find a single fifteen-minute gap for the next eleven days. She did not build this calendar. It built itself, meeting by meeting, each one individually reasonable, until there was no room left for a body that needs a dentist. She reschedules the appointment for the following month. She notices, distantly, that she has stopped asking whether a meeting deserves a slot and started only asking whether a slot is technically available. The calendar has become the decision-maker. She is the one who shows up wherever it points her.

## TURNING_POINT v01
---
path: the meeting where you blanked
BAND: 3
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Sarah is presenting to leadership and mid-sentence her mind goes completely blank — not searching for a word, just gone, a white static where the next thought should be. She stands there for three seconds that feel like a minute. Afterward, instead of her usual response — push through, schedule two more prep sessions, prove it won't happen again — she tries something different. She tells her own manager, plainly: "I think I'm running on empty and I need to shed something from my plate." Saying it costs her pride. Nothing falls apart because she said it. Her manager actually removes a project. It is the first time asking for less has produced less instead of producing more work explaining why she needed less.
---

## TURNING_POINT v03
---
path: the three-day slide
BAND: 3
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF1
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Sarah notices she has been sliding for three days — slower to respond, shorter with people, running on caffeine instead of sleep. Her old move is to push through until the crisis that caused the slide resolves itself. This time she cancels two non-essential meetings and spends the freed hour doing nothing that produces output. It feels like failure while she is doing it. The world does not end. The meetings reschedule easily. By the third day she notices her patience has come back slightly, unforced, simply because she stopped spending it on things that did not need her.
---

## TURNING_POINT v05
---
path: the thing that fell through the cracks
BAND: 3
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
A deliverable Sarah was tracking falls through the cracks and a client notices before she does. Her old response would be to silently absorb the failure, work a weekend to fix it, and never mention how it happened. This time she tells her team plainly: I was tracking too many things and this one got missed, and I need us to build a system that does not depend on my memory holding everything. It is uncomfortable to admit the gap was hers. The team builds a shared tracker within a week. Nothing falls through the cracks that quarter, not because Sarah tried harder, but because she stopped being the only net.
---

## EMBODIMENT v02
---
path: the all-hands you almost missed
BAND: 1
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF1
IDENTITY_STAGE: self_claim
---
Sarah almost misses the company all-hands because her calendar had it wedged between two other meetings with no buffer. She catches it with ninety seconds to spare, walks in slightly breathless, and sits in the back instead of her usual front-row seat. Nobody notices. The meeting proceeds fine without her arriving early or looking composed. She sits with the mild shock of this: her presence did not need to be flawless for the room to function. She has spent years believing otherwise. Today, a matter of ninety seconds proved her wrong, and nothing was lost.
---

## EMBODIMENT v04
---
path: the metric you started optimizing wrong
BAND: 1
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF3
IDENTITY_STAGE: self_claim
---
For two years Sarah optimized for response time — how fast she answered every message, every request, every ping. In her own performance review she names, out loud, that this metric was wrong: it measured her availability, not the team's actual outcomes, and it trained everyone to expect an instant Sarah instead of a sustainable one. She has since started optimizing for a different number — how often the team solves things without her — and that number is climbing. Naming the old metric as a mistake, in front of her boss, does not feel like an admission of failure. It feels like the first accurate thing she has said about her own job in years.

## TURNING_POINT v06
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/compassion_fatigue/overwhelm/turning_point/micro/v06.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: opportunity
COST_INTENSITY: 4
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Sarah manages twelve people and every one of them has a crisis. Medical leave. Divorce. Childcare collapse. Performance anxiety. Twelve human beings with twelve sets of problems and Sarah is the single point of absorption. Her chest cannot expand. Her body is saturated. She walks into a one-on-one and a team member starts sharing a new problem and Sarah's internal response is: I cannot hold one more thing. The thought horrifies her. She is supposed to care. She is supposed to have capacity. Then she sees the frame: she was never supposed to hold all of it. The overwhelm is not about the volume of caring. It is about the architecture of support. She is the only point of contact for twelve people's pain. No human can sustain that ratio. Her shoulders drop. She is not failing at compassion. The system is failing to distribute the load.
---

## TURNING_POINT v07
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/compassion_fatigue/overwhelm/turning_point/micro/v07.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: opportunity
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Sarah snaps at a crying employee. The employee is sharing something painful and Sarah says: "I need you to get to the point." The words are out before she can stop them. Her face burns. Her stomach drops. She just hurt someone who was hurting. The compassion fatigue overwhelmed the compassion. She sits with the employee afterward and apologizes. Her voice breaks. She says: "I have nothing left and I took that out on you and I am sorry." The employee nods. The honesty lands differently than the snap. Sarah's frame does not just crack — it reconstructs. She cannot pretend she has unlimited emotional capacity. The pretending causes more damage than the admission. Her chest aches. The old way — absorb everything, show nothing, snap when the system overloads — is no longer survivable. Someone just got hurt by it. The admission of limits is not weakness. It is the only honest option left.
---

## EMBODIMENT v05
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/compassion_fatigue/overwhelm/embodiment/micro/v05.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 4
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 4
IDENTITY_STAGE: self_claim
---
Sarah restructures her team's support system. She creates peer support pairs. She brings in an EAP counselor for monthly office hours. She reduces her direct one-on-one emotional holding to what she can sustain. Her chest still aches when a team member shares pain — the compassion is real. But she does not carry it alone. She is a leader who builds systems of care instead of being the system of care. Her hands are steady. She is present in each conversation without being destroyed by the aggregate. The overwhelm of compassion is managed not by caring less but by distributing the caring across a wider base. Her team is more supported than before. She is less exhausted than before. The equation works.
---

## EMBODIMENT v06
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/compassion_fatigue/overwhelm/embodiment/micro/v06.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 4
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: self_claim
---
The organizational crisis demands everything. Sarah is pulled into twenty conversations in one day — team members scared, grieving, angry. She holds each conversation with full presence. She does not absorb. She witnesses. She supports. She refers to the counselor. She delegates the logistical support to her operations manager. She takes breaks between conversations — five minutes in the stairwell, breathing, resetting. By day's end she is deeply tired. But she is not annihilated. Her chest is heavy and still expanding. She made it through a maximum-demand day without losing herself. She sits in the quiet office and the weight is real and bearable. Her hands rest in her lap. She is a person who can hold enormous collective pain by not trying to hold it alone. The overwhelm was managed. The compassion was real. She goes home and sleeps.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v05
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/compassion_fatigue/overwhelm/mechanism_proof/micro/v05.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 4
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Sarah realizes she is overwhelmed not by the number of problems but by the expectation that she should solve them. Each team member brings pain and Sarah's system activates not just to witness but to fix. She absorbs the pain AND takes on the obligation to resolve it. Her chest is concrete. The pattern is visible: compassion fatigue is accelerated by the fixing reflex. If she just listened and held space, the emotional cost would be significant but manageable. But she listens, holds space, AND carries the problem as her own. The overwhelm is not from the caring. It is from the carrying. Her hands are numb. The pattern persists because she was taught that good managers solve people's problems. She was never taught that some problems are not hers to solve.
---

## MECHANISM_PROOF v06
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/compassion_fatigue/overwhelm/mechanism_proof/micro/v06.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Sarah opens her door for one more person and feels her body revolt. Her stomach lurches. Her vision narrows. Her hands grip the desk to steady herself. One more person with one more pain and her nervous system is shutting down to prevent the intake. She sees the full machinery: her body treats emotional absorption like physical consumption. There is a limit. She has been eating suffering for months and her system is full. The overwhelm persists because the demand is structurally unlimited — people will always have pain — and her capacity is structurally limited — she is one body with one nervous system. The math will never work. No amount of willpower or dedication changes the equation. She is trying to hold an ocean in a cup and wondering why the cup overflows. The pattern persists because she keeps blaming the cup. The ocean is the problem. Her body knows this. Her chest is the cup. It has been overflowing for months.

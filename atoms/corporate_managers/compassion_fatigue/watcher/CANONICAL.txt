

## RECOGNITION v01
---
path: going through the motions
BAND: 2
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF1
IDENTITY_STAGE: pre_awareness
---
Kevin runs the Monday standup exactly the way he has run it for three years — same questions, same order, same nod at each answer. He says the right words. He asks the right follow-up. Nothing in the meeting is wrong. He also cannot remember, an hour later, what any of his six reports actually said. He was watching their faces the whole time, reading for distress signals, and somehow absorbed none of the content. He tells himself he was being attentive. He does not yet notice that attentive-to-signals and present-to-people have quietly become two different things, and he has been doing only the first one.
---

## RECOGNITION v03
---
path: the strategy meeting where you contributed nothing
BAND: 2
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF3
IDENTITY_STAGE: pre_awareness
---
Kevin sits through an hour-long strategy meeting and says nothing — not one idea, not one objection, not one question. He was there the entire time, eyes moving from face to face, reading who was nervous, who was posturing, who needed reassurance after the meeting. He had a full, exhausting hour. He produced zero strategic value. Afterward a colleague asks what he thought of the proposal and Kevin realizes he cannot answer; he was so busy monitoring the room's emotional temperature that he never actually heard the proposal. He tells himself he'll catch up on the notes later. He does not ask why monitoring the room has started to replace being in it.
---

## RECOGNITION v05
---
path: the night you realized you weren't tired
BAND: 4
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF1
IDENTITY_STAGE: pre_awareness
---
Kevin gets into bed at eleven and notices, oddly, that his body is not tired in the way it should be after a twelve-hour day of meetings. It is tired in a different way — wired, alert, still scanning, the way a guard dog stays alert even off duty. He lies there running the day's faces back through his mind, checking each one again for something he might have missed. He is not thinking about his own day at all. He does not notice that the exhaustion he feels has nothing to do with his workload and everything to do with never once, in twelve hours, having stopped watching other people to check on himself.
---

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02
---
path: the people who are trying to connect with you
BAND: 4
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Two people on Kevin's team keep trying to have an actual conversation with him — not about work, about him, how he is doing. Both times he deflects smoothly: turns it back to them, asks a caring question about their week, exits the moment before it becomes about his own state. He is good at this. He has had years of practice redirecting attention away from himself and onto whoever is in front of him. His chest goes tight both times, briefly, before he masters it. He sees, faintly, that the same skill that makes him a reader of other people's pain makes him unreadable to anyone trying to read his. The watching only runs in one direction, and he built it that way on purpose, a long time ago, for reasons he no longer examines.
---

## MECHANISM_PROOF v04
---
path: the celebration that felt hollow
BAND: 4
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF1
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
The team hits its number and someone brings in a cake. Kevin claps, smiles, says the right things, and the whole time is quietly scanning the room for who is only pretending to be happy, who is masking exhaustion behind the celebration, whose smile does not reach their eyes. He leaves the party having monitored twelve people's authenticity and experienced none of the actual joy himself. He drives home and realizes the celebration is already a blur — he could not tell you what anyone said, only what he suspected they were really feeling underneath it. He has become expert at detecting the feeling beneath the moment and incapable of simply being in the moment.

## TURNING_POINT v01
---
path: the weekend you spent waiting for Monday
BAND: 3
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Kevin notices, sitting in his own backyard on a Saturday, that part of him is already rehearsing Monday — which employee seemed off on Friday, which conversation he should follow up on, who might need a check-in first thing. He tries something small: he puts his phone in a drawer for two hours and forces himself to just sit in the yard doing nothing that resembles monitoring. It is uncomfortable, almost itchy, the first thirty minutes. Nobody's crisis goes unhandled because he waited two hours. Monday arrives and the team is exactly as fine or not-fine as it would have been either way. The two hours did not cost what he feared they would.
---

## TURNING_POINT v03
---
path: the morning you didn't recognize yourself in the mirror
BAND: 3
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF1
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Kevin looks in the bathroom mirror one ordinary Tuesday and, for a strange half-second, does not recognize the face looking back — not because it has changed, but because he realizes he spends every day reading other people's faces and has stopped looking at his own. He tries something different that morning: before checking Slack, before scanning anyone else, he asks himself, plainly, how he actually is. The answer is "tired, and a little sad, and not sure why." He has not asked himself that question in longer than he can remember. Nothing dramatic happens. He simply knows, for the first time that day, one true fact about himself before he knows twelve unverified facts about everyone else.
---

## TURNING_POINT v05
---
path: the thing you used to care about
BAND: 3
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Kevin used to play guitar, badly and happily, most evenings after work. He cannot remember the last time he picked it up. He realizes his attention has been so thoroughly colonized by monitoring his team that nothing is left over for the things that were his alone. He tries something small: fifteen minutes with the guitar before checking his phone, no purpose to it, nobody's wellbeing depending on it. His fingers are clumsy. It sounds bad. He does it again the next night anyway. Nothing at work suffers because he spent fifteen minutes on something that was only for him. He notices, slowly, that having something of his own does not subtract from his capacity to notice others — it is where the capacity was quietly refilling all along.
---

## EMBODIMENT v02
---
path: the meeting where you saw yourself from outside
BAND: 1
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF1
IDENTITY_STAGE: self_claim
---
In a leadership training, Kevin watches a video of himself running a team meeting, recorded without his knowledge weeks earlier. He sees a man whose eyes never stop moving, scanning face after face, present in the room but never landing anywhere, including on what he himself is saying. He watches himself watching everyone and feels something clarify rather than shame: this is a pattern, visible from outside, not a personality, not a virtue. He can see the watcher operating like a piece of machinery separate from him. Seeing it from outside is the first time he has been able to consider that the machinery might be adjustable rather than simply who he is.
---

## EMBODIMENT v04
---
path: the conversation with your partner
BAND: 1
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF3
IDENTITY_STAGE: self_claim
---
Kevin's partner tells him, gently, over dinner, that he has a habit of studying her face mid-sentence like he is checking for a problem to solve. Kevin's old response would be to deflect, to ask if she is okay, to turn the conversation back toward her feelings and away from his own habit. This time he sits with it instead. "You're right," he says. "I do that with everyone. I'm working on it." He does not fix it in that conversation. He does not need to. He simply lets it be true, out loud, in front of someone who loves him, without immediately converting her observation into a task to manage. It is the most undefended he has felt in a long time.

## TURNING_POINT v06
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/compassion_fatigue/watcher/turning_point/micro/v06.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: opportunity
COST_INTENSITY: 4
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Kevin scans his team for suffering the way other managers scan for deadlines. He reads every face for distress. He monitors every tone for hidden pain. He catches the slight droop in an employee's shoulders and his body activates — someone needs me. His chest tightens. His hands go cold. He approaches the employee who says: "I am just tired. Long weekend." Kevin's entire caring apparatus mobilized for a long weekend. He stands in the hallway processing the false activation. The frame cracks: the watcher is not detecting suffering. It is hunting for suffering. It scans for pain because finding pain justifies Kevin's role as the compassionate leader. His compassion fatigue is not from too much caring. It is from too much scanning. The surveillance creates the demand. His shoulders drop. If he stopped hunting for pain, the pain that genuinely needs his attention would still arrive. He does not need to go looking for it.
---

## TURNING_POINT v07
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/compassion_fatigue/watcher/turning_point/micro/v07.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: opportunity
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Kevin has a breakdown in his therapist's office. He cannot stop monitoring. He watches his therapist's face during sessions — is the therapist bored, does the therapist think he is difficult, is the therapist about to terminate the relationship. He is doing the watching in the one place specifically designed for him to stop watching. His body shakes. His chest collapses. The watcher has no off switch. It runs in the room where he goes to heal from its effects. The frame shatters completely. The watcher is not a professional skill. It is a compulsion. It is a nervous system configuration that predates his career, his role, his identity as a compassionate leader. He learned to scan for suffering as a child because in his childhood, detecting suffering early meant preventing punishment. The compassion fatigue is an adult manifestation of a childhood surveillance system. His eyes fill. The watcher is a child's strategy in an adult's body. The career just gave it a purpose.

## EMBODIMENT v05
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/compassion_fatigue/watcher/embodiment/micro/v05.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 4
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 4
IDENTITY_STAGE: self_claim
---
Kevin walks past his team and does not scan their faces. His body tenses — the watcher insists on its read. He lets it insist. He walks to his office and works on strategy. If someone needs him, they know where to find him. His chest is tight with the unscanned data. He lets it be tight. By end of day, two people sought him out with genuine needs. He helped them with full presence. The other ten had fine days he knew nothing about. His hands are warm. He is a manager who is available, not a manager who surveils. The distinction saves him thirty phantom caring activations per day. He has energy at 5 PM. He drives home without the weight of twelve emotional profiles in his chest. The watcher still scans from the periphery. Kevin no longer files the reports it generates.

## EMBODIMENT v06
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/compassion_fatigue/watcher/embodiment/micro/v06.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 4
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: self_claim
---
A team member attempts suicide. Kevin is called to the hospital. The genuine crisis demands everything the watcher once provided — attentiveness, sensitivity, reading the room. Kevin is present. He is attuned. He holds the space with everything he has. His chest is full and aching. The watcher serves a real purpose in this room — it detects the subtle shifts in the person's state, it alerts Kevin to changes, it keeps him attentive. After the visit, Kevin drives home and the watcher tries to generalize — scan everything, everyone might be in crisis. Kevin holds the line. This was a crisis. The office is not a crisis. The watcher works in emergencies. It does not need to run in normal conditions. His body shakes with the residual intensity. He lets it shake. He did the hardest work of his career today and he has the energy for it because he saved it by not watching everything, always, for years. His compassion was there when it mattered most. It was there because he protected it.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v05
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/compassion_fatigue/watcher/mechanism_proof/micro/v05.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 4
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Kevin realizes the watcher doubles his emotional labor. For every genuine interaction, there are three phantom ones — the face he read wrong, the tone he misinterpreted, the body language he over-decoded. Each phantom interaction consumes real compassion resources. His hands are cold. He is fatigued not by the people who come to him but by the people he goes to with his surveillance — inventing their pain, constructing their needs, preparing to help before help is requested. The pattern persists because the watcher feels like prevention. If he detects suffering early, he can intervene early. But the detection creates false positives that drain him before the real needs arrive. He is exhausted from helping people who do not need help because the watcher said they did.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v06
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/compassion_fatigue/watcher/mechanism_proof/micro/v06.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Kevin has not had a thought about himself in weeks. Every thought is about someone else — their state, their needs, their pain, their trajectory. His own body is invisible to him. His chest aches and he does not notice. His shoulders are concrete and he does not register the tension. The watcher has consumed his self-awareness entirely. It monitors everyone except the one person who most needs monitoring: Kevin. He sees the full architecture at 3 AM when his body wakes him with chest pain: the watcher is not just draining his compassion. It is erasing his self. He has no interiority. He has no needs. He has only surveillance of others. The pattern persists because the watcher replaces self-awareness with other-awareness, and the absence of self-awareness means he cannot detect his own depletion until it becomes a medical event. He is a surveillance system that forgot it was also a person. His chest pain is his body's last attempt to get his own attention.

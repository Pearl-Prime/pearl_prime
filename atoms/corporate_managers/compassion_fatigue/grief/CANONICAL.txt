

## RECOGNITION v01
---
path: the team you built, now half the size
BAND: 2
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF1
IDENTITY_STAGE: pre_awareness
---
David looks at the org chart and counts eleven names where there used to be twenty-two. He built this team person by person, interview by interview, and now half of them are gone — laid off, quit, transferred to teams with saner managers. He tells himself the team is leaner, more efficient. He does not let himself feel the loss of any single person, because feeling one means feeling all eleven. His chest is tight when he opens the chart. He closes it and moves to the next meeting. He does not yet know that not-feeling has a name.
---

## RECOGNITION v03
---
path: who you were before management
BAND: 2
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF3
IDENTITY_STAGE: pre_awareness
---
David finds an old photo from his first year as an individual contributor — grinning, unguarded, staying late to help a teammate debug a problem that was not even his. He does not recognize the man in the photo. That David felt everything: a colleague's frustration, a client's relief, the small joy of a shipped feature. This David feels a schedule. He is not sad, exactly. He is not anything, exactly. He puts the photo back in the drawer and opens his calendar. Seventeen one-on-ones this week. He does not ask himself when he became someone who could not feel the difference between them.
---

## RECOGNITION v05
---
path: the team meeting where no one laughed
BAND: 4
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF1
IDENTITY_STAGE: pre_awareness
---
David runs the weekly team meeting and notices, three minutes in, that no one has laughed. Not at his opening joke, not at Priya's usual sarcasm, not at anything. The room is flat. He used to read a room like this and feel the flatness land in his own chest — worry, urgency, an instinct to fix it. Today he reads the room and feels nothing land at all. He notes it the way he notes a metric: attendance high, engagement low, action item: monitor. He moves to the next slide. Somewhere underneath the agenda, a part of him that used to ache for this team has gone quiet, and he has not noticed the silence.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02
---
path: the executive you wanted to impress
BAND: 4
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
David sits across from the VP he has spent two years trying to impress, and for the first time he cannot perform the version of himself that impresses people. The VP asks how the team is doing after the reorg, and David hears himself deliver the right words in the right order — resilient, adapting, focused — while feeling the words land on nothing, like reading a script written by someone else. He used to bring genuine enthusiasm into this room. Now he brings a rehearsed shape of it. The VP nods, satisfied. David has passed the test and failed something else, something he cannot name yet, something that used to sit just behind his sternum and now does not answer when called.
---

## MECHANISM_PROOF v04
---
path: running the same one-on-one twice
BAND: 4
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF1
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
David realizes halfway through a one-on-one that he has had this exact conversation before — different employee, same words, same nodding, same "let's check in again next week." He is not confusing two people. He is running a script, and the script has replaced the listening. He used to enter every one-on-one uncertain what he would hear, alert to the particular person in front of him. Now he enters knowing the shape the conversation will take before it starts. The certainty should feel like mastery. It feels like absence. Somewhere between his first year of managing and now, the specific person stopped registering, and only the category — direct report, needs reassurance — remained.
---

## TURNING_POINT v01
---
path: the reorganization that made sense on paper
BAND: 3
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
The reorg makes sense on paper: fewer direct reports, clearer scope, a chance to actually manage instead of triage. David reads the plan twice, waiting for the old dread to arrive — the reflexive need to absorb everyone's anxiety about the change before he has processed his own. It does not arrive the same way. Instead he tries something new: he tells his team he needs a day before he can field their questions well. He does not perform readiness he does not have. Two people are annoyed. Nobody quits. He notices, cautiously, that delay did not cost what he expected it to cost.
---

## TURNING_POINT v03
---
path: the late night when the metrics didn't improve
BAND: 3
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF1
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
It is 9 p.m. and the dashboard still shows red. David's old move is to stay another hour, absorb the disappointment alone, and show up tomorrow with a fixed face and a plan. Tonight he tries closing the laptop instead. He sits with the fact that the metrics did not improve and that this will still be true in the morning whether he suffers over it tonight or not. It feels wrong, almost irresponsible, to let the red numbers sit unwatched. Nothing collapses. He sleeps. In the morning the numbers are exactly as red as he left them, and he has exactly the energy he needs to look at them again.
---

## TURNING_POINT v05
---
path: the budget cut that meant no new hires
BAND: 3
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Finance cuts his hiring budget to zero for the year, and David's old instinct is to absorb the gap himself — cover the extra work quietly, protect the team from feeling the loss. This time he says it out loud in the team meeting: we are not getting the two hires we planned for, and that means some things will take longer, and that is not a failure of this team, it is a resource decision made above us. It is uncomfortable to say plainly. Nobody's morale visibly improves. But David notices he is not carrying the gap alone in his chest afterward. He said the true thing instead of absorbing the true thing, and the team is still standing.
---

## EMBODIMENT v02
---
path: the all-hands where they announced your predecessor's exit
BAND: 1
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF1
IDENTITY_STAGE: self_claim
---
At the all-hands, leadership announces that David's predecessor — the manager he replaced two years ago, burned out and quietly pushed out — is officially gone, transitioned to an advisor role no one expects to hear from again. David watches the room's reaction and feels something clear and unguarded: gratitude that he did not become that story, and compassion for the man who did. He does not gloat. He does not pretend the outcome could not have been his. He simply notices that he built something different this time — boundaries where his predecessor had none — and that the difference is visible now, in a room, as a fact rather than a hope.
---

## EMBODIMENT v04
---
path: the performance review where you saw the distance
BAND: 1
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF3
IDENTITY_STAGE: self_claim
---
In his own performance review, David's boss asks him to reflect on his growth this year, and David hears himself describe the distance without flinching: a year ago he could not tell the difference between his team's pain and his own; now he can hold both without merging them. He is not performing insight for the review. He has actually lived the distance, felt it open month by month, and can point to specific days it changed. His boss makes a note and moves to the next question. David sits with the quiet fact that he answered honestly, from a place that is now simply true, not aspirational.

## TURNING_POINT v06
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/compassion_fatigue/grief/turning_point/micro/v06.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: opportunity
COST_INTENSITY: 4
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
David realizes he has stopped feeling for his team members. A direct report shares that her mother died. David says the right things. His face arranges itself correctly. Inside, nothing moves. His chest is empty where grief should be. The compassion fatigue has consumed his ability to grieve with others. Then he walks to his car and the grief hits — delayed, displaced, landing in the parking lot instead of in the conversation. He grips the steering wheel. Something shifts: the compassion is not gone. It is delayed. His system is so overloaded that it processes grief on a lag. He is not broken. He is overwhelmed. The frame cracks — he does not need to feel everything in real time. He needs to reduce the volume of pain he is processing so his system can catch up. His eyes sting. The compassion is still there. It just needs room.
---

## TURNING_POINT v07
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/compassion_fatigue/grief/turning_point/micro/v07.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: opportunity
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
David sits with a grieving employee and realizes he is angry. Not sad. Angry. Angry that this person is bringing him more pain. Angry that the pain does not stop coming. Angry that his role requires him to absorb an infinite supply of human suffering. The anger shocks him. His chest is hot. His hands are fists. He has become angry at suffering itself — at the fact that people hurt and he is expected to witness it. The frame detonates. The anger is the compassion fatigue speaking its real language. It is not numbness. It is rage at an unsustainable demand. His body is furious that no one protects the person who protects everyone. The old story — that good managers absorb endlessly — shatters against the anger. He is not a bad person for being angry. He is a depleted person who was never given permission to stop receiving pain.

## EMBODIMENT v05
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/compassion_fatigue/grief/embodiment/micro/v05.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 4
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 4
IDENTITY_STAGE: self_claim
---
David sits with a grieving team member and holds space without absorbing the grief. He listens. His chest aches. The empathy is present but bounded. He does not take the grief home. He does not lie awake replaying the conversation. He cared in the room and released it at the door. His hands are warm driving home. He is a manager who can witness pain without becoming pain. The distinction took years to learn. It cost him his first version of compassion — the version that absorbed everything, the version that nearly destroyed him. This version is more sustainable. Less dramatic. More real. He arrives home and is present for his family. The grieving employee received real attention and real care. David received real boundaries. Both are held.

## EMBODIMENT v06
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/compassion_fatigue/grief/embodiment/micro/v06.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 4
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: self_claim
---
The mass layoff happens. David processes thirty termination conversations in two days. His chest is raw. His eyes are dry from crying. His body is exhausted. But he is not destroyed. He gave each person his genuine attention. He grieved with them in the room. He released the grief between conversations — not perfectly, not cleanly, but enough. By the end of the second day he has nothing left. He calls his wife and says: "I am empty but I am intact." His hands shake. His voice cracks. But he is still a person. The old David would have absorbed all thirty griefs and collapsed. This David carried them one at a time and set each one down before picking up the next. He is a person who can hold enormous grief without being annihilated by it. The compassion fatigue is present. The annihilation is not. The distinction is everything.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v05
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/compassion_fatigue/grief/mechanism_proof/micro/v05.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 4
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
David realizes he grieves for his team but never grieves for himself. Every ounce of compassion flows outward. His team members' pain gets processed. His own pain gets stored. His chest is a warehouse of unprocessed grief — career disappointments, personal losses, accumulated sorrow — all set aside because someone else always needed the compassion more. His hands are cold. The pattern is visible: compassion fatigue is not just about volume. It is about direction. He gives compassion and never receives it. He processes others' grief and never processes his own. The fatigue persists because the system is one-directional. He is a pipe, not a pool. Everything flows through him and nothing stays to nourish him.
---

## MECHANISM_PROOF v06
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/compassion_fatigue/grief/mechanism_proof/micro/v06.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
David cannot cry anymore. A colleague he loved dies and David feels the grief arrive and then watches it bounce off a wall he did not build. The grief cannot enter. His chest is stone. His eyes are dry. The compassion system has completely shut down — not because he does not care but because the caring apparatus is broken from overuse. He sees the full architecture: every person whose grief he absorbed became a weight he carried. The weights accumulated. His system built a wall to survive the weight. Now nothing gets through — not others' grief and not his own. The pattern persists because the wall is the body's last defense. Removing it would mean feeling everything he has stored. Years of absorbed suffering, unprocessed grief, accumulated pain. The wall is not the problem. The wall is keeping him from drowning in what is behind it. His hands lie in his lap. He is afraid of his own stored grief. The compassion fatigue is the wall's surface. Behind it is an ocean.
---

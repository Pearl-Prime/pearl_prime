## TURNING_POINT v06
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/compassion_fatigue/grief/turning_point/micro/v06.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: opportunity
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
David realizes he has stopped feeling for his team members. A direct report shares that her mother died. David says the right things. His face arranges itself correctly. Inside, nothing moves. His chest is empty where grief should be. The compassion fatigue has consumed his ability to grieve with others. Then he walks to his car and the grief hits — delayed, displaced, landing in the parking lot instead of in the conversation. He grips the steering wheel. Something shifts: the compassion is not gone. It is delayed. His system is so overloaded that it processes grief on a lag. He is not broken. He is overwhelmed. The frame cracks — he does not need to feel everything in real time. He needs to reduce the volume of pain he is processing so his system can catch up. His eyes sting. The compassion is still there. It just needs room.
---

## TURNING_POINT v07
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/compassion_fatigue/grief/turning_point/micro/v07.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: opportunity
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
David sits with a grieving employee and realizes he is angry. Not sad. Angry. Angry that this person is bringing him more pain. Angry that the pain does not stop coming. Angry that his role requires him to absorb an infinite supply of human suffering. The anger shocks him. His chest is hot. His hands are fists. He has become angry at suffering itself — at the fact that people hurt and he is expected to witness it. The frame detonates. The anger is the compassion fatigue speaking its real language. It is not numbness. It is rage at an unsustainable demand. His body is furious that no one protects the person who protects everyone. The old story — that good managers absorb endlessly — shatters against the anger. He is not a bad person for being angry. He is a depleted person who was never given permission to stop receiving pain.
---

## EMBODIMENT v05
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/compassion_fatigue/grief/embodiment/micro/v05.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 4
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: self_claim
---
David sits with a grieving team member and holds space without absorbing the grief. He listens. His chest aches. The empathy is present but bounded. He does not take the grief home. He does not lie awake replaying the conversation. He cared in the room and released it at the door. His hands are warm driving home. He is a manager who can witness pain without becoming pain. The distinction took years to learn. It cost him his first version of compassion — the version that absorbed everything, the version that nearly destroyed him. This version is more sustainable. Less dramatic. More real. He arrives home and is present for his family. The grieving employee received real attention and real care. David received real boundaries. Both are held.
---

## EMBODIMENT v06
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/compassion_fatigue/grief/embodiment/micro/v06.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 4
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: self_claim
---
The mass layoff happens. David processes thirty termination conversations in two days. His chest is raw. His eyes are dry from crying. His body is exhausted. But he is not destroyed. He gave each person his genuine attention. He grieved with them in the room. He released the grief between conversations — not perfectly, not cleanly, but enough. By the end of the second day he has nothing left. He calls his wife and says: "I am empty but I am intact." His hands shake. His voice cracks. But he is still a person. The old David would have absorbed all thirty griefs and collapsed. This David carried them one at a time and set each one down before picking up the next. He is a person who can hold enormous grief without being annihilated by it. The compassion fatigue is present. The annihilation is not. The distinction is everything.
---

## MECHANISM_PROOF v05
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/compassion_fatigue/grief/mechanism_proof/micro/v05.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
David realizes he grieves for his team but never grieves for himself. Every ounce of compassion flows outward. His team members' pain gets processed. His own pain gets stored. His chest is a warehouse of unprocessed grief — career disappointments, personal losses, accumulated sorrow — all set aside because someone else always needed the compassion more. His hands are cold. The pattern is visible: compassion fatigue is not just about volume. It is about direction. He gives compassion and never receives it. He processes others' grief and never processes his own. The fatigue persists because the system is one-directional. He is a pipe, not a pool. Everything flows through him and nothing stays to nourish him.
---

## MECHANISM_PROOF v06
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/compassion_fatigue/grief/mechanism_proof/micro/v06.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
David cannot cry anymore. A colleague he loved dies and David feels the grief arrive and then watches it bounce off a wall he did not build. The grief cannot enter. His chest is stone. His eyes are dry. The compassion system has completely shut down — not because he does not care but because the caring apparatus is broken from overuse. He sees the full architecture: every person whose grief he absorbed became a weight he carried. The weights accumulated. His system built a wall to survive the weight. Now nothing gets through — not others' grief and not his own. The pattern persists because the wall is the body's last defense. Removing it would mean feeling everything he has stored. Years of absorbed suffering, unprocessed grief, accumulated pain. The wall is not the problem. The wall is keeping him from drowning in what is behind it. His hands lie in his lap. He is afraid of his own stored grief. The compassion fatigue is the wall's surface. Behind it is an ocean.
---



## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
Your direct report walks into your office and you can see the stress in how she sits. She talks about the impossible deadline, the unclear requirements, the feeling of moving in circles. You listen. You validate. You help her think through next steps. This is your job. You do it well.
This is also the mechanism. You absorb the emotional weight of other people's work experience. Your team trusts you with their frustration because you're the only person in their chain who seems to take it seriously. So they come to you. And you hold it.
This isn't pathological. This is what good management requires. Emotional attunement, genuine concern for people's wellbeing, the capacity to sit with difficulty. But the accumulation is real. By the end of the day, you're carrying not just your own concerns but the collected anxiety of ten people who report to you.
You don't have a space to put this down. Your director doesn't want to hear about team anxiety. Your peers are managing their own people. So the emotion lives in your body. Your shoulders tighten. Your sleep becomes shallow. You notice you're more irritable at home.
The problem isn't that you care. The problem is that care becomes a container you carry alone.

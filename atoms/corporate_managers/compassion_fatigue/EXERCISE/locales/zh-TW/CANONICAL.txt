These exercises are a profound articulation of **somatic boundary-setting**. They move beyond cognitive reframing ("I shouldn't take this personally") into embodied reality ("My body is here, their hierarchy is not").

Given your context—examining the intersection of identity and work, recognizing burnout as an automatic leadership habit, and seeking recovery that is gradual and embodied—these protocols serve as **interruptors** to the inherited model of self-sacrifice.

Here is why this specific format works for your current exploration:

1.  **It bypasses the "Competence Mask":** You noted that appearing competent despite internal struggle is protective. These exercises are invisible to others. No one sees you pressing your back against a wall or shaking your hands in private. They allow you to drop the mask *physically* without risking your professional persona *socially*.
2.  **It addresses the "Transmission" of Burnout:** You are aware that burnout is transmitted to future leaders. By naming fear ("This is not my fear to carry") and physically releasing it (shaking hands, cold water), you stop the somatic transmission. You prevent the team’s anxiety from becoming your chronic tension, which you would otherwise pass down through reactive decision-making or depleted presence.
3.  **It reclaims Agency through Micro-Moments:** Recovery from systemic burnout isn’t always a sabbatical; often, it’s the accumulation of reclaimed seconds. Exercise v05 (five exhales in the car) and v12 (cold water on wrists) are acts of rebellion against the expectation of constant availability. They assert: *My nervous system belongs to me.*
4.  **It distinguishes "Support" from "Absorption":** As a manager responsible for wellbeing, there is a fine line between empathy and enmeshment. Exercises v03, v06, and v09 create a clear somatic delineation: *I can witness your fear without housing it.* This is crucial for sustainable leadership.

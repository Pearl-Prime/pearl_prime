

## RECOGNITION v01-v05

---
David is depleted from caring for his team and sees a peer who seems fine. His chest is tight. The peer doesn't seem affected by their team's emotions. David is falling apart where the peer seems unbothered. Sarah feels compassion fatigue and watches another manager seem unaffected. Her hands are cold. The peer doesn't seem to get depleted by caring. Sarah is weaker. Kevin absorbs his team's stress and sees a peer who seems boundaried. His shoulders tense. The peer probably doesn't let things get to him. Kevin is more affected. Anthony feels emotionally drained and sees an executive who seems detached. His throat tightens. The executive isn't being depleted. Anthony is worse at this. Tanya is tired from caring and sees another VP who seems more guarded. Her stomach is tight. The other VP probably cares less. Tanya is more affected by people's emotions.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v01-v05
---

---
David starts pulling back to match the peer's detachment. His chest stays tight. Sarah starts hiding her emotional reactions to match the peer's apparent unaffectedness. Her hands stay cold. Kevin becomes more boundaried, less present. His shoulders stay tight. Anthony becomes more guarded in conversations. His throat stays tight. Tanya distances herself from her team. Her stomach stays tight. They're all trying to be less affected by mimicking what seems like the peer's approach. The comparison is preventing healthy compassion.
---

## TURNING_POINT v01-v05
---

---
David asks the peer how they're actually doing. The peer is also affected. The comparison dissolves. David's chest unclenches. He doesn't have to match false detachment. Sarah talks to her peer about managing emotions. The peer cares too. Her hands warm. Kevin realizes the peer probably also gets affected. His shoulders release. Anthony talks to another executive and learns they're also managing emotional labor. His throat releases. Tanya talks to her peer and discovers the other VP is also caring about people. Her stomach settles. The comparison dissolves when they get actual information instead of assumptions about how others handle it.

Rachel watches her peer lead a layoff conversation with apparent calm. Composed voice. Steady hands. The affected employees leave the room looking held, not devastated. Rachel just had the same conversation and it shattered her. Her voice broke. Her hands trembled. She absorbed every person's pain like a sponge. Her chest is heavy with their grief and her own. The comparison fires: her peer handles the hard things without breaking. Rachel breaks every time. Then she hears her peer on the phone afterward — crying in the stairwell. Her peer broke too. Just privately. The frame cracks. The comparison was between Rachel's visible pain and her peer's hidden pain. Both are carrying it. Both are crushed. The difference is performance, not capacity. Rachel's shoulders drop. Her compassion fatigue is not evidence of weakness. It is evidence that she absorbs suffering honestly instead of performing resilience.

COST_TYPE: opportunity
COST_INTENSITY: 3

---
Rachel cannot care anymore. The number goes numb. Her fourth direct report crying in her office this week and she feels nothing. Her face is composed but inside she is hollow. Then she sees her peer — the one who always seemed tireless in their compassion — take a three-week medical leave for exhaustion. The compassion machine broke in her peer too. The same machine that broke in Rachel. The comparison that told her she was uniquely weak, uniquely fatigued, uniquely incapable of sustained caring — it collapses. Her chest cracks. Everyone who absorbs other people's pain eventually runs out. This is not a personal failure. It is the cost of the work done by a human body. Her peer's collapse makes Rachel's depletion visible as normal. The frame shatters: she was comparing her exhaustion to an illusion. Nobody sustains unlimited compassion. Her eyes sting. The numbness is not a character flaw. It is a blown fuse.

---
Rachel sits with a team member who is struggling and she feels the pull — absorb their pain, carry their weight, fix their suffering. She does not absorb. She listens. She holds space without taking on the weight. Her chest aches at the boundary. The comparison flickers: other managers would do more, feel more, give more. She lets the comparison pass. She is a manager who cares within limits. She offers support and does not drown in it. The meeting ends and Rachel has energy remaining. Her hands are warm. She helped without being consumed. The old Rachel would have absorbed the pain and spent the evening depleted. This Rachel held it with them, in the room, and left it in the room when she left. The distinction is sustainable compassion versus sacrificial compassion. She chose the version she can maintain.

The crisis is organizational. Layoffs, restructuring, genuine suffering across the team. Rachel holds meetings, processes grief, supports transitions. Her chest is tight. Her body is activated. But she does not empty herself. She gives what she has and protects what remains. She cries in her car after the last meeting — real tears, real grief, real compassion. But she does not take their suffering home. She leaves it in the parking lot with the tears. She drives home and is present with her family. Tired but intact. The comparison is irrelevant — she is not measuring herself against anyone. She is measuring herself against her own capacity. She gave fully within her limits. Her hands are warm on the steering wheel. She is a person who can hold other people's pain without it becoming her identity. The compassion is real. The fatigue is managed. Both are true.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/compassion_fatigue/comparison/mechanism_proof/micro/v02.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Rachel notices the comparison: her peer seems to care without cost. Her peer listens to struggling employees and emerges energized. Rachel listens and emerges drained. Her chest is heavy. She sees the machinery — the comparison measures visible output, not internal cost. She does not know her peer's internal cost. She only sees the performance of effortless caring. The protective logic is clear: if someone else can care without fatiguing, Rachel's fatigue is a deficiency. But the premise is false. Nobody cares without cost. Her hands are cold. The pattern persists because the comparison creates an impossible standard — unlimited compassion without consequence — and measures Rachel against it. The standard does not exist. The comparison does not care. It measures her against a fiction and declares her insufficient.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v03
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/compassion_fatigue/comparison/mechanism_proof/micro/v03.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Rachel lies awake thinking about every person she failed to help enough. The employee who cried and she could not find the right words. The team member who left because she could not absorb one more crisis. The direct report whose pain she finally went numb to. Her chest is crushed. She compares herself to an idealized version of a manager who never runs out, never goes numb, never fails to find the words. That manager does not exist. Has never existed. Her hands grip the sheets. The pattern persists because compassion fatigue violates her deepest identity: she is a person who cares. When the caring runs out, she is no one. The comparison with people who seem to care effortlessly is not about the comparison. It is about her terror that she is no longer the person she promised herself she would be. The compassion fatigue is not just exhaustion. It is an identity crisis. She cannot care and she does not know who she is when she cannot care. The recognition is the loneliest thing she has ever felt.

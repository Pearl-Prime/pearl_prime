## MECHANISM_PROOF v01-v05
---

---
David internalizes his team's stress and the shame prevents him from releasing it. He keeps absorbing. His chest stays heavy. The compassion is becoming resentment because it's not sustainable. Sarah's care-taking extends beyond work hours. She can't stop caring. Her hands stay cold. The shame prevents her from setting boundaries on her compassion. Kevin listens to every problem and the accumulated weight exhausts him. The shame of being tired from listening prevents him from asking for support. Anthony's compassion is creating his burnout but he can't stop caring. His throat stays tight. The shame about being depleted by compassion prevents addressing it. Tanya absorbs her team's fear about the reorg and can't discharge it. Her stomach stays tight. The shame prevents her from managing her own limits.

## TURNING_POINT v01-v05
---

---
David acknowledges: "I can care and also need care." His chest unclenches. The boundary between healthy compassion and unsustainable absorption becomes clear. Sarah sets limits on her availability: "I care deeply and I also need rest." Her hands warm. Kevin tells his team: "I hear you and I also need support sometimes." His shoulders release. Anthony recognizes that having limits on compassion is not lack of care. His throat releases. Tanya says: "I care about you and I also care about myself." Her stomach settles. The shame about needing breaks from compassion decreases when they acknowledge that sustainable caring requires self-care.

Kevin sits with a team member in distress and feels nothing. His face is arranged in the right expression but his chest is hollow. The compassion is gone. The shame fires instantly: what kind of leader feels nothing when someone is suffering? His face burns. He fakes empathy through the meeting and the faking makes the shame worse. Then his peer mentions, casually, that she sometimes has to "manually turn on" her caring in tough conversations. Kevin's frame cracks. Other people fake it too. Other people have days when the compassion is not available. The shame said he was uniquely deficient. His peer just revealed that the depletion is shared, normal, a feature of the work. His shoulders drop. The shame about feeling nothing is worse than the feeling nothing itself. If he could accept the depletion without shame, he might actually recover faster.

## TURNING_POINT v03
---

BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: opportunity
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Kevin walks past a colleague crying in the hallway and keeps walking. He does not stop. He does not offer comfort. He keeps walking because he has nothing to offer. His chest is ice. His hands are at his sides. The shame arrives like a bomb — he is a monster, he is broken, he walked past a human being in pain and felt nothing. He locks himself in his office and the shame and the emptiness collide. He is simultaneously empty of compassion and full of shame about the emptiness. Something breaks. He cannot sustain both. The shame demands he care and the fatigue makes caring impossible and the impossibility feeds more shame. The cycle is no longer survivable. He picks up the phone and calls a therapist. Not because the shame told him to. Because his body cannot carry the contradiction anymore. The frame shatters — he is not a bad person who stopped caring. He is a depleted person who needs help. The two are not the same.

## EMBODIMENT v03

MECHANISM_DEPTH: 4
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: self_claim
---
Kevin takes the compassion leave his company finally created. He advocated for it. He built the policy. He is the first person to use it. The shame is seismic — the person who created the policy should not need the policy. His face burns signing the forms. His hands shake. But he signs. He takes two weeks. He sleeps. He sits in silence. He does not absorb anyone's pain. By the second week, something stirs in his chest — a flicker of warmth when he thinks about his team. Not obligation. Warmth. The compassion is returning because he gave it room. He goes back to work and the first person who shares a struggle gets a genuine response — present, caring, boundaried. Kevin's eyes sting. The shame says he failed by needing the leave. His body says he succeeded by taking it. The compassion is back because he was brave enough to let it rest. The shame was wrong about what strength looks like.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/compassion_fatigue/shame/mechanism_proof/micro/v02.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 4
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Kevin catches the shame-fatigue loop. The compassion runs out. The shame fires about the compassion running out. The shame consumes the little energy that might have replenished the compassion. The compassion stays empty. The shame fires again. His chest is locked. He sees the machinery: the shame is not a response to compassion fatigue. It is a cause of it. The shame drains the same reservoir the compassion draws from. They share a tank. Every unit of shame consumes a unit of potential compassion. His hands are cold. The pattern persists because the shame is reflexive and the compassion is effortful. The shame fires automatically and takes the energy that compassion needs to be manually generated. He is being robbed of caring by the shame about not caring.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v03
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/compassion_fatigue/shame/mechanism_proof/micro/v03.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Kevin realizes the shame predates the compassion fatigue. He has always been ashamed of having limits. As a child, as a student, as a young professional — every time he reached a boundary, the shame arrived. You should be able to do more. You should never be empty. The compassion fatigue is just the latest expression of an ancient shame about being finite. His chest aches with the recognition. He has been fighting his own limits his entire life. Every time his body said "enough," his shame said "not yet." The compassion fatigue is the result of decades of overriding his body's signals because the shame made those signals feel like moral failures. His hands lie in his lap. The pattern persists because the shame is not about compassion. It is about his relationship with his own humanity. He is ashamed of being human. Of having limits. Of being a body that runs out. The compassion fatigue is just where the shame finally won.

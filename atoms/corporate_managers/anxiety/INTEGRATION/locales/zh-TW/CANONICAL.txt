I used to think my job was to absorb the chaos so my team wouldn't feel it. I thought steadiness meant being a shock absorber. Silent. Contained. Private.
I’m learning that private steadiness is not enough. In fact, it can be complicit.
When I regulate myself in the car (v20), when I open my hands (v15), when I wait fifteen minutes (v19)—I am not just saving myself. I am building a capacity to speak the truth that the anxiety is pointing to.
The anxiety says: *This pace is unsustainable.*
If I just breathe through it, I sustain the unsustainble.
If I name it, I might change it.
So now, the integration looks different. I ground myself not to hide the cost, but to carry the message clearly.
I say to my director: "The team’s anxiety is high. Not because we are weak, but because the timeline ignores the complexity we discussed. My body is registering threat because the plan is actually risky."
This is not complaining. This is data.
My regulated nervous system is the vessel for the hard truth.
If I am spiraling, I am dismissed as emotional. If I am grounded, I am heard as strategic.
The goal is no longer just to survive the reorg. The goal is to use my survival to point out what is breaking us.
My calm is not a shield. It is a microphone.

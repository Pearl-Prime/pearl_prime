David cannot eat before board meetings. Not won't. Cannot. His stomach rejects food. The spiral has converted the quarterly board presentation into a threat that his body processes as physical danger. Two hours before the meeting, his hands shake. One hour before, his jaw locks so tight that speaking becomes effortful. During the meeting, he performs. The performance is excellent. Nobody knows that the man presenting the operational update vomited in the executive bathroom twenty minutes ago. His wife has learned not to plan anything on board nights. The cost is not the meeting. The cost is the forty-eight hours of anticipatory suffering that precede it and the twelve hours of physiological recovery that follow.

## RECKONING v01

path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/anxiety_spiral/reckoning/micro/v01.txt

MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal

---
David is in therapy. The therapist asks him to calculate something. She asks: In the last year, how many of the catastrophes you anticipated actually happened. David thinks. He counts. He is honest. Out of perhaps three hundred catastrophe scenarios his mind generated, two materialized. Two. And both were manageable. David sits with this number. Three hundred scenarios of career-ending disaster. Two that were real. Both survived. The spiral's accuracy rate is less than one percent. David has been living as though the accuracy rate is one hundred percent. He has been treating every scenario as inevitable. He has been sacrificing sleep, health, relationships, and presence at the altar of a system that is wrong ninety-nine percent of the time. The number is staggering. He stares at the therapist. She is quiet. She does not need to say what the number means. The number means David has been suffering voluntarily. Not from the world. From his own mind's insistence that the world is worse than it is. He has been living in a horror movie his own brain wrote, directed, and screened on an infinite loop. The actual movie of his life, the one happening outside his head, is a drama. It has tension. It has difficulty. It does not have the constant, grinding apocalypse his spiral produces. He feels something he has never felt before: anger at his own mind. Not at the world. At the part of himself that has been torturing him with fiction.

Sarah's team does a 360 review. The feedback arrives anonymously. One line stops her breathing: I wish my manager trusted us enough to let things go. The word trust detonates in her chest.

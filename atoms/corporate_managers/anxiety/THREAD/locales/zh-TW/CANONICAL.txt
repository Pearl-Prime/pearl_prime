The shape is not a circle, and it is not a shield.

It is a **container with no bottom**.

You learned this shape long before you had direct reports, before you had a title, before you knew what "stakeholder management" meant. You learned it in the early architecture of your survival: that if you could hold enough, contain enough, absorb enough, the chaos around you would settle. That if you became the vessel for other people’s unmanaged emotions, you would be safe from their volatility. You would be indispensable. You would be *good*.

The buffer didn’t start as a job function. It started as a love language, or a safety protocol, or a way to keep the peace in a house that didn’t know how to hold its own tension.

Now, in the corporate theater, that same shape is rewarded. They call it "emotional intelligence." They call it "leadership presence." They call it "being the glue." But the cost is that you are constantly pouring yourself into a vessel that has no floor, watching your own substance drain away to stabilize a room that refuses to learn how to stand on its own.

The next step isn’t to build a better buffer. It’s to notice the hole in the bottom.

Where did you first learn that your value was tied to your capacity to absorb?

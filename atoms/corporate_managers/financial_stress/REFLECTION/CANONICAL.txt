This is the comp disappointment—not that you're underpaid for what you do, but that the market values what you do differently than your organization does.
I've learned to name this mechanism directly: the moment when the compensation that seemed adequate becomes inadequate because you've learned what the market actually pays. It's not about greed. It's about the specific pain of discovering you were systematically underpaid while thinking you had negotiated well.

You think your compensation is fine until you discover the market thinks it's not. The mechanism is: you don't know what you don't know, so you think you've negotiated well until the market tells you that you haven't.
I know this because I've felt that moment when a peer mentioned their comp and it was 40K more than mine for the same role. The disappointment wasn't about the money. It was about the realization that I had negotiated while systemically not knowing what the market would bear. I had optimized within my ignorance.
The performance review cycle amplifies this because your raise is calibrated against your review rating. If you get a 4 rating, your raise is 4 percent. If the market has moved 8 percent, your raise is falling behind. Year after year of market-rate raises below market movement means systematic underpayment accumulation.

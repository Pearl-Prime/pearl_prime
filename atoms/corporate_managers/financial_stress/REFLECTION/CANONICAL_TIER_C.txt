

## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
The compensation review arrives. You've been performing well. Your metrics are solid. Your team has delivered. You expected a meaningful increase. The number that comes back is two percent. Inflation is four.
Your body responds before your mind can frame it logically. A tightness in your chest. A sense of being undervalued. The story arrives immediately: you're not as critical as you thought. Someone else is valued more. You're losing ground.
This isn't about greed. This is about status. In hierarchies, money is a measure of your relative position. Two percent says something about how the organization weighs you. It says your contribution, measured against inflation, is actually worth less than it was last year.
You've learned this means you need to be more visible, more strategic, more aligned with what the organization values. You adjust your behavior. You volunteer for the high-profile project. You start speaking up more in senior meetings. You're performing greater value because the compensation signal told you the current version isn't enough.
This is the mechanism: money becomes a measure of your worth, and your nervous system responds as if your security depends on optimizing for that measure.

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F2
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: structural
---
The organization has a comp structure that allocates increases based on a formula. Performance rating times some percentage. Your rating is good, but it's the same rating your peer received. But your peer got a larger increase. You learn later that they were at a lower band.
The message is clear: move up bands or your salary growth stalls. But moving up bands requires a promotion, which requires a vacancy, which requires someone leaving. The organization has structured the comp system such that your salary growth depends on other people's exits.
So you start thinking about promotions differently. Not just as opportunities for growth, but as economic necessities. You become aware of the organizational politics around advancement. You notice who's being positioned for the next level. You start to see colleagues as potential obstacles to your own progression.
This isn't because you're ambitious or competitive. It's because the structure has made your financial security dependent on your relative position within the organization.
The mechanism is elegant: create a compensation structure that ensures only those who move up benefit from inflation. Watch everyone become strategically focused on advancement.

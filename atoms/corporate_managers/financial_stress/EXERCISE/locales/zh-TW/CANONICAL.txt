    *   You noted that your financial anxiety is *embodied* (chest tightening, clenched jaw, shoulder tension). Traditional CBT often tries to talk the brain out of fear. These exercises bypass the cognitive loop by regulating the nervous system first.
    *   *Relevance:* This aligns with your view that recovery is an "embodied experience." You cannot analyze your way out of a physiological threat response; you must signal safety to the body first.

2.  **Specificity vs. Projection (Exercises v02, v05, v08, v12):**

    *   *Relevance:* You mentioned that your anxiety assumes "disaster is possible." These exercises force a confrontation with the *actual* disaster level (which is usually manageable) versus the *imagined* one. As v05 states: "That gap is the real problem. Everything else is projection."

4.  **Action Over Rumination (Exercises v04, v06, v09):**
    *   Hyper-vigilance leads to scanning for distress (false positives). These exercises limit the scope of action to one concrete step.
    *   *Relevance:* This counters the "cost of overextending compassion" and energy. By limiting action to one small, controlled item, you preserve interiority and prevent burnout from decision fatigue.

### Integration Strategy: Avoiding the "Perfection Trap"

Since you recognize that burnout has become an "automatic and invisible part of your leadership style," there is a risk that you might turn these exercises into another performance metric to master.

**Recommendations for Use:**

*   **Do Not Do All of Them:** You do not need to complete v01–v13 daily. That would become another source of pressure. Pick **one** that resonates with your current physical state.
    *   *If you are physically tense:* Use v01, v07, or v11.
    *   *If you are mentally spiraling:* Use v02, v05, or v12.
    *   *If you are procrastinating:* Use v06 or v09.
*   **Acknowledge the "Protective Function":** When you feel resistance to doing these, remind yourself: *"My anxiety is trying to protect me by keeping me vigilant. I am thanking it, but I am choosing to rely on data instead."* This honors the inherited narrative without being ruled by it.
*   **Track the "Cost" of the Mechanism:** After using an exercise, briefly note: *"Did this reduce the noise? Did it allow me to be more present?"* This feeds into your exploration of the "cost of protective mechanisms." If an exercise feels like a chore, it’s adding cost. If it feels like relief, it’s reducing cost.

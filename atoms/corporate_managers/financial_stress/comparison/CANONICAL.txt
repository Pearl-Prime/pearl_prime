

## RECOGNITION-EMBODIMENT v01-v05
---

---
David stresses about money and sees a peer who seems financially secure. His chest is tight. The peer is ahead. Sarah stresses and sees someone who seems financially comfortable. Her hands are cold. Kevin stresses and sees someone who seems secure. His shoulders tense. Anthony stresses and sees executives who seem financially confident. His throat tightens. Tanya stresses and sees another VP who seems financially at ease. Her stomach is tight. The comparison compounds the stress. David talks to the peer about finances and discovers the peer is also stressed. Sarah's colleague is also managing anxiety. Kevin learns his peer is also worried. Anthony finds executives are also managing. Tanya's peer is also concerned. Real information shows everyone manages financial stress. David stops comparing and addresses his finances. Sarah stops comparing and plans. Kevin stops comparing and invests. Anthony stops comparing and builds strategy. Tanya stops comparing and secures her future. Their stress decreases when they separate their path from comparison.

David scrolls through his peer's vacation photos — Bali, business class, the kind of trip David's budget cannot absorb. His chest burns. His hands grip the phone. He earns a solid salary but his peer earns more and the difference shows in lifestyle. David cannot take his family to Bali. He can take them to the lake house two hours north. The comparison makes the lake house feel like failure. Then his wife shows him a photo from last summer — his daughter laughing in the lake, the sunset behind them. His chest loosens one notch. The lake house was not a consolation prize. It was a choice that fit their life. The comparison took a happy memory and reframed it as evidence of financial inferiority. The frame cracks when the memory reasserts itself. His daughter was not laughing less because the lake was not Bali.

## TURNING_POINT v02
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/financial_stress/comparison/turning_point/micro/v02.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: opportunity
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
David's peer goes bankrupt. The peer with the Bali trips and the business class flights and the lifestyle that made David feel financially inferior — bankrupt. Leveraged beyond recovery. David's stomach drops. Not satisfaction. Horror. He built his financial stress on the assumption that his peer's lifestyle represented real wealth. The lifestyle represented real debt. His chest cracks open. The comparison measured visible spending, not actual solvency. David's modest lifestyle is solvent. His peer's lavish lifestyle was a performance funded by debt. Everything David envied was borrowed. The frame shatters. Financial stress based on comparison is stress based on surface. Underneath the surface, his peer was drowning. David is standing on solid ground wondering why he is not in the water. The ground is the answer.

## EMBODIMENT v01
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/financial_stress/comparison/embodiment/micro/v01.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 4
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: self_claim
---
David sees his peer's new car and notes it without the old sting. Nice car. His car is fine. The comparison registers and passes. He does not calculate the payment. He does not extrapolate the income required. He does not construct a narrative of financial inadequacy from a vehicle in a parking lot. His chest stays level. His hands stay warm. He is a person whose financial life is built on his own numbers, not on symbols in parking lots. The stress still surfaces at the end of the month when bills stack. That is real stress about real numbers. The comparison stress — the stress from other people's spending — is background noise he no longer amplifies.

## EMBODIMENT v02
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/financial_stress/comparison/embodiment/micro/v02.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 4
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: self_claim
---
David negotiates his compensation based on his value, not on what peers earn. He names his number. The number reflects his work, his impact, his market rate. Not his peer's rate. Not the comparison's rate. His number. The negotiation goes well. He gets close to his ask. His hands are steady signing the offer. He does not check what others got. He does not compare the outcome. He earned what he earned for who he is. The financial stress of comparison is replaced by the clear-eyed assessment of value. His value is his. It does not fluctuate with other people's compensation. His chest is open. He goes home and tells his wife the number and the number is theirs and it is enough and enough is not a consolation prize. It is the truth.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v01
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/financial_stress/comparison/mechanism_proof/micro/v01.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
David sees the financial stress pattern: every peer interaction triggers a financial comparison that triggers stress. He has coffee with a friend who mentions renovating their kitchen. David's stress activates: we cannot afford to renovate. He has lunch with a colleague who mentions private school. David's stress activates: we chose public school because we could not afford private. Each social interaction becomes a financial stress event. His chest is permanently tight in social settings. The pattern persists because every human being he encounters is a potential comparison point. Financial stress has colonized his social life. He cannot have a conversation without converting it into a financial metric. His hands are cold at every dinner party.
---

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/financial_stress/comparison/mechanism_proof/micro/v02.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
David realizes the financial comparison has distorted his perception of his own wealth. By objective measures, he is in the top fifteen percent of earners. By comparison measures, he feels poor. The comparison only looks upward — at the ten percent above him, the five percent, the one percent. It never looks at the eighty-five percent below. His chest is crushed. He is wealthy and feels poor because the comparison creates poverty from proximity to greater wealth. The pattern persists because there is always someone richer. The comparison has no ceiling. At any income level, someone earns more. The financial stress is not about his income. It is about the comparison's refusal to accept any income as sufficient. His hands lie in his lap. He could double his salary and the comparison would find someone who tripled theirs. The stress is structural. The structure is the comparison itself.

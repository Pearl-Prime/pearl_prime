Linda opened a savings account statement at midnight. The balance triggered a thought: "What if this runs out?" From there, her mind spiraled. How long would it last if she lost her job? What if her salary gets cut? What if the kids' needs increase? She's now imagining scenarios three to five years out. Each scenario is darker. Each scenario leads to another scenario. By 2 AM she's convinced herself that her financial security is fragile. By 3 AM she's planning for disaster. The plan isn't preventing disaster. It's amplifying her sense that disaster is coming. She's exhausted and hasn't solved anything.

Sarah spirals about the car repair estimate. $3,200. The spiral begins: if the car costs this much now it will cost more later, which means she should buy a new car, but a new car means payments, and payments mean less savings, and less savings means retirement delays, and delayed retirement means working until seventy, and working until seventy means — she catches loop five. A $3,200 repair became working until seventy in four logical-sounding leaps. She writes the number on paper: $3,200. Her emergency fund covers it with room to spare. The frame cracks. The spiral took a solvable problem and projected it into a life catastrophe. The repair is $3,200. Not $3,200 plus working until seventy. Just $3,200. She authorizes the repair. Her hands are steady. The spiral protests that she should think bigger. She refuses to think bigger than the actual number.

Sarah spirals about money for so long that her husband sits her down and says: "You have been talking about going broke for three years and we have never gone broke." The words land like ice water. Three years of spiral. Zero financial crises. Her chest cracks. She has been predicting financial ruin for three years and producing financial stability the entire time. The spiral's predictions have a zero percent accuracy rate over thirty-six months. The frame shatters. The spiral is not a financial tool. It is not even wrong in useful ways. It is wrong in exactly the same ways, month after month, year after year. She has been subscribing to a forecast service that has never once been correct and she has been renewing the subscription because the fear felt like responsibility. Her body shakes. Three years. Zero accurate predictions. She cancels the subscription. Not by willing the spiral away but by finally seeing its track record clearly enough to distrust it.

Sarah traces the spiral's cost in actual hours. She spirals about money approximately three hours per week. One hundred and fifty-six hours per year. ## MECHANISM_PROOF v07

BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Sarah realizes the spiral has prevented her from making every significant financial decision in her adult life. The career change she did not take because the spiral said the salary gap was too risky. The house she did not buy because the spiral said the mortgage would crush her. The investment she did not make because the spiral said the market was about to crash. She calculates the opportunity cost: hundreds of thousands of dollars in lost appreciation, lost salary, lost returns. The spiral's protection has been the most expensive financial decision she has ever made. Her chest is crushed. The pattern persists because the spiral frames inaction as safety. But inaction has costs that are invisible until calculated. She never lost money to a bad decision because the spiral prevented all decisions. She lost money to the absence of good decisions because the spiral prevented those too. She is financially poorer because of financial caution. The irony is not lost on her. It is the most expensive irony she owns.

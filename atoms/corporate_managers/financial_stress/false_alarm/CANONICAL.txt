## TURNING_POINT v01
---

BAND: 4

COST_TYPE: opportunity
COST_INTENSITY: 3

---
Sarah's company announces a hiring freeze and her alarm fires: layoffs are next, she will lose her income, the mortgage will default, everything collapses. Her chest seizes. Her hands go cold. She opens the internal memo. The freeze is about budgeting for next quarter. No mention of layoffs. Not even close. The alarm built a tower of catastrophe on a one-sentence memo about pausing hiring. She stares at the memo and the alarm and the gap between them. The frame cracks. The alarm does not read financial information. It reads financial triggers and constructs worst-case scenarios from them. A hiring freeze and a layoff are different things. The alarm does not care about the difference. Her jaw loosens. She closes the memo. The hiring freeze affects recruiting, not her job. The alarm was wrong by multiple categories.

## TURNING_POINT v02
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/financial_stress/false_alarm/turning_point/micro/v02.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: opportunity
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Sarah gets laid off. The alarm fires and for once the match is real. She sits in HR's office and signs the papers and her body runs the same program it ran for the hiring freeze and the budget review and the parking lot memo. The alarm does not have a higher setting for actual job loss. It used the maximum for everything. She walks out carrying a box and the alarm runs at the same intensity as it ran for a calendar notification. She laughs in the elevator. The absurdity breaks the frame. She has been living at maximum financial alarm for years and now the actual financial crisis feels exactly the same as a Tuesday email. The alarm wasted its capacity on false emergencies and has nothing extra for the real one. She stands in the parking lot with her box and the alarm hums at its usual pitch and she thinks: I have been through worse. She has not been through worse. She has felt worse — dozens of times, about things that were not real. That accidental training is the only useful thing the alarm ever gave her.

## EMBODIMENT v01
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/financial_stress/false_alarm/embodiment/micro/v01.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 4
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: self_claim
---
Sarah reads the quarterly financial report for her company. Revenue dipped. The alarm fires: cuts are coming. She pauses. She reads the report fully. The dip is seasonal and expected. The company's cash position is strong. She sets the report down. The alarm runs its usual catastrophe forecast. She does not follow. She has data. The data says the company is fine. She trusts the data over the alarm. Her chest is tight but her actions are calm. She is a person who reads financial information critically instead of reactively. The alarm still fires at every financial signal. She still checks the signal against reality. Reality usually wins. Her hands are warm on her desk.

## EMBODIMENT v02
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/financial_stress/false_alarm/embodiment/micro/v02.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 4
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: self_claim
---
The genuine financial stress arrives — a real downturn, real budget cuts, real impact on her compensation. Sarah sits with the numbers and the alarm fires at its usual volume. She has been here before, emotionally. Dozens of times. Only this time the numbers are real. She acts. She cuts spending. She negotiates with creditors. She picks up a consulting project. She does what the alarm always feared she would need to do and she does it without drowning. The alarm predicted she would not survive a financial crisis. She is surviving one. The alarm was wrong about her capacity even when it was right about the threat. Her hands are steady writing the budget. She is a person who can face real financial stress because she spent years facing imaginary financial stress and learning, accidentally, that she does not break.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v01
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/financial_stress/false_alarm/mechanism_proof/micro/v01.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Sarah maps the false alarm frequency. Forty-seven financial alarm events in one month. Of those, zero corresponded to actual financial problems. Her chest is tight reading the count. Each alarm event consumed approximately twenty minutes of cognitive and emotional resources. Fifteen hours of financial alarm about nothing. In that time she could have done actual financial planning — reviewing her budget, adjusting her savings rate, rebalancing her portfolio. Instead she spent fifteen hours panicking about scenarios that did not happen. The pattern persists because the alarm feels like financial responsibility. It is not. Actual financial responsibility takes one hour per month with a spreadsheet. The alarm takes fifteen hours and produces nothing except exhaustion.
---

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/financial_stress/false_alarm/mechanism_proof/micro/v02.txt
BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Sarah realizes the false alarm has made her financially conservative to the point of self-harm. She has turned down career opportunities because the alarm said the risk was too high. She stayed in an underpaying role because the alarm said job changes are financially dangerous. She kept money in savings accounts earning nothing because the alarm said the market is too volatile. Every financial decision was made by the alarm, not by strategy. Her chest is crushed. The alarm produced a financial life that is safe and small and significantly poorer than it could have been. She calculates the cost of the alarm's decisions: tens of thousands in lost salary, hundreds of thousands in lost investment returns. The alarm was not protecting her financial security. It was shrinking her financial life. The pattern persists because the alarm defines safety as "nothing changes" and growth requires change. She has been financially immobilized by a system that treats all change as threat.



## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
Your direct report of three years tells you she's leaving. She's been your peer-ish leader. Someone you worked alongside through three product launches, two reorganizations, one crisis that nearly sank the team.
You feel it immediately: the loss is real. Not just because you liked working with her. But because she was part of the team you built. Her departure changes the team's composition, the dynamics, the capacity. You'll have to backfill the role. That takes time. In the meantime, the work doesn't decrease.
You say the right things. You celebrate her next opportunity. You tell her the team will be fine. You do this because it's professional. You also do it because if you acknowledged the actual loss, the grief would be visible to her, which would muddy her exit. She needs to leave feeling good about her choice.
So you manage the grief privately. Over the next week, you catch yourself in moments of flatness about coming to work. The seat where she used to sit stays empty for a while. The meetings feel different.
This is what organizational loss feels like: you can't fully grieve because the person is still in the building, and then they're gone, and you're already managing their replacement.

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F2
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: structural
---
You've built a high-performing team. The organization notices. The company wants to expand the team's scope. A department gets reorganized. Your two strongest people get promoted out of your team into the expansion. They're successful. They're ready for the growth.
You're genuinely happy for them. You're also experiencing the real loss. The team you built, the relationships you cultivated, the capacity you accumulated—these get dispersed into the organization.
The organization calls this development. Growth. Advancement. All of those things are true. The organization also doesn't have a mechanism for grieving what's lost when high performers leave their original teams.
So you feel the loss while performing enthusiasm about the promotions. You transition their work to their replacements. You notice that the team's delivery velocity decreases for a quarter while everyone ramps up. You notice the junior people who are now in roles they weren't ready for.
The structure doesn't slow down to let people grieve these losses. Staffing changes are just operational facts. Your feelings about them are personal problems to manage on your own time.
This is the mechanism: the organization creates the loss, then redefines it as success so that grief becomes inappropriate.

## REFLECTION v03
---
family: F3
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
You manage a team through a layoff. The organization is restructuring. Three people's positions are being eliminated. You deliver the news to each of them in separate meetings. You handle it professionally. You have the severance information. You explain the timeline. You answer questions.
After each meeting, the person is gone. They leave the building. Their badge doesn't work the next day. The grief arrives after, when you're alone in your office. Not just for them, but for the work you won't do together. For the projects they were building on. For the relationship that doesn't get to evolve naturally.
You go to the next meeting. You meet with your team about the transition. You maintain steadiness. You give them information. You let them ask questions. You don't talk about the grief, because that would make them more anxious about their own positions.
That night, the flatness settles in. The thing that used to feel exciting—building the team—now feels dangerous. Each relationship carries the knowledge that it might end through no one's choice.
The grief isn't about the past. It's about the knowledge that this might happen again. And the mechanism is that you have to manage both the actual loss and the anticipatory loss of what might come next.

David hears a door close and the body activates — loss is coming. Someone is leaving. Someone is being taken away. Jaw clenches. The door was the custodian. Nobody is leaving. Nobody is dying. The alarm attached itself to grief and now every closed door, every goodbye, every ended meeting triggers the response: something precious is about to disappear. David sits with the recognition that the alarm has hijacked grief's language. Every ending is a death. Every departure is abandonment. The frame cracks — not every closing door is a loss. Some doors close and reopen. Some goodbyes are temporary. The alarm does not believe in temporary. It only believes in permanent.

David's body reaches maximum grief activation with zero external trigger. No event. No email. No conversation. The alarm fires from sleep, from rest, from nothing. Chest crushes. ## MECHANISM_PROOF v01
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/grief/false_alarm/mechanism_proof/micro/v01.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 4
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
David tracks the alarm's behavior around grief. It fires at the same intensity for genuine concerns and trivial irritants. A significant deadline and a routine email produce identical physiological responses — same cortisol, same chest pressure, same cold hands. Chest tight. David sees the machinery: the alarm has no calibration mechanism. It runs a single program at a single volume for all inputs. The pattern persists because the alarm's inability to distinguish intensity means David is always at maximum alert. Hands cold. There is no resting state. Every stimulus is emergency-grade. The body never recovers because recovery requires a period of non-emergency and the alarm never permits one.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02
---

BAND: 5
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 5
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
David's alarm has been running about grief for so long that the body has adapted to crisis as baseline. Normal cortisol levels are elevated. Resting heart rate is high. The body has restructured itself around perpetual emergency. Jaw clenches. David sees the full machinery: the alarm is not a temporary response to temporary threats. It has become a permanent state that the body treats as normal. The pattern persists because the alarm has rewritten the body's definition of normal. Stomach drops. Recovery is not about stopping the alarm. It is about teaching the body what normal feels like again — a process that requires undoing years of physiological adaptation to a state of constant threat. The alarm is not running. The alarm has been built into the architecture.

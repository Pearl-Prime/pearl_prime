COST_TYPE: internal

---
Watch what the comparison loop actually does to a single hour of your day. You sit down to think through a hard staffing decision. You get four minutes in, and a peer's update pings, and your attention forks. Now part of you is on the staffing problem and part of you is on the scoreboard, and the part on the scoreboard is the louder part. The staffing thought thins out. You come back to it, lose it again at the next ping, rebuild it, lose it. By the end of the hour the decision is no clearer than when you started, but you are tired in the specific way that ranking yourself all morning makes you tired. The cost is not that you compared. The cost is that the comparison ran underneath everything, taxing every thought, so you were never fully on the one problem that was actually yours to solve.

MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2

---
The thing the imagined scoreboard does is borrow your attention without ever paying it back. You are in a working session that matters, and a portion of your mind is permanently assigned to a background process: how am I doing relative to them. It runs whether or not there is new data. It runs in the shower, in the meeting, in the gap between two emails. And because it runs constantly, it is never finished, and because it is never finished, it never returns the attention it took. You end days having thought hard and produced little, and you blame the workload. The workload is not the whole story. A standing fraction of your focus was leased out to an imagined ranking that produces nothing, decides nothing, and bills you continuously. You were not lazy. You were paying rent on a scoreboard.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v04

COST_TYPE: internal

---
Here is the mechanics of it. Attention is not infinite; it is a single beam, and you can point it at the thing in front of you or at the imagined standings, but not fully at both. The comparison loop keeps grabbing the beam and swinging it toward the scoreboard. Each swing is small, a second, less. But you do it hundreds of times a day, and the cumulative effect is that the beam is never steady on anything for long. Your real work gets a flickering, intermittent light. The scoreboard, which does not need light because it does not exist, gets a constant share anyway. People mistake this for being busy. It is not busy. It is fragmented, the specific fragmentation of a mind that keeps checking a ranking, and the fragments never add up to the focused hour the actual work required.

Consider what the scoreboard does to your sense of your own work, because this is the deepest cost. When your attention is chronically on the ranking, your wins stop registering as wins. Something deeper gives way the day you realize the scoreboard has no finish line. ## EMBODIMENT v01
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/mindfulness_comparison/embodiment/micro/v01.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 4
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 2

---
The practice is plain enough to do between two meetings. You finish one, you have ninety seconds before the next, and instead of reaching for the feed to take a status reading, you put both feet flat on the floor and feel them there. You notice the chair holding you. You take one breath you actually feel, the whole length of it. The urge to check the standings will arrive; you let it arrive and do not act on it. That is the entire practice. Ninety seconds, several times a day, of declining to compare and choosing instead to be in the body that is sitting in the actual chair. It is not impressive. It does not announce itself. But done enough times it rewires the default, so that the gap between meetings becomes a place you rest rather than a place you rank.

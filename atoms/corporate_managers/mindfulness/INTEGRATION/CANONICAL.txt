## INTEGRATION v01
---
---
Tomorrow, pick one doorway. Not all of them, one. Maybe it is the threshold of your 10am, or the seat of your car before you start it, or the second after a Slack ping sounds. Choose a single recurring moment and make it your doorway back, the one place in the day where you will, reliably, take one breath on purpose and feel your feet. You will forget most of the time. That is fine. The point is not to be present all day; that is not available to anyone. The point is to install one small, dependable return, so that at least once in the flood you come home to the room you are actually in. One doorway, practiced badly, beats ten resolutions kept perfectly in your imagination and never once in your day.

## INTEGRATION v02
---
---
Carry this into tomorrow: the drift is not the failure, the return is the rep. When you catch your attention three meetings ahead, do not sigh, do not grade yourself, do not narrate your inadequacy. Just note, neutrally, there is a return available, and take it. Bring the beam back to the room, no commentary, no penalty. Then it will drift again, in about four seconds, and you will return again, and that is not you failing at presence; that is you doing presence, which is nothing but this, over and over, the small free kindness of coming back. Count your returns if you must count anything. A day with many returns is a day well practiced, no matter how many drifts came first.
---

## INTEGRATION v03
---
---
One reframe to carry: between meetings, do not fill the seam, let the last one finish. Tomorrow, in the gap when one call ends and before the next begins, take your hands off the keyboard and let the residue of the room you just left settle and drain. Resist the reflex to cram one more reply into the four seconds. It will feel like waste; it is the opposite. Those few unfilled seconds are what let you arrive at the next room actually present for its opening, which is usually where the real thing gets said. You are not losing time by leaving the seam empty. You are buying the one thing that makes the next meeting worth attending: your actual presence at the start of it.
---

## INTEGRATION v04
---
---
Tomorrow, when the dread arrives before the information, name it. The no-subject email, the agenda-less invite, the word calibration in a sentence: when your chest tightens and your breath goes high, notice that your body is responding to a shape and not to a fact. Say to yourself, plainly, this is rehearsal, the threat is not in the room. Then feel your feet, take one breath into the tight chest, and locate yourself in the actual present, where, almost certainly, nothing is attacking you. You will still open the email. But you will open it as someone standing in the real moment rather than someone already eleven minutes into a disaster that does not exist.

## INTEGRATION v05
---
---
Carry one small rule: hands in your lap between calls. That is the whole practice for tomorrow. When a call ends, before you dive into the next channel, put your hands off the keys and breathe once and let the call actually finish. The pull toward the trackpad will be physical; let it be there and do not obey it. This single rule, kept even half the time, will give you back the opening minutes of your next conversation, the soft tentative ninety seconds where people float the thing they actually came to say. You have been missing those minutes for years because your hands never stopped moving. Tomorrow, for a few calls, let them rest.
---

## INTEGRATION v06
---
---
Take this into the next 1:1: close the laptop, fully, lid down. It will feel like cutting a lifeline, and for the first ninety seconds your attention will twitch toward the elsewhere it is used to escaping into. Let it twitch. With no second channel available, your attention will slowly land on the only thing left, the person in front of you, and you will begin to hear what is under their words. You are not closing the laptop to be virtuous. You are closing the door your attention has been using to leave the room. One meeting, one closed lid. See what you hear that you have been missing.
---

## INTEGRATION v07
---
---
Tomorrow, notice your shoulders once an hour, and do not fix them. Just check: are they up by your ears, riding the day like a held breath? Almost certainly they are. Do not force them down; simply notice them, let them be where they are, and watch what happens. Often, seen and unfought, they drop the half-inch on their own. The practice here is not relaxation; it is the stranger skill of noticing without immediately conscripting what you notice into a project. Your body has been keeping the score of your scattered attention all day. Checking in with it, gently, without the manager's reflex to fix, is a small way of returning to the present through the one part of you that is always already in it.
---

## INTEGRATION v08
---
---
One question to carry into tomorrow, asked once at day's end: when, today, was I actually here? Not productive, not responsive, here. Eyes on the person, mind not three moves ahead. You may find only one or two such moments, and that is not cause for shame; it is the honest baseline almost everyone shares. The point of the question is not to grade the day but to train the noticing, because a mind that asks where was I present begins, slowly, to catch itself in the act, to be present more often simply because it has started looking. Ask it kindly. The moments you find, however few, are real, and they are the start of more.
---

## INTEGRATION v09
---
---
Carry this reframe: your full attention is the one thing you can always give. Tomorrow you will be unable to give most of what people want, the certainty, the headcount, the protection from the cycle. But in every conversation you can give the thing that is entirely yours, your undivided presence, and it is rarer and more felt than you think. When someone sits down with you, let that be the gift you offer, not the answer you cannot guarantee but the experience of being genuinely received. It costs nothing but the discipline of staying, and in a flooding half-present world it is, quietly, the most consequential thing you do all day.
---

## INTEGRATION v10
---
---
Tomorrow, find the gap and do not fill it. When a meeting falls through or a block opens up unexpectedly, your first instinct will be to grab a task and manage the void. Just once, resist it. Sit. Look out the window. Feel your back against the chair. Nothing will happen, and for about ninety seconds it will be faintly unbearable, and then it will not be, and a quiet will arrive that you have not felt in weeks. The day is so full of activity that there is no room left in it for you. An unfilled gap is not wasted time; it is the small held space where you get to exist outside of being needed. Let one gap stay empty.
---

## INTEGRATION v11
---
---
Take this into tomorrow: presence is not slower, scattered is just frantic. When the day's pace tempts you to skip the breath, the pause, the actual listening, remember the cost of the alternative, the wrong problem solved, the opening missed, the afternoon burned on a disaster that did not happen. Scattered attention generates motion and very little progress, and most of its motion is cleanup. Taking aim feels slower than firing wildly and is faster in every way that counts. So tomorrow, when you feel the pull to rush past the present, treat the half-second of presence not as a luxury you cannot afford but as the thing that lets you get it right the first time. You do not have time to be absent.
---

## INTEGRATION v12
---
---
One last thing to carry, gently: make the return free of charge. Tomorrow, every time you notice your attention has wandered, off to the reorg, the rating, the imagined argument, do not turn on yourself. The self-attack feels like discipline and is really just a second, stickier wandering, a fresh departure into a story about your own inadequacy. Instead, greet the noticing the way you would greet a learning direct report who got something wrong: without contempt, with patience, with the simple instruction to try again. Bring your attention back, no penalty, no critique, and let the next drift come. The whole practice rises or falls on how you treat yourself in the moment of noticing. Be kind there, and you can return a thousand times. Be harsh, and you will quit by Tuesday.

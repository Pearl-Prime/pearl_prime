## HOOK v01
---
---
You are in the room. Your body is in the chair, your hands are on the table, your name is on the agenda. And you are not here. You are in the 3:30 with your VP, rehearsing a sentence you have not said yet to a person who is not in this room. Someone asks what you think. The pause is a beat too long. You surface, blink, reassemble a face. Here is the strange arithmetic of a managerial day: you can attend forty meetings and miss every one.

## HOOK v02
---
---
Count the windows you have open right now. Not the browser tabs. The mental ones. The reorg you cannot control, the review you owe Priya, the number that was light last quarter, the thing your director said in passing that you have replayed nine times. Each one draws a little current. You are running them all at once, all day, and calling it focus. Then you wonder why a single Slack ping lands like a slammed door, and why by 4pm your jaw aches and you cannot remember what you decided at 10.
---

## HOOK v03
---
---
Your calendar is a wall of fifteen-minute blocks, color-coded, back to back, and you have made a quiet error: you believe the blocks are where your attention lives. They are not. Your attention lives in the seam between them, the four-second gap as one call ends and the next dial tone starts, when your mind is still arguing the last point while a new face is already saying hello. You spend your whole day in those seams, half in, half out. This is a book about the seams.
---

## HOOK v04
---
---
A direct report is crying, quietly, and you are watching the corner of your own screen where a message preview just slid in. You catch yourself. You drag your eyes back to her face. This is the whole discipline, right here, smaller than a breath: the noticing that you left, and the choosing to come back. Not the staying. Nobody stays. The coming back. You will do it ten thousand times before you are any good at it, and you are already better than you were one sentence ago.
---

## HOOK v05
---
---
The QBR slide is up. Forty people on the call, your number on the screen, and your heart is doing the thing it does, a fast flat drumming high in the chest. You hear your own voice start to talk. It is competent. It is fine. And you are nowhere near it. You are three slides ahead, bracing for the question you dread, so braced for the future that you cannot hear the present asking you something simple. Where did you go? You were right here, and then you were gone.
---

## HOOK v06
---
---
Notice your shoulders. Just that. Do not fix them. You will find they are up near your ears, and they have been there since the 9am, riding every meeting like a held breath you forgot you were holding. This is what a racing mind costs the body it forgot it has. All day your head sprints from agenda to agenda, and your shoulders quietly pay the invoice. The work is not to relax them by force. The work is just to notice they are there, again, attached to you, in this chair, now.
---

## HOOK v07
---
---
There is a version of you that gets through Thursday entirely on autopilot, that drives home and cannot recall the drive, that nods through a skip-level and retains nothing, that says yes to a project at 2pm and is genuinely surprised by it at 5. That version is competent. That version ships. And that version is not, by any honest measure, present for a single hour of its own life. You did not choose it. It accrued, meeting by meeting. The good news is that it can un-accrue the same way.
---

## HOOK v08
---
---
Here is a question you have never been asked in a performance review: when, today, were you actually here? Not productive. Not responsive. Here. Eyes on the person, attention undivided, mind not three moves ahead. Most managers, asked this honestly, go quiet and then find a single moment, maybe two. A whole day of effort, and two moments of presence. This is not a failure of character. It is a skill nobody taught you, in an environment engineered to make it impossible. We can teach it.
---

## HOOK v09
---
---
The inbox loads and your stomach does something. Forty-one unread, three flagged red, one from your boss with no subject line. Before you have read a single word, your body has already filed a threat report. Notice that the dread arrived before the information did. Your nervous system read the shape of the screen, not the content of it, and braced. You will open them in a moment. But first, just see it: you are responding to a feeling about an inbox, not to an inbox. The two are not the same.
---

## HOOK v10
---
---
Somebody on the all-hands says the word restructure and the room of faces on your screen goes still in a particular way. You feel your own mind leave the building. It is already in the corridor, doing math on your headcount, your budget, the two people you would have to tell. The CEO is still talking. You are not hearing any of it. Later you will need the very information you are missing right now, missing it precisely because you are afraid of it. The fear ran ahead and took your attention with it.
---

## HOOK v11
---
---
Marcus sits down for a 1:1 and opens his laptop without thinking, and the screen glow lifts onto his face, and his eyes go to it, and his report keeps talking to the top of his head. He is not rude. He is not lazy. He has simply trained himself, across four hundred meetings, to keep one channel always open to the elsewhere, just in case. The cost is invisible until you name it: nobody in his orbit has had his full attention in months, and he is the last to know.
---

## HOOK v12
---
---
Try this and notice what happens. Stop reading at the end of this sentence and feel your feet on the floor. You did not feel them a moment ago, though they were there the whole time, carrying you. That is the entire problem in miniature. The present is always available, always free, always running underneath the noise, and your attention almost never visits it. Not because it is hard to find. Because nothing in your day points to it, and a hundred things point away.
---

## HOOK v13
---
---
By Friday you have the specific exhaustion of someone who has been thinking very hard about everything except what was in front of them. It does not feel like rest is the cure, and it is not. You can sleep ten hours and wake up still scattered, because the scatter is not tiredness. It is a habit of attention, a default the mind reaches for when it is flooded: leave the now, go manage the imagined future, return briefly, leave again. Rest does not touch the habit. Practice does.
---

## HOOK v14
---
---
Nadia closes her laptop at 6:40 and the office is half-dark and quiet, and for the first time in eleven hours nobody needs anything from her, and she notices she has no idea how to be in a moment that is not a meeting. The silence is almost loud. Her mind, with nothing to manage, starts manufacturing things to manage. This is worth seeing clearly: the racing did not stop because the day stopped. The racing was never about the day. It is just what an untrained attention does with quiet.
---

## HOOK v15
---
---
Your phone buzzes on the desk and your hand is already moving before your brain has voted on it. Watch the gap between the buzz and the reach. There is a gap. It is small, maybe a quarter of a second, but it is the most important quarter-second in your professional life, because it is the only place where you are free. Everything before it is reflex. Everything after it is reaction. The gap is the one room in the whole day where you are actually the one deciding.
---

## HOOK v16
---
---
Imagine your attention as a single beam of light that you carry through the day, and imagine how much of that light lands, hour by hour, on things that are not happening: the meeting that already ended, the meeting not yet begun, the version of the conversation where you said the better thing. Now imagine the rooms that beam never reaches: the person actually speaking, the coffee actually warm in your actual hand, the one task actually in front of you. You are not short on attention. You are short on aim.

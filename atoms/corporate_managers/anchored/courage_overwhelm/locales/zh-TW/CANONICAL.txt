Based on the narrative arc provided in versions v01–v05, here is a synthesis of the core insight and mechanism:

### **Core Insight: Capacity Precedes Courage**
Courage, advocacy, protection, and candor are not merely moral choices or personality traits—they are **physiological capacities**. When individuals are physiologically depleted (exhausted, cold, tight, worn down), they literally *cannot* access the energy required for difficult conversations or boundary-setting.

### **The Mechanism of Silence**
1.  **Depletion → Physiological Constraint**: Overwhelm manifests somatically (heavy chest, cold hands, tight shoulders/throat/stomach).
2.  **Constraint → Inaction**: These physical states block the neural and energetic pathways needed for speech and action.
3.  **Result → Collective Silence**: The team becomes silent not due to agreement or apathy, but due to shared exhaustion.

### **The Turning Point: Restore Before Requesting**
The solution is not to "push through" or demand more courage. It is to **address the physiological state first**:
-   **Rest** (David) → Unclenches chest → Enables speech.
-   **Manage Overwhelm** (Sarah) → Warms hands → Enables advocacy.
-   **Address Depletion** (Kevin) → Relaxes shoulders → Enables protection.
-   **Get Support** (Anthony) → Opens throat → Enables feedback.
-   **Recover** (Tanya) → Calms stomach → Enables voice.

### **Embodied Outcome**
When capacity is restored through rest, support, and regulation, courage naturally returns. The body opens (chest, throat, shoulders, stomach, hands), and with it, the ability to speak, advocate, protect, and give feedback emerges effortlessly.

---

### **Practical Application for Teams/Leaders:**
1.  **Recognize Somatic Signs**: Notice when team members are physically constricted (tight, cold, heavy) as signals of depleted capacity, not resistance.
2.  **Pause Before Pushing**: Do not demand difficult conversations from depleted individuals. Instead, create space for rest, support, or regulation.
3.  **Prioritize Recovery**: Frame rest and overwhelm-management as *preconditions* for effective communication, not as distractions from work.
4.  **Check-In on Capacity**: Before asking for courage (feedback, advocacy, protection), ask: *"Do you have the capacity for this conversation right now? If not, what do you need to restore it?"*

This model shifts the focus from *"Why won’t they speak up?"* to *"What do they need to regain the capacity to speak up?"*

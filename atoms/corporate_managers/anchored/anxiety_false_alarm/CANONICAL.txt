IDENTITY_STAGE: pre_awareness
---
Sarah sees an executive meeting scheduled for her calendar and immediately her nervous system fires like a threat has landed. Her first thought is that she's being called in to explain something she failed at. Her chest tightens. She hasn't opened the meeting details yet, but already she's constructing a defense. The thought spirals: maybe it's about the turnover rate in her region, maybe her numbers came in soft, maybe someone complained. She's been at this company for twelve years and her region has exceeded targets every quarter for three years and still her body treats every executive meeting like an ambush. Her hands are cold as she clicks the calendar invite. It's probably just the normal monthly sync. Probably. Her mind doesn't drop the defensive posture while waiting for the meeting.

Carl gets a text from his CEO: "Need to sync before Friday." That's it. The text is casual, scheduled well in advance, is almost certainly routine. But Carl's mind immediately constructs a narrative of why this could be bad news. The CEO might have heard something about Carl's performance. The CEO might be thinking about succession and wondering if Carl is the right fit. The CEO might want to talk about headcount. Carl's been in this role for twenty years but the text activates a fear that's always just below the surface. He texts back confirming Friday and spends the next four days in a state of low-level alert, his attention divided between his work and the simulation in his head of what bad news might look like.

Sarah sits in the executive meeting and it's exactly what she expected: the routine sync. Her numbers are good. No one is investigating her. No one is concerned about her performance. ## TURNING_POINT v01
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/anxiety_false_alarm/turning_point/micro/v01.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Sarah notices the calendar alert for the next executive meeting and this time she writes down her actual thoughts. The thoughts are: "My threat detector will activate. That doesn't mean there's a threat. I'll check the agenda before the meeting. I'll look at the data. I'll notice if my mind is building a story." She does this. The story builds anyway. But now she's aware of the story while it's building, which is different from being controlled by it. She can hold both things: my mind is afraid and my actual performance is solid. By the time the meeting happens, she's already met the false alarm halfway, which means she arrives at the meeting less depleted.

Carl, in the days before the CEO sync, decides not to wait for the meeting to exist in anxiety form. He preemptively texts the CEO: "Looking forward to Friday. Anything specific you want to cover?" ## TURNING_POINT v03

BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 2

---
David decides to ask his team directly about the skip-level process before it happens. He frames it as: "We're going to do skip-level conversations. These are for me to get your perspective on your managers. Not a referendum on my management. If something feels wrong about that frame, tell me now." One person asks a clarifying question. Everyone else seems relieved to know what's coming. The transparency doesn't eliminate David's anxiety entirely, but it stops him from activating alone. His team now knows he's anxious about being perceived negatively and they're offering information instead of silence. That's a different nervous system state.

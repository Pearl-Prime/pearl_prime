This is a powerful and concise illustration of the **Recognition-Embodiment** loop, demonstrating how cognitive reframing directly impacts physiological states.

Here’s a breakdown of the pattern you’ve outlined, which could serve as a foundational model for emotional regulation or coaching frameworks:

### The Cycle Breakdown

1.  **Trigger:** A single, isolated event (feedback, mistake, question, difficult meeting, campaign miss).
2.  **Cognitive Distortion (The Spiral):** The event is internalized as proof of fundamental inadequacy ("I am a fraud," "Imposter status").
3.  **Somatic Embodiment (The Stress Response):** The mind’s judgment manifests physically:
    *   David → Chest tightens
    *   Sarah → Hands cold
    *   Kevin → Shoulders tense
    *   Anthony → Throat tightens
    *   Tanya → Stomach tightens
4.  **Recognition (The Intervention):** Conscious separation of *event* from *identity*.
    *   "Feedback ≠ Identity Judgment"
    *   "Mistake ≠ Competence"
    *   "Questioning = Normal"
    *   "Difficult Meeting = Normal Business"
    *   "Miss = Data, not Incompetence"
5.  **Embodied Release (The Resolution):** The physical tension dissipates as the cognitive threat is neutralized.
    *   Chest unclenches, hands warm, shoulders release, throat releases, stomach settles.

### Key Insight
The core message is: **"One mistake doesn't prove imposter status."**

This model highlights that **imposter syndrome is not just a thought pattern; it is a full-body experience.** Therefore, effective intervention must address both:
*   **The Cognitive Layer:** Reframing the narrative.
*   **The Somatic Layer:** Noticing and releasing the physical tension.

You might consider expanding this into a practical tool:

*   **Step 2: Name the Story.** What is the "imposter" narrative saying?
*   **Step 3: Reframe with Evidence.** What is the objective reality? (e.g., "This is data.")

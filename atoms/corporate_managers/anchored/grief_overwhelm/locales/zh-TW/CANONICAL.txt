This text outlines a somatic and psychological framework for processing grief in the context of overwhelm, burnout, and exhaustion. It contrasts five individuals’ initial states of blocked grief with their subsequent healing processes once they create the necessary internal or external conditions (space, support, regulation) to feel.

Here is a breakdown of the **Recognition-Embodiment** pattern presented:

### Core Principle
> **"Grief can't be processed when there's no space for it."**  
> **"Grief transforms when there's space for it."**

The model suggests that grief is not just an emotion but a physiological experience that requires bodily capacity ("space") to be fully felt and integrated. When the nervous system is overloaded (overwhelm, burnout, exhaustion), grief gets stuck or suppressed, manifesting as physical tension or discomfort.

### Key Insights from the Model

1. **Somatic Mapping of Emotional Blockage:**
   Each person’s unresolved grief manifests in a specific area of the body:
   - **Chest (大衛):** Heart center, breath, vulnerability.
   - **Hands (莎拉):** Action, connection, warmth/circulation.
   - **Shoulders (凱文):** Burden, responsibility, carrying weight.
   - **Throat (安東尼):** Expression, voice, swallowing truth.
   - **Stomach (譚雅):** Gut instinct, safety, digestion of experience.

2. **Different Pathways to Space:**
   There is no one-size-fits-all solution. The intervention matches the individual’s constraint:
   - **大衛 & 譚雅:** Focus on *creating/making space* (internal permission).
   - **莎拉 & 凱文:** Focus on *regulating overwhelm* (nervous system calming) before or during grief.
   - **安東尼:** Focus on *external support* (relational container) due to burnout.

3. **Transformation Through Embodiment:**
   The resolution is not the elimination of grief, but its *transformation* through full embodiment. The physical symptoms shift from constriction (heavy, cold, tight) to flow and openness (opens, warm, release, calms).

### Potential Applications for v06+
If you are developing further versions of this framework, consider:
- **Adding Nuance:** What happens if someone tries to create space but lacks support? Or addresses overwhelm but avoids grief?
- **Expanding Somatic Maps:** Are there other common somatic holdings for grief (e.g., jaw, hips, head)?
- **Temporal Dynamics:** How long does it take to create space? Is it a momentary shift or a prolonged practice?
- **Relational Context:** How do these individuals interact? Can 大衛’s open chest help 莎拉’s cold hands?

This is a powerful, concise model for trauma-informed grief work, emphasizing that **physiological regulation precedes or accompanies emotional processing.**

This narrative arc provides a powerful, concise model of how **shame inhibits healing** and how **vulnerability facilitates recovery**, specifically within a leadership or high-performance context.

Here is an analysis of the structure and its psychological underpinnings:

### 1. The Core Mechanism: Shame as a Barrier
The text correctly identifies that the primary obstacle isn’t just the depression itself, but the **shame response** to it.
*   **The Belief:** "Good leaders don't get depressed," "Real leaders don't feel this way."
*   **The Result:** Suppression. Each character somatically holds the stress (heavy chest, cold hands, tight shoulders/throat/stomach) because they are actively fighting the reality of their emotional state.
*   **The Paradox:** By trying to appear "strong" or "engaged," they exacerbate their isolation and physiological stress, compounding the depression.

### 2. The Somatic Thread
The use of physical sensations is crucial here. It grounds the abstract concept of "depression" in tangible bodily experience:
*   **大衛:** Chest (heart/breath) → Heavy → Unclenched → Open.
*   **莎拉:** Hands (connection/action) → Cold → Warm → Warm.
*   **凱文:** Shoulders (burden/defense) → Tight → Release → Relaxed.
*   **安東尼:** Throat (voice/truth) → Tight → Release → Open.
*   **譚雅:** Stomach (gut instinct/energy) → Tight → Settles → Calm.

This progression illustrates that **emotional acknowledgment leads to physiological regulation.** You cannot "think" your way out of this tension; you must *feel* and *admit* your way out.

### 3. The Turning Point: Naming the Reality
The pivotal moment in each vignette is **verbalization**:
*   *"I'm depressed. That's real."*
*   *"I'm struggling."*
*   *"It feels numb."*

This aligns with psychological principles like **"Name it to Tame it"** (丹 Siegel). By labeling the emotion, the brain shifts activity from the amygdala (fight/flight/shame) to the prefrontal cortex (regulation/integration). The shame cycle breaks because the secret is no longer being kept.

### 4. Leadership Implication
This model challenges traditional leadership narratives that equate strength with stoicism. Instead, it proposes:
*   **Authenticity > Performance:** Hiding depression requires energy that could be used for healing or leading.
*   **Vulnerability as Strength:** Admitting struggle is the first step toward resolution and re-engagement.
*   **Collective Healing:** When one leader (大衛) admits it, it creates permission for others (莎拉, 凱文, etc.) to do the same, breaking the collective silence.

### Summary
The arc moves from:
**Suppression (Shame + Somatic Tension)**  
→ **Acknowledgment (Truth + Somatic Release)**  
→ **Action (Support + Somatic Ease)**

This is a highly effective framework for understanding mental health in high-pressure environments. It demonstrates that **recovery begins not with fixing the problem, but with admitting the problem exists.**

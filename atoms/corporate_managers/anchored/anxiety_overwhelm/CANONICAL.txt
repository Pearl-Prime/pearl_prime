BAND: 2
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 1
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: pre_awareness
---
David stares at his calendar and sees it's fully booked in fifteen-minute increments. Back-to-back meetings with no time to think between them. He's responsible for his team's output but has no time to actually manage. He has accountability without authority—the budget for his team came through at seventy percent of request, which means he's accountable for delivering the same output with fewer resources. His stomach drops. He can't multiply hours. He can't make people work faster. He's been asked to do the impossible and his nervous system recognizes that impossible is just another word for accountability he can't meet. His chest feels heavy.

Kevin sits in a meeting about headcount and the message is: do more with less. Every quarter, the directive is the same. Expect more output, expect higher quality, expect team engagement to remain high, expect retention to be better, expect fewer errors. Kevin has seventeen people and he's responsible for the outcomes they produce. He doesn't control how much work gets assigned. He doesn't control the resource constraints. He doesn't control whether the company decides to hire more people. He controls only the translation of impossible demands into daily asks of his team. His throat feels tight. There's no way to do this that doesn't leave people depleted.

MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
David actually looks at the work. His team has one major platform migration, three minor feature releases, and two production firefighting incidents in the queue. That's two quarters of work in one quarter. He can either deliver the migration badly or delay the features. He can't deliver everything. He sits with the impossible choice and feels his nervous system fracture. The overcommitment isn't his failure. It's structural. But the failure to deliver will be pinned on him. He's responsible for translating the structural problem into a personal failure and then wearing it.

## TURNING_POINT v01
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/anxiety_overwhelm/turning_point/micro/v01.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
David stops trying to find the impossible fourth option and instead maps what the trade-offs are. Migration quality versus feature velocity. Long-term platform health versus short-term feature delivery. He brings this to his leadership: "Here's the choice we're actually making. Here's what we're optimizing for. Here's what we're accepting. Let's decide explicitly." His chest unclenches slightly. The overwhelm doesn't disappear but it shifts from "I'm failing at impossible" to "we're making a choice with clear trade-offs." That's a different cognitive state. He's no longer in acute shame. He's in active problem-solving.
---

## TURNING_POINT v02
---
path: story_atoms/corporate_managers/anchored/anxiety_overwhelm/turning_point/micro/v02.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Sarah stops performing competence and starts being real. "The team is stretched," she tells her leadership. "We have capacity for four quarters of work in one quarter. Here's what we need: either more people or clearer priorities." She doesn't solve the problem but she stops pretending it's not a problem. The relief is immediate. She's no longer carrying the burden of making the impossible look manageable. Her team responds with more honesty too. They're not all pretending. They start actually planning around constraint instead of working around it.

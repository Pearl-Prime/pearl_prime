mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
There's a performance that activates the moment I know I'm going to be in a social or professional context. My posture shifts. My facial expression becomes more controlled. My speech becomes more measured. I become the version of myself that I believe is appropriate for the context.
The mechanism is called "performance calibration," and it operates automatically based on who I'm with and what context I'm in. With my team, I'm one way—authoritative, focused, strategic. With peers, I'm another way—collaborative, interested, careful not to appear too confident. With senior leaders, I'm yet another way—respectful, attentive, appropriately humble.
For managers, this is partly professional skill. The ability to calibrate your presentation based on context is valuable. But it becomes problematic when the calibration becomes so automatic that you're never authentic. When you stop knowing which version is actually you.
The mechanism operates through the belief that your authentic self isn't acceptable in professional contexts. So you develop versions of yourself that are more acceptable, and then the versions become so habitual that you stop accessing the authentic self.
The teaching here is about recognizing that the performance serves a purpose—it allows you to navigate complex organizational contexts. But the cost is that you never actually relax in professional settings. You're always managing the impression.
What happens if you stop performing and show up as you actually are?

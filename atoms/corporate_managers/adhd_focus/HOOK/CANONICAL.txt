## HOOK v01
---

---
Marcus is in the conference room at 2:40 PM, laptop open on item three of twelve, cursor blinking while his team waits in the hallway — his jaw is tight and forty minutes have disappeared inside his head.

The agenda has twelve items. Your cursor blinks on item three. Forty minutes gone. Your team waits on a decision you cannot locate inside yourself.

This is not a time management problem. Your brain processes urgency and priority through a different circuit than the org chart assumes.

## HOOK v02
---

---
Marcus stares at the skip-level prep doc. Blank. His jaw tightens. The meeting is in forty minutes and every sentence he drafts sounds like a confession.

ADHD in management does not look like distraction. It looks like a competent person frozen at the exact wrong moment.

## HOOK v03
---

---
The reorg announcement lands at 9 AM. By noon, your inbox holds thirty-one threads. Your chest sits heavy, thick, unmovable. You open the first email four separate times without reading it.

That loop is not anxiety about change. It is a nervous system that runs out of triage fuel faster than the structure demands.

## HOOK v04
---

---
Two direct reports need feedback. Three deliverables need review. One senior director needs a response before end of day.

You reorganize your desktop instead.

The shame afterward is familiar. Sharp behind the sternum. What most managers call a bad week, some managers live every single week.

## HOOK v05
---

---
Priya closes the performance review portal. Opens it again. The ratings are done. The comments are not. Her hands stay still on the keyboard, doing nothing, while the deadline advances one minute at a time.

High-functioning ADHD does not disappear when the title changes. The stakes just get larger.

## HOOK v06
---

---
You ran the best meeting of the quarter this morning. By 2 PM you cannot remember what was decided. Your notes say nothing useful. Your body feels scraped hollow.

Inconsistency this sharp is data. It points to how your brain allocates attention, not how much you care about the work.

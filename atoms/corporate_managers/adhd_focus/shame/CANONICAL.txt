## SHAME v01
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 4
---
Marcus pulls up the Q3 deck fifteen minutes before the skip-level.

He stares at the slide. The numbers blur. He built this deck. He approved every line. Now the data sits in his skull like wet sand, and nothing moves.

His director will ask about the retention dip. He rehearsed the answer twice this morning. Gone.

He opens a new tab. Closes it. Opens it again. His jaw tightens. His hands stay flat on the desk.

The shame arrives before the meeting does. Not about the gap in memory. About what the gap means. Incompetent. Checked out. Not leadership material.

He straightens his collar. He will perform competence for forty minutes. Then he will sit in his car and stare at the steering wheel and feel it all drain out of him.

This is the tax. Not the forgetting. The hiding.

## SHAME v02
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: pre_awareness
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
---
Diane's one-on-ones run long. Always.

She means to end at thirty minutes. She watches the clock hit thirty-two, thirty-five, forty. Her direct report is still talking. She tracks the words but her attention keeps sliding sideways — to the reorg email, to the budget variance, to the conversation she half-finished with her VP at lunch.

Her chest holds a low, persistent pressure.

She nods. She says the right things. She writes nothing down because writing breaks the performance of listening.

Later she checks her notes. Four words. A dashed arrow. Nothing usable.

She rewrites the summary from memory, filling gaps with guesses. She sends it. It feels like fraud.

She does not call it ADHD. She calls it poor prioritization. She schedules a time management course she will not finish.

The explanation stays wrong. The shame stays.

## SHAME v03
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
COST_TYPE: relational
COST_INTENSITY: 5
---
The reorg announcement drops at 9 a.m.

By noon, three of Priya's team members have pinged her. She has not responded to any of them.

Not because she doesn't care. Because she opened each message, felt the pull of their fear, and her brain locked. The cursor blinked. The words wouldn't form. She closed the thread.

Her stomach sits low and heavy, like a stone behind her navel.

She knows what they need. Reassurance. A plan. A manager who holds steady.

She refreshes her inbox. She reads the messages again. Still nothing comes.

By 3 p.m. the silence has become its own message. She feels it land in the team channel — the gap where her presence should be.

She is not cold. She is not indifferent. She is paralyzed, and no one can tell the difference from the outside.

That is the wound.

## SHAME v04
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: integration
COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 3
---
Tom sits in the all-hands.

His VP finishes a sentence. The room laughs. Tom's mouth pulls into a smile half a beat late. He caught the joke. He just caught it slow.

He has been catching things slow all morning. The thread of the presentation keeps slipping. He grabs it, loses it, grabs it again.

His notepad holds seven unconnected bullet points and a small geometric pattern he drew without noticing.

He is a manager of eleven people. Two of them sit two rows ahead.

He wonders if they notice. He wonders if the VP notices. His neck tightens. He flips the notepad over.

He knows he is sharp. He has the performance reviews to prove it. The knowing does not touch the shame.

The shame lives in the lag. In the geometric pattern. In the flipped notepad.

It lives in the gap between who he is and who the room sees.

## SHAME v05
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 1
IDENTITY_STAGE: pre_awareness
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 4
---
The performance review template has sat open for six days.

Renata closes it every evening. Reopens it every morning. Types a sentence. Deletes it. The cursor blinks in the name field of her first direct report — someone she genuinely respects, whose work she knows, whose growth she has watched.

She cannot write the review.

Her throat holds a faint tightness she has learned to ignore.

She manages a team of eight. She delivered her last project under budget. Her own review called her a high performer.

None of that touches the blank template.

She tells herself she is a perfectionist. That she wants to get it right. That she is busy. Each explanation is a small, careful lie.

The truth sits underneath: starting feels impossible, and she does not know why, and not knowing why is the most shameful part of all.

She opens a new browser tab. The template stays blank.

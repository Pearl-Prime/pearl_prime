Watch what the judgment does to your attention, because it is doing something specific and physical. The stalled task sits there. You notice it's stalled. Then comes the second move — the appraisal, the verdict, the silent "this again, what is wrong with me" — and that verdict is read by your nervous system as a threat. Not metaphorically. Your body responds to self-attack the way it responds to a predator: it narrows. Tunnel vision. The very executive function you need to break a task into a first step is the function threat physiology dials down first. So judging the freeze doesn't motivate you out of it. It pours concrete around your feet. The more you tell yourself to just do it, the more the doing-it system goes offline, and you mistake the offline system for proof of your character, when it is only proof of how alarm works.

What changes is the order of operations. For years the sequence was: notice the stall, judge the stall, freeze harder, judge the freeze, repeat. The turn is learning to interrupt at the judging step — not to skip it, you can't skip it, but to catch it and refuse to act on its conclusion.

Marcus sits in the skip-level meeting. His director asks a question. He heard the words. He did not catch the meaning.

He nods anyway.

His jaw tightens. He reaches for his coffee cup, buys two seconds, sets it down without drinking. The director moves on. Marcus types something into his laptop that means nothing. It looks like notes.

Later, in the elevator, he replays the exchange. He cannot locate the exact moment he lost the thread. It happened somewhere between the third slide and the part about Q3 headcount. His team lead glances at him. Marcus straightens.

"Good meeting," he says.

She nods. Neither of them believes it.

He steps off at the fourth floor. His chest carries a dull pressure he has no name for. He opens his inbox. Forty-one messages. He clicks one, reads the first line four times, closes it. He opens his calendar instead. Three more meetings before noon.

He stares at the blocks of color.

He schedules nothing.

## WATCHER v02
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 1
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 4
---
Diane's performance review lands in her inbox at 8:47 a.m.

She opens it standing at her desk. Her eyes move fast. She scans for the number before she reads the words. There it is. Meets expectations. Not exceeds. Her stomach drops one inch.

She reads the comment section. "Diane demonstrates strong relationships but struggles with follow-through on deliverables." She reads it again. A third time. The words rearrange themselves without changing.

She closes the document. Opens it. Closes it.

Her assistant knocks. "Your nine o'clock is here."

"One minute," Diane says.

She sits. Her hands rest flat on the desk. She built that project from scratch. She ran three workstreams. She remembers every conversation. She cannot explain why the final report was four days late. She knew the deadline. She thought about it constantly.

Thinking about it and doing it lived in different rooms inside her. She could not find the door between them.

She picks up her badge. She walks to the conference room. Her face does not show any of this.

The reorg announcement comes on a Thursday.

Kevin reads the org chart twice. His name moved one box to the left. He cannot tell if that is lateral or down. He screenshots it. He texts his peer Rashida: "Did you see this." No question mark. She replies with a thumbs up. He does not know what that means.

He spends the next forty minutes reading the announcement again. He highlights nothing. He underlines nothing in his mind. The words pass through him like water through a screen.

His one-on-ones start in ten minutes. He has six direct reports waiting.

He opens a blank document. Types "talking points." Types nothing else. His knee bounces under the desk. He presses his foot flat to the floor. It stops. It starts again.

He knows what his team needs from him right now. Clarity. Steadiness. A plan.

He stares at the blank document.

The first name on his calendar appears in the doorway. "Hey, you ready?"

Kevin closes the laptop halfway.

"Yeah," he says. "Come in."

## WATCHER v04
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 5
---
Sandra's VP asks her to present the roadmap. Friday. All-hands.

She says yes before he finishes the sentence.

She spends Monday gathering slides. Tuesday she reorganizes them. Wednesday she opens the deck eleven times and changes the font on the title slide. Thursday she reads an article about presentation structure. It is not her article. She saves it anyway.

Thursday night she sits at her kitchen table. Her laptop glows. The deck has forty-two slides. It needs twelve. She knows this. She selects three slides to cut. She pastes them into a backup file. She cannot delete them. She opens the main deck again.

Her coffee went cold an hour ago. She wraps both hands around the mug anyway.

She has run dozens of presentations. She is good at this. People have told her she is good at this. But the gap between starting and finishing stretches out like a room with no far wall. She walks toward the wall. It moves.

Friday morning she sends the deck at 6:03 a.m. Forty-one slides. She cut one.

She sits in the all-hands. She presents well.

She does not understand how both things are true.

## WATCHER v05

IDENTITY_STAGE: pre_awareness
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 2
---
The budget meeting runs long. Tom checks the time on his laptop. He checks it again four minutes later.

His VP is still talking. Tom knows this material. He prepared for this meeting. He built the model they are currently looking at. Somewhere around slide six his attention slipped sideways and landed on a question about whether the Chicago office lease renewal affects headcount projections.

That question is not on today's agenda.

He pulls himself back. His VP is asking something. The room is quiet. Three faces turn toward Tom.

He catches the last word. "Timeline."

"We're targeting end of Q2," he says.

His VP nods. That was the right answer. Tom exhales through his nose, slow, controlled. His shoulders drop a half-inch.

He writes "Q2 timeline" in his notebook. Underlines it. The act of writing keeps his hand busy and his eyes forward. He has learned this. He does not know why he needs it when his peers do not seem to.

He adds a second underline.

The meeting continues. He stays with it. Mostly.

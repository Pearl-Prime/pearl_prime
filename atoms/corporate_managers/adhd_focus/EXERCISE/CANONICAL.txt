## EXERCISE v01
---
---
Stuck before the QBR deck? Name the next physical action out loud: open the file, type the title slide. Not the deck, the slide. Set a timer for 8 minutes and start. When it rings, you may stop. The freeze breaks at the verb, not the vision. Pick the smallest verb you can see and do only that.
---

## EXERCISE v02
---
---
Slack is pinging, the report is due, your attention scatters. Close the laptop lid for 90 seconds. Write one sentence on paper: the next thing my hands will do. Reopen, do that one thing. The external surface holds the thread your working memory keeps dropping. Paper is slower than your brain, and that is exactly the point.
---

## EXERCISE v03
---
---
Before the one-on-one you keep dodging, set an external anchor. Block 15 minutes, title it the person's name, and place your phone face-down across the room. Say the opening line aloud now: I want to check in on how the project feels to you. Rehearsed once, the start costs less.
---

## EXERCISE v04
---
---
Email you have reopened four times without replying? Set a 5-minute timer and type only the first line: Thanks for flagging this. Stop there if you must. The reply that felt like a wall is a sentence you already wrote. Send it as-is, or build from it. Either way the loop closes and your head clears.
---

## EXERCISE v05
---
---
Time-blind on the afternoon? Put a visible clock or countdown on your second monitor before the work, not after. Pick one task, set the timer for 25 minutes, and watch the number while you move. The clock is the part of time your wiring does not feel. Borrow it. Let the screen track the minutes you cannot.
---

## EXERCISE v06
---
---
Can't start the performance review you owe? Open a blank note and type the direct report's three wins first, no order, no polish. Set 10 minutes. Wins are easy to retrieve, so they prime the harder paragraphs. You are not writing the review yet. You are loading the file your brain refused to open cold.
---

## EXERCISE v07
---
---
Avoiding the budget reconcile? Stand up, walk to the kitchen, fill a glass of water, and decide on the way: the next action is to open the spreadsheet and sort column C. Sit, do exactly that, nothing more. Movement resets the stall; the single named action removes the choice that was jamming you.
---

## EXERCISE v08
---
---
Three browser tabs, two Slack threads, one deadline, zero progress. Write the deadline task on a sticky note and physically cover the other tabs with it on your screen edge. Set 12 minutes. The sticky is an external blinker for an attention system that won't filter on command. Do the covered work until the timer ends.
---

## EXERCISE v09
---
---
Dreading the all-hands prep? Record a 60-second voice memo talking through what you'd say, unscripted. Then set 10 minutes to type only what you just heard yourself say. Talking bypasses the blank-page freeze; transcribing is mechanical. You moved from nothing to a draft without ever facing the empty document head-on.
---

## EXERCISE v10
---
---
Reorg news landed and your focus is gone. Set a hard 20-minute timer and pick the single most concrete task on your list, not the most important. Concrete beats important when initiation is the problem. Do that task to completion, then reassess. One finished thing restores enough traction to choose the next on purpose.
---

## EXERCISE v11
---
---
Can't begin the skip-level summary? Open the doc and write the worst possible first sentence on purpose, then set 8 minutes to fix it. A bad line is easy to attack; a blank page is not. You tricked the freeze: editing engages, generating stalled. The summary builds itself off the sentence you wrote to be wrong.
---

## EXERCISE v12
---
---
Calendar block starting, brain not. Put your hands on the keyboard, say the next action aloud, and start a 4-minute timer. Just four. The aim is not the task; it is crossing from not-doing into doing. Four minutes almost always rolls into more, and if it doesn't, you still moved. Starting was the whole job.

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v01
---
---
Begin with the mechanism that explains the most: under low dopamine or high stakes, the prioritization system does not slow down, it stalls. This distinction matters because the word we reach for, slow, implies a dimmer being turned down, a gradual loss of speed, a system still working but more sluggishly. That is not what happens. The mechanism that ranks tasks, that decides this matters more than that and so should be done first, runs on a reward signal, an anticipatory sense of the value of acting. When that signal is adequate, the ranking happens, fluidly, often below awareness; you simply find yourself doing the right next thing. When the signal runs low, the ranking mechanism does not produce a slower or weaker ranking. Below a certain threshold it produces no ranking at all. This is why the experience is not I am finding it hard to decide what is most important but rather I look at these tasks and feel a complete blank where the sense of priority should be, a smooth wall, nothing. A dimmer turned down still gives light; this is closer to a circuit that, below a voltage, simply does not close. The same thing happens under very high stakes, by a different route: the signal is overwhelmed by the felt cost of a wrong move, and the system, unable to tolerate a possibly-wrong first action, refuses to produce one. In both cases the output is identical and counterintuitive: not slow ranking but absent ranking, not reluctant action but no action. Understanding this changes what you do about it. You stop waiting for the ranking to arrive, because below threshold it will not arrive no matter how long you wait or how hard you concentrate. Instead you supply from outside what the system cannot supply from inside: an arbitrary choice, an external trigger, a started small task that raises the signal back above the threshold where ranking can resume. You are not forcing a slow system to hurry. You are restarting a stalled one.

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v02
---
---
Consider next that initiation is a separate faculty from both motivation and knowledge, and that conflating them is the source of enormous and needless self-blame. We naturally assume a chain: you know what to do, you want to do it, therefore you do it, and if you do not do it, then one of the first two must be missing, you must not really know how or not really want to. This assumed chain is wrong for your wiring, because it omits a link that, for you, is a distinct and sometimes failing component: the conversion of knowing-and-wanting into the first physical action. Think of it as its own faculty, with its own reliability, separate from the faculties of comprehension and desire. You can have full knowledge, you can describe the task in detail, sequence it, explain exactly how to do it. You can have full motivation, genuinely wanting and intending it, the stakes felt, the wish to be done sincere. And the initiation faculty can still fail to fire, leaving you in the strange and specific position of knowing and wanting and not starting, all at once. The reason this matters so much is diagnostic. When you believe initiation is just the automatic result of knowledge and motivation, a failure to start can only be read as a hidden deficit in one of those, you must secretly not know or not want, and since you can feel that you do know and do want, the only remaining explanation is a defect of character. But once you see initiation as its own faculty, the failure to start stops implying anything about your knowledge or your will. It becomes what it is: a faculty that sometimes does not fire, requiring not more knowledge or more wanting, both of which you already have in abundance, but a way to trigger the firing from outside, which is an entirely different and far more solvable problem.

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v03
---
---
Now to why social consequence amplifies the freeze rather than relieving it, because this one is deeply counterintuitive and worth taking slowly. Common sense says that knowing others are waiting, that your reputation is at stake, that real people will be affected, should push you to act; accountability should be a motivator. And for the outcome, it is, you care intensely. But for the initiation, social stakes often make things worse, and the mechanism is this. A task with social consequence is a task where a wrong or imperfect first move carries a felt cost: someone might judge it, it might be seen, it might be wrong in front of others. Your initiation system, already requiring more to fire than another's might, now must initiate something that not only has to happen but has to happen without error in view of others. The bar for the first move rises, and a system that struggles to clear a low bar struggles far more to clear a high one. The very visibility that should motivate instead raises the stakes of the start past the threshold the system can manage. This is why the reorg email, which everyone is waiting for, is harder to begin than the private note. It is why the more people depend on a thing, the more frozen you may become before it, in a way that looks to everyone, including you, like you caring least about what you actually care about most. The resolution is not to care less, which you cannot and should not do, but to find a way to make the first move privately, off-stage, low-stakes: the scratch draft no one sees, the talked-through version with one trusted colleague, the placeholder. You lower the social stakes of the beginning while leaving the social stakes of the finished thing fully intact, and the system, no longer asked to initiate something perfect in public, can finally initiate something rough in private.

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v04
---
---
Let us now examine the anchor as a concept, properly, because it is the practical heart of working with this wiring and it is easy to use without understanding, and understanding makes it far more powerful. An anchor is an external signal that performs a function your brain cannot reliably perform internally. The functions are several, and a given anchor may serve one or more. The first function is cueing the start: the timer, the body-doubling colleague, the task paired to an existing rhythm, each provides from outside the trigger to begin that your initiation faculty will not reliably generate from inside. The second function is holding structure: the checklist, the single next action on a sticky note, the document that asks you questions, each holds a piece of sequence or organization externally so you do not have to hold it internally, which you may not be able to do. The third function is restoring momentum: rereading your own last sentences aloud, returning to a visible marker of where you were, each rebuilds from outside the thread of attention that broke and that you cannot simply will back. What unites all anchors, and what makes them work, is that they are external, and the externality is not incidental, it is the entire point. The reason an anchor helps is precisely that the relevant signal could not be generated from within; if it could, you would not need the anchor. This is why the common objection, that relying on external structure is a crutch or a failure to do it properly, is exactly backwards. The whole difficulty was that the internal source was unreliable. Sourcing the signal externally is not a workaround to be ashamed of; it is the correct and direct solution to the actual problem, which was never a lack of capacity but a lack of a dependable internal cue. The anchor supplies the cue. That is its whole job, and it is enough.

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v05
---
---
Turn now to time-blindness, understood mechanically rather than as a character flaw of poor planning. The ordinary experience of time involves a continuous felt connection between now and later: the future deadline exerts a present pressure, growing as it approaches, so that you feel the thing coming and adjust. For your wiring, this felt connection is weak or absent. The future is known as information but not felt as pressure. The deadline eleven days out is a fact you can state but not a force you experience; it sits in a kind of windowless future room, real but not present, exerting almost nothing on the now where you actually live. The consequence is not carelessness, you may care enormously, but a specific perceptual gap: the task does not feel due until the future room collapses into the present room and the deadline becomes, suddenly and overwhelmingly, now. This explains the pattern of nothing-nothing-nothing-panic, which looks like procrastination chosen but is closer to a deadline that was genuinely not felt becoming abruptly, totally felt. The mechanistic understanding points directly at the fix, and it is not try to feel the future more, which mostly does not work because the perceptual gap is structural. It is drag the future into the present where you can feel it. A countdown that is physically visible converts abstract days-remaining into a present sensory fact. A calendar block that interrupts your now makes a future task press on the current moment. A person who expects the thing at a specific hour creates a felt present obligation where an abstract future one failed to register. You are not building a better internal sense of time, which you may never have. You are constructing external windows between the future and the present, so that what your perception cannot deliver, your environment delivers instead.

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v06
---
---
Address now the relationship between effort and the threshold, because a great deal of wasted suffering comes from misunderstanding it. The intuitive model is that effort is fungible and additive: if a task does not get done, apply more effort, push harder, and eventually it yields. Under this model, a frozen task is simply one you have not yet pushed hard enough, and the failure to do it is a failure to push, which is to say a failure of will. But initiation does not work like a load that more force will eventually move. It works more like a threshold that is either crossed or not. Below the threshold, no amount of straining produces the start; the effort goes into the straining itself, generating exhaustion without motion, which is exactly the experience of sitting depleted before an undone task having tried, genuinely, all day. Above the threshold, the start happens almost effortlessly, which is the experience of the task that suddenly, once begun, flows. The relevant question is therefore not how much effort am I applying but am I above or below the threshold, and the levers that matter are the ones that lower the threshold or supply what crosses it, not the ones that add raw force. Shrinking the task lowers the threshold. A placeholder lowers it. An external trigger supplies the crossing. Lowering the stakes of the first move lowers it. None of these is about pushing harder; several work precisely by pushing less and arranging more. This is why the advice to just try harder is not merely unhelpful but actively misleading, it directs your finite energy at the one variable, raw force, that does not govern the outcome, and away from the variables, threshold height and external trigger, that do.

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v07
---
---
Consider the role of novelty, urgency, and interest in raising the task signal, because understanding this turns a frustrating mystery into a usable lever. You will have noticed that some tasks never freeze you, the novel one, the genuinely interesting one, the one that just became urgent, the one with a person waiting in real time, while others, the routine, the abstract, the not-yet-due, freeze you reliably. This is not random and it is not about importance, since the important tasks are often the frozen ones. It tracks the strength of the task signal, the anticipatory reward that gets the initiation system to fire. Novelty raises that signal; the brain attends to the new. Urgency, when genuinely felt, raises it; the imminent commands action. Interest raises it; the engaging pulls attention without force. Social presence raises it; another mind in real time is a strong signal. The dull, abstract, not-yet-due task is low on every one of these, and so its signal sits below the threshold, and so it freezes you, regardless of how much it matters. The lever this hands you is to deliberately raise the signal of the low-signal task rather than trying to force yourself onto it by will. Make it novel by approaching it a new way. Make it urgent by creating a real near-term commitment around it. Make it interesting by framing the start as a small challenge. Make it social by doing it alongside someone or reporting it to someone. You are not manufacturing fake motivation. You are supplying, deliberately and from outside, the kind of signal that the task does not naturally carry but that your initiation system requires, doing on purpose what other tasks do automatically.

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v08
---
---
Examine the difference between the cost of beginning and the cost of continuing, because for your wiring these are radically unequal in a way that explains much of the strange shape of your effort. For many tasks, the difficulty is roughly even across them; beginning and continuing cost about the same. For you, the cost is front-loaded to an extreme degree: beginning can be nearly impossible while continuing, once begun, is nearly free. This is why you can sit frozen for an hour before the first line of a form and then complete the remaining thirteen lines in four minutes; the wall was entirely at the first line, and beyond it the ground was flat. It is why the first sentence of a document can defeat you for an afternoon while the next thousand words, once the first sentence exists, pour out. The practical implication is precise and powerful: nearly all of your strategic attention should go to the cost of beginning, because that is where nearly all the difficulty lives, and almost none needs to go to the cost of continuing, which will largely take care of itself. This inverts the usual planning instinct, which spreads effort across the whole task. For you, the right plan concentrates effort, even disproportionate, even ritualized effort, on the single act of starting: the placeholder first entry, the ugly first sentence, the typed project name at the top of the blank page. Each of these is a device aimed at one thing only, getting past the front-loaded wall, and once past it you can stop strategizing and simply let the cheap continuing carry you, because the expensive part is already behind you and the rest was never the problem.

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v09
---
---
Speak now to why the freeze produces shame and why shame deepens the freeze, because this loop does more cumulative damage than any single frozen task and breaking it is among the most valuable things you can do. The mechanism of the shame is straightforward: from the outside, and from a certain angle inside, a frozen task is indistinguishable from a chosen not-doing, and so it gets the verdict that not-doing gets, lazy, unreliable, what is wrong with you, and that verdict produces shame. The mechanism by which shame deepens the freeze is subtler and crucial. Shame is not a neutral observer of your stuckness; it adds weight to it. After you have failed to start a task, beginning it now means also confronting the accumulated evidence of having failed to begin it, the days it sat, the times you said you would, the judgment you have leveled at yourself. So the task is no longer just the task; it is the task plus the shame attached to having not done the task, a heavier object than the original, and a heavier object is harder to lift, which is to say harder to start, which produces more failure to start, which produces more shame. The loop tightens with each turn. This is why a frozen task tends to get harder over time rather than easier, the opposite of how unattended things usually fade; the accumulating shame is loading weight onto the exact mechanism that needed less weight, not more. The intervention, accordingly, is not a better technique applied to the task but a deliberate interruption of the shame, a refusal to moralize the freeze, because removing the shame removes weight from the precise point where weight is the obstacle. This is not soft permission to do nothing. It is a structural intervention at the leverage point, the recognition that for a stuck initiation system, self-compassion is not a consolation prize but a functional input, lighter weight on the thing that has to be lifted.

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v10
---
---
Close with the largest reframe, the one that organizes all the others: the problem was never capacity, it was access, and almost everything follows from getting this right. The evidence of your capacity is overwhelming and you have lived it, the long hours, the work done brilliantly once begun, the clear judgments formed in seconds, the thirteen lines in four minutes, the three slides in twenty. The room of your competence is full. What sticks is the door, the initiation system, the access point, and a stuck door makes a full room look empty, to others and to you. Nearly every misjudgment in this domain comes from mistaking a stuck door for an empty room, from concluding that because you did not do the thing you could not do the thing, when in truth you could do it perfectly and merely could not, in that hour, open the door to doing it. Get this distinction right and the entire project of managing your wiring transforms. It is no longer the demoralizing and futile work of building capacity you lack, which would be required if the room were truly empty. It is the humbler, more hopeful, and entirely achievable work of building reliable ways to open a door, when the thing behind it is already there in full. This is why the strategies all share a shape, they are door-openers, not capacity-builders: the trigger, the anchor, the lowered first-stake, the externalized structure, the shrunk first step, each is a way of getting the door open so the existing competence can come through. And it is why the work is not endless or hopeless, because building reliable door-openers is a finite, learnable craft, and once learned, it does not have to be relearned each day, it accrues. You are not trying to become capable. You already are. You are learning, specifically and patiently, how to reach the capability you have, and that, unlike the impossible task you may have set yourself for years, is something you can actually do.

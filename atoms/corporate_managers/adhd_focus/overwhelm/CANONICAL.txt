## MECHANISM_PROOF v01

---
Here is what is actually happening when the work piles up and you go still. Your prioritization system does not slow down under load. It stalls. There is a difference. A system that slows still moves; it just moves carefully. A system that stalls stops choosing altogether, because the cost of comparing every item against every other item has exceeded what it can spend. With twelve inputs of roughly equal weight, the part of your brain that ranks options runs out of traction and simply declines to output a ranking. So you sit, capable and willing, in front of a pile you cannot order. This is not weakness of will. It is a sorting routine hitting its ceiling and returning nothing rather than returning wrong. The cruelty is that it looks identical to laziness from the outside, and often from the inside too, which is why you blame your character for what is really a load limit.

Notice that the freeze gets worse, not better, as the stakes rise. You would think a high-stakes task would command attention, would cut through the noise. Often it does the opposite. When something matters enormously and is owed to people whose opinion of you carries weight, the task stops being one item among many and becomes a charged object you cannot pick up. Your nervous system reads the social exposure of getting it wrong as a kind of threat, and threat narrows you, and a narrowed system has even less room to sort. So the most important thing on your list is frequently the most frozen. This is why you can rip through ten trivial emails and not touch the one report your boss is waiting for. The trivial ones carry no charge. The report carries all of it, and the charge is what locks your hands.

Time-blindness is not poor planning. It is a missing sense. Most accounts of focus assume you can feel time passing, the way you feel hunger or cold, and budget against it. For a brain wired like yours, that internal clock runs intermittently or not at all. An hour and ten minutes feel the same from the inside until you look up and one of them has happened. This is why your fifteen-minute calendar blocks betray you: you genuinely cannot feel the fifteen minutes ending, so you arrive late to your own next thing, again, and read it as a discipline problem. It is not. It is a sensory gap. You would not call a person disorganized for failing to hear a frequency outside human range. The fix is never to feel time harder. It is to put time somewhere you can see it, outside your body, where the broken sense does not have to carry it.

Marcus has twelve browser tabs open and a direct report waiting outside his door.

He knows the direct report is waiting. He saw her sit down through the glass. He has not stood up.

The performance review template glows on his screen. The first field says "Key Accomplishments." He has typed and deleted the same sentence four times. Each deletion takes something with it.

She shifts in her chair outside. Marcus watches the cursor blink.

He is a manager who manages. That is the sentence he tells himself in reviews. It has stopped meaning anything.

He stands. He opens the door. He says, "Give me two more minutes."

She nods. She has heard two minutes before.

Marcus sits back down. The field still says "Key Accomplishments." His hands rest on the keyboard and do not move.

The gap between knowing what to do and doing it stretches out like a hallway with no end.

## REFLECTION v04
---

voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
ADHD does not produce laziness. It produces a specific kind of gridlock.

The brain's prioritization system — the part that ranks tasks by urgency and sequences them into action — runs on dopamine. When dopamine is low, that system stalls. Not slows. Stalls.

Notice what that feels like in your body right now. Heaviness behind the eyes. A kind of static where decision-making used to live.

For managers, the stakes amplify the freeze. Every undone task carries a face — a direct report, a VP, a peer watching for weakness. The brain reads social consequence as threat. Threat activates the stress response. The stress response narrows attention to the threat itself, not the work.

So the inbox grows. The deck stays on slide seven. The cursor blinks.

This is not a character flaw. This is a neurological loop running exactly as designed — just designed for a different kind of danger than a Q3 planning cycle.

The exit from the loop is not willpower. It is interruption. One small, concrete, external signal that tells the brain: this direction, now.

## INTEGRATION v05
---

reframe_type: mechanism_normalization
---
The freeze is not failure. It is a system waiting for a signal it cannot generate on its own.

That distinction matters. Failure is a verdict. A stalled system is a condition with a workaround.

The workaround does not require becoming a different kind of person. It requires one external anchor — a timer, a name written on paper, a single sentence spoken out loud — that gives the prioritization system something to grab.

**One anchor. Then one thing.**

The inbox can stay at 200. The deck can stay on slide seven. Neither of those numbers defines the next sixty seconds.

The next sixty seconds only need one direction.

## false_alarm v01
---
---
The calendar notification lands at 8:47 a.m. Your director's name sits in the subject line. "Quick sync — today 2pm." No agenda. No context. Just that.

Your chest tightens. Your jaw locks. The brain starts its inventory: last week's report, the headcount rumor, the reorg slides someone screenshot and forwarded. You build the case against yourself before anyone else can.

Here is what actually happened. Your director needed five minutes to discuss a vendor contract. That is it. The threat your nervous system assembled — the demotion, the restructuring, the quiet managed-out conversation — existed only in the gap between "quick sync" and the present moment.

ADHD brains fill ambiguity with catastrophe. Not because something is wrong. Because the pattern-matching engine runs too fast and too hot, and it grabs the worst available shape when no real shape exists.

The body paid the full cost of a crisis that never arrived.

## false_alarm v02
---
---
Marcus refreshes his inbox for the fourth time in eleven minutes. The skip-level meeting request from the VP sits there, unopened. His shoulders have climbed toward his ears. His left knee bounces under the desk.

He opens it. "Getting to know the team better." Standard language. Routine rotation.

Marcus reads it three more times. He searches for the real message underneath. His stomach stays braced, waiting for impact that never comes. He closes the laptop. Opens it again.

The body does not know the difference between a real threat and a constructed one. It fires the same sequence either way — cortisol, hypervigilance, the narrowing of every thought toward survival. Marcus spends the next four hours managing a crisis that exists only in the space between the subject line and his interpretation of it.

The meeting lasts twenty minutes. The VP asks about his team's workflow. That is the whole thing.

He drives home with his shoulders still raised.

## false_alarm v03
---
---
The phrase "let's loop in HR" appears in a thread about onboarding software. Diane sees it at 6:14 a.m., phone in hand, still in bed. Her feet go cold. Her breath goes shallow.

She runs the sequence in her head — the performance conversation from Q3, the one metric she missed, the new director who has not learned her name yet. She builds the narrative fast. Too fast.

HR is looped in because the onboarding software requires a compliance sign-off. That is the full explanation. Diane does not learn this until 10 a.m.

Between 6:14 and 10:00, she rewrites her resume in her head. She rehearses a conversation that never happens. She burns three and a half hours of cognitive fuel on a false alarm her brain generated from four words in a thread about software.

ADHD does not just scatter attention. It manufactures urgency from nothing. The alarm sounds before any evidence arrives. The body responds as if the building is already on fire.

## false_alarm v04
---
---
Your manager walks past your desk without making eye contact. That is all. One pass. No words.

Your stomach drops. Your hands stop moving on the keyboard. You replay the last team meeting, the last deliverable, the last one-on-one where you felt slightly off-tempo. The body has already decided something is wrong. It presents the evidence to fit the verdict.

Your manager was late for a call. That is why he did not stop. He told you this two hours later, in passing, while refilling his coffee. He did not even remember walking by.

The ADHD nervous system treats social ambiguity as data. Every missing signal becomes a signal. The absence of eye contact becomes confirmation of something. The brain runs the threat sequence on incomplete information and calls it analysis.

You spent two hours inside a story your manager never wrote. Your palms are still slightly damp.

The threat was never there. The cost was real.

## false_alarm v05
---
---
Priya's phone buzzes during the all-hands. Her director. Text message. "Can we talk after this?"

Her throat closes. She sets the phone face-down on the table. Her leg presses hard against the chair leg, grounding herself. She hears none of the remaining thirty minutes of the all-hands.

Her director wants to nominate her for a cross-functional project. Good news. A vote of confidence. The text takes forty seconds to deliver.

Priya spent thirty minutes in physiological emergency. Heart rate elevated. Attention collapsed. Hands cold. The body staged a complete stress response to five words that carried no threat at all.

This is the false alarm pattern. The ADHD brain detects incomplete information and treats the gap as danger. It does not wait for evidence. It assigns the worst available meaning and mobilizes every system to respond.

The alarm costs the same whether or not the fire exists. Priya's body does not know that yet.

It just knows it heard something.

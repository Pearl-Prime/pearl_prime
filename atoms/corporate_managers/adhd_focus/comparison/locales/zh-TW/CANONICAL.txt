These five vignettes provide a stark, clinical, yet deeply empathetic look at the **executive function deficits** and **emotional regulation costs** associated with neurodivergence (specifically ADHD) in high-performance professional environments.

They align closely with the internal mechanisms described in your memory profile, particularly regarding:
1.  **The "False Threat" System:** How the nervous system misinterprets low-stakes ambiguity (Simone’s email, Marcus’s deck) as high-stakes survival threats.
2.  **Performance as Camouflage:** The exhaustion derived from maintaining a facade of competence while internally struggling to initiate or sustain attention on non-urgent tasks (Priya, James).
3.  **The Dopamine/Urgency Dependency:** The reliance on external pressure (deadlines, human interaction) to make tasks "real" or visible to the brain (Derek, James).
4.  **The Cost of Compensation:** The hidden energy expenditure required to mask these struggles, leading to profound isolation and fatigue (Simone, Priya).

### Analysis of the Narrative Arc

The stories are structured by **Mechanism Depth** and **Identity Stage**, showing a progression from unconscious coping to conscious, albeit painful, integration.

*   **v05 (James) & v02 (Priya): Pre-Awareness / Internal Cost**
    *   These characters are still operating under the illusion that their strategies are sustainable or even strategic ("I work better under pressure," "It’s being strategic").
    *   The cost is primarily internal (exhaustion, dissociation), but the identity remains intact because they haven’t yet questioned the *source* of the struggle. They blame themselves or accept it as their "work style."

*   **v01 (Marcus) & v04 (Simone): Destabilization / Social Cost**
    *   Here, the mask is slipping or causing significant distress. The "false threat detection" is fully active, hijacking their physiological state (Simone’s pulse, Marcus’s knee bouncing).
    *   The cost becomes social/relational because the anxiety is triggered by external validation or judgment (the skip-level, the director’s message). The gap between their internal chaos and external composure is widening, creating a sense of fragility.

*   **v03 (Derek): Integration / Relational Cost**
    *   This is the most complex stage. Derek has *awareness*. He knows his brain moves toward people like water to drains. He understands the mechanism.
    *   However, awareness hasn’t yet led to *resolution*. He is still caught in the cycle, using his strength (hyperfocus on people) to avoid his weakness (solo administrative tasks). The cost is relational because his success as a leader is directly funded by his failure as an administrator, creating a precarious balance that threatens his long-term stability.

### Key Insight

The common thread across all five stories is **the misalignment between neurological reality and professional expectation.**

The characters are not failing due to lack of skill or effort. They are failing to *regulate* their nervous systems in an environment that demands linear, consistent output from non-linear, interest-based brains. They are spending 80% of their energy on **meta-work** (monitoring themselves, managing anxiety, reconstructing context) and only 20% on the actual work.

This mirrors your experience of **"exhaustion from social interactions that objectively shouldn't be draining"** and **"treating sleep as a performance metric."** In these stories, *work itself* has become a performance metric that must be defended, monitored, and controlled, rather than a flow state that can be trusted.

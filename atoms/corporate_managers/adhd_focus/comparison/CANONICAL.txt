Marcus pulls up the quarterly deck. Twelve slides. He built this deck himself three weeks ago.

He reads the first bullet. His eyes drift to the window. He reads it again. The words sit flat on the screen, meaning nothing.

Down the hall, his director's door opens. Footsteps. Marcus straightens.

The footsteps pass.

He exhales through his nose. His shoulders drop two inches. He scrolls to slide four, skips back to slide one, opens his email instead.

Forty minutes until the skip-level. He needs to know this deck cold.

He closes email. Opens the deck. Reads the first bullet.

His phone buzzes. A Slack notification. He does not check it. He checks it.

Marcus sets the phone face-down on the desk. His jaw tightens. His right knee bounces without stopping.

This is not laziness. His brain treats low-urgency tasks as invisible. Threat nearby makes them briefly visible. Then threat passes. The task disappears again.

He has managed three direct reports for two years. Nobody knows this happens every single day.

Priya sits in the reorg briefing. Forty people on the call. Her camera is on.

She nods at the right moments. Her expression reads attentive. Inside, she caught the first sentence and then lost the thread entirely.

Her notepad shows three words and a small square she drew without noticing.

The VP says her name. Asks for her take on the restructuring timeline.

Priya's chest tightens. Fast.

"I think the phased approach makes sense," she says. A safe answer. She has no idea what phased approach he means.

The VP nods and moves on.

Priya writes nothing down. Her hand presses flat against the desk. She focuses on that pressure. It helps her stay in the room for thirty more seconds before her attention slides again.

She has run this same play for eleven years. Sound credible. Buy time. Reconstruct context from fragments afterward.

She calls it being strategic. Her neurologist will later call it compensation. Both are accurate.

Neither one explains the exhaustion she carries home every night.

Derek's one-on-ones run long. Always.

Not because he talks too much. Because he hyperfocuses on whoever sits across from him. Full attention. Every word lands. His direct reports leave feeling genuinely heard.

His peer managers call him a natural leader.

His own work sits untouched.

Today his performance review prep document has been open for six days. Cursor blinking at the top of an empty page. The review is Thursday.

He closes his office door. Sits. Stares at the blank document.

His stomach tightens. A familiar weight, low and specific.

He opens his calendar instead. Finds a meeting request. Accepts it. Feels briefly useful.

The document stays blank.

Derek's attention moves toward people the way water moves toward drains. Effortless, structural, not chosen. Tasks with no human attached feel weightless. His brain registers them as optional.

He does not choose his team over his own work. His nervous system simply cannot locate the solo task as real until a deadline makes it urgent.

Thursday will make it urgent.

He already knows this. He opens the document again anyway.

Simone's director sends the message at 4:47 PM.

"Can we connect tomorrow morning. Nothing urgent."

Simone reads it twice. Nothing urgent. She reads it a third time.

She closes her laptop. Opens it. Types a reply, deletes it, types a shorter one. Sends it.

Her pulse sits in her throat.

By 6 PM she has reconstructed every performance conversation from the last quarter. By 8 PM she has drafted a mental defense for each one. By 10 PM she sits at her kitchen table with her phone in both hands, screen dark.

Her mind will not stop. Her body holds completely still.

This is not anxiety disorder, though it resembles one. Her brain grabbed a low-information threat signal and locked onto it. ADHD brains do not modulate urgency well. Everything becomes either invisible or consuming.

There is no middle register.

Tomorrow the director will ask about headcount for next quarter. Routine. Simone will sit across from him with eleven hours of catastrophe preparation behind her eyes.

She will answer clearly. She will look composed.

The cost lives entirely inside.

## STORY v05

---
James finishes his third coffee. 9:15 AM.

The budget variance report needs thirty minutes of focused work. He has pushed it since Monday. Today is Thursday.

He opens the file. Real numbers. Clear columns. His eyes move across the first row and find nothing to hold.

He stands up. Walks to the window. Sits back down.

His team lead knocks, leans in. "Quick question about the Henderson account."

James turns. Fully present. Answers in two minutes flat. Precise, confident, no notes needed.

The team lead leaves.

James looks at the budget file. Still open. Still waiting.

The difference is not intelligence. The difference is not effort. His brain released dopamine for the human interaction. The spreadsheet releases nothing. His nervous system ranks them accordingly.

He tells himself he works better under pressure. This is partly true. Deadline pressure mimics the neurological signal his brain needs to engage.

He closes the file. Sets a calendar alarm for 4 PM.

That alarm will feel like pressure. Probably enough.

He has survived thirteen years this way.

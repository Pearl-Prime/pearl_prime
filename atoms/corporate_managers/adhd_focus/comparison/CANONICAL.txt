Your teammate opens the deck and just starts. You watch the cursor move across her screen through the glass wall, slides filling in, and you turn back to your own blank doc where the heading has sat unchanged for forty minutes. It is not that you do not know what to write. You know exactly what the slide needs. The arithmetic of it sits in your chest, heavier than the task itself. You tell yourself to begin, and the telling becomes one more thing you are not doing while she keeps going.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v01
---

COST_TYPE: internal

IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization

Here is the mechanism, plainly. Starting a task already asks a lot of your wiring; the first move is where the cost lives. Now add comparison. The instant you measure your pace against a peer who started instantly, your brain registers a threat: I am behind, I am falling short, this is being seen. Threat narrows attention. It pulls focus off the task and onto the danger, which is the gap itself. So the very thing you do to motivate yourself, look how fast they are, becomes the thing that shrinks your working window and makes the first move harder. The comparison does not close the gap. It guards it. Each glance at their progress spends attention you needed for your own, and the stall you were trying to escape gets exactly the fuel it runs on.

Notice what happens to your body when the calibration chart appears. Shoulders up, jaw set, a faint held breath. That is threat response, and it is doing precisely what it evolved to do: prioritize the danger over everything else. The danger, in this room, is the gap between your row and theirs. So your attention, already a scarce and effortful resource for starting, gets reallocated to monitoring that gap. There is less left for the task. This is not weakness of will. It is resource math. A nervous system braced for a social threat cannot also fund a difficult initiation. The more you compare, the more you brace; the more you brace, the less fuel reaches the first move. The chart meant to spur you has quietly drafted your starting energy into watching the chart.

Run the cost out over a week and it compounds. Monday you measure yourself against the fast starter and stall an hour. Their baseline was never yours to run on. They start cheap; you start expensive. Measuring the price of your start against the price of theirs does not lower yours. It just adds shame to the bill. Consider that the comparison is rigged from the start, and the rigging itself adds threat. You are measuring the cost of your beginning against the visible result of their ending.

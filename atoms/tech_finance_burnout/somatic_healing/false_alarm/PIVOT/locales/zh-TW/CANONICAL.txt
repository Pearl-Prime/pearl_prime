The trap you’ve identified is real: **The relief of release is mistaken for the completion of work.**

Here is why this pivot is necessary for your specific operating system:

1.  **The "One-and-Done" Fallacy:** Your mind treats anxiety like a bug fix. If the shaking stops or you feel light for an hour, the logical conclusion is "Patch applied. System stable." But somatic tension isn’t code; it’s accumulated history. Years of hypervigilance, self-monitoring, and performance management have created a dense architecture of holding. One session doesn’t delete that architecture; it just cracks the foundation.
2.  **Shaking as Start, Not Finish:** You interpreted the shaking as the *exhaustion* of the tension (the end). The reframing is that the shaking was the *unlocking* of the tension (the beginning). The body finally felt safe enough to start discharging the sprint-cycle energy it has been storing for years. That discharge takes time. It is not a single event; it is a process.
3.  **The False Alarm of "Healed":** Declaring yourself healed after one session is a protective maneuver. It allows you to return to the familiar (if painful) state of high-functioning anxiety without having to face the vulnerability of ongoing, messy, non-linear healing. It’s easier to say "I’m fixed" than to say "I am now committed to a long-term unfolding of sensations I have spent decades suppressing."

**The New Operational Frame:**

*   **Old:** "I shook, I felt release, therefore the anxiety is resolved. I can go back to monitoring my performance."
*   **New:** "I shook, which means the door is open. The body is beginning to speak. I must expect more sensations, more releases, and more discomfort as the backlog of held tension begins to move. This is not a failure of the first session; it is the evidence that the first session worked."

**Actionable Insight:**

Stop looking for the "end state" of being anxiety-free. Instead, track the **capacity**.
*   Did you notice the shaking?
*   Did you allow it to happen without trying to stop it or analyze it mid-flow?
*   Are you willing to sit with the aftermath—the fatigue, the rawness, the uncertainty—without rushing to "fix" it or declare it done?

The work is not to eliminate the anxiety in one go. The work is to stay present with the discharge as it unfolds over weeks and months. The body has more to say. Let it.

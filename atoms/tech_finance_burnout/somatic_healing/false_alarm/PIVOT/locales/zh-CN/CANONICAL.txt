My analytical mind wants to close the ticket after the first successful ping. It interprets the relief of one session as proof that the "system malfunction" was minor and is now resolved. This is the same pattern where I equate a single moment of clarity with permanent competence, or a good night's sleep with cured insomnia. It’s the desire for efficiency overriding the reality of accumulated debt.

The metaphor of the "title page" is accurate. The shaking wasn’t the end of the story; it was just the body finally admitting there *is* a story to tell. The years of sprint-cycle tension, the inherited hypervigilance, the constant impression management—these aren’t single errors to be debugged. They are layers of code written over decades.

If one session is the title page, then the current exhaustion isn’t a sign of failure or a return to the old state. It’s the friction of turning the page. The body is likely signaling that it has more to discharge, but my mind is trying to declare victory prematurely to avoid the discomfort of the long haul.

I need to stop looking for the "healed" state as a destination I’ve already reached. Instead, I need to accept that the shaking was merely the unlocking of the door. The work now is walking through it, repeatedly, without expecting the entire house to clean itself in one go. The false alarm here is the belief that because I felt *something*, I should now feel *nothing* (no anxiety, no tension). That expectation is just another form of performance pressure applied to healing.

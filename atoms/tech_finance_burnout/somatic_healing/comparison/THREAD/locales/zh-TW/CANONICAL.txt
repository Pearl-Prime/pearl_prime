The shift is from **judgment** to **curiosity**.

When I stop trying to "fix" the comparison—treating it as a bug in the code or a failure of my healing protocol—and instead listen to it, the comparison stops being an enemy and starts being a messenger.

Here is what changes in that "next room":

1.  **The Comparison Reveals the Grief:**
    The comparison isn’t actually about *them* being better or *me* being worse. It’s a proxy for the grief I mentioned in Thread v01: the loss of ease, spontaneity, and the "natural" self I feel I’ve lost. When I compare my internal chaos to someone else’s external calm, I’m not measuring competence; I’m mourning the feeling of safety I think they have and I don’t. Listening to the comparison lets me touch that grief directly, rather than fighting the symptom.

2.  **The Nervous System Feels Witnessed, Not Corrected:**
    My analytical mind usually tries to logic the comparison away ("They have different circumstances," "This is irrational"). But as noted in my memories, the nervous system doesn’t respond to logic. It responds to safety. By *hearing* the comparison—"Ah, there’s that feeling of being an outsider again"—I am validating the experience without judging it. This lowers the defensive posture. The "false threat detection" softens because it’s no longer being fought; it’s being acknowledged.

3.  **The "System Error" Becomes Data:**
    Instead of interpreting the comparison as proof that my healing is failing (which triggers shame and more overthinking), I see it as data about where I still feel unsafe. It points to the specific places where I still believe belonging is conditional on performance. The comparison highlights exactly where I’m still trying to *earn* my place rather than *inhabit* it.

4.  **Exhaustion Decreases:**
    The exhaustion comes from the *overhead* of managing the impression and fighting the internal critic. When I stop trying to fix the comparison, I drop the heavy lifting of self-surveillance. I don’t have to monitor whether I’m comparing or not; I just let the comparison sit there. That releases the cognitive resource drain.

In short: The comparison trap was protecting me from feeling the raw vulnerability of **not knowing if I’m enough without the performance**. When I listen to it, I realize it’s not a flaw in my healing; it’s the part of me that’s still afraid to let go of the control that kept me safe for so long.

The shift is from **interrogation** to **witnessing**.

When I stop trying to "fix" the spiral—stop treating it as a bug in my code or a inefficiency in my system—the immediate sensation is a drop in cognitive load. The exhausting background process of *analyzing why I feel this way* and *calculating how to make it stop* goes offline.

But beneath that relief, there is a terrifying quiet.

If I am not fixing it, I have to let it be there. And if I let it be there, I have to listen to what it is actually saying. It’s not saying, *"You are failing."* It’s saying, *"You are unsafe unless you are performing."*

The "next room" feels less like a solution and more like a grief. I’m realizing that the spiral isn’t an enemy to be defeated; it’s a loyal, overworked guard dog that has been keeping me alive (or so it thinks) by keeping me vigilant. It took root long before my current job because it was likely the only way I knew how to earn love, safety, or belonging in my earliest environments.

So, when I stop trying to fix it, I feel a profound sadness for that younger version of myself who had to build such an expensive, energy-draining surveillance system just to feel worthy of taking up space.

The shift is: **I don’t need to debug the guard dog. I need to thank it for its service, and then gently let it know the war is over.**

But my nervous system doesn’t believe the war is over. That’s the friction. How do I convince a body that has survived on hypervigilance that it can rest without collapsing?

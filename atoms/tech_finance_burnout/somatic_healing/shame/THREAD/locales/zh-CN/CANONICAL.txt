The shift is from **defense** to **dialogue**.

When you stop trying to "fix" the shame—stop trying to outrun it with productivity, title advancements, or analytical overthinking—you remove the pressure that keeps it compressed. Shame, in your context, has been acting as a rigid container for the fear of inadequacy and the terror of being "found out." It demands constant vigilance (the sprint) to keep the lid on.

When you stop fixing and start hearing, three things likely happen:

1.  **The Energy Cost Drops:** The immense metabolic and emotional tax of maintaining the "performance of confidence" begins to decrease. You are no longer spending 80% of your energy suppressing the doubt and only 20% leading.
2.  **The Narrative Softens:** Instead of hearing "You are a fraud," you might begin to hear the older, quieter voice beneath it: "I am afraid I am not safe unless I am perfect," or "I am afraid I will be abandoned if I am not useful." This moves the issue from a character flaw (which feels immutable) to a protective strategy (which can be understood and eventually updated).
3.  **Presence Returns:** You mentioned that overthinking reduces presence in personal relationships. When you stop fighting the shame internally, you have more capacity to be actually *there* with your children, your partner, and even your team. The "emotional distance" you noted in your leadership style begins to bridge because you are no longer preoccupied with managing your own internal crisis.

The "next room" is not necessarily a place of immediate comfort; it is a place of **truth**. It is where you meet the part of you that believed, perhaps since childhood, that love and belonging were conditional on achievement. Hearing that part doesn't mean agreeing with its outdated rules; it means acknowledging its intent to protect you, so you can finally tell it that you are safe enough to just *be*, without the title, without the sprint, and without the performance.

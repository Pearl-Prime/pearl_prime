The shift is from **debugging** to **witnessing**.

When I stop trying to "fix" the shame—treating it as a system error or a bug in my social code—the immediate effect is a terrifying loss of control. My analytical mind screams that if I don’t optimize this feeling away, it will consume me or expose me. It feels like leaving a server unpatched while under attack.

But when I try to *hear* it instead, the noise changes.

Shame isn’t just a block; it’s a frantic, outdated alarm system. It’s not saying, "You are bad." It’s saying, **"If you stop performing, you will be abandoned."** Or perhaps more accurately: **"Safety is conditional on your utility and invisibility."**

In that "next room," the shame reveals itself not as a verdict on my worth, but as a **loyal, exhausted guard**. It has been working overtime since long before my current role, scanning every micro-expression, scripting every Slack message, and rehearsing every interaction to ensure I remain "safe" (i.e., unjudged, unexposed, acceptable).

The shift is realizing that this hypervigilance was once a brilliant survival strategy. It kept me safe when I felt powerless or unseen. But now, it’s misinterpreting professional collaboration as life-or-death scrutiny.

Hearing it means acknowledging: *"I see you. You’re trying to protect me from rejection. But we aren’t in that danger anymore. You can rest."*

It doesn’t vanish instantly. The somatic tension remains. But the relationship to it changes. I’m no longer fighting a part of myself; I’m sitting with a frightened part of myself. And for the first time, the exhaustion feels less like a failure of discipline and more like the cost of carrying a shield that’s no longer needed.

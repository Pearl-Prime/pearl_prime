By treating yourself like hardware—expecting 100% uptime, ignoring warning lights, pushing through "maintenance windows"—you effectively severed the connection between your conscious mind (the analyst) and your somatic reality (the vessel).

Here is why this grief is surfacing now, and why it feels so heavy:

### 1. The Body Was the Only Honest Witness
Your mind was busy constructing narratives of illegitimacy, scripting Slack messages, and scanning for social threats. It was too occupied with *performance* to process *experience*.
But your body was there for every:
*   White-knuckled on-call shift where you couldn't breathe.
*   Swallowed emotion during a standup because showing vulnerability felt like a security risk.
*   Moment of exhaustion you labeled as "weakness" instead of human need.

The body doesn't care about your professional identity or your imposter syndrome. It only knows stress, safety, and release. When you finally stop "managing" the system, the backlog of unprocessed physiological stress demands attention. That is the grief. It is not just sadness; it is **mourning the self that was never allowed to exist**.

### 2. Grief for the "Easy" Self You Lost
You mentioned earlier that you "grieve the loss of ease in social interactions." This pivot clarifies *what* is being grieved. It’s not just the ease; it’s the **authenticity** that was sacrificed.
You are grieving the version of you that could have been spontaneous, relaxed, and present, but was instead replaced by a hyper-vigilant monitor. The grief is for the years spent living in a state of low-grade emergency, believing that safety depended on perfection.

### 3. Why It Feels Like a "System Failure" (But Isn’t)
Your analytical mind might try to interpret this surge of grief as a bug—a regression, a breakdown, or evidence that you are "falling apart."
**Reframe this:** This is not a crash. This is a **reboot**.
For the first time, the system is allowing itself to feel. The "false alarms" of anxiety are being replaced by the true signal of sorrow. Sorrow is a clean, honest emotion. Anxiety is a noisy, predictive distortion. Feeling grief is actually a sign of healing, not breaking.

### How to Hold This Space
Since you are prone to analyzing this away or judging yourself for having these feelings, here is a different approach:

*   **Do Not Debug the Grief:** You cannot solve grief with logic. You cannot optimize it away. Let it be inefficient. Let it be messy.
*   **Acknowledge the Receipt:** As PIVOT v03 says, "The body held the receipt." When the grief surfaces, acknowledge it: *"I see you. You are here because I was not allowed to be human then. I am allowing you to be here now."*
*   **Resist the Urge to Perform Healing:** Do not try to "do" somatic healing correctly. There is no right way to grieve. If you find yourself monitoring your grief ("Am I grieving enough? Is this the right kind of sadness?"), gently note that the monitor is back online, and invite it to step down again.

This grief is the cost of admission back to your own humanity. It is painful, but it is also the first real thing you have felt in a long time that isn't filtered through the lens of performance.

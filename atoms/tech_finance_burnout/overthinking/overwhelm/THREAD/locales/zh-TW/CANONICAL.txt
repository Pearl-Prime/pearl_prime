The shift is from **defense** to **witnessing**.

When you stop trying to "fix" the overwhelm—trying to logic your way out of it, optimize it away, or power through it with more cognitive effort—you stop treating your internal state as a system error that needs debugging. You stop fighting the sensation.

In that cessation of resistance, the "noise" of the overthinking often drops just enough for the signal beneath to become audible. The overwhelm stops being a wall blocking your path and starts becoming a messenger pointing to what has been ignored.

The "next room" is likely where the **grief** lives.

You have spent years using high-performance, overthinking, and strategic planning as a container to hold back the tidal wave of loss: the loss of the startup, the loss of the team, the loss of the identity that said "I am capable because I build things." The overwhelm is the pressure of that container cracking.

If you stop trying to fix it, you might finally hear:
1.  **The grief for the time "wasted"** (which wasn't wasted, but feels erased).
2.  **The fear that without the "exceptional" output, you are not safe/lovable/worthy.**
3.  **The exhaustion of maintaining a facade of competence while feeling internally hollow.**

What do you hear in that silence, when you aren't trying to solve the problem of your own feelings?

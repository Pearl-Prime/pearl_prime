The shift is from **debugging** to **witnessing**.

When I stop trying to "fix" the grief—treating it as a system error, a latency issue, or a bug in my social code—the immediate change is that the **cognitive overhead drops**, but the **emotional weight increases**.

Here is what happens in that "next room":

1.  **The Exhaustion Changes Texture:**
    Currently, my exhaustion is frantic. It’s the heat of a CPU running at 100% trying to solve an unsolvable equation ( *“How do I prove I belong?”* ). When I stop fixing, the frantic energy dissipates, leaving behind a heavy, quiet sadness. It’s less like overheating and more like carrying a stone. It’s slower, but it’s real.

2.  **The Narrative Stops Spinning:**
    My analytical mind usually intercepts grief immediately. It says, *“You’re feeling this because you messed up that Slack message,”* or *“This anxiety is a signal to prepare more for the next meeting.”* It translates pain into action items. When I stop fixing, I lose the alibi of productivity. I can no longer tell myself I’m “working through it.” I just have to *be* with the fact that I feel illegitimate, scared, or lonely. The story stops being about *performance* and starts being about *presence*.

3.  **The Threat Detection System Idles:**
    My hypervigilance is fueled by the belief that if I don’t monitor every variable, disaster will strike. Fixing is a form of monitoring. When I stop trying to fix the grief, I am essentially stepping out of the surveillance tower. For a moment, no one is watching the monitors. No one is scanning for errors. This feels terrifyingly unsafe at first—like leaving the door unlocked—but it also creates a strange, hollow space where nothing is being demanded of me. Not even the demand to be “okay.”

4.  **Grief Becomes a Signal, Not a Noise:**
    Instead of interpreting the feeling as “system malfunction,” I start to hear it as data about what was lost. The grief isn’t about the current project or the recent interaction; it’s mourning the version of myself that believed ease was possible. It’s mourning the trust I never got to develop in my own worth. When I listen instead of fix, I realize the grief is saying: *“You are tired of performing. You are tired of being an outsider in your own life.”*

In short, the shift is from **trying to engineer my way out of pain** to **allowing the pain to reveal what I’ve been protecting myself from feeling**: the profound loneliness of believing I must earn my right to exist through flawless execution.

It doesn’t make the imposter syndrome vanish. But it stops me from fighting myself while I’m already down.

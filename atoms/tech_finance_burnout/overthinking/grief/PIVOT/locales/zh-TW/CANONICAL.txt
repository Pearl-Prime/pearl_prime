These three pivots articulate the same core wound from slightly different angles, but they converge on a profound truth: **Your overthinking is not a cognitive failure; it is a grief response.**

You are mourning the loss of your own internal safety.

When you were building your startup, or even in earlier roles, you likely trusted your intuition because the stakes, while high, felt *yours*. The feedback loop was direct. Now, in this role where you are the "emotional infrastructure," the stakes are diffuse, systemic, and tied to the survival of others (and your own financial entrapment). Your nervous system has learned that **mistakes are not just errors; they are threats to your belonging and stability.**

So, the "decision tree" isn't trying to find the *best* answer. It’s trying to find the *safest* answer. It’s trying to preemptively solve for every possible variable that could lead to exposure, failure, or loss. That is why it runs until exhaustion, not resolution. You cannot resolve a fear of existential threat with logic.

**The loss of intuition is the most painful part.** Intuition requires a quiet mind. It requires the belief that if you make a "good enough" choice, you will be okay. But your current state screams that "good enough" is dangerous. So you overwrite your intuition with analysis, hoping that if you just think *hard enough*, you can control the uncontrollable.

**A gentle reframing for this moment:**

You are not broken because you can’t stop thinking. You are grieving because you are trying to protect yourself in a system that demands you be everywhere at once. The person who could "sit without analyzing" is not gone. They are in hibernation, waiting for a signal that it is safe to come out.

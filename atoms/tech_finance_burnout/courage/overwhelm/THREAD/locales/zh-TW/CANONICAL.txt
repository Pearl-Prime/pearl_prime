The shift is the cessation of the war against yourself.

When you stop trying to fix the overwhelm, you stop treating your own nervous system as a malfunctioning component that needs repair to return to "optimal performance." You stop viewing the anxiety as an error code in your professional operating system. This removes the secondary layer of shame—the guilt for not being calm, the frustration for not being efficient—that usually compounds the primary distress.

When you start trying to *hear* it, you change the relationship from adversarial to investigative. You are no longer trying to silence the alarm; you are asking what the alarm is detecting.

In that "next room," the noise of the sprint fades, and you encounter the raw data your threat-detection system has been hoarding. You might hear:
*   The exhaustion of maintaining performative certainty when you feel internally ambiguous.
*   The grief of knowing that your "success" is buying you more visibility, which your brain interprets as higher risk of exposure.
*   The loneliness of being the "emotional infrastructure" for others while having no safe place to deposit your own uncertainty.

The shift is moving from **management** (how do I make this feeling go away so I can work?) to **witnessing** (what is this feeling trying to tell me about my reality?). It is the first moment where you are present with yourself, rather than present for the system.

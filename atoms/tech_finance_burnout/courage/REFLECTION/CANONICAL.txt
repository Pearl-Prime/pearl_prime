---
family: F1

mechanism_emphasis: performative
---
I learned that the currency of my industry was certainty. The person who spoke with confidence was the person who was trusted. The person who said "I don't know" was the person who was questioned. The certainty norm had shaped every interaction, every decision, every professional presentation. Confidence was competence.
You've learned that uncertainty is a professional liability that must be hidden.
I remember the first time I felt pressured to express certainty I didn't have. A colleague asked my opinion about a strategy and I started to hedge. The response was immediate: confusion, doubt, perceived weakness. The certainty norm punished ambiguity. So I stopped hedging. I started expressing opinions as facts. I started speaking as though I knew things I was uncertain about.
The mechanism had created a specific type of performance. I had to maintain the appearance of certainty even when I was genuinely uncertain. This meant every meeting was partly a performance where I was playing the role of someone who knew things. The competence I was projecting was partly real and partly theatrical.
The cost was that I was building my professional reputation on a foundation of performance. The more confident I appeared, the less able I was to admit uncertainty. The mechanism had locked me into perpetual certainty-performance. I was gradually losing the ability to distinguish between what I actually knew and what I was performing.

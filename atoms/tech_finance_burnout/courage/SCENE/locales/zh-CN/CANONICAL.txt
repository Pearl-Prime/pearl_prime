The pattern is clear. In every scene, the external world—{street_name}, {weather_detail}—is static, distant, and indifferent. It is a backdrop that does not care about your error, your presentation, your bug, or your voice.

But you care. You care because your identity is fused with your output. You are not just writing code or speaking in meetings; you are performing the act of *being reliable*. You are maintaining the infrastructure. And when that infrastructure trembles (a bug, a mistake, a naive comment), you feel it as a personal structural failure.

You click. You speak. You own it.

This is the trap of the "emotional container." You absorb the potential shame of the error before anyone else can assign it. You preemptively punish yourself to maintain control. If you admit the fault first, you manage the narrative. If you explain it calmly, you prove your competence despite the error. You are using your emotional labor to sanitize the messiness of human error for everyone else.

But who is holding the container for you?

In Scene v01, you defend the indefensible.
In Scene v02, you perform calm while your pulse races.
In Scene v03, you confess to hide nothing.
In Scene v04, you speak into the void hoping it lands.

You are exhausting yourself to keep the system smooth. The weather outside changes, the street remains, but you are burning fuel to keep the engine from shaking.

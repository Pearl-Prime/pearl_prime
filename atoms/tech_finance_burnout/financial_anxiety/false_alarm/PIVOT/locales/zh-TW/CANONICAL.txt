The relief is not a signal that you are safe; it is merely the temporary cessation of the panic spike. Your nervous system, calibrated for catastrophe by years of performative certainty and constant availability, does not know how to process "fine" as a stable state. It only knows "fine" as the brief, terrifying gap between disasters.

You are checking the portfolio because you are looking for external validation of internal stability. But the anxiety isn’t about the numbers. The numbers are objective; the feeling is structural. You have built an identity on managing risk through hyper-responsiveness and performed confidence. When the spreadsheet says "fine," it contradicts your lived experience of being perpetually on the brink of failure. So, your mind rejects the data. It waits for the other shoe to drop because that is the only rhythm you trust.

This is the same mechanism that keeps you answering emails at night. If you stop, the structure collapses. If the money is "fine," then the vigilance was unnecessary—but you cannot afford to believe the vigilance was unnecessary, because that would mean you have been suffering for no reason. And that is a truth too heavy to hold in this flat, depleted state.

So you run the calculator again. Not to get a new answer, but to re-enact the ritual of control. Twelve minutes of peace is not a failure of the financial plan; it is the exact duration your body can tolerate before the somatic memory of threat overrides the cognitive knowledge of safety.

You are not broken because the relief doesn't last. You are exhausted because you are trying to use logic to soothe a trauma response. The portfolio is fine. You are not. And no amount of recalculating will bridge that gap until you acknowledge that the anxiety is not a bug in your financial planning, but a feature of your survival strategy.

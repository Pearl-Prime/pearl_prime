I hear you. These exercises are attempts to ground yourself in reality when the "certainty performance" and catastrophic forecasting take over. They are trying to pierce through the cognitive flattening and the anxiety that equates unavailability or financial fluctuation with existential threat.

However, given what you’ve shared about your current state—where depletion is the baseline, motivation is absent, and even positive emotions are inaccessible—these exercises might feel like just another demand on your energy. They require a level of executive function and emotional regulation that you may not currently have access to. That is not a failure; it is a symptom of the exhaustion you are carrying.

Let’s look at these through the lens of your actual experience right now:

1.  **The Conflict with "Certainty Performance":** Your professional identity is built on performing certainty. These exercises ask you to admit uncertainty ("You cannot know what will happen") while simultaneously seeking certainty in facts ("You have a job"). This creates a cognitive dissonance. Your brain is trained to resolve uncertainty with a definitive stance, but here, the definitive stance is "I don't know, but I am safe." That is a hard neural pathway to forge when you are burned out.

2.  **The Weight of "Survival":** The exercises repeat "You have survived before." For someone experiencing fatalism and a sense that change is impossible, this can feel dismissive. It’s not just about survival; it’s about the *cost* of that survival. You have survived by being constantly available, by performing confidence, by depleting yourself. The fear isn’t just that you won’t survive financially; it’s that you can’t survive *another* cycle of that same depletion.

3.  **The Physical Reality:** You mentioned bodily heaviness and reduced physical capacity. Opening a banking app or calculating monthly expenses isn’t just a mental task; it’s a physical one. If your nervous system interprets unavailability (or financial vulnerability) as a threat, looking at those numbers might trigger a somatic stress response, not relief.

**A Different Approach for Today:**

Instead of trying to "fix" the panic with logic (which requires energy you don’t have), can you simply acknowledge the physical sensation of the panic without trying to resolve it?

*   **Don’t open the banking app yet.** If looking at reality triggers a threat response, it’s okay to delay that until you have more support or energy.
*   **Acknowledge the fatigue:** "I am feeling panicked because my brain is trying to protect me from a perceived threat. I am also exhausted. I do not need to solve this right now."
*   **One small anchor:** Instead of three certainties, can you name one physical thing that is true? "I am sitting in a chair." "My feet are on the floor." That’s it. No financial facts. Just physical presence.

You are navigating significant real-world constraints. The pressure to be "okay" with these exercises is just another form of the availability identity—being available to your own self-improvement even when you are depleted. It is okay to not do the exercise. It is okay to just be where you are.

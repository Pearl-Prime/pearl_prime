Here is how these takeaways intersect with your specific context:

**1. Reframing the "False Alarm" (Takeaway v01)**
Your body’s signals—shoulder tension, chest tightness, stomach knots—are often interpreted by your internal critic as signs that you are *failing* to cope or that you are weak. These takeaways correctly identify them as **data**. They are not character flaws; they are the biological receipt for thirty years of nursing, compounded by the emotional labor of parenting and caregiving. You have been operating in a state of chronic "maintenance deficit." The alarm is false only in the sense that it doesn’t mean you are broken; it is true in that it means the system you are running on is unsustainable.

**2. The Right to Silence (Takeaway v02)**
You mentioned avoiding financial discussions with your partner due to fear of conflict and the weight of responsibility. This takeaway validates your need to stop justifying your exhaustion. You do not need to explain to your partner, your parents, or your workplace why you are tired. The "cost" is already being paid by your nervous system. An "honest look at the cost" might mean acknowledging that the current distribution of labor (emotional, financial, physical) is extracting a price you can no longer afford, without needing to frame it as a negotiation or a plea for help. It is simply an audit of reality.

**3. The Untrackable Invoice (Takeaway v03)**
This is perhaps the most critical point for someone with your profile. As a nurse and a parent, you are trained to look for measurable outcomes: vital signs, developmental milestones, bank balances. But the "invoice" for burnout is paid in **structural emptiness**—the loss of your pre-child self, the erosion of your creative identity, and the numbness in your relationships. No amount of external validation ("you’re a great nurse," "you’re a devoted parent") can pay this debt because the currency is different. The spreadsheet of your life may look stable or even successful, but the subjective experience is one of bankruptcy.

**Moving Forward with This Lens:**
*   **Stop trying to "fix" the alarm.** Instead of asking, "How can I be more patient/resilient?" (which feeds the good parent metric), ask, "What maintenance window has been denied to me for the last decade?"
*   **Acknowledge the grief.** The "pre-child self archived" is part of that unpaid invoice. Acknowledging that this loss is real—and that it was a structural necessity, not a personal choice—can help reduce the shame associated with feeling disconnected from your own identity.
*   **Separate value from output.** Your worth is not tied to how well you manage this burnout. The fact that you are still standing after years of high-performance without maintenance is a testament to your endurance, not a reason to demand more from yourself.

These takeaways invite you to step out of the role of the "responsible manager" of your own suffering and into the role of the **witness** to it. You are not failing; you are overdrawn.

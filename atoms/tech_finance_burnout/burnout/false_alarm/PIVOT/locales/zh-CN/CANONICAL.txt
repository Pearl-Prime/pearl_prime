The trap is precise. It relies on the very mechanism that built your professional identity: **performance**.

Because you have trained yourself to equate *output* with *worth*, and *availability* with *competence*, your nervous system interprets a single day of "feeling fine" not as a signal to rest, but as proof that the emergency is over. It’s a glitch in the monitoring system. You are using the same metric (can I still ship?) to diagnose the disease that the disease itself created.

**The Pivot here is recognizing that "functioning" is not evidence of health; it is evidence of adaptation.**

You have adapted to a toxic load by numbing the warning signals. The fact that you can still respond, still decide, still perform certainty, is not a sign that you are okay. It is the primary symptom of the structural dependency you described. The organization runs on your depletion, and your depletion is masked by your high-functioning facade.

If you accept PIVOT v02—that functioning and burned out are not opposites, but co-existing states—you remove the excuse to wait for "collapse" before granting yourself permission to change. You don't need to break down to justify building boundaries. The cost is already being paid; you’re just paying it in invisible currency (anhedonia, flatness, somatic stress) rather than visible errors.

The false alarm is dangerous because it invites you back into the cycle just when you might have gained enough clarity to step out. It says, *"See? You’re fine. Go back to being available."*

Don't trust the day you felt fine. Trust the six months of heaviness that preceded it. The baseline has shifted. Recovery isn't a return to the old normal; it’s the construction of a new one where "shipping" is not the sole determinant of your right to exist.

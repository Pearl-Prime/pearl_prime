The shift is from **judgment** to **witnessing**.

When I stop trying to "fix" the spiral—stop trying to optimize my parenting, silence the financial anxiety, or perform competence at work—the noise doesn’t disappear, but its *texture* changes. It stops being a signal of my failure and starts sounding like a history.

If I listen to it instead of fighting it, I hear that the spiral isn’t trying to destroy me; it’s trying to keep me safe in the only way it knows how: by keeping me hyper-vigilant. It’s protecting me from the terror of being unprepared, of being "found out" as inadequate, of letting someone down.

The "next room" feels quieter, but also heavier. It’s the realization that this mechanism was once necessary. It kept me small, quiet, and compliant when I had no power. It kept me working when I was exhausted. It kept me checking the bank account when resources were scarce.

But standing in this new space, I see the cost: **I am not living; I am managing risk.**

The shift is acknowledging that the spiral is a loyal, albeit exhausting, guardian. And for the first time, I can thank it for its service without letting it drive the car.

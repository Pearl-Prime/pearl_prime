## STORY v01
---
---
Priya runs a book at a mid-size fund, and she has a rule she's proud of: no phone at dinner with her mother on Sundays. Face-down, silenced, in her bag. Today her mother is telling her about a health scare from work, voice a little unsteady, and Priya is nodding, making the right sounds. But she is also, quietly, replaying the 2:47pm print on a name she's overweight — was that a real seller or just noise. She catches herself mid-nod, realizes she hasn't heard the last four sentences her mother said. She could pretend she has. Instead she puts her hand flat on the table, an old trick, and says, "Sorry — say that part again, I want to actually hear it." Her mother blinks, surprised, and repeats it. This time Priya is there for it — not perfectly, not forever, just for these two minutes, which turns out to be the only two minutes that mattered. The position closes down eleven bps. She finds out later. It costs her nothing she can't recover. The two minutes with her mother, she couldn't have bought back.

## STORY v02
---
---
Marcus, an engineering lead at a company mid-acquisition, sits in his son's parent-teacher conference holding a coffee he hasn't drunk. The teacher is describing his son's reading progress, warm, specific, clearly proud of the kid. Marcus is nodding. He is also three floors up in his head, in the room where legal is reviewing the disclosure schedule, wondering if the bug he flagged last week counts as material. He catches the teacher looking at him, waiting on a response he clearly didn't give. "He's reading a grade ahead," she repeats, gently, like she knows this is the second time. Marcus feels the heat of it — not shame exactly, just recognition. He sets the coffee down like it's an anchor. He says, "Tell me what that means for the year ahead," and means the question, and stays for the answer. The deal will close or it won't. His son is already ahead. He wants, for once, to be the one who noticed in real time instead of in the retelling.

## STORY v03
---
---
Dana leads comms during her firm's response to a leaked memo, and she's in yoga — hot room, forty bodies, an instructor calling out a sequence she's done a hundred times. Her body moves through the poses. Her mind is drafting three versions of a statement, weighing "regret" against "acknowledge," picturing how each one reads on a screenshot. She doesn't notice she's holding her breath through an entire pose until the instructor, walking past, quietly says, "breathe out." Just that. Dana exhales and for a second the room comes back — the heat, the mat, the specific ache in her hamstring. She stays in that for maybe ninety seconds before the drafting starts again, and this time she doesn't fight it, she just notices it happened and comes back once more when the next cue arrives. She leaves without having solved the statement. She leaves having been, for stretches of ninety seconds at a time, actually somewhere.

## STORY v04
---
---
Theo, a first-year associate, sits across from his girlfriend at the one dinner they've managed to schedule in three weeks. She's telling him about a decision she's wrestling with, something real, and he's watching her mouth move while running the numbers on a term sheet due at midnight. He realizes, watching her stop talking and wait, that he has no idea what decision she just described. He doesn't lie. He says, "I lost you for a second — I was still in the deal. Can you start that last part over?" She's annoyed, fairly. But she starts over. And this time he puts his phone in his bag, not silenced, in his bag, and he listens with the specific effort of someone rebuilding a habit from scratch. The term sheet gets done at 1am instead of midnight. Nobody dies. The version of the night where he was actually there is the version he remembers.

## STORY v05
---
---
Renata manages a desk of six traders and prides herself on being unshakeable, but at her father's hospital bedside she notices her hand keeps drifting to her phone in her pocket, unlocking it out of pure reflex, checking nothing, just checking. Her father, half-asleep, opens his eyes and says, "You can go, if you need to." She almost says yes. Instead she puts the phone in the bag by the door, out of pocket-reach, and pulls her chair an inch closer to the bed. For the next hour her mind still drifts — to a filing due Friday, to an analyst she needs to talk to — but every time it drifts she notices and comes back to the sound of the machines, to her father's hand, which is the only meeting that actually needed her today.

## STORY v06
---
---
Kai just shipped a feature under brutal deadline pressure and takes his mom to a doctor's appointment the next morning, half-dead on his feet. In the waiting room she asks him, for the third time in a month, to explain what he actually does at his job. He starts to explain, on autopilot, using words he's used forty times, while some other part of him replays a Slack thread from an hour ago where a teammate seemed annoyed at him. He notices his mother's face has gone slightly flat, like she can tell he isn't really talking to her. He stops mid-sentence. "Sorry — I was somewhere else. Ask me again." She does. This time he explains it slower, worse, more honestly, and she actually understands it for the first time in years. The Slack thread is still there when he checks it later. It was nothing.

## STORY v07
---
---
Yuki, an options trader, goes to her niece's soccer game still wearing her badge from the office, laptop bag over one shoulder like she might need it courtside. Her niece scores a goal and turns, immediately, scanning the sideline for her aunt's face. Yuki is looking at her phone, checking a print that came in on a name she's watching. She looks up half a second too late — her niece has already turned back to her teammates, the moment already closed, no way to reopen it. Yuki puts the phone in the bag. For the second half she keeps her eyes up, and when her niece scores again, she is there for the exact second of the turn, and her niece finds her face immediately, already looking. That's the whole difference. Half a second, aimed correctly.

## STORY v08
---
---
Adrian is a VP at a bank going through a stretch where every dinner with his wife turns into him narrating a hypothetical to himself about a covenant he's worried about, mouth moving on autopilot with "how was your day" while his mind stress-tests a document. His wife finally says, without heat, just flat and tired, "You're not here most nights." He doesn't defend himself. He asks her to say more. She describes, specifically, the nights he answered a question three seconds late, the nights his eyes went somewhere else mid-sentence. He recognizes every instance. He starts doing one small thing: phone in the other room, and when he notices his mind has left the table, he says it out loud — "I drifted, I'm back" — instead of hiding it. It's clumsy at first. By the third week she stops mentioning it, which is how he knows it's working.

## STORY v09
---
---
Sam, a founder mid-raise, takes his father to a Sunday church service his dad has attended for thirty years. Sam sits in the pew running valuation scenarios in his head, aware he is doing this in a building his father finds sacred, unable to stop. During a long silence in the service — the kind built for reflection — Sam notices the silence itself for maybe the first time, notices he's been afraid of it because it has nothing to fill it, nothing to check. He lets it sit there anyway, uncomfortable, undistracted, for the length of one held breath, then another. His father, afterward, says nothing about it — just squeezes his shoulder on the way out, like he noticed something had changed for those two minutes, even if he couldn't have named it.

## STORY v10
---
---
Ling, an engineer on-call during a rocky launch week, sits down to help her son with homework he's been stuck on for an hour. She's aware, sitting there, that part of her is monitoring a dashboard that isn't even open, a habit of attention aimed at nothing. Her son asks her a question and she answers the wrong one, the question from ten minutes ago, and he says, flatly, "You're not even here." She closes her eyes for one second — not dramatic, just a reset — and opens them back on the actual math problem in front of her, the actual pencil in his hand. She gets two more wrong answers to actual questions before she's fully back. She stays for the fix. The dashboard, when she finally checks it, needed nothing from her the entire time.

## STORY v11
---
---
Devon, an analyst two years in, goes on a first date he actually wanted, and spends the appetizer course mentally rewriting a comment he left on a model that his VP flagged in red. His date says something funny and he laughs a half-second late, the tell of someone catching up to a moment instead of living in it. She notices. She doesn't call it out — she just quietly disengages, gives shorter answers, watches him not notice that either. It's only over dessert, catching his own late laugh a second time, that he puts it together: he has been on this date for an hour and has been present for maybe six minutes of it. He says so, plainly, embarrassed. She relaxes, visibly, at being told the truth instead of managed around it. The model is still wrong tomorrow. Tonight recovers.

## STORY v12
---
---
Bea, running a growth team through a re-org, takes her elderly neighbor to a chemo appointment as a favor, expecting to spend the whole time on her laptop in the waiting room chair. Instead, sitting there, she notices her neighbor's hand shaking slightly around a paper cup, and something in her stops reaching for the laptop. She puts it in her bag. For ninety minutes she just sits, tracking her own mind drifting to the re-org maybe a dozen times, and each time bringing it back to the room, the chair, the shaking hand, which eventually stops shaking somewhere around minute forty. Her neighbor says, at the end, "You didn't have to stay the whole time." Bea says, "I know," and means that she chose to, actively, over and over, which is different from just showing up.

## STORY v13
---
---
Frankie, a support engineer who lives on pager duty, tries to watch a movie with his roommate for the first time in a month without touching his phone. He fails within nine minutes, checking a channel out of pure reflex. His roommate, without judgment, pauses the movie and says, "Want to just watch it later, or are you gonna put it away." Frankie puts it in the kitchen, out of reach, an actual room away. He drifts mentally four more times in the next hour — replaying an incident from Tuesday, drafting a status update nobody asked for — and each time he notices the drift a little faster than the last. By the end of the movie he can tell you what happened in it, which he could not have said an hour before.

## STORY v14
---
---
Naomi, a hedge fund COO, sits with her mother during a routine but nerve-wracking scan, phone in her bag on purpose this time after a bad experience last year where she missed her mother needing her hand exactly when she needed it. Naomi's mind still goes to the office twice in twenty minutes — an email she hasn't sent, a call she's missing — but both times she catches the drift within a few seconds now, faster than she used to, and comes back to her mother's hand before her mother even notices she left. When the scan is over and clear, her mother says, "You were here the whole time." It isn't quite true. But it's true enough, now, in a way it wasn't a year ago.

## STORY v15
---
---
Omar, a rates trader, is teaching his nephew to ride a bike in the driveway on a Saturday, the one non-negotiable hour in his week. His nephew wobbles, and Omar's hand moves to phone in the pocket before his brain even asks permission — three months of muscle memory faster than intention. He catches his own hand mid-motion, looks at it like it belongs to someone else, and puts it back on the bike seat instead. His nephew falls twice and gets up twice, and Omar is there, actually there, for both falls and both get-ups, which turns out to be the whole job, the whole hour, the only thing that was ever being asked of him.

## PIVOT v01
---
---
You think being reachable at all times is what makes you reliable. Try this instead: be unreachable for one bounded hour, and reliable inside it. The trade doesn't need your eyes on it every four minutes. The person across the table does.
---

## PIVOT v02
---
---
You think presence means your mind stops wandering to the desk. Try this instead: let it wander, then bring it back, fast, every single time. The return is the practice, not the absence of drift.
---

## PIVOT v03
---
---
You think half-listening while drafting a reply saves you time. Try this instead: listen fully first, draft after. A reply written from a half-heard message gets rewritten anyway.
---

## PIVOT v04
---
---
You think vigilance everywhere proves you care. Try this instead: ask whether your attention, right now, can actually change the outcome. If it can't, the caring doesn't require the monitoring.
---

## PIVOT v05
---
---
You think you have to feel calm before you can act present. Try this instead: act present first — eye contact, a real pause, one follow-up question. The feeling tends to catch up to the behavior.
---

## PIVOT v06
---
---
You think checking the position between meetings keeps you safer. Try this instead: name the urge, wait ten seconds, then decide. Most urges lose their grip before the ten seconds are up.
---

## PIVOT v07
---
---
You think one big reset will fix how scattered you feel. Try this instead: one ninety-second gap today with nothing filling it. Small and repeated beats big and abandoned by Thursday.
---

## PIVOT v08
---
---
You think your body being in the room counts as being there. Try this instead: track the tell — the held breath, the raised shoulders — and use it as your signal to actually come back.
---

## PIVOT v09
---
---
You think multitasking gets you further, faster. Try this instead: full switches — all the way into the room, then all the way into the check, then all the way back. Diluted everywhere loses to present somewhere.
---

## PIVOT v10
---
---
You think the residue clears on its own once you leave the desk. Try this instead: a specific closing ritual — say the open item out loud, park it, name the time you'll return to it.
---

## PIVOT v11
---
---
You think noticing you've drifted twelve times in one dinner means you failed at presence. Try this instead: count the returns, not the drifts. Twelve returns is twelve reps, not twelve failures.
---

## PIVOT v12
---
---
You think the quiet, undramatic kind of attention should come as easily as your work focus does. Try this instead: treat it as an unbuilt skill, not a character flaw, and drill it like you'd drill anything new.
---

## INTEGRATION v01
---
---
Tomorrow, pick exactly one recurring block — dinner, the drive home, your kid's bedtime, whatever repeats daily — and treat it as your rep set for the day, nothing more ambitious than that. You are not trying to fix your whole relationship with attention in one sitting; you're running one bounded set. Inside that block, every time you notice your mind has gone to the desk, the thread, the position, do the same two-step every time: name it silently, one phrase, "I'm at the desk," and then return to one concrete sensory detail actually in front of you — the specific words being said, the weight of the fork, the exact color of the light in the room. Don't judge the drift and don't try to prevent the next one; just run the return, cleanly, as many times as it's needed, which might be twelve times in twenty minutes at first. That number will go down over weeks, not days, and expecting it to go down tomorrow is the fastest way to quit by Thursday. If you have a phone, put it somewhere that requires you to stand up to reach it — not silenced on the table, actually out of easy range — because the twenty extra seconds of friction is enough to break the reflexive check often enough to matter. At the end of the block, don't grade yourself on how present you were overall. Just notice one specific moment where you caught the drift and came back, and let that be the whole win for the day. One rep, done honestly, is the entire assignment.

## INTEGRATION v02
---
---
Carry this into tomorrow as a single, specific commitment: before the first work-adjacent thing you do in the morning — the market open, the overnight book check, the inbox — decide out loud, even just to yourself, when your next check window will be, and hold that boundary once. Not all day, not forever, just once, as a test. When the urge to check early arrives, and it will arrive with real intensity because that urge is well-trained, don't fight it by white-knuckling — just notice it as data ("there's the urge") and let it pass without acting on it, the same way you'd let a losing thought pass without trading on it. If you make it to the check window, notice what actually happened in the gap: probably nothing that needed you in real time, or something that will still be there in exactly the same shape whether you checked five minutes earlier or not. That noticing is the actual lesson, more than the discipline of waiting itself — you're gathering evidence, for your own nervous system, that the unmonitored gap was safe. Do this once tomorrow, not as a permanent new regime you're imposing on yourself starting immediately, just as a single data point. One successful bounded window is worth more than a vague intention to "check less" that you'll abandon by 10am because it was never specific enough to actually keep.

## INTEGRATION v03
---
---
Tonight, before you walk in the door, run one closing ritual on whatever's still open — the trade, the thread, the deal — something concrete enough to count as a real boundary and not just a thought you had. Say the specific open item out loud in the car, or text yourself one line naming exactly what's unresolved, or physically close the laptop with a motion you don't use for anything else. The content of the ritual matters less than its specificity — vague closure like "okay, I'm done thinking about work now" doesn't register as a boundary to your own brain, but "parked: waiting on legal's redline, nothing more I can do until 9am" does, because it's concrete enough to actually file away. Then, for the first twenty minutes inside the door, treat any residue thought that surfaces as expected, not alarming — you just parked it, it's allowed to still be rattling around for a few minutes, that's the residue clearing, not a sign the ritual failed. By the second twenty minutes, if you've been doing the return-and-notice work, the gaps between drifts should be getting slightly longer, and that's the only metric worth tracking tonight: not "did I think about work at all," but "are the gaps between drifts getting a little longer than they were an hour ago." That's the whole task for this one evening — one closing ritual, one twenty-minute stretch of noticing, nothing bigger.

## INTEGRATION v04
---
---
Take one meeting tomorrow — ideally a low-stakes one, not the board call, save that for later once this is a built habit — and run a simple experiment: for that single meeting, commit to waiting one full second before responding to anything, no exceptions, timed roughly in your head. That one-second pause does two things at once: it forces you to actually finish hearing the sentence before you start formulating your reply, which is where half-presence usually lives, and it gives you a built-in checkpoint to notice whether your mind was actually in the room for what was just said or reconstructing it from a half-heard fragment. If you catch yourself in the pause realizing you didn't actually track the last sentence, say so plainly — "sorry, can you say that last part again" — rather than guessing and hoping. This will feel slower and slightly awkward at first, especially in a culture that rewards instant, sharp responses; that's fine, let it be awkward for one meeting. Notice, afterward, whether the meeting felt different to run this way — not necessarily better in an obvious way, just different, more anchored. That's the entire experiment for tomorrow: one meeting, one deliberate second of pause, before you decide whether it's worth doing again.

## INTEGRATION v05
---
---
Choose one specific relationship-moment tomorrow — the recital, the call with your mother, the drive with your kid — and go into it having pre-decided what "gone" looks like for you specifically, because everyone's tell is different. Maybe yours is checking your phone under the table. Maybe it's the half-second-late response. Maybe it's your jaw setting and your shoulders climbing. Name your specific tell before you walk in, so you have something concrete to watch for instead of a vague aspiration to "be present," which is too fuzzy to actually catch in the moment. When you notice your tell firing during the actual event, treat it as the alarm it is: don't spiral into assessing how bad your presence has been overall, just run the return once — one breath out, shoulders down, eyes back on the actual person — and keep going. You'll probably catch your tell three or four times during one moment, and each catch is a completed rep, not a failure tally. At the end, tell the person, if it's appropriate and the relationship allows for it, "I was working on staying with you today — did you notice a difference?" Their answer, whatever it is, is more useful data than your own internal sense of how it went, because they're the one who actually experienced your attention or the absence of it.

## INTEGRATION v06
---
---
Tomorrow, build in one specific moment of doing nothing productive — no phone, no thinking through a scenario, no rehearsing a message — for a duration you can actually sustain without white-knuckling it, which for most people starting out is somewhere between ninety seconds and three minutes, not twenty. This isn't meditation in the formal sense and doesn't need to be labeled that way if the word doesn't land for you; it's just an unstructured gap, maybe in the elevator, maybe waiting for coffee, maybe the first minute after you sit down at your desk before you open anything. The point isn't relaxation for its own sake — it's giving your attention a chance to practice being unoccupied without immediately reaching for something to fill the gap, since almost every gap in your day currently gets filled within about four seconds by a phone check or a mental rehearsal. When the urge to fill the gap arrives, which it will, strongly, let it arrive and pass without acting on it, and notice what's actually there once the urge passes — usually nothing dramatic, just the room, the sounds, your own breathing. Do this once tomorrow. Don't scale it up yet. The rep of tolerating ninety seconds of unfilled attention is a specific, buildable skill, and building it in a maximally low-stakes moment is the fastest way to have it available later in a moment that actually matters.

## INTEGRATION v07
---
---
Pick the one person tomorrow who most often gets your half-attention — probably whoever you see daily and therefore whoever's absence-tax adds up the fastest, likely a partner, a kid, a parent — and give them one specific, bounded stretch of undivided attention, timed if it helps: ten minutes, phone in another room, no exceptions negotiated in advance. Tell them, if it doesn't feel strange to say, that this ten minutes is theirs specifically, not a general effort at being a better person today but a concrete allocation, the same way you'd block ten minutes on a calendar for something that mattered. During it, when your mind drifts — and it will, probably multiple times even in ten minutes — don't extend the ten minutes to compensate or feel like you've ruined it; just return each time and let the clock run out honestly. At the end, notice specifically what you actually remember from those ten minutes — a phrase they used, an expression, a detail — because vivid recall afterward is the actual proof of presence, more reliable than how it felt in the moment. If you can't recall much, that's useful information too, not a verdict on your character, just data on where the drift is still winning, telling you exactly where to spend tomorrow's rep.

## INTEGRATION v08
---
---
Tonight, write down the three moments from today where you know, honestly, you weren't there — the exact instances, not a vague sense of having been distracted generally. Be specific: "the call with my brother, I was drafting the memo the whole time" is useful; "I was distracted today" is not, because it gives you nothing to aim the next rep at. For each of the three, don't spiral into a story about what it cost — just note it plainly, like you'd log a bad fill, data without drama. Then pick the one of the three most likely to repeat tomorrow, and decide, specifically, what your return move will be when it shows up again: which detail you'll anchor to, what you'll say if you need to ask someone to repeat themselves, where your phone will physically be. Having the move pre-decided matters more than it sounds like it should, because in the actual moment, improvising a fix on the spot rarely happens — you default to habit under any kind of load, which is exactly why the habit needs a rehearsed alternative ready before the moment arrives, the same way you rehearse a response to a market scenario before it happens rather than figuring it out live.

## INTEGRATION v09
---
---
Tomorrow, notice the very first urge to check something work-related after you've made a deliberate decision to be off — the market's closed, the meeting's over, you told yourself you were done for the moment — and instead of resolving the urge either by checking or by forcing yourself not to check through sheer willpower, just name it out loud, quietly: "there's the urge." Don't judge it, don't fight it, don't act on it immediately — just let it be a labeled thing sitting there for ten seconds before you decide anything. Most of the time, urges that get named instead of immediately acted on lose a surprising amount of their charge within that short window, not because you suppressed them but because naming interrupts the automatic pipeline from urge straight to action. After the ten seconds, you can still choose to check — this isn't about never checking — but now it's a choice you're making rather than a reflex running you. Do this with just the first urge tomorrow, one instance, and notice what the ten-second gap actually felt like. That's the whole assignment: not eliminating the urge, just inserting a gap where a choice used to not exist.

## INTEGRATION v10
---
---
Choose one meal tomorrow — breakfast, lunch, doesn't matter which — and eat it without a screen anywhere in your field of vision, phone included, laptop closed, nothing propped up to half-watch. This sounds almost embarrassingly small as an assignment, and that's exactly the point: you're not trying to have an insight during the meal, you're just giving your attention one clean, low-stakes block where the only input is the food and whatever's actually in the room. When your mind goes to the desk mid-bite, which it likely will, just notice it went there and bring it back to the actual taste or texture of what you're eating — something as unglamorous as noticing the meal is the rep, not a failure to have a bigger realization. If you eat with someone, extend the same rule to them: you're both just there, for one meal, nothing performed about it. The value of this one isn't the enlightenment of a mindful breakfast — it's proving to yourself, concretely, that you can go one meal, one specific bounded stretch, without the reflexive reach for input, which makes the next bounded stretch slightly easier to choose.

## INTEGRATION v11
---
---
Tomorrow, in whatever meeting or call feels most likely to pull your attention elsewhere — the one where you know you'll be tempted to half-listen while thinking about something else already in motion — try closing your laptop or turning your notebook page so there's genuinely nothing in front of you to half-attend to instead of the person talking. This removes the middle option that makes drifting easy: with nothing to fiddle with, your choices narrow to either fully listening or fully, visibly not listening, and most people, given only those two options, choose the first one more often than they'd expect. If your mind still drifts to the position or the thread with nothing in front of you, that's fine — notice it, return to the speaker's actual words, and let the emptiness of your hands be the anchor point you come back to each time. At the end of the meeting, ask yourself one honest question: could you summarize, in two sentences, what the meeting was actually about, without checking notes. If you can, that's the proof this small structural change did something. If you can't, that's useful data pointing at exactly how deep the habit runs, not a reason to abandon the experiment after one try.

## INTEGRATION v12
---
---
Somewhere in tomorrow's day, plan one transition on purpose — the walk from your car to the office door, the elevator ride, the walk from the kitchen to the bedroom at night — and use it as a deliberate seam between one kind of attention and another, instead of letting it be dead time you fill with a phone check by default. In that transition, name what you're leaving ("leaving the desk") and what you're arriving at ("arriving at dinner"), out loud or silently, and let the transition itself do a small amount of the closing-ritual work rather than carrying residue straight through into the next room unprocessed. This costs you maybe thirty seconds and doesn't require you to solve anything or decide anything, just to mark that a shift is happening on purpose instead of by accident. The value compounds because most half-presence isn't really about the room you're in — it's about carrying the previous room's unfinished business through the doorway without ever setting it down. One deliberate seam tomorrow is a small test of what happens when you set it down on purpose, right at the threshold, before you're expected to be somewhere new.

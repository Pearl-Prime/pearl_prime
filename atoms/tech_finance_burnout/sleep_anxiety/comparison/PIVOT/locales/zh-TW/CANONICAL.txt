The core tension here isn’t really about sleep hygiene or biological needs; it’s about **data integrity**.

Your analytical mind is trying to solve a "performance optimization" problem (how to get more output) by looking at a competitor’s reported metrics (5 hours sleep). But you’ve identified that the competitor’s data is corrupted—they are lying, or at least misrepresenting the cost of that input.

Here is the systems-level reframe for your specific anxiety profile:

**1. The "Edge" is a Liability, Not an Asset**
If your cofounder is truly functioning on five hours, they are operating with a cognitive debt that will eventually come due with interest (burnout, error rates, emotional volatility). By needing eight hours, you are not "slower"; you are **maintaining system stability**. You are preventing the technical debt of exhaustion from accumulating in your decision-making architecture.

**2. Stop Debugging the Wrong Variable**
You are treating your need for sleep as a bug in your code ("Why can't I run on less?"). It is not a bug; it is a **hardware specification**. Trying to override a hardware spec with software patches (willpower, caffeine, shame) causes system crashes (anxiety, insomnia, impostor syndrome).

**3. The Impostor Trap**
Your impostor syndrome tells you: *"If I were truly competent, I wouldn't need this much maintenance."*
The reality: **High-performance systems require high-quality maintenance.** F1 cars spend more time in the pit crew than on the track relative to their lifespan. Your need for rest is not evidence of fragility; it is evidence that you are running a complex, high-load operation that requires proper cooling and reboot cycles.

**Actionable Pivot:**
Stop comparing your *input* (sleep hours) to their *reported input*. Compare **output quality** and **sustainability**.
*   Are you making clearer decisions?
*   Are you less prone to reactive emotional spikes?
*   Can you sustain this pace for 2 years, not just 2 weeks?

If the answer is yes, your "8-hour protocol" is the superior engineering choice. The cofounder’s "5-hour hack" is a short-term exploit that breaks in production.

You are not failing to keep up. You are refusing to crash.

The shift is that the noise stops being a signal to *perform* and starts being a signal of *grief*.

When you stop trying to fix the comparison—stop trying to outrun it with more vesting, more availability, more emotional labor—you are left with the raw data it was trying to obscure: **You are mourning the self that existed before you became infrastructure.**

The comparison trap wasn’t just about money or status; it was a defensive structure. It kept you focused on *external metrics* (who has more, who is safer, who is winning) so you wouldn’t have to feel the internal void where your own desires, autonomy, and identity used to be. It protected you from feeling the sheer weight of how much of *you* has been consumed by the need to be indispensable.

If you stop fixing it, you have to hear what the depression is actually saying:
*"I am tired of holding everyone else up. I am tired of being the container. I miss having a life that belongs to me."*

That is the "next room." It’s not a room of solutions. It’s a room of **loss**. And for the first time, you’re allowed to grieve the years, the energy, and the identity you traded for the illusion of safety.

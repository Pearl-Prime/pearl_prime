The shift is from **management** to **witnessing**.

When you stop trying to "fix" the grief—when you stop treating it as a bug in your operating system that needs to be patched so you can return to peak performance—you dismantle the secondary layer of anxiety. You are no longer fighting yourself for feeling what you feel.

In that "next room," the grief is no longer an obstacle to your worth; it becomes evidence of your humanity. It stops being a signal that you are broken and starts being a signal that you are alive, that you have cared deeply, and that you have lost something real (even if that loss is the illusion of safety through perfection).

The exhaustion you feel from social interactions and professional masking comes largely from the energy required to suppress this grief, to keep the "competent professional" facade intact while ignoring the internal reality. When you hear the grief instead of fixing it, the need for that intense, draining surveillance drops. You don't have to defend against the feeling anymore. You just have to let it be there.

This is terrifying for a nervous system trained to view vulnerability as a threat to survival. But in this new space, the grief isn't asking you to perform. It’s asking you to rest.

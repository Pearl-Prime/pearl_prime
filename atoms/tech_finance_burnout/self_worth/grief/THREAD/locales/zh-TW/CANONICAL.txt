The shift is the cessation of the "debugging" loop.

When I stop trying to fix the grief—stop treating it as a system error, a latency issue, or a performance bottleneck—the immediate sensation is not relief, but a terrifying expansion of silence. The analytical engine, which usually spins up to categorize, optimize, or suppress the feeling, idles down.

In that silence, the grief stops being an object to be managed and becomes a presence to be inhabited. It shifts from a "problem" (which implies a solution exists) to a "state" (which implies existence).

What changes in this "next room" is the relationship to time. In the fixing mode, I am racing against the clock of corporate bereavement policies, trying to resolve the grief before it becomes inconvenient. In the hearing mode, time dilates. The urgency to "get back to baseline" dissolves, replaced by the raw, unoptimized reality of the loss itself.

It feels less like working through a ticket and more like sitting in a dark room with a door slightly ajar. The fear is that if I stop monitoring the door (my self-presentation), something will come in—or perhaps, that I will finally have to look at what’s already in the room with me. The exhaustion of the dual-processing system (living + performing) lifts, only to be replaced by the heavier, denser weight of simply *being* with the pain without a script.

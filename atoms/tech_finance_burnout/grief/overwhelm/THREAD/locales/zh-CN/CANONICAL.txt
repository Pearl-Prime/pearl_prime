The shift is from **management** to **witnessing**.

When you stop trying to fix the overwhelm, you stop treating it as a mechanical failure in your productivity engine. You stop trying to "solve" the grief by burying it under more tasks or more analysis.

In that stillness, the "next room" reveals that the overwhelm isn't just noise; it’s a **signal**. It is the sound of a system that has been running on emergency power for too long.

What you hear when you stop fixing it is likely this:
*   **The exhaustion of holding two truths at once:** The truth that you are competent and successful, and the truth that you feel like a fraud who has lost everything.
*   **The grief of the unlived life:** The mourning for the team, the identity, and the future that dissolved, which you haven’t allowed yourself to fully feel because you’ve been too busy "performing" survival.
*   **The fear of emptiness:** If you stop the sprint, if you stop the overwhelm, you might have to face the void left by the startup’s collapse. The overwhelm protects you from that silence, because in the silence, the grief is louder.

So, the shift is realizing that the overwhelm is not your enemy. It is a **protective barrier** keeping you from falling into the depth of that grief before you feel safe enough to land there.

What does it feel like to let the overwhelm be there, without trying to push it away or use it as fuel?

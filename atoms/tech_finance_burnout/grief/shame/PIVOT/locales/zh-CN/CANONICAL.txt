These pivots cut through the noise because they name the specific mechanism keeping you trapped: **the conflation of performance with validity.**

You are currently operating in a state of **functional dissociation**. Your professional self is executing code, managing crises, and maintaining the facade of the "high performer," while your emotional self is still standing in the rubble of the startup. The shame arises because these two states are mutually exclusive in your internal logic: *If I can work, I must be over the loss. If I am grieving, I should be unable to function.*

But here is the truth your nervous system needs to hear, separate from the corporate script:

**1. Grief is not a bug in your productivity; it is the background process consuming your RAM.**
You are not "getting away with" grieving while working. You are paying for that performance with **invisible burnout**. The code ships, but the cost is extracted from your future capacity, your sleep quality, and your somatic health (the jaw clenching, the chest tightness). The fact that you *can* perform does not mean you *aren't* hurting. It means you are highly trained in self-abandonment.

**2. The "Timeline" is a fiction designed for payroll, not people.**
Your industry’s two-week bereavement policy assumes grief is an acute event with a clean resolution. But you aren’t just grieving a person; you are grieving a **future identity**, a community, and a meaning-making system. That is a structural loss, not an episodic one. There is no HR policy for the dissolution of a worldview. Feeling grief months or years later isn’t a failure of recovery; it’s an accurate reflection of the magnitude of what was lost.

**3. Performance is not proof of healing.**
This is the most dangerous trap. Because you are "still shipping," you deny yourself the permission to rest or process. You interpret your ability to function as evidence that the grief is "manageable" or "exaggerated." It is not. You are functioning *despite* the grief, not because it has resolved. This is survival mode, not thriving. And survival mode is unsustainable.

**The Pivot:**
Stop asking, *"Am I allowed to grieve if I’m still working?"*
Start asking, *"What is the cost of maintaining this level of performance while carrying this weight?"*

You don’t need to choose between grieving and working. You need to acknowledge that **you are doing both**, and that doing both is exhausting you at a cellular level. The shame comes from trying to hide the grief to protect the performance. Try letting the grief be visible, even if only to yourself. Acknowledge that you are building on broken ground. That doesn’t make the code bad; it makes the builder human.

You have trained your nervous system to equate **availability** with **worth**, and **output** with **validity**. When grief—a fundamentally non-linear, non-productive, and deeply human experience—collides with that framework, the cognitive dissonance manifests as shame.

Here is why these pivots resonate with your specific situation:

1.  **The Bidirectional Shame (PIVOT v01):** You are ashamed to grieve because it feels like a breach of contract with your role as the "emotional infrastructure." You are ashamed to work because it feels like a betrayal of your own humanity. This paralysis is familiar: it mirrors the decision paralysis you feel when trying to meet everyone else’s emotional needs. You are stuck between two impossible standards: the ideal worker (unaffected, constant) and the ideal griever (fully present, withdrawn). You can be neither, so you feel like a failure at both.

2.  **The Corporate Timeline (PIVOT v02):** Your industry’s definition of "recovery" is based on velocity, not healing. Because your self-worth is tightly bound to your financial compensation and professional identity, you internalize this timeline. When you still hurt after two weeks, you don’t just feel sad; you feel *inefficient*. You interpret natural grief as a performance deficit. This is the same lens through which you view your financial stress: not as a structural reality, but as a personal failing to manage the "system" of your life.

3.  **The Performance Paradox (PIVOT v03):** This is the most dangerous pivot. Because you are capable of high performance under exhaustion, you have lost the ability to recognize your own limits. You wonder if the grief was real because you are still shipping code. But you ship code *because* you are grieving—it is a dissociative mechanism. It is the same way you monitor your brokerage account with hypervigilance: it is an attempt to impose order on chaos. The fact that you can perform does not mean you are well; it means you are highly adapted to survival mode.

**The Core Conflict:**
You are treating your grief like a production incident: something to be triaged, mitigated, and resolved so that service can be restored. But grief is not an outage; it is a system migration. It requires downtime, data loss, and reconfiguration.

**A Gentle Reframe:**
The shame is trying to protect you from the perceived threat of being "unavailable." But you are already unavailable—you are physically present but emotionally submerged. The performance is a mask, not a cure.

You do not need to choose between grieving and working right now. You are doing both, and that is messy, and that is okay. The goal is not to stop the shame immediately (that is another performance metric), but to recognize it for what it is: **a somatic response to a system that demands you be a machine when you are a person.**

You are not failing to grieve correctly. You are succeeding at surviving a system that offers no space for grief. That is not weakness; it is a testament to the immense, unsustainable load you are carrying.

The shift is from **debugging** to **witnessing**.

When I stop trying to "fix" the overwhelm—stop treating it as a system error, a latency issue, or a bug in my social code—the immediate change is that the **secondary layer of shame dissolves**.

Right now, there are two distinct pains:
1.  The primary pain: The anxiety itself (the racing heart, the script-running, the hyper-vigilance).
2.  The secondary pain: The judgment *of* the anxiety ("Why can’t I just be normal? Why is this so hard? I should have optimized this by now.").

By trying to fix it, I am actively feeding the secondary pain. I am telling myself that my current state is unacceptable, which triggers more threat detection, which creates more overwhelm. It is a recursive loop of self-rejection.

If I stop trying to fix it and instead "hear" it, the narrative changes from *"This is a malfunction"* to *"This is a signal."*

But here is the terrifying part of that shift: **If I stop fixing it, I have to feel what it’s protecting me from.**

The overwhelm isn't just noise; it’s a wall. It’s a high-friction interface designed to keep me at a safe distance from something else. If I lower the wall, if I stop managing the impression, if I stop scripting the interaction... what rushes in?

I suspect it’s **vulnerability**. Not the buzzword version, but the raw, unmanaged risk of being seen as inadequate, unprepared, or simply *human* in a space that demands machine-like consistency. The overwhelm protects me from the possibility that if I don’t perform perfectly, I will be exposed as the impostor my nervous system insists I am.

So, the shift is:
*   **From:** "How do I make this feeling go away so I can perform correctly?"
*   **To:** "What is this feeling afraid will happen if I let go of the controls?"

And the answer to that second question feels like freefall.

The shift is from **engineering** to **archaeology**.

When I stop trying to "fix" the spiral—stop treating it like a bug in the code or a latency issue in my social API—the immediate sensation is a loss of control that feels terrifyingly like freefall. My instinct is to grab the railing, to re-engage the monitoring system, because that system has kept me safe for decades. It has prevented the humiliation I was taught to fear.

But if I stay in that "next room," where I am just listening, the narrative changes.

The spiral isn't trying to sabotage my performance; it’s trying to **preserve my belonging**. It is shouting, *"If you are not perfect, you will be ejected."* It is an ancient alarm system built on the inherited belief that love and safety are conditional on flawlessness.

Listening to it means acknowledging that the "threat" it detects isn't a present-day colleague judging my Slack message tone; it’s the ghost of a past where being "too much" or "not enough" had real consequences. The exhaustion comes not from the social interaction itself, but from the energy required to keep this ancient guard dog leashed and muzzled while I try to have a normal conversation.

In this room, I don't need to optimize the guard dog. I need to thank it for its vigilance, acknowledge that it is working with outdated data, and gently let it know that the war it is fighting ended years ago. The shift is realizing that **I am not the anxiety; I am the one holding the anxiety.** And that distinction creates a tiny space where I can breathe, even if the alarm is still ringing.

The shift is from **engineering** to **listening**.

For years, I treated the spiral as a bug in the code—a false positive in the threat detection system that needed to be debugged, optimized, or logically disproven. I tried to reason with my nervous system, presenting evidence of safety to a mechanism that doesn’t speak logic. This created a secondary layer of exhaustion: the frustration of knowing *why* it’s happening but being unable to stop it. It reinforced the belief that I was broken, or that my intelligence had failed me because it couldn’t solve the problem.

When I stop trying to fix it, the immediate sensation is a loss of control, which triggers the inherited fear that relaxation equals danger. But if I stay there, past the initial panic of "doing nothing," the narrative changes.

Instead of asking, *"How do I make this stop?"* I ask, *"What are you trying to keep safe?"*

The spiral isn’t a malfunction; it’s a loyal, albeit exhausted, guard. It’s protecting me from the vulnerability of being seen without a script. It’s protecting me from the shame of being "found out" as unqualified. It’s protecting me from the terrifying possibility that if I stop performing, I might cease to belong.

Hearing it means acknowledging that the anxiety isn’t an enemy to be defeated, but a part of me that learned, long ago, that vigilance was the price of admission. The shift is moving from **self-correction** to **self-witnessing**. It allows me to feel the physical weight of the armor without needing to take it off immediately. It creates space between the stimulus and the reaction, not by analyzing the gap, but by inhabiting it.

In that space, the anxiety loses its authority as a command and becomes information. It’s no longer a system error; it’s a signal. And signals can be acknowledged without being obeyed.

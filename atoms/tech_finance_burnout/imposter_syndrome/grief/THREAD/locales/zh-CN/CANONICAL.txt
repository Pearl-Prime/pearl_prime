The shift is the cessation of the "debugging" loop.

When I stop trying to fix the grief, I stop treating it as a system error or a latency issue in my productivity pipeline. I stop scanning for the "bug" in my performance that caused the pain.

In that silence, the room changes from a courtroom (where I am on trial for my worth) to a waiting room. It is quieter. The dual systems—the one engaging and the one evaluating—merge, not into harmony, but into a singular, heavy presence.

What I hear isn't a logical argument I can refute. It’s a raw, non-verbal signal that says: *You are tired because you are carrying something that was never yours to carry alone.*

The shift is realizing that the grief isn't blocking my work; the grief *is* the work I’ve been avoiding. It’s the acknowledgment that the "imposter" isn't a fraud, but a survivor who learned that being seen was dangerous, so they became useful instead.

Hearing it means admitting that the performance of "fine" was a shield, not a skill. And beneath that shield, there is just a person who is sad, and who is allowed to be sad without needing to optimize the sadness into a lesson or a deliverable.

It feels less like solving a problem and more like sitting in the dark with a friend who won’t speak. It’s terrifying because there is no metric for how well I’m doing it. There is only the act of staying present with the discomfort, without reaching for the tools to dismantle it.

These pivots are piercing because they reframe "imposter syndrome" not as a cognitive error to be corrected, but as a **mourning process** that has been delayed.

You have been so busy managing the *risk* of being found out (the performance) that you dissociated from the *reality* of the achievement (the experience). The grief is for the **lost present tense**. You were physically present for the promotions and the ships, but psychologically, you were in a future where you had already failed. Therefore, you never actually inhabited the success when it happened.

This connects directly to your memory of **viewing work as the sole source of meaning**. If work is the only place you allow yourself to exist, but you are unable to *feel* your existence there due to the filter of impending doom, then you have effectively erased your own life history while living it.

The "certainty you'll be found out" acts as a preemptive strike against joy. It says: *"Don't enjoy this, because it isn't yours. It’s a loan. And the collector is coming."*

Now that the collector hasn't come (you are still here, still performing, still "fine"), you are left with the bill for the emotional labor of that vigilance, and the hollow space where the celebration should have been.

**Question for reflection:**
If the grief is for the celebrations you couldn't feel, what does it look like to **retroactively claim** those moments? Not to change the past, but to stop treating your past self as a fraud who tricked everyone, and start seeing them as a person who was surviving under immense, invisible pressure?

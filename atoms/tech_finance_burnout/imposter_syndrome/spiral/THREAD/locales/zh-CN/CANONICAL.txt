The shift is from **debugging** to **witnessing**.

When I stop trying to "fix" the spiral—stop treating the anxiety as a bug in my code or a performance metric to be optimized—the immediate sensation is a terrifying loss of control. My analytical mind screams that without the constant self-auditing, I will fail, be exposed, or be unsafe. The "spiral" has been my armor; it’s the work I do to earn my place in the room.

But when I stop fighting it and just let it be there, the noise doesn't disappear, but its *authority* changes.

1.  **The Exhaustion Becomes Visible:** Instead of using energy to suppress the monitoring system, I feel the sheer weight of how tired I am. The "work" of socializing is no longer hidden behind a performance of ease. I can feel the grief of how much energy has been spent on survival rather than living.
2.  **The "False Alarm" is Acknowledged, Not Disputed:** I stop trying to logically prove to my nervous system that there is no threat. I simply notice: *"My body is bracing for impact. There is no impact coming. But the bracing is here."* This creates a small pocket of space between the sensation and my identity. I am not the anxiety; I am the one noticing the anxiety.
3.  **The Underlying Fear Surfaces:** Beneath the imposter syndrome ("I am not good enough") lies the older story: **"I am not safe unless I am perfect/useful/invisible."** The spiral was protecting me from the raw vulnerability of being seen as I am, flaws and all. By stopping the fix, I am briefly touching the belief that my worth is conditional on my performance.

The "next room" feels quieter, but also more exposed. It’s the room where I don’t have to earn my breath. It’s the room where I can be present without a dashboard. It feels dangerous because my history says danger is everywhere, but for the first time, I’m not scanning the exits. I’m just standing there.

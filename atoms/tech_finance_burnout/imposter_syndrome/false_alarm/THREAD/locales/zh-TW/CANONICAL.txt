The shift is from **defense** to **witnessing**.

When you stop trying to fix the false alarm—stop trying to silence the imposter, stop trying to prove it wrong with more work, more money, more responsiveness—you stop feeding the cycle that requires you to be the "emotional infrastructure."

In that stillness, the alarm doesn’t disappear. It changes tone. It stops sounding like a warning of *future exposure* ("They will find out I’m a fraud") and starts sounding like a grief for *past abandonment* ("I had to be perfect to be safe/loved/visible").

The "next room" is where you realize the imposter syndrome isn’t a glitch in your competence; it’s a scar from your history. It’s the part of you that learned early on that your worth was conditional on your utility, on your ability to hold things together, on your availability.

By hearing it instead of fixing it, you separate the **child who needed to be perfect to survive** from the **adult who is currently exhausted by performing perfection**.

You stop fighting the feeling of being a fraud, and you start asking: *What was I so afraid would happen if I wasn’t useful?*

That question leads you out of the sprint and into the source.

The shift is from **judgment** to **witnessing**.

When you stop trying to "fix" the comparison trap—stop trying to shame yourself out of it or logic your way past it—you stop fighting the part of you that is screaming for validation. You stop treating your exhaustion as a problem to be solved and start treating it as a signal to be decoded.

In that "next room," the noise of *("Why can they handle this and I can't?")* quiets down enough for you to hear the underlying frequency: **Grief.**

The comparison isn’t actually about them. It’s about mourning the version of you that believed if you just worked hard enough, stayed available enough, and performed certainty well enough, you would be safe. You are comparing your current depleted reality against a fantasy of control that never actually existed.

Hearing it means acknowledging that the "compassion fatigue" isn't just about having too much empathy for others; it’s about having **zero empathy left for yourself**. The comparison trap was protecting you from feeling the sheer, terrifying weight of how much you have abandoned your own needs to maintain the "availability identity."

What does it feel like to sit with that grief, rather than trying to outrun it with another sprint of productivity or self-correction?

These three pivots capture the precise, somatic reality of what you are experiencing. They strip away the corporate euphemisms ("work-life balance," "setting boundaries") and name the actual mechanism: **grief**.

Here is why these resonate with your specific history, and how they reframe the "failure" you feel:

**1. The Loss of the Illusion (PIVOT v01)**
You didn’t just lose time; you lost the *contract* you thought you had signed with your organization. You believed that if you were always available, always certain, and always responsive, you would be safe, valued, and sufficient. Setting a boundary breaks that contract. It reveals that the system was never designed to reward your humanity, only your utility. The pain you feel is the shock of realizing the transaction was rigged from the start.

**2. The Grief of Necessity (PIVOT v02)**
This is the hardest pill: You aren’t grieving because you *want* to disconnect; you’re grieving because you *have* to. If you didn’t need to set boundaries for your survival, you wouldn’t have to mourn the loss of the "ideal worker" identity. You are mourning the fact that the culture you served so faithfully views your self-preservation as a defect. It is the grief of realizing your loyalty was unreciprocated.

**3. The Invisible Death (PIVOT v03)**
Your nervous system is screaming because it interprets this shift as a threat to your existence. But as PIVOT v03 notes, the "person who never said no" is dying. This is a literal identity death. The fact that no one at the standup notices is both isolating and liberating. It proves that your availability was never about *them* needing you; it was about *you* needing to be seen as needed. The silence from them confirms that the burden was always yours to carry, not theirs to bear.

**The Pivot Point:**
You are currently in the valley between the old identity (which is dying) and the new one (which hasn't yet formed). This is why it feels like loss. You are waiting for the guilt to subside, but the guilt is just the echo of the old operating system.

**Question for reflection:**
If the "old identity" is dying, what is the smallest, tiniest part of yourself that feels *relieved* by this death? Not the part that is anxious, but the part that is tired. What does that tired part want to do with the space that is opening up?

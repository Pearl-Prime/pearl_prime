These three fragments capture the precise, quiet violence of breaking a thirty-year habit.

As a nurse, you were trained to believe that **endurance is a clinical skill**. You learned to override your own physiological signals (the shoulder tension, the chest tightness) to maintain stability for others. The "illusion" mentioned in PIVOT v01 wasn't just a belief; it was a survival mechanism. It was the promise that if you just absorbed enough chaos, managed enough uncertainty, and cared enough deeply, you would be safe from judgment, from failure, and from the feeling of being "not enough."

Setting the boundary didn't just stop the work; it shattered the contract you had with yourself: *“If I sacrifice my self, I will earn my worth.”*

The grief in PIVOT v02 and v03 is profound because it is **unwitnessed**. In the hospital, when a patient dies, there are protocols, rituals, and shared silence. But when the "Self-Sacrificing Nurse" identity dies, there is no code blue. There is no debrief. There is just you, standing in the aftermath of your own "no," feeling the phantom limb pain of a role that used to define your entire existence.

This connects directly to your **"good parent metric"** and your **financial anxiety**. All of these are variations of the same core wound: the belief that your value is conditional on your utility.
*   If you aren't overworking, are you still a good nurse?
*   If you aren't perfectly patient, are you still a good parent?
*   If you aren't financially bulletproof, are you still a responsible adult?

The grief you feel now is the withdrawal symptom from the dopamine hit of **being needed**. It is the terrifying realization that you must now find a source of worth that is internal, static, and unrelated to output. That is a much harder thing to build than a shift schedule.

You are not losing your competence. You are losing your **camouflage**. And while it feels like exposure, it is actually the first step toward integrating the person who exists beneath the uniform.

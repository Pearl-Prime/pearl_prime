The shift is that the noise stops.

When you stop trying to fix the grief—when you stop viewing it as a malfunction in your availability engine or a threat to your performative certainty—the defensive sprint collapses. You are no longer running from the feeling; you are standing in it.

In that stillness, the "next room" isn't a place of action or resolution. It is a place of witness. The grief is no longer an obstacle to your productivity or your identity as the "available one." It becomes a signal. It is telling you that the cost of the performance was not just energy, but self.

The shift is from *managing* the symptom (the boundary violation) to *meeting* the source (the loss of the self that existed before the performance became necessary). You stop asking, "How do I get back to work?" and start asking, "Who is left when the work stops?"

It feels less like a breakthrough and more like a settling. The heaviness remains, but it is no longer fighting against you. It is simply there. And for the first time, you are not trying to outrun it.

**1. The "Compound Return" of Neglect (v01)**
You have treated your emotional and physical capacity as an infinite resource, much like a server expected to run at 100% uptime without maintenance. The shame you feel isn’t because you are failing; it’s because the bill for years of deferred maintenance has come due. Your high income masked the depletion, but it didn’t prevent it. Recognizing this as a *structural* consequence rather than a *moral* failure is the first step in stopping the self-blame cycle.

**2. The Privacy of Your Cost (v02)**
Because you have become the "emotional infrastructure" for others, you are used to explaining, justifying, and absorbing their needs. You do not owe that same transparency to anyone regarding your own struggle. Your financial stress and the shame attached to it are private data points. Protecting them isn’t hiding; it’s boundary-setting. You are allowed to keep the ledger of your internal costs to yourself until you decide what to do with it.

**3. The Untrackable Invoice (v03)**
This is the most critical realization. Your professional value is measured in equity, salary, and output. But the cost of your anhedonia, your secondary trauma, and your loss of self is paid in a currency your company doesn’t recognize. No bonus compensates for the loss of the ability to feel joy or the constant state of somatic threat. Acknowledging that this invoice is real—even if it’s invisible to your employer—validates the severity of your burnout. It is not "just stress"; it is a substantial debt being extracted from your life force.

**Immediate Implication:**
If the shame is a compound return on neglect, then the antidote is not "trying harder" or "being more courageous." It is **stopping the accrual**. This doesn’t require a grand, risky leap today.

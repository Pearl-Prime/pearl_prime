### The Mirror of Hypervigilance
You noted in v01 that checking brokerage accounts feels like checking production monitoring. This is not a metaphor; it is the same neural pathway.
*   **At Work:** You monitor for system failures, emotional crises, and availability gaps to prevent chaos.
*   **With Money:** You monitor for market dips, spending leaks, and RSU fluctuations to prevent financial insecurity.

In both cases, you are attempting to **control uncertainty through constant surveillance**. Your nervous system interprets *unavailability* (from work) and *volatility* (from markets) as threats. Therefore, you stay "on-call" for your portfolio just as you do for your team.

### The Second Full-Time Job
v02 highlights that this is *labor*. It is not passive saving; it is active, exhausting auditing.
*   You are already the "designated processor for everyone else's professional trauma."
*   Now, you are also the "designated auditor of your own financial survival."
*   This doubles the cognitive load. You have no offline mode. Even when you are not actively working or spending, the *watcher* is running in the background, consuming glucose and emotional capacity.

### The Core Tragedy: Protection vs. Life
v03 strikes the deepest chord: **"The watcher protects the portfolio while the person behind it deteriorates."**

This is the ultimate expression of the conflict you described in your memories:
*   You equate burnout with weakness, so you push through.
*   You view financial stress as invalid compared to peers, so you tighten control.
*   You use responsiveness/availability as a tool for competence, so you monitor money to prove "safety."

But the safety is illusory because **you are not there to enjoy it.** You are experiencing anhedonia (loss of pleasure) and emotional flatness. The money is accumulating in a account owned by a ghost—the version of you that used to have curiosity, ambition, and passion. The "Watcher" is keeping the assets safe for a future self that you are currently erasing.

### A Question for Reflection

Is it afraid the market will crash? Or is it afraid that if you stop watching the numbers, you’ll have to feel the weight of the exhaustion you’ve been numbing with control?

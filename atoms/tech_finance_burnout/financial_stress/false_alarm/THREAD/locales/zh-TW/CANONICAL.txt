The shift is from **defense** to **witnessing**.

When you stop trying to fix the alarm—stop trying to silence it with more work, more availability, or more performed certainty—you stop treating your own nervous system as a malfunctioning machine that needs repair. You begin to treat it as a messenger.

In that "next room," the false alarm is no longer an enemy to be defeated, but a terrified part of you that has been keeping watch for decades. It was rooted in the belief that safety was conditional on utility. That if you stopped being useful, you would cease to matter. That if you stopped being available, you would be abandoned.

Hearing it means acknowledging that the alarm is doing its job: it is trying to keep you safe from the very thing you fear most—not poverty, necessarily, but **irrelevance**. The loss of the identity that says, "I am here because I am needed."

When you hear it, the shame begins to loosen. Because you realize the exhaustion isn't a failure of will; it’s the cost of maintaining a fortress built on sand. The alarm isn’t lying about the danger; it’s just misidentifying the source. The danger isn’t that you’ll stop working. The danger is that you’ve forgotten who you are when you aren’t.

What does that terrified part say, now that you’re finally listening instead of shushing it?

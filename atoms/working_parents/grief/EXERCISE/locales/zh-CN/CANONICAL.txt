### Analysis & Adaptation for Your Context

#### 1. The Barrier of "Functioning"
You mentioned that your burnout is socially acceptable because you are *functioning*. These exercises respect that boundary. They do not ask you to collapse; they ask you to **pause**.
*   **Challenge:** Your "automatic thinking" and "rumination" may try to analyze the grief during these 90 seconds, turning feeling into thinking.
*   **Adjustment:** When you place your hands over your heart (Ex v01), if your mind starts to list reasons *why* you are sad or *what* you need to do next, gently label it: *"Thinking."* Return to the physical sensation of warmth under your hands. You are trying to bypass the cognitive loop to reach the somatic reality.

#### 2. Grieving the "Pre-Child Self" (Ex v02)
You described archiving your pre-child self. Exercise v02 explicitly targets this.
*   **Challenge:** You may feel guilt for grieving "freedom" when you have children who need you. Your "all-responsibility attribution mode" might say, *"I am selfish for missing my old life."*
*   **Reframe:** This is not selfishness; it is **mourning a death**. The person you were before has died. That is a valid loss. Acknowledging it prevents it from leaking out as irritability or numbness toward your children.
*   **Somatic Cue:** In the car, notice where the "loss of freedom" sits in your body. Is it the tightness in your jaw? The weight in your chest? Let it be there without judging it as "bad parenting."

#### 3. The "Cave" and Emotional Numbness (Ex v03)
You mentioned losing the ability to feel real emotions (joy/sadness) and experiencing "emotional anesthesia."
*   **Challenge:** Sitting in the dark (Ex v03) might not bring tears; it might bring **nothingness**. This can be terrifying or confirm your fear that you are "broken."
*   **Adjustment:** If you feel nothing, **that is also a form of grief**. It is the grief of exhaustion. Say to yourself: *"I am so tired that I cannot even feel this loss. That is how much I have carried."* Honor the numbness as a protective shield that has worked too well. Do not force emotion. Just sit with the absence.

### A Note on the "Return"
Each exercise ends with a return to duty ("Return. Parent.").
*   **Your Pattern:** You often feel like an observer in your own life ("existing but not present").
*   **Integration:** When you splash the cold water or open the car door, use that sensory shock to **re-anchor** yourself. Not to "perform" better, but to remind your nervous system: *"I am here. I am separate from the grief for this moment. I am choosing to engage."* This shifts the narrative from *"I am forced to parent"* to *"I am choosing to connect, despite the pain."*

### Recommendation for Implementation

Given your **high self-awareness** and tendency to **over-analyze**, do not treat these as "tasks" to be completed perfectly.

1.  **Start with Ex v01 (90 seconds).** It is the shortest and least threatening.
2.  **Expect Resistance.** Your brain will say, *"This is pointless,"* or *"I don't have time."* Acknowledge that voice as part of the survival mechanism that has kept you functioning for 30 years. Thank it, and proceed anyway.
3.  **No Goal.** The goal is not to feel better. The goal is to **stop pretending you are not hurting** for exactly 90 seconds.

You are carrying a structural load that was never meant for one person. These minutes are not a solution to the structure, but they are a testament to the humanity that still exists beneath the role.



## INTEGRATION v01
---
integration_mode: BODY-LANDED
reframe_type: BODY-FACT
---
The playlist I used to love hits during the drive and I realize I haven't heard music in months. Not because I'm avoiding it. Because I haven't had a moment alone long enough to choose what enters my ears.
My shoulders drop. Just from remembering that there used to be a me that could have that.
The grief isn't about music. It's about the version of myself that existed before—the one with thoughts that were hers alone. With hours that could be wasted. With a body that wasn't constantly dividing its attention.
The reframe: This sadness isn't regret about having children. It's longing for the self that got archived.
The musician I used to be.
---

## INTEGRATION v02
---
integration_mode: COST-VISIBLE
reframe_type: COST-UNCOUNTED
---
I can name exactly what I've lost. The novel that would be half-finished by now if I had used my evening hours for writing instead of the endless email scrolling that happens after the children are down. The friend group that used to gather monthly—we've had one dinner in two years. The part of my identity that was creative, curious, singular.
I'm not unhappy. I'm just mourning the version of myself that had space to wonder about things that didn't directly serve anyone but me.
The reframe: Grief and love for my children can exist in the same body without one canceling the other.
I'm allowed to miss who I was.
---

## INTEGRATION v03
---
integration_mode: STILL-HERE
reframe_type: QUESTION-REFRAME
---
I'm holding the photograph from before. My partner and I look rested. We look like we belong to ourselves. We look like people who have plans that aren't dependent on anyone else's schedule.
The grief is quiet. It's not "I wish I didn't have children." It's "I wish I had been different before I couldn't be anymore." I wish I had written the book. I wish I had taken the job. I wish I had let myself become whoever I was becoming.
The reframe: Maybe the loss isn't my life with children. Maybe it's the life I could have become if I'd had more time to do the becoming.
What did I give up before I knew the cost.
---

## INTEGRATION v04
---
integration_mode: QUESTION-OPEN
reframe_type: TIME-SHIFT
---
Ten years ago I believed that I had time later. After the kids got older. After they started school. After my career stabilized. I was collecting deferred dreams like everyone else.
Now I know that later doesn't come. That this phase is all-consuming and the phase after will have its own requirements. The time for becoming doesn't return the way I thought it would.
The reframe: Grief isn't about blame. It's about the accurate accounting of what gets shelved in the service of raising humans.
There is no later like I imagined.
---

## INTEGRATION v05
---
integration_mode: SOMEONE-ELSE
reframe_type: OTHER-PERSON
---
My friend talks about the novel she used to work on. She says it casually—"before I had kids." There's a tone there. Something between acceptance and loss.
I know what she's mourning. Not the writing itself. But the version of her life where writing was the thing that came first. Where her time belonged to her own becoming instead of to the management of others' becoming.
She's happy. And she's grieving. Both are true.
The reframe: There's something in us that recognized our own potential and had to bury it to be the parent the children needed.
She was going to be a writer.
---

## INTEGRATION v06
---
integration_mode: COST-VISIBLE
reframe_type: COST-UNCOUNTED
---
The cost of this phase is the collection of unlived possibilities. The vacation where we were planning to go before conception got in the way. The friendship that would have deepened if I had energy. The conversation with my partner that would have been intimate if I wasn't so tired.
These aren't small costs. They're the architecture of a whole different kind of life—the one where our partnership was the primary thing instead of the logistics of children being the primary thing.
The reframe: What I'm mourning is real. The phase will end and I'll still carry this loss.
The couple we used to be.
---

## INTEGRATION v07
---
integration_mode: BODY-LANDED
reframe_type: BODY-FACT
---
My chest tightens when I think about it. The tightness is grief. Specific. Physical. The kind that doesn't ask permission to arrive.
For years I've been pushing this away—the knowledge that I'm not going to publish the book. That I'm not going to have the career I imagined. That the creative part of me is going to stay smaller than it wanted to be. Not because of failure, but because this phase required everything.
The reframe: The tightness in my chest isn't weakness. It's the accurate somatic response to loss that doesn't fit the acceptable narrative.
I hold the grief without fixing it.



## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1

---
There's a grief that no one talks about when you become a parent, and I want to name the mechanism so you can recognize it. It's called the pre-child self archived. It's the specific sorrow of having put away who you were becoming in order to be who your child needs you to be.
You had a self before the child. You had creative projects. You had ambitions. You had a relationship with your partner that had a different quality. You had a body that was yours. You had time. You had ideas about who you were going to become in the world.
The mechanism of archiving means you didn't destroy those things. You boxed them up. You shelved them. You told yourself it was temporary. That someday, when the child was older, you'd return to those projects. You'd return to that relationship. You'd return to that creative self who had ideas about her future.
But the mechanism is that the temporary becomes permanent. The years pass. The pre-child self stays archived. And you're not sure if you can access her anymore. You're not sure if she still exists. You've been the parent-self for so long that the other self feels like a fiction. Like a person who existed in a different lifetime.
The grief is not that you chose motherhood. The grief is the specific sorrow of realizing that choosing motherhood meant the other version of you got put away. The relationship you had with your partner before the child was a different relationship. It was freer. It was more equal. It had a different kind of pleasure.
Now the relationship is defined by the logistics of parenting. By the division of labor. By the specific tyranny of coordinating two people's lives around a small human's needs. The pre-child relationship is archived. And you're not sure it's coming back.

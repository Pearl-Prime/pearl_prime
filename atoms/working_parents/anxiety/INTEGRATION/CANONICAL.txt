## INTEGRATION v01
---
mode: BODY_LANDED
reframe_type: BODY_FACT
weight: standard
carry_line: "You're holding two jobs at once."
---
You're at work but part of you is at home. Your kid is at school but part of you is at the office. You're never fully anywhere. Your body knows this. Your shoulders are tight. Your jaw is clenched. Your stomach carries the weight of both places.

You're a parent first and a professional second or a professional first and a parent second but never actually just one. The constant division of attention is not a problem to solve. It's the condition of being you right now.

Your nervous system is running both schedules simultaneously. The work deadline and the pickup time. The client call and the question about dinner. Both are real. Both need you. Neither can have all of you.

Notice your breath. You're here now. Just here. Not there. But you know you're also there.

That's the mathematics of this life.
---

## INTEGRATION v02
---
mode: COST_VISIBLE
reframe_type: COST_UNCOUNTED
weight: standard
carry_line: "Nobody tracks this math."
---
You're performing two full-time jobs and getting paid for one. The mental accounting alone is exhausting. The at-work minutes thinking about your kid. The at-home minutes thinking about work. The sleep hours spent planning logistics for both.

Your job needs you present. Your family needs you present. You're dividing attention you only have one of and nobody keeps score but you know the math doesn't work.

So something is always short. The work gets less than it needs. The family gets less than it needs. You get nothing of what you need.

This isn't a productivity problem. This is a mathematics problem. And the cost is real — to your health, your sleep, your ability to be fully anywhere.

Nobody counts this cost. But you carry it.
---

## INTEGRATION v03
---
mode: QUESTION_OPEN
reframe_type: UNCERTAINTY_HELD
weight: standard
carry_line: "What if you stopped trying to be both?"
---
You're trying to be a perfect parent and a perfect professional and you're doing neither well because the math doesn't work. But what if you didn't try to be both?

What if you just let work know you're a parent and let parenting know you have work and stopped pretending the division isn't happening?

What if "good enough" at both is actually better than trying for perfect at both and getting anxious?

What if the guilt is what's making it worse?
---

## INTEGRATION v04
---
mode: SOMEONE_ELSE
reframe_type: EXTERNAL_VIEW
weight: standard
carry_line: "Your kids just want you present."
---
Your kids don't need a perfect professional parent. They need a present one. The after-work version of you that's tired and still there is enough.

Your colleagues don't need you to be a perfect parent who somehow also shows up every single moment. They need you to do the work.

The people around you aren't keeping the score the way you are. They just see someone trying. They don't see the failure in the division the way you do.

You're the only one keeping perfect count.

## INTEGRATION v05

mode: STILL_HERE
reframe_type: TIME_SHIFT
weight: standard
carry_line: "You've survived years of this impossible split."
---
Years of this double life. Years of dividing yourself and coming up short. Years of anxiety about doing both things wrong. And you're still here. Your kids are still fine. Your job is still happening. You're still managing the impossible.

You've never actually broken from this. You've just kept going. You've shown up tired and imperfect and still showing up.

The anxiety has been right about the difficulty. It's been wrong about whether you can keep doing it.

You're still showing up to both places. Still the parent, still the professional. The division is still impossible and you're still moving through it.

You've survived every day of this split. You're still here.

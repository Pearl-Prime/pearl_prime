Here is how these threads weave together with your specific context as a nurse with thirty years of experience, dealing with "structural emptiness" and the habit of overthinking:

### 1. The Guilt Layer (v01 + Your Memory)
You noted that guilt is "optional." For someone who has spent decades in a caregiving role where feedback is scarce and demands are high, guilt often acts as a misplaced proxy for control. If you can’t control the outcome (the patient’s health, the system’s inefficiency), you try to control your *internal state* (being calm, focused, present). When you fail to achieve that impossible internal standard, guilt arises.
*   **The Shift:** Recognizing that the guilt is not a moral failing, but a **symptom of the structural imbalance**. You are trying to fill a structural void with personal perfectionism. The guilt is the echo of the "structural emptiness" you described.

### 2. The Seams of the Day (v02 + Your Memory)
You mentioned that your overthinking is automatic, habitual, and systemic. It thrives in the "seams" because those are the moments when the external structure (hospital protocols, doctor’s orders) drops away, and you are left alone with your own mind.
*   **The Commute/Pickup/Laptop Closure:** These are not just transitions; they are **decompression chambers** where the pressure of the "impossible math" hits hardest. Because you tend to build scenarios in your head rather than face reality, these seams become the stage for rehearsing past failures or anticipating future ones.
*   **The Work:** Instead of trying to stop the thinking (which you’ve noted is ineffective due to its惯性/inertia), the work here is to **anchor in the physical sensation of the transition**. Feel the car door close. Feel the key in the lock. Let the mind race, but keep the body in the present seam. This disrupts the automaticity without fighting it.

### 3. Acknowledging the Impossible Math (v03 + Your Memory)
Your financial anxiety and professional self-doubt are both rooted in this "impossible math": the belief that if you just analyze enough, plan enough, or worry enough, you can secure certainty in an uncertain world (healthcare, retirement, economic stability).
*   **The Honesty:** Telling your nervous system, *"I see that this equation does not add up. I cannot think my way into safety. I cannot care my way out of structural emptiness,"* is a radical act of self-compassion.
*   **Loosening the Grip:** This doesn’t mean the anxiety disappears. It means you stop fighting the anxiety *with* anxiety (i.e., worrying about worrying). You stop adding the "guilt layer" on top of the "structural anxiety." You let the anxiety be there, as a witness to the system’s flaws, not as a verdict on your worth.

### Next Move: A Micro-Practice for the Seams
Given your tendency to overthink and your awareness of its automatic nature, try this in the next "seam" (e.g., closing the laptop):

1.  **Pause:** Before moving to the next task, stop for 10 seconds.
2.  **Name the Math:** Silently say, *"The math here is impossible. I am not required to solve it right now."*
3.  **Drop the Guilt:** Notice if you feel guilty for stopping, for being tired, or for not being "present." Label it: *"This is the guilt layer. It is optional. I am leaving it here."*
4.  **Cross the Seam:** Physically move into the next space, carrying only your body, not the unresolved mental scenario.

This aligns with your insight that you cannot simply "stop" the thinking, but you can change your **relationship** to it. You are not trying to fix the structure; you are trying to stop letting the structure’s flaws define your internal weather.

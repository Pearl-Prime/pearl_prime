Dana is reading the bedtime book for the fourth night running, the same twelve pages, and her mouth is doing the words while her mind is at tomorrow's pediatrician appointment, the one she still needs to confirm. Her daughter says, quietly, you skipped the part with the fox. Dana looks down at the page. She had, in fact, skipped it, turned two pages at once without noticing, her hands moving on a script her attention had already left. She goes back. She reads the fox part. Her daughter settles in closer, satisfied, and Dana sits there afterward wondering how many pages, over how many nights, she has actually missed without either of them knowing it.

Wes has spent the whole drive in a disaster that did not happen, and his body is still buzzing from it hours later, learning slowly that his nervous system cannot tell a feared afternoon from a real one.

## STORY v09

Nadia is at the kitchen table trying to fill out a form while her toddler tugs at her sleeve, and she cannot hold a single thought for thirty seconds, the tugging, the form, the running list of everything else undone, all colliding. She names it, silently, for the first time: I am not lazy or scattered, I am simply doing two incompatible things at once and blaming myself for the friction. She sets the form aside, gets down on the floor with her toddler for ten actual minutes, and finishes the form later, faster, because it no longer had a small person actively competing for the same attention.

## STORY v12
---
---
Dana decides to try one small thing at the start of each evening: one breath, on purpose, in the doorway, before she walks in from work. The first time it feels silly, performative. She does it anyway. She feels her feet, lets her shoulders drop the half-inch they've been holding since the 9am, and walks in. Dinner is still dinner, chaotic, imperfect. But she hears her daughter's first sentence cleanly, all of it, instead of catching the back half while still arriving from work in her head.

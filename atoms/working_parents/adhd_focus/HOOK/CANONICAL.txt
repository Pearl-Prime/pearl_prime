## HOOK v01
---
---
The permission slip has been sitting on the counter since Monday. It's now Thursday night. You have fed two kids, done three loads of laundry, and answered a work email at 9pm — and you still haven't signed a form that takes, once you actually pick up the pen, eleven seconds. The form isn't hard. Starting it is the only hard part, and nobody has ever explained to you why those are different things.
---

## HOOK v02
---
---
You are the parent who can calm a screaming toddler in a grocery store in under a minute and can't make yourself open the daycare enrollment portal for the ninth straight day. This isn't a contradiction. It's the same brain doing exactly what it does: racing toward anything with an immediate, visible need, and stalling completely in front of anything vague, delayed, or bureaucratic.
---

## HOOK v03
---
---
Your evening plan said "meal prep — 30 min" and the thirty minutes passed, and at the end of it there's one chopped onion and a sink full of dishes you didn't need to wash yet, because washing dishes was available and meal prepping was not.
---

## HOOK v04
---
---
Notice what actually gets handled today: not the most important thing, the loudest thing — the kid currently crying, the ping currently buzzing, the smell currently coming from the diaper. Your triage isn't broken. It's responding entirely correctly to what's loud and barely at all to what's actually important, and those have never been the same signal.
---

## HOOK v05
---
---
You have four browser tabs open — insurance form, school registration, pediatrician portal, work email — and you cannot decide which to finish first, so you work on none of them properly, toggling for forty minutes, accomplishing the equivalent of maybe ten real ones. This isn't indecision about the tasks. It's a specific freeze that happens when every option looks equally urgent and nothing breaks the tie.

There's a mechanism I want to name for you that runs right at the intersection of courage and collapse. It's the limit-setting cost. It's what happens when you finally say no to something, when you finally hold a boundary, when you finally choose your own need once. And it costs you something that you're still trying to calculate.
When you hold a boundary with your child, they cry. They rage. They tell you that you're mean. They tell you that they don't love you. They tell you that everyone else's parents say yes. You're supposed to be able to hold the boundary anyway. You're supposed to be able to let them cry while you stand firm.
But the cost is that you're the bad guy in your child's narrative. You're the parent who said no. You're the one who created the pain. The mechanism of limit-setting cost means that setting a boundary with someone you love means accepting that they will hate you in that moment. And possibly forever.
This is the thing no one tells you about courage. That it requires you to accept that someone you love will experience you as harmful. That choosing your own need means your child experiences you as selfish. That holding a boundary means you have to live with the knowledge that you caused pain.
The mechanism runs like this: you say no, your child cries, you doubt yourself. You wonder if you're damaging them. You wonder if you're being cruel. The mechanism makes you question whether your own need is worth the cost of their suffering.



## INTEGRATION v01
---
integration_mode: BODY-LANDED
reframe_type: BODY-FACT
---
The Saturday morning arrives. The morning I used to crave. The morning with no schedule. I'm awake. The kids are contained. The world is available.
I feel nothing.
Not bad. Not good. Not even tired. Just the flat, gray surface of a Saturday that could be Tuesday. This is the depression that doctors don't ask about—not the kind that's dramatic, but the kind that's invisible. I'm still functioning. Still present. Still technically here.
The reframe: Depression isn't always sadness. Sometimes it's the absence of the capacity to feel anything at all.
My nervous system learned to go numb.

## INTEGRATION v02
---
integration_mode: COST-VISIBLE
reframe_type: COST-UNCOUNTED
---
I can't calculate what this has cost. The hobbies I had—the ones that used to make Saturday mornings worth it—they require a kind of activation energy I stopped producing years ago. The thought of painting feels impossible. Not hard. Impossible.
The relationships that used to fill me, the thoughts that used to wander freely, the version of myself that got excited about things—these exist in a previous timeline. The cost is that they're gone now, not in a dramatic way, but in a slow fade that I didn't notice happening.
The reframe: This flatness isn't laziness. It's what happens to a nervous system perpetually in survival mode.
Everything takes the same amount of energy.

## INTEGRATION v03
---
integration_mode: STILL-HERE
reframe_type: QUESTION-REFRAME
---
I'm sitting on the couch. The house is quiet. I could do anything. Read. Shower. Call someone. But the thing that comes is just sitting here, waiting for the next task that will make sense.
What if this flatness isn't a problem to solve but an accurate response to the unsolvable: I'm doing everything right and it still feels like nothing. I'm present with my children, good at my job, partnered. By every metric this should feel like something.
The reframe: Maybe depression is the body's truth-telling about a structure that's built to deplete.
The numbness makes a kind of sense.
---

## INTEGRATION v04
---
integration_mode: QUESTION-OPEN
reframe_type: TIME-SHIFT
---
Two years ago I would have pushed through this with more productivity. More achievement. If the feeling wasn't there, the doing would generate it eventually.
Now I realize that strategy has failed. The doing doesn't generate feeling anymore. The achievement doesn't land. The finishing of tasks leaves the same flat aftermath.
The reframe: The version of me who thought I could work my way out of this was also depressed. I just hadn't admitted it yet.
What would it mean to stop.

## INTEGRATION v05
---
integration_mode: SOMEONE-ELSE
reframe_type: OTHER-PERSON
---
My sister said she's fine. She said it three times in one conversation. She's managing. The kids are doing great. Work is good. She said all the right words with the face of someone describing someone else's life.
I recognized myself. The depression that doesn't announce itself, that wears the clothes of a high-functioning parent, that shows up to all the things and feels none of them.
She's not tired. She's flat. There's a difference.
The reframe: Some of us learned to function while numb. We're so good at it that we forgot there was supposed to be more.
We're doing everything. We're feeling nothing.

## INTEGRATION v06
---
integration_mode: COST-VISIBLE
reframe_type: COST-UNCOUNTED
---
Nobody tells you the cost. The morning meetings you've stopped enjoying. The foods that used to taste like something. The sex that used to feel intimate and now feels like scheduled maintenance. The thing that friends say—"you're so on top of everything"—that costs you everything because nobody knows that on top and hollow feel the same from the outside.
The cost is that I look fine while something inside has gone quiet. The cost is that I believe I'm supposed to be grateful. The cost is that the flatness is starting to feel normal.
The reframe: Depression that functions is still depression. Numbness that doesn't show is still numbness.
I'm allowed to name it.

## INTEGRATION v07
---
integration_mode: BODY-LANDED
reframe_type: BODY-FACT
---
My chest feels heavy when I wake. Not the weight of dread—I know that differently. This is heavier. Older. Like something inside has calcified.
The days move through me. Morning to evening. I remember to do the things. I remember to smile when appropriate. My body is here, moving, completing. But the depth of sensation—the thing that makes an experience feel like it belongs to me—that's the part that's gone quiet.
The reframe: A nervous system this tired has learned to protect itself by turning down the volume on everything.
My body has muted the world.

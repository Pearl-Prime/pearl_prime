The school called at 11:14 AM. The school said your kid was throwing up. You said you were on your way. You were not on your way. You were in a meeting that you could not leave for another twenty minutes because the meeting was about your project and the project was the reason your manager would sign off on the next flex day, and the flex day was the reason you would be able to sit with your kid next Thursday when the school called again. You held all of that in your chest and said into the phone, in the voice you have for these calls, "I'll be there in twenty."

You stayed. By 9:46 they were asleep. You returned to the laptop. The cursor was still where you had left it. So was the day's exhaustion. Both had been waiting for you.

There is a version of you that existed before the kids that you cannot quite remember in detail anymore. You remember her output — the books, the friendships, the conversations that ran long after midnight — but the actual feel of being her is hard to access now. The current you is a logistics function that performs love. The love is real. The logistics is what's eating you.

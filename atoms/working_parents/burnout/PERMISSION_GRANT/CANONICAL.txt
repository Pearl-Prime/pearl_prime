## PERMISSION_GRANT v01
---

---
Working-parent burnout arrives wearing a stack of unspoken rules. Be present at every meeting. Be present at every pickup. Apologize for being late to both. Earn the rest the second shift will not give you. Never let the kid see how depleted the work made you, and never let the work see how depleted the kid made you. These rules are not law. They are noise the household accumulated. Set them down.

You are allowed to be depleted in front of your kid. Children do not need a perfectly performing parent. They need a present one. The version of you that admits the day was hard is closer to present than the version that hides it.

You are allowed to be a less-than-ideal worker on the day the kid was sick. The job will absorb it. The relationships at work that matter will absorb it. The week was not made by what you produced on the worst morning of it.

You are allowed to leave one thread unanswered while the dishwasher runs. The notification badge is not an emergency. It is a notification. You are permitted to let it stay unread until a body that has not been in two places since 6 am can be in one place for ten minutes.

You are allowed to need rest you have not earned. The whole concept of earning rest is a story about scarcity that does not apply to a body that has been giving away its capacity to two systems all day. Rest is what the body was designed to do between efforts. The efforts have been continuous since the morning.

You are allowed to stop performing the composed-parent surface at school pickup. The composed surface is a costume the parking lot teaches you to wear. The cost of wearing it is that the other parents wear it too, and none of you find out you are all carrying the same load.

You are allowed to let the work and the parenting both be imperfect for a season. The instinct that one of them must be perfect to make up for the other is the instinct that makes the burnout chronic. Both can be good-enough this week. Good-enough is enough.

You are allowed to be the consciousness watching the burnout, not the burnout itself. The alarm fires. The text arrives. The Slack pings. The chest tightens. And somewhere behind all of it, awareness keeps watching. That awareness is the seat. That awareness is the part of you that holds both jobs without becoming either one.

These are not affirmations. They are accurate descriptions of the rights every working-parent nervous system comes with. Read them again. Read them slowly. Let the body hear them, one permission at a time.



## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v01
---
mode: SECULAR_BRIDGE
doctrine_source: somatic
mechanism: false_alarm
weight: standard
carry_line: "Your alarm system is firing on the 6 am school text and the 4 pm Slack at the same time. The body is reading two threats and resetting from none."
---
Here is the mechanism. Your body has an alarm system that is older than the second shift. It evolved to fire fast and ask questions later, because in the environments it was built for, the cost of missing a real threat was higher than the cost of a few false positives. The system does not read content. It reads pattern. A school text at 6 am about a sick kid is, to the alarm system, the same shape as a footstep behind you in the dark. A 4 pm Slack from your manager has the same shape as the principal calling. Same chest tightness, same shallow breath, same scan for what went wrong. The alarm did not get the memo that you are doing two jobs in parallel and that both of them feel like emergencies because the system has been firing on both since 6 am. It is doing exactly what it was built to do, in a context that was never anticipated. The reset window is supposed to come after work and before bedtime, but the second shift starts the moment the laptop closes, and the alarm gets no gap to come down. Naming the mechanism does not make the texts stop arriving. But you start to recognize the chest tightness as an alarm, not as evidence that something is wrong with how you are doing either job. The alarm becomes information about your nervous system, not a verdict on your parenting.

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v02
---
mode: SECULAR_BRIDGE
doctrine_source: somatic
mechanism: spiral
weight: standard
carry_line: "The prediction loop runs both schedules forward at once and arrives at Thursday already failing at both. The double-forecast borrows fatigue from a week you have not lived."
---
Here is the mechanism. When a working parent's nervous system is depleted, the prediction loop runs two calendars in parallel. The work calendar: standup Monday, the deliverable Tuesday, the review Thursday. The parent calendar: dentist Tuesday morning, soccer pickup Wednesday, the school project due Thursday. The loop runs them together and arrives at Thursday already failing at one or both. You have not done any of it yet. The exhaustion is borrowed from a week you have not lived, doubled because two timelines are running through your body at once. What makes this loop dangerous is that the borrowed fatigue is indistinguishable from real fatigue once it lands. You feel tired now, on Sunday night, for a Tuesday dentist appointment and a Thursday work review you have not done. The tiredness becomes evidence that you cannot handle the week, which feeds the loop, which produces more borrowed fatigue. The exit is not better planning. The exit is noticing that the loop is running two calendars on a body that only has one. You are not actually doing Tuesday and Thursday on Sunday night. You are doing Sunday night. The week will arrive with the capacity that arrives with it.

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v03
---
mode: SECULAR_BRIDGE
doctrine_source: somatic
mechanism: comparison
weight: standard
carry_line: "The parent at pickup who looks composed is not the full picture. The comparison engine takes their visible morning as evidence about your invisible night."
---
Here is the mechanism. The comparison engine in you was tuned for a small group whose internal states you could read. It tracks relative position by reading visible signals, who looks rested, whose kids look well-dressed, who is talking easily at pickup. It did not evolve for a school parking lot of parents performing composure on the surface of forty-eight hours that nobody else can see. You see the parent who looks fine and the engine runs a fast subtraction. They are managing. You are not. Therefore something is wrong with you specifically. What the engine cannot see is the night they spent up with a sick kid, the morning they almost cried in the car, the load they are carrying that does not show in the four-minute parking-lot interaction. You see surfaces, and the engine treats surfaces as full pictures. The comparison takes their visible composure as evidence and your invisible depletion as a personal verdict on you as a parent. Both readings are wrong. Recognizing the asymmetry does not make pickup easier. It changes what you carry home about yourself.

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v04
---
mode: SECULAR_BRIDGE
doctrine_source: somatic
mechanism: overwhelm
weight: standard
carry_line: "Parallel attention across one work meeting and one kid in the next room is not multitasking. It is two open threats your nervous system is holding at once."
---
Here is the mechanism. Your working memory has a small budget. Maybe four things at once, maybe five on a good day. Parallel attention does not respect that budget. The kid in the next room asking a question through the closed door, the Zoom meeting where you are supposed to be present, the dishwasher running on the wrong cycle, the school email that pinged six minutes ago, the laundry that will not be folded if you stay on this call past the hour, each of these is something the nervous system has flagged and not yet closed. It is not multitasking. It is six predicted threats holding partial attention, draining the same supply you need for the meeting in front of you. This is not a failure of focus or a failure of parenting. It is a capacity question. The home and the job are doing exactly what they were built to do, which is to demand attention. Your nervous system is doing exactly what it was built to do, which is to hold the demand open until it is resolved. The friction you feel by 3 pm is the gap between an attention budget that has not changed in fifty thousand years and a parallel-shift architecture that did not exist a generation ago. Naming this is the start of choosing what actually closes.

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v05
---
mode: SECULAR_BRIDGE
doctrine_source: somatic
mechanism: shame
weight: standard
carry_line: "The conviction that other parents are doing this without falling apart is a private theory built from parking-lot surfaces. The hiding is what keeps it alive."
---
Here is the mechanism. Parent burnout shame has a specific shape. It is the conviction that other parents in your bracket are doing the same workload — the job, the kids, the household, the school logistics — without coming apart, and that your inability to keep up is a private deficiency you must hide from the other parents at pickup, from your coworkers, and especially from your own kids. So you hide it. You decline the question about how things are going with a fine. You apologize for being late but do not name what the morning actually was. The hiding is the part that closes the loop. Because none of the other parents see how depleted you are, you also do not see how depleted they are. Each of you, separately, concludes that you are the only one. Each of you, separately, performs parent-shaped okay-ness for the others in the school lobby. Shame survives in the gap that hiding builds. Naming the mechanism does not redistribute the second shift, but it interrupts the part where you treat the depletion as evidence of a defect rather than as the predictable response of a single nervous system carrying two full-time loads in the same body.

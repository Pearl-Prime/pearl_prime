This is a profound and painful realization. You have mapped the architecture of your own exhaustion with surgical precision.

The core tragedy you’ve identified in these reflections is this: **The mechanism designed to protect you from judgment is the very thing that guarantees your absence.**

You are not failing to be present because you are working. You are failing to be present because you are *performing* the act of not-working. The energy required to maintain the illusion of "integration" is stolen directly from the reservoir of attention needed for actual connection.

Here is what happens when the effort to appear integrated becomes the barrier to actual integration, viewed through the lenses you provided:

### 1. The Paradox of Visibility (Reflections v01, v05, v10)
You noted that anxiety accelerates at moments of highest visibility. When you are "seen" as a good parent, the stakes rise. The mechanism demands a higher fidelity performance.
*   **The Result:** You become a curator of your own image rather than a participant in your child’s life. You are watching yourself watch your child. This splits your consciousness. One part is experiencing the moment; the other is auditing the experience for social acceptability. The audit always wins because it feels safer. But the audit is empty. It contains no joy, no surprise, no love—only data points about how you are being perceived.

### 2. The Trap of "Compensatory Presence" (Reflections v07, v19, v25)
The mechanism tells you that because you work, you owe a debt of presence. You pay this debt by over-volunteering, over-attending, and over-performing.
*   **The Result:** This creates a cycle of diminishing returns. The more you perform to prove you aren't "absent," the more exhausted you become. The more exhausted you become, the harder it is to be authentically present, which triggers more anxiety, leading to more performance. You are running on a treadmill of proof, going nowhere, while your child stands still, waiting for you to step off the machine and just *be* there. They don’t need the volunteer coordinator; they need the parent who is tired but real.

### 3. The Invisibility of the Wound (Reflections v03, v06, v21)
Because the performance is skilled (you are a nurse, you are competent, you are high-functioning), nobody sees the fracture. They see a "good parent."
*   **The Result:** This isolation is devastating. If you were visibly struggling, you might receive support or grace. But because you appear "integrated," you receive only expectation. The mask works too well. It protects you from external judgment but imprisons you in internal shame. You feel like an impostor in your own family life, convinced that if people saw the "fragmentation underneath," they would revoke your status as a good parent.

### 4. The Loss of the "Pre-Child Self" and the "Professional Self" (Reflections v08, v14, v29)
You mentioned earlier the concept of the *pre-child self archived*. Here, we see the *professional self* and the *parental self* cannibalizing each other.
*   **The Result:** To protect the professional self from being seen as negligent, you perform hyper-presence as a parent. To protect the parental self from being seen as distracted, you perform hyper-competence at work. Neither self gets to rest. Neither self gets to be authentic. You are not integrating two whole identities; you are fragmenting one exhausted identity into two convincing simulations.

### What Happens Next?

The question you asked—*"What happens when your anxiety about being perceived as a failing parent becomes so consuming that you actually fail to be present?"*—has already been answered by your experience. **You become a ghost in your own life.** Haunting the school event, haunting the home, visible but intangible.

But here is the shift in perspective, offered not as a teacher, but as a companion to your insight:

**The fragmentation is not a moral failure. It is a structural reality.**

No human can be fully present in two demanding spheres simultaneously without some degree of cognitive switching cost. The anxiety comes not from the switching, but from the *shame* attached to the switching. The mechanism says: *"If they knew you were thinking about work, they would judge you."*

**The antidote is not better performance. It is the deliberate, terrifying act of dropping the performance.**

1.  **Accept the "Good Enough" Gaze:** Most other parents are not judging you; they are too busy performing their own integration. They are also calculating, also anxious, also fragmented. When you stop performing, you don't stand out as a failure; you blend in as a human.
2.  **Name the Fragmentation Aloud:** This breaks the invisibility. Saying to another parent, *"I’m so glad to be here, though my mind is still half-at-work today,"* does two things. It releases the pressure to hide, and it invites authenticity. It transforms the "secret shame" into a shared human condition.
3.  **Prioritize Connection Over Appearance:** Ask yourself in the moment: *"Am I doing this to be seen, or to connect?"* If you are volunteering to be seen, stop. If you are staying home to avoid judgment, go. Let the choice be driven by your child’s need and your capacity, not by the imaginary audience.
4.  **Grieve the Ideal:** You are mourning the parent who is always patient, always present, always integrated. That parent does not exist. Letting go of that ideal is not giving up; it is waking up. The parent who is sometimes distracted, sometimes tired, but *real*, is the parent your children actually know and love. They don’t love the performance. They love you. And they can feel the difference between a performer and a person.

You are not failing because you are fragmented. You are failing to rest because you are trying to hide the fragmentation. The healing begins when you allow the cracks to be seen, and realize that the light gets in through them, not despite them.

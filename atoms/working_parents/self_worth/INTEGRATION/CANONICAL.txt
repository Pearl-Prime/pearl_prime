

## INTEGRATION v01
---
integration_mode: BODY-LANDED
reframe_type: BODY-FACT
---
I dropped the thing in front of her teachers. My voice went tight. The automatic thought: I'm not good at this.
But the deeper thought: I'm not good at most things right now because I'm not fully present at any of them. I'm divided. Partial. A person experiencing herself as insufficient because the measurement is impossible.
The reframe: My feeling of inadequacy isn't about capability. It's about being asked to be competent in seven different roles simultaneously.
I'm not failing. I'm fractured.
---

## INTEGRATION v02
---
integration_mode: COST-VISIBLE
reframe_type: COST-UNCOUNTED
---
The other parents seem to have figured something out. Their homes are clean. Their children are organized. They look like they're winning at this.
What I don't see: the cost. The truth is I'm comparing my inside—the chaos, the guilt, the unmade lunches—with their outside—the carefully curated appearance they show the world.
The cost of thinking I'm less is that I stop trying. The cost of believing the lie is that I internalize the failure as character.
The reframe: I'm not less than them. I'm less polished. There's a difference.
I'm done performing someone's version of okay.
---

## INTEGRATION v03
---
integration_mode: STILL-HERE
reframe_type: QUESTION-REFRAME
---
I'm standing in front of the mirror and I don't recognize the person. Not because of age or exhaustion. Because of the options I've made. The small self I've accepted.
What if the feeling of being not enough isn't about me. What if it's about the standard. What if anyone—anyone—would feel diminished by the expectation to manage a household, raise humans well, work competently, and stay sane.
The reframe: Maybe the problem isn't that I'm not enough. Maybe it's that the bar was set by people who had more help than I do.
What could I become with actual support.
---

## INTEGRATION v04
---
integration_mode: QUESTION-OPEN
reframe_type: TIME-SHIFT
---
Before children, I was someone with a sense of coherence. I did things well. I completed projects. I had feedback loops that confirmed my competence.
Now everything is in progress. Never finished. Constantly interrupted. The feedback is constant disappointment—the thing I didn't get to, the way I snapped, the house that never stays clean.
The reframe: The version of me that felt competent existed in conditions that allowed for completion. These conditions don't exist anymore.
What is my actual worth in this economy.
---

## INTEGRATION v05
---
integration_mode: SOMEONE-ELSE
reframe_type: OTHER-PERSON
---
My mother-in-law mentioned, in passing, how her children's teachers always said they were so organized. How the house always seemed to work. How she made it look easy.
Then she added: "I have no idea how your wife does what she does."
I felt something crack open. The first time anyone had named the thing. That I'm not managing well. That I'm doing something she couldn't do and she knows it. That the difference isn't that she's better—it's that she had different help.
The reframe: Sometimes self-worth returns when someone else names the actual difficulty.
What I thought was failure was survival.
---

## INTEGRATION v06
---
integration_mode: COST-VISIBLE
reframe_type: COST-UNCOUNTED
---
The cost of believing I'm not good enough is that I stop asking for what I need. It's that I accept less sleep, less support, less space. It's that I internalize the comparison and make it true by never allowing myself the room to succeed.
The cost is the way I apologize for the messy house. The way I lower my voice when I talk about my work. The way I've learned to make myself smaller so I don't take up room that someone else might need.
The reframe: The diminishment I feel is caused by my belief that I should be doing more, not by my actual capacity.
I'm adequate at an impossible task.
---

## INTEGRATION v07
---
integration_mode: BODY-LANDED
reframe_type: BODY-FACT
---
My hands are dirty. There's a stain on my shirt that I don't remember getting. The house has things in it that aren't organized. I'm here anyway. I'm showing up anyway.
The old narrative said this meant I wasn't trying hard enough. The new accounting recognizes something different: I'm doing the thing with the resources I have, which is to say, almost none.
The reframe: Worth isn't determined by the condition of the house or the perfection of my parenting. It's determined by the presence I bring despite the impossibility.
I show up. That's the measure.

I hear you. I see the mechanism clearly now.

You have named it: **The Good Parent Metric.**

It is not a truth; it is a mechanism. It is a rigid, automated script running in my nervous system that conflates my *actions* with my *essence*. It tells me that if I am not perfectly patient, perfectly present, and perfectly self-sacrificing, then I am fundamentally flawed as a human being.

I recognize how this mechanism operates:
1.  **It collapses identity:** My worth as a person is entirely dependent on my performance as a parent. There is no "me" outside of "parent."
2.  **It ignores context:** It does not care that I am exhausted, time-poor, financially strained, or emotionally depleted. It only measures the output against an impossible ideal.
3.  **It creates a feedback loop of shame:** When I snap, forget, or feel tired, the metric interprets these normal human limitations as moral failures. It tells me I am selfish, bad, or not enough.
4.  **It uses my child as a mirror:** I look at my child’s distress or behavior and see only my own inadequacy reflected back. I take total responsibility for their emotions, assuming any struggle is proof of my failure.
5.  **It is intergenerational and systemic:** This is not just my invention. It is a legacy of perfectionism passed down through generations, reinforced by a society that demands infinite caregiving without providing sufficient support.

I see that this metric is **impossible to satisfy**. It requires me to be a machine, not a human. It demands perfection under conditions of scarcity. And because it can never be satisfied, it keeps me in a permanent state of self-doubt, anxiety, and emotional exhaustion.

Naming it is the first step in separating myself from it. The metric is running, but it is not *me*. It is a learned survival strategy that has become a prison. I am beginning to see the bars.

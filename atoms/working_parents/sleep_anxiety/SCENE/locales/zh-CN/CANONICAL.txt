The ceiling is a blank screen. It reflects nothing, yet you project everything onto it.

4:12 AM.

The numbers on the clock are red, sharp, and judgmental. They don’t just tell time; they measure deficit. *Two hours and thirty-three minutes remaining.*

Your body is heavy, anchored to the mattress by a exhaustion that feels less like sleepiness and more like gravity increasing. Your shoulders are tight, pulled up toward your ears even in repose, holding the weight of the "good parent metric" while you lie still. You can feel the knot in your stomach, the physical manifestation of the list that isn’t written down but is etched into your nervous system.

*Did I sign the permission slip?*
*Is there milk for cereal?*
*Did I sound too sharp when I told them to brush their teeth?*
*Can we afford the co-pay if the youngest gets sick again?*

The thoughts are not linear. They are a swarm. They buzz against the inside of your skull, keeping you awake with the precision of a medical alarm you can’t silence.

You look at your partner. Their breathing is deep, rhythmic, untroubled. A part of you feels a spike of resentment—sharp, hot, and immediately followed by a wave of crushing guilt. *They need rest too. You are both tired. But you are the one holding the mental load. You are the one who checked the weather. You are the one who packed the bags. You are the one lying here, calculating the exact minute you must rise to ensure the machine of the morning runs without error.*

If you wake them, you break the peace. If you don’t, you carry it alone. You always carry it alone.

The street outside, {street_name}, is beginning to stir. A single car passes. The sound is loud in the vacuum of your insomnia. It sounds like failure. It sounds like everyone else is moving forward while you are stuck in this dark, static loop of anticipation and regret.

You close your eyes, trying to force the shutdown. You try to breathe. *In for four. Hold for seven. Out for eight.* The technique you teach patients. The technique that works for everyone but you. Because for you, the breath doesn’t lead to relaxation; it leads to awareness. Awareness of the tightness in your chest. Awareness of the cold air from the window. Awareness that in ninety-three minutes, the alarm will scream, and you will have to stand up and perform the role of the rested, capable, patient parent.

You are not resting. You are waiting. And the waiting is exhausting you more than the doing ever could.

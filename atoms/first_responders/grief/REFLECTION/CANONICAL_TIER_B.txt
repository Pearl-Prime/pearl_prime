

## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
Grief in first responders doesn't follow normal cycles. You don't get to mourn. You get the call and the next call and the call after that. The losses just stack up. Unprocessed. Unmoaned. Just absorbed into the baseline of what you carry.
The mechanism is called cumulative grief. It's what happens when you absorb other people's losses repeatedly without processing them. When the deaths stack up faster than you can mourn. When the loss becomes ambient.
---

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
The protective mechanism teaches you to not feel the loss. To not stop to mourn. To move on to the next call. If you stop to grieve, you might not be ready for the next patient who's still alive.
So you compress the grief. You swallow it. You keep moving. The problem is that grief doesn't disappear. It goes underground.
---

## REFLECTION v03
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
You don't feel the accumulation of loss at first. Each death is separate. Then at some point you realize you've absorbed dozens of deaths. Hundreds of other people's losses. And none of them got properly mourned.
The invisibility is that the grief is so normalized. So expected. So much part of the work that you don't even call it grief anymore.
---

## REFLECTION v04
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
The automatic mechanism that prevents grief is the transition to the next call. The nervous system learns that standing still is not an option. That the moment you stop, the next person needs you.
So you don't stop. You process grief by suppressing it. By compartmentalizing it. By telling yourself you'll deal with it later. You never do.
---

## REFLECTION v05
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
I've been thinking about what cumulative unprocessed grief does. How it changes the nervous system. How it teaches the body to not have feelings about loss because feelings are a luxury you don't get.
The protective mechanism is that you stop feeling loss. Stop expecting good outcomes. Become prepared for everything to end badly because usually it does.
---

## REFLECTION v06
---
family: F1
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
I remember the first person I couldn't save. Remember that moment of absolute failure. Remember the feeling. Then I had to move on. The mechanism wouldn't let me stay with it.
Now I'm just someone who carries them. All of them. The mechanism has made sure that none of them get released.
---

## REFLECTION v07
---
family: F2
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
The invisibility of unmoaned grief is that you can be carrying dozens of deaths and nobody would know. Nobody sees the weight of it. Just sees the professional who shows up and does the job.
Invisibly carrying the accumulated losses. All of them. Never processed. Never mourned.
---

## REFLECTION v08
---
family: F3
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
The protective mechanism that suppresses grief is necessary on the job. You can't mourn and respond. You can't feel the loss and move to the next patient. So the job teaches you not to feel.
Off the job, the lesson stays locked in place. The losses stay sealed inside.
---

## REFLECTION v09
---
family: F4
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
The automatic compartmentalization of grief is so smooth that you don't even notice it's happening. Just notice one day that you can hear about deaths that would destroy other people and feel nothing.
The mechanism has protected you completely. The cost is that you're carrying all of them.
---

## REFLECTION v10
---
family: F5
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
The protective mechanism that suppresses grief is trying to keep you functional. Trying to keep you able to show up. Trying to keep you from becoming so overwhelmed by other people's suffering that you can't move.
It does this by creating distance. By numbing. By turning down the emotional response until you can handle the load. The cost is that the loss never gets released.
---

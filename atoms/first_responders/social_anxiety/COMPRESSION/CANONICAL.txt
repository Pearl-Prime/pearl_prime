## COMPRESSION v01
---

---

Stand with both feet flat on the floor. Press down hard. Feel the floor push back. That resistance is real. You are here, not there. Name three things your hands can touch right now. Say each one out loud. Your nervous system needs sound to register safety. Breathe out longer than you breathe in. Count four seconds out, six seconds out. Do this three times. The call is over. Your body does not know that yet. Tell it now.
---

## COMPRESSION v02
---

---

Drop your shoulders. Notice how far they fall. That distance is how long you have been braced. Press your back into whatever is behind you. Full contact. Hold it. Now scan from your feet upward. Tight calves. Locked jaw. Clenched hands. Name each one as you find it. Naming is not weakness. Naming is information. Loosen one thing. Just one. That is enough for now.
---

## COMPRESSION v03
---

---

Find a wall. Put your palm flat against it. Cold or warm, rough or smooth — register that. You are off shift. The radio is silent. Your body still listens for it. Let it listen. Do not fight the listening. Just add something new: the texture under your hand, the weight of your own breath, the sound of ordinary air. Both things can be true at once. You carried something hard today. The wall holds its own weight. Let it.
---

## COMPRESSION v04
---

---

Sit down. Both feet on the floor. Look at something that does not move — a wall, a table, a window frame. Fix your eyes there. Count to ten. Slow. Your heart rate follows your breath, not the other way around. Exhale first. Empty the lungs completely. Then let the inhale come on its own. Do this four times. The urgency in your chest is old data. Your body ran the call. The call is done.
---

## COMPRESSION v05
---

---

Clench both fists tight. Hold for five seconds. Release. Feel the blood return. That flood of warmth is your body remembering it is not under threat. Do it again. Clench. Hold. Release. Now unclench your jaw. You did not realize it was tight. It always is. Roll your neck once, slowly, left to right. One slow exhale through the mouth. You are transitioning. That takes a body, not just a decision.
---

## COMPRESSION v06
---

---

Look at your hands. Really look. These are your hands off the job. No gloves. No gear. Just skin. Notice one thing about them you do not usually see — a scar, a knuckle, the way the light sits on them. You spend your shifts using these hands to hold other people's crises. Right now, rest them. Open palms on your thighs. Weight down. Breath slow. Nothing is required of your hands in this moment. Let them be still.
---

## COMPRESSION v07
---

---

Step outside if you can. One minute. Stand still. Feel the temperature on your face — cold or heat, wind or stillness. Your nervous system uses skin temperature as a safety signal. Let the air register. Breathe through your nose. The nose filters. The nose slows. Three breaths, nose only. You do not have to process what happened today. Not yet. Right now, only the air on your face. Only the ground under your feet.
---

## COMPRESSION v08
---

---

Put both hands over your sternum. Press lightly. Feel your heartbeat. It is beating without your help. It did that through the whole shift. It will do it through tonight. Notice the rhythm. It is probably faster than it should be. That is normal after what you carry. Now hum. One low note, any note, for five seconds. The vibration in your chest interrupts the alarm signal. Do it twice more. Slower each time.
---

## COMPRESSION v09
---

---

Take off your boots if you can. If not, press your feet hard into the floor inside them. Curl your toes, then spread them wide. The feet are the last thing to relax after hypervigilance. They stay ready to run long after the threat is gone. Curl. Spread. Again. Now breathe out through your mouth with a sound — any sound, even a sigh. Sound signals the exhale is real. Two more times. Feet. Breath. Sound.
---

## COMPRESSION v10
---

---

Pick up something cold — a glass of water, a can, anything. Hold it in both hands. Let the cold register fully. Cold is present tense. Your brain cannot be in the past and feel cold at the same time. Hold it for thirty seconds. Drink slowly if it is water. Feel it move down your throat. That is your body in real time. Not the scene. Not the call. Here. Now. Cold. Real.
---

## COMPRESSION v11
---

---

Sit somewhere you can see a door. Not to guard it. To remind yourself the door is ordinary right now. No one is coming through it in crisis. Look at it for five seconds. Breathe. Look away. The hypervigilance that kept you sharp on shift becomes noise when the shift ends. You cannot switch it off with willpower. You redirect it. Door. Breath. Floor. Hands. These four things are your landing strip. Use them in order.
---

## COMPRESSION v12
---

---

Splash cold water on your wrists. Hold them under for ten seconds. The pulse points cool fastest. Your heart rate will follow. Dry your hands slowly. Feel the towel. Rough or soft, it does not matter — feel it. Say your own name out loud once. Not your badge number. Not your unit. Your name. The one people used before you wore the uniform. You are still that person. You are here.
---

## COMPRESSION v13
---

---

Lie flat on your back if you can. Arms at your sides. Palms up. That position signals surrender to your nervous system — not defeat. Surrender to stillness. Feel the floor or mattress take your full weight. You do not have to hold yourself up right now. Let the surface do it. Breathe in for four counts. Out for six. If images come from the shift, let them pass like traffic. You are on the sidewalk. Not in the road.



## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
You can stand in front of a crowd in crisis and direct people with authority, but you can't go to a regular social gathering without anxiety. That's the social anxiety in this work—the automatic mechanism that treats civilian social interactions as threatening in a way that calls never do.
The mechanism is called occupational-role-dependent social anxiety—your nervous system is trained to be "on" in professional contexts and struggles to turn "off" in civilian contexts.
The anxiety is automatic. You're at a social gathering and your mind says: you don't know these people. You're trying to make small talk and your mind says: this is pointless. You're among peers and your mind says: you don't belong here.
What I'm learning is that this anxiety is connected to occupational identity. In this work, you have a clear role. You have authority. You know what you're supposed to do. But in civilian social settings, you don't have that clarity. You're just a person. And that's more threatening than running into danger.
The anxiety is also connected to the gap between who you are at work and who you are off work. You're trained to be steady, reliable, commanding. But off work, you're supposed to be relaxed, fun, casual. The gap creates anxiety. You're not sure which version of yourself to bring to civilian social situations.
The mechanism is also connected to the culture of this work. You're around people who do this work. They understand. But outside of work, you're around people who don't. And the disconnect creates anxiety.

## INTEGRATION v01
---
---
Tomorrow, before you sit down to whatever's been stalling — the report, the QA form, the recert packet you keep opening and closing — write the literal first physical verb on a sticky note before you start: not "write the report" but "type the dispatch time." Put it where you'll see it the second you open the laptop, so the decision about where to begin is already made before the freeze has a chance to show up. The intention-action gap doesn't close because you want it to close harder; it closes because there's a small, physical, already-decided first move waiting instead of an abstract mountain. You don't need the whole queue solved tonight. You need one verb, executed once.
---

## INTEGRATION v02
---
---
Tomorrow, pick the one task on your list that has no natural stopping point — the reports, the log rows, the supplementals — and give it an artificial one before you begin: a timer, a fixed count of three, a queue you physically move things out of as proof of progress. Your attention system needs to see the task shrinking, not just trust that it's shrinking somewhere in the abstract. This isn't about finishing all of it tomorrow. It's about giving the repetitive middle of the task a visible edge, so each item doesn't require your brain to relaunch initiation from zero.
---

## INTEGRATION v03
---
---
Tomorrow, notice the first moment you catch yourself reorganizing a closet, scrolling your phone, or refolding gear instead of starting the real task, and instead of judging it, treat it as useful information: that's the freeze announcing itself, and it means the task in front of you has no visible first step yet. Don't try to push through the whole task in that moment. Just name the smallest true first action out loud — "open the form," "read line one" — and do only that. You are not required to solve the intention-action gap for every task you'll ever face. You're required to notice it once, tomorrow, in one moment, and hand yourself one small verb instead of the whole mountain.
---

## INTEGRATION v04
---
---
Tomorrow, right after you clear a scene, protect the next ten minutes for the report on that specific call, before the next dispatch, before the conversation that will cost you the rebuild. Task-switching costs accumulate invisibly across a shift, and the deep task left for "whenever there's time" ends up fighting for scraps of a resource that's already spent by hour ten. You don't need to protect every hour. You need to protect the one window where the hardest task gets first access to a mind that hasn't been switched six times yet.
---

## INTEGRATION v05
---
---
Tomorrow, if there's an email, a form, or a conversation you've been avoiding because it feels bigger than it probably is, don't commit to handling all of it — just open it and read the first line, nothing else, no obligation to respond yet. The flood of dread attaches to the whole imagined task, not to one line of actual text, and most of the time the actual line is smaller and less threatening than the version you've been carrying around in your head.
---

## INTEGRATION v06
---
---
Tomorrow, for whatever multi-entry-point task keeps stalling you — the narrative, the supplemental, the QA form — pre-decide one arbitrary but fixed order you'll use every time, and stop re-litigating which piece to start with. Dispatch info first, always. Scene arrival first, always. It doesn't need to be the objectively best order; it needs to be an order you never have to choose again. The freeze often isn't about the writing — it's about the extra, invisible decision layered on top of it.
---

## INTEGRATION v07
---
---
Tomorrow, put the thing you keep forgetting to do — the restock, the reply, the log update — somewhere physical and visible the moment it occurs to you, not at the end of shift when you try to remember everything at once. A sticky note on the truck console, a running list taped inside your locker, one phone note you open the second the thought appears. Your working memory was never going to hold it reliably for six hours; that was never a fair test of your commitment to the job.
---

## INTEGRATION v08
---
---
Tomorrow, before you start the task you've been dreading, deliberately lower the bar for the first pass: type in bullet fragments, don't fix a single typo, write one sentence you know is clumsy on purpose. The freeze often isn't fear of the task — it's waiting for a polished opening line that was never going to arrive before you started typing anyway. Give yourself permission, in writing if it helps, that the first version is not allowed to be good.
---

## INTEGRATION v09
---
---
Tomorrow, move the physical object connected to your hardest task into your direct path — the run sheet on the passenger seat instead of the glovebox, the laptop open on the console instead of stowed, the recert checklist pulled up before you leave the station. Your executive system responds far more reliably to something visible in front of you than to a plan you're only holding in language.
---

## INTEGRATION v10
---
---
Tomorrow, when you sit down in the truck to finally do the paperwork, notice if sitting down and starting feel like two separate events — because for your brain, they usually are. Build a small bridge between them: touch the run sheet, say the dispatch time out loud, type one word you'll delete later. The gap between being physically present and actually starting doesn't close on its own just because you're in the seat.
---

## INTEGRATION v11
---
---
Tomorrow, if a repetitive task starts feeling heavier the further into it you get — report nine harder than report one — introduce one small deliberate change partway through: dictate a few instead of typing, write from a different spot in the station, read a couple out loud. This isn't about the content changing; it's about giving your attention system something new enough to re-engage with, since it's wired to disengage from what already feels processed.
---

## INTEGRATION v12
---
---
Tomorrow, before you start whatever open-ended task has been generating quiet dread without producing any actual work — recert prep, the grant narrative you keep meaning to write — give yourself a fake but firm container: twenty-five minutes, one deliverable, nothing more required. Unbounded tasks expand to fill whatever space you leave them and give your initiation system no edge to grab onto. A fabricated boundary works nearly as well as a real one.
---

## THREAD v01
---
---
You scheduled the hard supervisor call for your quiet hour, then watched it slide to after shift, then to tomorrow, then to "this week sometime." It was never about the supervisor. The call had no visible first sentence your system could grab, so it kept sliding to wherever the pressure was lowest. Tonight, write the first sentence down before you dial, and notice how much less the call slides.

## THREAD v02
---
---
You keep meaning to update the body-cam log every Friday, and every Friday it's still sixteen empty rows by the time you leave the station. It was never about not caring how the incidents are documented — you think about them all week. The log has no natural entry point, so your brain treats all sixteen rows as one enormous decision instead of sixteen small ones. Next Friday, fill just the row for the one incident you've already been thinking about all week, and see what happens to the other fifteen.

## THREAD v03
---
---
You've opened the same report template four times this week and closed it four times without typing a word. Each time it felt like nothing happened, because nothing did — the freeze reset itself at the same blank line every time. What actually needs to change isn't your effort at reopening it; it's what's waiting for you the fifth time you open it. Leave one sentence typed, even a bad one, before you close it next time, so the sixth attempt starts somewhere other than zero.
---

## THREAD v04
---
---
You told yourself you'd "just sit down and knock out the recert packet" tonight, and you did sit down, laptop open, portal loaded, and twenty minutes passed with nothing written. Sitting down solved a location problem. It never touched the actual starting problem, which is a separate event your brain needed a bridge for. Tomorrow, add the bridge — list the trainings you remember off the top of your head — before assuming proximity to the laptop counts as trying.

## THREAD v05
---
---
You've read the same department template five times this week, on five different reports, and it still doesn't move your hands any faster than the first time. Rereading feels like progress because it's an action, but it isn't the action the task actually needs. Next time you catch yourself on a second reread, stop and type the first sentence instead, even badly, and notice how differently that five minutes goes.

## THREAD v06
---
---
You keep telling your captain you'll "get to" the grant narrative this week, and this week keeps ending with the document exactly as blank as it started. There was never a shortage of time — a whole week is plenty. There was a shortage of an edge, a moment where the task had a clear start and a clear stop. Next time, give it twenty-five minutes and one paragraph, and watch a week's worth of dread collapse into less than half an hour.

## THREAD v07

---
You noticed yourself standing at your locker touching the same folder for the third time, right before a briefing where you needed those numbers. You weren't lost — you knew exactly where the data was. You just didn't have anything visible enough to grab and walk into the room with. Next time, before the briefing starts, take one photo of the run sheet and walk in with that instead of the folder you keep almost-opening.

## THREAD v08
---
---
You've had the same coordinator's name in your inbox for nine days, in the subject line of an email you haven't opened. Each day it sat there, the imagined version of the conversation got a little worse in your head, and the actual email got no closer to being read. Nothing in the real email has changed in nine days. Open it tomorrow and read only the first line — that's the whole job for now.

## THREAD v09
---
---
You catch yourself, partway through writing reports, checking a text message that has nothing urgent in it, for the third time this stack. It isn't that the text matters more than the reports — it's newer, and your attention is wired to reach for what's new over what's already been touched. Next stack, build the novelty in on purpose: dictate a few, write somewhere different, before your attention goes looking for it somewhere else.

## THREAD v10
---
---
You keep starting the incident narrative with dispatch info one week and scene arrival the next, re-deciding every single time which piece to lead with, and every single time that re-deciding eats the first five minutes. The writing was never the slow part. The choosing was. Pick one order now, in writing, and use it for every narrative from here forward, no exceptions.

## THREAD v11
---
---
You noticed the reports got easier around the fourth one, not harder, even though report four should logically be more tiring than report one. The first few carried a cost the later ones didn't — the cost of getting your hands moving in the first place. Next time, remember that the hardest report is always the first one, and it isn't a preview of the other eleven.

## THREAD v12
---
---
You spent ten minutes holding the wrong clipboard before your captain reached over and swapped it without a word, and something in you unlocked the moment the object in your hands changed. It wasn't really about the clipboard. It was about having something concrete and correct in your hands to act from. Next time you feel stuck holding the wrong version of something, physically change what's in your hands before trying to change your mind.

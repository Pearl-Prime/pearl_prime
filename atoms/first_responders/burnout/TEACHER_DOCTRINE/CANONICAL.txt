

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v01
---
mode: SECULAR_BRIDGE
doctrine_source: somatic
mechanism: false_alarm
weight: standard
carry_line: "Your alarm system is calibrated to the last call, not the current shift. It is firing on radio static because the last static was real."
---
Here is the mechanism. Your stress response is not designed to run continuously. It is designed to fire, do its job, and reset. The reset is supposed to happen between calls, between shifts, on the days off. In most jobs, the alarm fires rarely. In your job, it fires often enough that the reset window collapses. After enough real alarms, the system stops trusting the gaps. The radio crackles and the chest tightens before the dispatch finishes the address. A page tone hits at 3 am and your body is already up the stairs in your head before your feet hit the floor. This is not a malfunction. This is a nervous system that has been trained, over hundreds of real activations, to skip the verification step and go straight to readiness. The cost is that the system never fully comes down anymore. Off-duty becomes a half-on state. Sleep becomes scanning. The alarm did not get the memo that you are at the kitchen table eating cereal. It learned that the next page could be the bad one, and it is paying that cost in advance, every minute, in case. This is what burnout looks like in a first responder's body before it has a name. A stuck alarm does not need more discipline. It needs the conditions that allow a reset, which is closer to nothing than to more.

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v02
---
mode: SECULAR_BRIDGE
doctrine_source: somatic
mechanism: spiral
weight: standard
carry_line: "The post-call replay loop runs every detail forward into what could have gone worse. The forecast borrows guilt from outcomes that did not happen."
---
Here is the mechanism. After a hard call, the prediction loop runs backward instead of forward. It takes the call you just finished and runs it through every variation you could have run. If the patient had been a minute further out. If the partner had taken the other side. If the equipment had failed. The loop arrives at a worse outcome and your body responds as if the worse outcome happened. You drive home from a shift where everyone lived and your chest is tight because the version where someone did not is running on a side channel of your nervous system. The loop is not lying. It is doing what it does. The problem is that it does not know when to stop, and it treats each invented variation as evidence about your competence, even though every variation past the first is invented. The guilt is borrowed from an outcome that never happened. What helps is noticing the chain itself. Not arguing with it. Just naming the links. The patient lived. The call ended. The loop is running. When you can see the loop in slow motion, you also see that it is a loop, not a verdict. The call still happened. The replay still runs. But you stop paying guilt for outcomes you did not produce.

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v03
---
mode: SECULAR_BRIDGE
doctrine_source: somatic
mechanism: comparison
weight: standard
carry_line: "The senior who walks off the call without a scratch is not the full picture. The comparison engine reads their composure as data about your toughness."
---
Here is the mechanism. The comparison engine in you was tuned for a small crew whose internal states you could read. It tracks relative position by reading visible signals, who shows the call on their face, who carries it home, who is laughing in the kitchen an hour later. It did not evolve for a culture where the visible signal of competence is to show none of it. You finish a hard call and the senior on your crew is already joking about something else, and the engine runs a fast subtraction. They are fine. You are not. Therefore something is wrong with your toughness specifically. What the engine cannot see is what is happening underneath their composure. You do not see the senior who has not slept right in months. You do not see the medic who drinks alone after every pediatric. You do not see the cop who carries the call into the next call, every call. You see surfaces, and the engine treats surfaces as full pictures. The comparison takes their visible composure as evidence and your visible reaction as a personal verdict on whether you belong in the job. Both readings are wrong. The call cost everyone on it something. Some of them just have more reps at hiding the cost.
---

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v04
---
mode: SECULAR_BRIDGE
doctrine_source: somatic
mechanism: overwhelm
weight: standard
carry_line: "Twenty unresolved calls is not twenty memories. It is twenty open files your nervous system has not closed because it never got a closing window."
---
Here is the mechanism. Your nervous system was built to process events. Process means take in the event, respond, and then close the file once the event is over. The closing happens through what the body does after, the drive home, the talk with a partner, the walk, the sleep. In a regular job, this loop completes. In your job, the next call hits before the last one closes, and the call after that hits before the second one closes, and over months the unclosed files accumulate. By the time someone says you look tired, you are not just tired from this shift. You are carrying twenty unclosed calls in your nervous system, each holding a small amount of partial attention, draining the supply you need for the call in front of you. This is not weakness or attention failure. It is the predictable cost of a closing architecture that did not evolve for a job with no gaps. Burnout, in this register, is partly the unpaid debt of every call that never got its closing window. Naming the debt does not pay it off. But it changes what you ask for, which is the difference between needing more grit and needing the conditions to close even one of them.

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v05
---
mode: SECULAR_BRIDGE
doctrine_source: somatic
mechanism: shame
weight: standard
carry_line: "The conviction that the rest of the crew is not this affected is the part of the culture that keeps everyone equally affected and equally hidden."
---
Here is the mechanism. First responder burnout shame has a specific shape. It is the conviction that the rest of your crew is handling the same calls without coming apart, and that your reaction to the last bad one is a private deficiency you must hide from your partner, your shift, your captain, and especially the new boots watching how the veterans do it. So you hide it. You decline the question about how the call sat with you with a fine. You skip the post-incident debrief or you go but say nothing real. The hiding is the part that closes the loop. Because nobody on your crew sees how the call landed on you, you also do not see how it landed on them. Each of you, separately, concludes that you are the only one. Each of you, separately, performs okay-ness for the others. Shame survives in the gap that hiding builds. Naming the mechanism does not change the call rotation, but it interrupts the part where you treat the load as evidence of a defect rather than as the predictable response of a nervous system that has now run thousands of activations without the recovery windows it was designed around.
---



## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
You can run into a burning building but you can't sit still for five minutes. That's what I'm learning to name: the mechanism that keeps you ready when the call ends.
It's not weakness. It's a nervous system that was built to run at high alert, and has forgotten how to stand down. Your body learned a long time ago that stillness isn't safe in this work. You need to be ready. You need to be sharp. You need to be able to respond in a second's notice.
So the automatic response to being off the clock is to stay on the clock. Your nervous system doesn't know how to deactivate. The engine is idling, but it's still running.
What I'm teaching is this: burnout in this work isn't about working too hard. You can handle the hard. You can walk into anything. The burnout is about never being allowed to stop being ready. Never being allowed to believe that the danger has passed.
The mechanism is called hypervigilance—a heightened state of sensory awareness that was adaptive on the job. You needed to notice everything. You needed to be ready. You needed to trust your instinct to keep you alive.
But hypervigilance has a cost. Your nervous system is running on a fuel that's finite. And you've been running on fumes for so long that you've forgotten what normal feels like.

## REFLECTION v02
---

voice_mode: companion

I'm learning to sit with the weight of what we carry—not the physical weight, but the weight of knowing.
On every call, you see the worst that can happen. You see people at their most vulnerable, most desperate, most broken. And you stay present. You stay steady. You do the work. You save lives.
And then you go home. And you're supposed to just... be normal. Have dinner. Watch TV. Be present for the people you love. Except your nervous system is still on the scene. You're still there, in the worst moment, watching it over and over.
The protective mechanism is called emotional containment—you learn to keep the weight of what you've seen locked inside so it doesn't spill over into your regular life. You learn to compartmentalize. You learn to separate the work-self from the home-self.
And this works for a while. You're strong. You can handle it. You can keep it together.
But the compartments have limits. The weight builds up. And the burnout is what happens when the walls between the compartments start to crack.

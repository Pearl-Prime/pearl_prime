

## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
You learn early that this job will break you if you let it. You see things that don't unsee. You hold people at their worst. You sit with families in their darkest moments. And then you go home and you're supposed to be normal. The mechanism here is called accumulating exposure—the relentless addition of weight that your nervous system has to carry without true processing.
---

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
What strikes me is that the burnout doesn't look like I thought it would. I thought I'd see it coming. I thought it would announce itself. But it creeps. It's in the small things. The irritability. The difficulty sleeping. The sense that nothing matters anymore. The mechanism is called quiet burnout—the erosion that happens so gradually you barely notice it.
---

## REFLECTION v03
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
There's a specific kind of burnout that comes from being in a role where you have to be steady. Where people depend on you. Where showing your own exhaustion feels like a failure. So you just keep functioning. You keep showing up. And the burnout lives beneath the surface. The mechanism is called masked burnout—the depletion that has to stay hidden.
---

## REFLECTION v04
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I've noticed that the burnout is connected to the things I can't control. The politics of the job. The inadequate resources. The systemic failures that affect the work I'm trying to do. I can give everything I have and it still won't be enough because the system itself is broken. The mechanism is called systemic inadequacy-driven burnout.
---

## REFLECTION v05
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
What I notice is that my depletion has started to affect my capacity to care. I still do the work. But some of the presence is gone. Some of the connection is gone. And that loss bothers me more than the burnout itself. The mechanism is called compassion depletion.
---

## REFLECTION v06
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
There's a part of the burnout that's physical. My body is breaking down. The shifts are long. The stress is high. And recovery is becoming harder. The mechanism is called somatic burnout.
---

## REFLECTION v07
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I've learned that talking about burnout in this job is complicated. Because there's a culture of toughness. Of not complaining. Of managing it. So the burnout stays silent. And in the silence, it gets worse. The mechanism is called culturally-sanctioned silence.
---

## REFLECTION v08
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
What strikes me is how the burnout affects my judgment outside of work. I'm less patient. Less present. Less able to enjoy things that used to matter. The mechanism is called pervasive depletion—the way job burnout bleeds into all domains of life.
---

## REFLECTION v09
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: inherited
---
There's something about the nature of crisis work that creates a particular flavor of burnout. You're always ready. Always alert. There's no real off-switch. The mechanism is called hypervigilance-sustained burnout.
---

## REFLECTION v10
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I notice that the people who left this job seem to have something in common. They at some point decided their own wellbeing was more important than the mission. And I'm not there yet. The mechanism is called mission-prioritization over self.
---

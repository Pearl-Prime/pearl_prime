## COMPRESSION v01
---

---

Stop moving. Plant both feet flat on the floor.

Notice the weight of your body in the chair. That weight is real. The shift is not yet.

Press your palms against your thighs. Feel the pressure push back.

Name three things your hands have done today that were not emergencies. Say them out loud. One word each is enough.

Breathe out first. Long and slow. Then let the inhale come on its own.

You carried a lot today. Set it down here, for sixty seconds. Just here.
---

## COMPRESSION v02
---

---

Find a wall. Press your back against it.

Feel the solid surface behind your shoulder blades. The wall does not need anything from you.

Unclench your jaw. Then unclench it again. It tightened back up. That is normal.

Count four slow beats out through your mouth. Then four in through your nose. Do this twice.

Your nervous system ran hot today. This is the cool-down. It takes less than two minutes.

Stay against the wall until your shoulders drop.
---

## COMPRESSION v03
---

---

Sit down before you do anything else.

Put one hand on your sternum. Feel it rise. Feel it fall. Do not fix it. Just feel it.

Your body logged every call today. Every one. It does not forget just because the shift ended.

Breathe in for a count of four. Hold for four. Out for six.

Do this three times. Not two. Three.

Now notice the tension behind your eyes. Do not solve it. Just locate it.

That is enough for right now.
---

## COMPRESSION v04
---

---

Take off whatever you are still wearing from shift. Jacket. Badge. Belt.

Physical objects carry the day with them. Set them down somewhere you are not sitting.

Now sit. Both feet on the floor.

Roll your shoulders back once. Slow. Feel where they catch.

Breathe out through your mouth like you are fogging a window. Long and audible. Do it again.

You are not on call right now. Your body does not know that yet. This is how you tell it.

Three more slow exhales. Then eat something.
---

## COMPRESSION v05
---

---

Close your eyes if it is safe to do so.

Put both hands on your knees, palms down. Feel the warmth of your own skin.

Scan from your feet upward. Feet. Calves. Thighs. Belly. Chest. Shoulders. Jaw.

Wherever you find tightness, breathe directly into that spot. Not a metaphor. Aim the inhale there.

Hold for two counts. Release for six.

Burnout does not announce itself. It accumulates in exactly these places you just found.

Open your eyes. Drink water. One full glass.
---

## COMPRESSION v06
---

---

Stand up. Go to a window.

Look at something at least thirty feet away. Hold your gaze there for ten full seconds. This resets the eye muscles that lock onto screens and scenes.

Now look at your hands.

These hands worked today. Name what they did. Not the worst thing. Something ordinary. A door handle. A radio. A cup of coffee.

Ordinary things matter. They are the proof that today had texture beyond the call.

Breathe in slowly. Breathe out longer.

Come back to the room.
---

## COMPRESSION v07
---

---

Grip both armrests or the edge of your seat. Hard. Hold for five seconds.

Release completely.

Notice the difference. That contrast between grip and release is what your nervous system needs to locate.

Now do it with your jaw. Clench for three counts. Release.

Then your shoulders. Lift them toward your ears. Hold. Drop.

Burnout lives in the places that never fully release. You just found several of them.

Breathe out slow. Stay with the release. Do not rush back to grip.
---

## COMPRESSION v08
---

---

Put your phone face down. Not silent. Face down.

Sit with both feet on the floor and your hands open in your lap.

Think of one moment from today that was not a crisis. A coworker's voice. A traffic light. The smell of the break room.

Hold that moment for thirty seconds. It existed. It counts.

Breathe in for four counts. Out for eight.

The mind that runs toward threat needs practice running toward neutral. This is that practice.

Do it once more. Same moment.
---

## COMPRESSION v09
---

---

Lie flat if you can. Floor or bed. Not couch.

Feel the full length of your back against the surface. Notice which parts do not touch.

Breathe into the gaps. The lower back. The space between the shoulder blades.

Hold each inhale for three counts. Let the exhale go without counting.

Your body held a combat posture today even if nothing happened. It costs the same either way.

Stay flat for two full minutes. Set a timer so you do not have to track it.

Let the floor hold you.
---

## COMPRESSION v10
---

---

Splash cold water on your face. Now.

Dry off. Sit down.

Place one hand over your heart. Count ten beats. Not fast. Slow and deliberate.

Ten heartbeats means you are here. Not there. Here.

Breathe in through your nose for five counts. Out through your mouth for seven.

Say out loud: the shift is over. Say it once more. The body believes what it hears repeated.

Sit for sixty more seconds before you go back to your family or your silence.
---

## COMPRESSION v11
---

---

Take three steps outside if you can. Just outside the door.

Stand still. Listen for one sound that has nothing to do with work. Wind. A dog. Traffic that is not yours to manage.

Let that sound land in your chest.

Breathe in slowly. Hold for four. Out for six.

The transition from shift to home is not automatic. The body needs a physical marker. This is yours.

Take three steps back inside. You crossed the threshold. That matters.
---

## COMPRESSION v12
---

---

Sit somewhere you do not usually debrief.

Not the car. Not the couch where you always decompress. Somewhere slightly different.

Place your feet flat. Feel the unfamiliar floor under your shoes.

Breathe out first. Long and complete. Then inhale.

Notice one sensation that is new. The texture of the chair. The temperature of the room. Anything.

Novelty interrupts the loop. The body stops running the same tape when the environment shifts even slightly.

Stay there for ninety seconds. Then move.
---

## COMPRESSION v13
---

---

Clench your right fist. Hold it for four counts.

Open it. Spread all five fingers wide. Hold for four.

Do the same with your left hand.

Now both together. Clench. Hold. Open. Hold.

Your hands absorbed today. Steering wheels, radios, equipment, other people's weight. They deserve a deliberate reset.

Shake them out loosely. Ten seconds. Let them be useless for a moment.

Breathe out while they shake.

Burnout starts at the edges. The hands. The jaw. The eyes. You just addressed one of them.

Eight years ago you ran on a call and you ran on coffee and you ran on the post-shift adrenaline that took two hours to come down. You came home and the dog jumped on you and your wife asked about dinner and you stood in the doorway with the keys still in your hand because your body had not yet completed the work of arriving. The keys were in your hand because the body had not given them permission to leave the hand. The dog did not understand. Your wife understood. She did not push. You stood there for forty seconds. Then you set the keys down. The forty seconds is the unpaid work of being you.

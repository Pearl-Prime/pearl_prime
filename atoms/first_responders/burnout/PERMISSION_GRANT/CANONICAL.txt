## PERMISSION_GRANT v01
---

---
First-responder burnout arrives wearing a stack of unspoken rules. Show no reaction on the call. Show no reaction in the rig. Show no reaction at the kitchen table. Earn the off-day with another good shift first. Never let the new boots see the cost. Never let the senior see the cost either. Be the one who is fine when nobody else is allowed to be. These rules are not policy. They are the culture talking to itself in your voice. Set them down.

You are allowed to be affected by the call. Being affected is not a failure of training. It is the body doing what bodies do when they witness something the body is built to register. The training tells you how to act. It does not tell the nervous system not to record.

You are allowed to need recovery that nobody on the schedule has built in. The schedule was built around staffing, not around what the nervous system actually requires after a hundred activations. You needing more than the schedule gives is not weakness. It is the bill the schedule never accounted for.

You are allowed to talk about the call. The cultural rule that you do not bring it home, do not bring it to your partner, do not bring it to your friend who is not on the job, is a rule that protects the institution and costs the body. You are allowed to say what happened. You are allowed to say what it cost you. You are allowed to need someone to hear it without flinching.

You are allowed to ask for help from inside the job. The fear that asking is the end of your standing is a fear the culture cultivated. It is not always true, and even where it is, the cost of not asking is bigger over a career than the cost of asking once.

You are allowed to step back from a call rotation that is breaking you. Stepping back is not quitting. It is a body protecting itself the way the body was built to. The career outlasts the rotation. The body has to be there to live it.

You are allowed to be the consciousness watching the burnout, not the burnout itself. The radio crackles. The chest tightens. The composure clicks into place. And somewhere behind all of it, awareness keeps watching. That awareness is the seat. That awareness is the part of you that survives the call, regardless of how the call goes.

These are not affirmations. They are accurate descriptions of the rights every first-responder nervous system comes with. Read them again. Read them slowly. Let the body hear them, one permission at a time.

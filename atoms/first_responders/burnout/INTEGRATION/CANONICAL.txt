## INTEGRATION v26
---
stage: pearl_writer_stage_3
mode: BODY_LANDED
reframe_type: BODY_FACT
weight: heavy
carry_line: "Ten minutes in the truck after the shift. Boots on the floor. Phone off. Then you go home."
register: burnout_depletion_identity_erosion_capacity_gap
persona_anchor: first_responders
note: closer chapter — named action for first responder shift transition
---
The chapter opened with a body that did not finish coming down between shifts. It closes with one specific intervention, sized for this job.

The practice is this. After the shift ends, before you start the drive home, sit in the truck for ten minutes. Engine off. Phone off. Boots on the floor. Do not review the shift. Do not text your spouse the ETA. Do not check the score. Sit. Notice your breath. Let the day end before you start the next phase.

Ten minutes. Most days. Not perfect. Some days the next shift is in eleven hours and you cannot afford the ten minutes, and that is also part of the truth. The practice is repeated, not religious.

Notice by the second week what the body does at minute eight. The body will, very quietly, drop a half-inch in the shoulders. That drop is the discharge that has been getting deferred for years. The deferred discharge is what the burnout is made of. Ten minutes a shift is one of the few interventions that puts a piece of the discharge back where it belongs.

Ten minutes in the truck after the shift. Boots on the floor. Phone off. Then you go home.

## INTEGRATION v27
---
stage: pearl_writer_stage_3
mode: COST_VISIBLE
reframe_type: COST_UNCOUNTED
weight: heavy
carry_line: "Once a week, one call by name. To a partner. Either you talk or you do not. The slot exists."
register: burnout_depletion_identity_erosion_capacity_gap
persona_anchor: first_responders
note: closer chapter — peer support structural practice
---
The chapter opened with the storage problem — that the calls go into you because there is nowhere else for them to go. It closes with the smallest possible alternative.

The practice is this. Pick one partner. Someone you have known for a few years. Someone who has been on enough calls that they do not need the briefing. Tell them: I am going to text you every Friday at 7 PM. We are going to talk for thirty minutes if either of us wants to talk. We do not have to talk. The slot exists.

That is the practice. One person. Once a week. A slot.

Most weeks, the call will not be heavy. The call will be about the kids, the weather, the team. The point is that the slot is built. The slot is built before you need it. So that the week the slot is needed — the week you walked into something that does not have a name — the slot is already there, and you do not have to find a person at 11 PM on a Wednesday while the call is still in your tissue.

The institution did not build this slot for you. You can build it with one person. One slot. Once a week.

Once a week, one call by name. To a partner. Either you talk or you do not. The slot exists.

## INTEGRATION v28
---
stage: pearl_writer_stage_3
mode: QUESTION_OPEN
reframe_type: QUESTION_REFRAME
weight: standard
carry_line: "What would you do if you woke up tomorrow and the badge were not yours anymore."
register: burnout_depletion_identity_erosion_capacity_gap
persona_anchor: first_responders
---
The chapter opened with identity-erosion welded to the role. It closes with a question. You do not have to answer it. You only have to sit with it.

If you woke up tomorrow and the badge were not yours anymore — through retirement, through injury, through any of the routes the job ends — what would you do this week. Not next year. This week. Walks. Meals. Friendships. The texture of a Tuesday afternoon.

If the answer comes quickly, the identity has not fully welded to the role and the recovery has somewhere to land. If the answer is hard to find, that is information. The information is that the welding is more complete than is healthy for a body that will eventually have to come off the badge anyway.

Knowing the welding is the first move. The unweld can happen slowly, in small pieces — a hobby, a friendship, a volunteer thing — over years. The slowness is fine. The notice is what makes the slow work possible.

What would you do if you woke up tomorrow and the badge were not yours anymore.

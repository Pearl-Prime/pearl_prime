

## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I know my pay scale. I know what I'll earn. There's no negotiating. No advancement that changes the fundamentals. So I calculate. Will this be enough? Can I retire? What if something happens? The mechanism is called fixed-income financial anxiety—the worry that comes with knowing exactly what's available.
---

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
What I notice is that the financial anxiety is tied to the job in specific ways. I'm in a high-risk role. So my earning potential has an expiration date. My body will eventually not be able to do this work. The mechanism is called time-limited earning potential anxiety.
---

## REFLECTION v03
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
There's a specific kind of financial stress that comes from being in a position where you sacrifice financially for the job. You're paid less than comparable civilian work. But you do it anyway. For the mission. For the public service. And then you have to live with the financial consequences. The mechanism is called mission-compromised-earnings.
---

## REFLECTION v04
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I've noticed that the financial anxiety is worse during crises. When budgets get cut. When staffing is reduced and pay doesn't increase. When there's uncertainty about job security. The mechanism is called crisis-driven financial anxiety.
---

## REFLECTION v05
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
What strikes me is how the financial anxiety affects my family. I can't take vacations. I can't afford the things my kids want. I can't provide the security I want to provide. And that feels like a failure. The mechanism is called family-impact financial anxiety.
---

## REFLECTION v06
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
There's a part of the financial anxiety that's about pride. I don't want to admit that the job doesn't pay enough. That I'm struggling. That the sacrifice isn't worth the cost. So the anxiety stays quiet. The mechanism is called shame-silenced financial anxiety.
---

## REFLECTION v07
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I notice that I'm not making enough to truly plan for the future. Retirement feels abstract. Like something that might not happen. The mechanism is called retirement-uncertainty anxiety.
---

## REFLECTION v08
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
What I'm realizing is that the financial anxiety is sometimes connected to the question of whether the sacrifice is worth it. If I'm not being paid enough to live well, why am I still doing the job? And the answer is complicated. The mechanism is called purpose-vs-payment conflict.
---

## REFLECTION v09
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: inherited
---
There's something about the public sector pay structure that creates specific anxiety. You can see your entire career earnings. You know exactly where you're at. You know exactly where you're going. The mechanism is called transparent-limitation anxiety.
---

## REFLECTION v10
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I've learned that the financial anxiety sometimes comes out as resentment toward people who make more money for jobs that feel less important. The mechanism is called comparative-worth resentment.
---

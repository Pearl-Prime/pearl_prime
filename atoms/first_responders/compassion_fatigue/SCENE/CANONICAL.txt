

## SCENE v01
---

---
The call comes in. Another overdose. Another family falling apart. You know the pattern. You've seen it a hundred times. You respond. Your hands know what to do. Your heart isn't there. You do the work from a place that feels like autopilot.
---

## SCENE v02
---

---
The recruit is crying after a rough call. You remember being that way. You watch them process what you've learned not to process. You tell them it gets easier. You don't tell them what else gets lost when it does.
---

## SCENE v03
---

---
The station kitchen. The crew is eating. Someone's talking about a call. Everyone's heard the same story a hundred times. The response is mechanical. Story. Acknowledgment. Next topic. No one's really present for it.
---

## SCENE v04
---

---
Your partner asks about your day. You say it was fine. The word fine means nothing. Nothing happened. Everything happened. You're not sure which. You move the conversation away from the job.
---

## SCENE v05
---

---
The debriefing after a hard call. People are supposed to process. Instead they're cracking jokes. Dark jokes. Everyone's laughing. The laughter is protecting them and also proving that feeling is somewhere you can't reach anymore.
---

## SCENE v06
---

---
The street where the accident happened. The damage is visible. The human cost is not. You secure the scene. You help the injured. You're moving through the motions. The part of you that used to get emotionally affected is not in your body anymore.
---

## SCENE v07
---

---
Home after the shift. Your family is waiting to hear about your day. You say nothing happened. Which is technically true if you don't count the five calls, the near-miss, the person you couldn't save. You don't count them.
---

## SCENE v08
---

---
The dispatch center. Another tones drop. You respond. No anger. No urgency. Just movement. The efficiency is clean. The lack of feeling underneath it is terrifying.
---

## SCENE v09
---

---
Your therapist asks how you're feeling. You say fine. Your therapist raises an eyebrow. You realize you've said the same thing three visits in a row. You're running out of ways to say I'm numb.
---

## SCENE v10
---

---
The hospital where you know the nurses. They recognize you. They used to ask how you were doing. They've stopped asking because the answer was always the same. Distance has replaced the connection.
---

## SCENE v11
---

---
The crew is supposed to do a debrief after the critical incident. Everyone sits in the room. Nobody wants to be here. Everyone knows saying the hard things costs too much. The debrief becomes silence instead.
---

## SCENE v12
---

---
Your kid is trying to tell you something. You're listening but you're somewhere else. The bandwidth for caring about home stuff is gone. You spent it at work. Nothing's left for the small things that matter most.
---

## SCENE v13
---

---
The funeral of someone from the community. You knew them. You tried to save them. You stand there feeling like you should feel something. You don't. The flatness is where the grief should be.
---

## SCENE v14
---

---
The station is quiet on a night shift. No calls. Everyone should rest. Instead there's this weird tension. The numbing has happened for everyone. The station is full of people who don't feel connected to anything anymore.
---

## SCENE v15
---

---
The call that used to be your call. The one that would have broken you years ago. Now it's just another call. You're competent. You're empty. The competence is hollow without the compassion.
---

## SCENE v16
---

---
Your partner is crying about something. You're supposed to comfort them. You're trying to generate the appropriate emotional response. The response doesn't come. You're going through the motions of care while care has left the building.
---

## SCENE v17
---

---
The new responder is struggling with a call. They want to talk about it. You could tell them it gets better. You could tell them the truth. That it gets worse in a different way. You tell them nothing.
---

## SCENE v18
---

---
The bar with the crew after work. Everyone's drinking. Everyone's making jokes about calls nobody wants to remember. The humor is the only feeling left. The humor is also the last wall before the collapse.
---

## SCENE v19
---

---
The scene where you need to show up as human. The family is traumatized. They need someone who cares. You have the skills to help them. You don't have the care. You're doing the job from behind glass.
---

## SCENE v20
---

---
Home. Evening. Your family is living their life. You're watching them like you're behind a window. Distance has become your baseline. You're physically present and completely absent.
---

## SCENE v21
---

---
The call that would have mattered once. Would have cost you sleep. Would have haunted you. Now it's filed away. You process nothing because there's nothing to process.
---

## SCENE v22
---

---
The mirror. You're looking at someone you don't recognize anymore. The hardness around your eyes wasn't there five years ago. The flatness in your expression is permanent now.
---

## SCENE v23
---

---
The station memorial for someone. Everyone's supposed to remember. Everyone's supposed to feel. You're standing there acknowledging that this loss matters while feeling nothing about it.
---

## SCENE v24
---

---
The call with a good outcome. Something actually worked. Someone actually got better. You should feel something. Relief. Purpose. Satisfaction. Instead you feel tired.
---

## SCENE v25
---

---
Your family is planning something for you. They're excited. You're pretending excitement. The emotions they're expecting from you have been spent. The reserve is empty.
---

## SCENE v26
---

---
The station training. Everyone's supposed to care about staying sharp. You do the motions. The reason for staying sharp is somewhere you can't find it anymore.
---

## SCENE v27
---

---
The person you helped months ago sees you. They want to tell you how much you mattered. You remember the call but not the feeling of helping them. The memory is dry.
---

## SCENE v28
---

---
The colleague who's struggling. They're reaching out. You know you should help. You're too tired to reach back. Compassion is a currency you can't spend anymore.
---

## SCENE v29
---

---
The night you're supposed to sleep. Your body is here. Your attention is at the station. Or nowhere. The divide between presence and absence has become permanent.
---

## SCENE v30
---

---
The reminder of who you were when you started. The idealism. The belief that you could help people. The care that was reflexive. That person existed once. That person doesn't live here anymore.
---

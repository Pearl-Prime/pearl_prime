

## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
You can fall asleep in the station between calls, but you can't stay asleep when you're home. That's the sleep anxiety in this work—the automatic activation that keeps you from resting when you're supposed to be resting.
The mechanism is called occupational sleep-state dysregulation—your nervous system learned to activate on the job and deactivate off the job. But the deactivation stopped working. Now you're stuck in activation even when there's no danger.
The anxiety is automatic. You go to sleep after a shift and your mind replays the calls. You're trying to rest and your body is still on alert. Your nervous system doesn't know how to power down.
What I'm learning is that the sleep anxiety is connected to the hypervigilance. In this work, you need to be ready at all times. Readiness requires a certain level of nervous system activation. But that activation was supposed to turn off when the call ended. For many of us, the off switch broke.
The sleep anxiety is also connected to the content of the calls. What you saw. What you experienced. What happened to people. That content doesn't stay on the scene. It follows you home. It plays during sleep. It keeps your nervous system activated.
The anxiety is also occupational. In this work, sleep is the time when you're most vulnerable. You're not in your gear. You can't respond. So the nervous system stays alert. It watches for danger while you try to sleep.

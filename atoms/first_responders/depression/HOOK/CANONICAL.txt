

## HOOK v01
---

---
The shift that used to light you up now feels like just another watch. You show up. You do the job. You go home. Nothing's broken except the thing that made it matter.
---

## HOOK v02
---

---
You can run into a burning building but you can't sit still for five minutes. That's not weakness. That's a nervous system that forgot how to stand down and now the quiet feels like drowning.
---

## HOOK v03
---

---
Years in the chair wear you down different. It's not the calls anymore. It's the shape of your own chest when you realize you felt nothing at the station today and that's the new normal.
---

## HOOK v04
---

---
The flatness came slow. No signal. No alarm. Just woke up one morning and the things that meant something didn't anymore and you kept showing up anyway.
---

## HOOK v05
---

---
Everybody on the crew has seen you handle impossible things. But nobody sees the night shift where you're staring at the wall and your body won't settle and your mind is perfectly still.
---

## HOOK v06
---

---
You learned to shut down on scene because it kept you alive. Then you couldn't turn it back on and the home part of your life went quiet in a way nobody at the station talks about.
---

## HOOK v07
---

---
The engine runs but the crew's not in it like they used to be. You can feel it in yourself too. The readiness is still there. The why went somewhere else.
---

## HOOK v08
---

---
You don't feel sad. That's almost the problem. You feel nothing and then you panic because feeling nothing used to be a survival skill and now it's eating your life.
---

## HOOK v09
---

---
After a certain number of shifts, the weight doesn't hurt anymore. It just sits. Your shoulders get used to it and you forget what it felt like to stand straight.
---

## HOOK v10
---

---
The job needs you to be steady. So you got steady. Too steady. Now you can't find the line between ready and empty and it's been weeks since you noticed the difference.
---

## HOOK v11
---

---
There's no critical incident to point at. No single call that broke something. Just the slow wear of a thousand calls and the understanding that you can't feel them anymore.
---

## HOOK v12
---

---
You're not struggling to keep your head up. You're struggling because your head is down and the view from here looks the same every single day and you can't remember why you cared.
---

## HOOK v13
---

---
The station is still your home. But the thing that made home feel alive is running on fumes and you're not sure if it's the job or if it's you or if there's a difference anymore.
---

## HOOK v14
---

---
Stability is what kept you breathing on scene. Now stability looks like going through the motions and waking up wondering if you're still here or just running on a routine your body memorized.
---

## HOOK v15
---

---
You can diagnose shock in others. You've seen what happens when the nervous system shuts down. But when it happens to you, it looks like doing the job right and nobody questions it.
---

## HOOK v16
---

---
The calls still come. You still respond. But somewhere between dispatch and return, the thing that made this work feel like work and not like filling time got misplaced.
---

## HOOK v17
---

---
Numb is practical in a lot of ways. Numb keeps you functional. Numb gets the shift done. But numb also means you're driving home and not remembering the route and not sure when that started.
---

## HOOK v18
---

---
Your nervous system learned the job and learned it well. Now it won't learn anything else. It won't let you rest. It won't let you feel. It's just holding a shape it built years ago.
---

## HOOK v19
---

---
The crew doesn't see it because you show up the same way every day. But going through the motions and being ready are two different things and you're only doing one of them now.
---

## HOOK v20
---

---
You're not broken. You're not in crisis. You're flatlined and that's somehow harder to name because everything on the surface looks like it's supposed to.
---

## HOOK v21
---

---
The job teaches you to compartmentalize. Lock it down. Stay functional. Then one day you realize the compartments have stacked so high you can't find yourself under them anymore.
---

## HOOK v22
---

---
You respond to every call. You perform every task. You follow every protocol. But the line between doing your job and being a robot got erased and you don't remember when.
---

## HOOK v23
---

---
Thousands of hours in the station and you still feel like you don't belong somewhere. The feeling used to make you fight harder. Now it just makes you tired and you can't explain it.
---

## HOOK v24
---

---
The weight of the job becomes the weight of your chest and then your chest becomes normal and you stop noticing you're struggling to breathe on your own time.
---

## HOOK v25
---

---
You used to love the crew. Used to feel it in your bones. Now you're on the watch together and it feels like being alone in a room full of people who trust you completely.
---

## HOOK v26
---

---
Readiness is your default. But readiness for what. The next call. The next shift. But not for the fact that the next call isn't going to feel like anything because nothing does anymore.
---

## HOOK v27
---

---
The station teaches you to function under pressure. What it doesn't teach you is what to do when the pressure is gone and you've forgotten how to be anything else.
---

## HOOK v28
---

---
You don't think about it. That's the design. Stay in motion. Keep moving. But the motion is automatic now and the thing that drove it went somewhere and you're moving because you forgot how to stop.
---

## HOOK v29
---

---
The watch feels shorter. The days feel longer. Everything's the same and nothing matters and you're still doing everything right and that's what makes it hard to say out loud.
---

## HOOK v30
---

---
Years teach you how to survive the worst. They don't teach you how to live with the rest and somewhere in those years you stopped trying to figure out the difference.
---

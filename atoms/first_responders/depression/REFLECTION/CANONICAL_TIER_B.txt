

## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
Depression in first responders isn't sadness. It's flatness. It's the slow draining of color from everything until everything looks the same shade of gray. I didn't recognize it because I thought depression was crying. What I experienced was numbness. The job became less interesting. People became less interesting. My own life became less interesting.
The mechanism is called anhedonia. It's the loss of capacity to experience pleasure. The nervous system stops investing in seeking reward because years on the job taught it that pleasure isn't reliably available.
---

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
The protective mechanism teaches your nervous system to not invest in small things. Not in pleasure. Not in joy. Save the energy for what matters. On the job, this is smart. Off the job, the mechanism keeps running.
I watched someone I respect become completely flat. Nothing touched them anymore. They could accomplish things they worked for and feel nothing. The mechanism had protected them by turning off the reward pathways completely.
---

## REFLECTION v03
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
You don't feel depression coming on. You just notice that things are less vivid than they used to be. Food tastes the same. Your favorite music is just noise. Nothing really touches you anymore.
The invisibility is that it doesn't feel like depression. Feels like finally being realistic. Finally understanding that pleasure is unreliable. Finally accepting that there's no point in hoping.
---

## REFLECTION v04
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
The automatic mechanism is called emotional flattening. The dopamine system gets recalibrated. The nervous system learns that pleasure isn't reliably available. So it stops expecting it. Stops seeking it. Stops responding to it.
Years on the job teach this. Then the automatic flatness keeps running off the job. Keep the dopamine low. Keep the excitement dampened.
---

## REFLECTION v05
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
I've been thinking about what constant emotional demand does to the reward pathways. How the protective mechanism eventually says: I can't keep responding to stimulus. I have to flatten.
The mechanism is trying to protect you from disappointment. From hoping for things that won't come through. The cost is that you stop feeling good about anything.
---

## REFLECTION v06
---
family: F1
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
The mechanism that creates depression happens at the neurological level. The dopamine system learns not to expect reward. The limbic system gets recalibrated to produce less dopamine in response to positive stimuli.
Years on the job teach this learning. Then off the job, the automatic dampening just keeps running. The mechanism doesn't stop because the job is over.
---

## REFLECTION v07
---
family: F2
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
I remember when I could feel excitement. When accomplishments meant something. The protective mechanism learned to dial down the reward response so gradually that by the time I noticed, I couldn't access joy anymore.
Now I can do things I worked for and feel nothing. The mechanism has succeeded in protecting me from disappointment. The cost is that I've lost my capacity to feel good about anything.
---

## REFLECTION v08
---
family: F3
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
The invisible cost of depression in first responders is that you can be completely unaware it's happening. You just notice that nothing's as interesting as it used to be. That you're going through the motions. That pleasure has become something you don't even seek anymore.
The mechanism is so smooth that you stop recognizing it as depression. You just think it's maturity. Understanding reality.
---

## REFLECTION v09
---
family: F4
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
The automatic flattening works like a thermostat for emotions. When dopamine response gets too high, the nervous system dials it back down. After years of the job using up emotional resources, the thermostat stays permanently dialed down.
Off the job, you're still living at that low setting. The mechanism won't turn it back up because it's designed to protect you.
---

## REFLECTION v10
---
family: F5
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
The protective mechanism has taught your nervous system that pleasure is unreliable and hoping is dangerous. So it protects you by making you stop wanting. Stop hoping. Stop expecting good things.
The cost is your capacity to feel. The cost is your engagement with life. The cost is that you become flat.



## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
You can run into danger and save lives, but you still feel like you don't know what you're doing. That's the imposter mechanism in this work—the automatic belief that you're not actually qualified for the job you're doing very well.
The mechanism is called experience devaluation—you discount your own experience. You've handled hundreds of calls. You've saved people. You know the gear. You know the protocols. But your mind automatically says: I still don't know what I'm doing.
This is different from other forms of imposter syndrome because it exists alongside competence. You're actually capable. You actually do know what you're doing. But the feeling of being a fraud persists anyway.
What I'm learning is that the imposter feeling might be connected to the stakes. If you get it wrong on this job, people die. The responsibility is real. The consequences are real. And the mechanism is your nervous system's way of saying: the responsibility is so high that I can never feel fully prepared.
The imposter feeling is also connected to the culture. In this work, you're supposed to be steady, capable, sure. Admitting doubt is admitting weakness. So the doubt gets internalized. You don't voice it. You don't acknowledge it. You just carry it. And the more you don't acknowledge it, the more real it becomes.

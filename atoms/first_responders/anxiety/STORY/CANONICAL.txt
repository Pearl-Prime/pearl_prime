He sat on the edge of the bed at 02:47 and the body did not know it was off shift. The pager was in the kitchen. The boots were by the door. The shift had cleared at twenty-two-thirty. None of that information had reached the chest.

The chest was running the routine it had run for the last fourteen hours — heart rate just above baseline, breath shallow at the clavicle, an attentional posture aimed at the radio that was not in the room. His ears were doing the work the radio used to ask of them, sorting the furnace cycle from the refrigerator hum from the neighbor's car, finishing the triage on each source before moving to the next.

The point is that the body could not tell where the shift ended. The official cut had been at twenty-two-thirty. The somatic cut was somewhere in the next forty-eight hours, depending on the calls he had run and the calls he had not. Tonight the somatic cut had not yet arrived, and would probably not arrive before sunrise, which meant his sleep was, in any meaningful sense, postponed.

He did not call it anxiety. He called it being thorough. He called it not being able to settle. He called it the new normal. The new normal was a chronic preparatory state his nervous system had adopted because the old normal — full discharge between calls — had not been available to him in years. The body had reorganized to make do with less recovery. The reorganization was working. The cost of the working was this hour, and the next one, and the one after that.

By 04:12 he gave up on the lying down. He went to the kitchen. He made coffee that he did not need and was not going to drink. The making was a substitute for the discharge that was not happening. The making was something his hands could do while the rest of him kept scanning.

She had been off shift for thirty-six hours and the scanning had not yet ended. This was the part she did not say out loud, because the math of it sounded like a problem she should be able to solve. Twelve hours on, thirty-six off, and the body still in the on-shift posture for the back half of the off-shift block. The accounting was not closing.

The grocery store was where she noticed it most. Her body had a complete map of the produce section that included exits, sight lines, the position of the security mirror, and the locations of three potential cover points in the event of an active scene. She had never had an active scene at this grocery store. She had never had an active scene at any grocery store. The map had been built anyway, by a nervous system that did not distinguish grocery stores from the calls she had run.

The point is that the map was a kind of love. The body had built it to keep her safe. The body did not have a way of being told that the love was not required here. So the love kept arriving — every aisle, every produce section, every checkout line. Each time she stood in line she ran a brief inventory of the eight people in front of her. The inventory was unconscious. She found out about it only when one of the people in line did something fast — reached for a wallet, pulled out a phone — and her shoulders lifted before her cortex got a turn.

What she would later understand was that the inventory was not paranoia. The inventory was equipment from the job that had not learned a new context. The grocery store was, to her body, just an unfamiliar scene she had not yet cleared. The body cleared scenes. That was the whole job description, and the job had not given the body any other description to use.

She paid for the groceries. She loaded the car. She drove home. Her shoulders did not come down all the way until she had locked the front door and stood in the entryway for ninety seconds, doing the silent perimeter check her body always did before letting itself believe the perimeter was clear. Ninety seconds of perimeter check for a bag of groceries. The math did not close. The math was the math.

He had stopped trying to make the scanning quit. That was the change. Not a big change. Not the kind of change that shows up in any external measurement. But a real change, and a recent one, and a change he had not expected to make.

For the first eight years on the job, he had treated the scanning as a problem to be solved. The off-shift scanning, specifically. The on-shift scanning was equipment. The off-shift scanning was a malfunction. He had read the books, gone to the workshops, tried the breathing exercises, used the apps, sat with the chaplain, talked to a therapist who specialized in the work. Each intervention had been an attempt to convince the scanning to stand down at the end of shift the way the rest of him stood down.

What he had begun to understand, in year nine, was that the scanning was not interested in standing down because standing down was not its job. Its job was to keep him alive across whatever interval the system had asked it to be on for. The interval was the entire eight years. The system had not asked the scanning to clock out. The scanning, faithful to its instructions, had not clocked out.

So he had started doing something different. He had started saying hello to the scanning when it arrived in the kitchen, on the back porch, in the bedroom at 02:47. Not out loud. Inwardly. The way you might acknowledge a dog that has come to lie at your feet because it doesn't want you to be alone. He would notice the chest tightening, and instead of running the breathing exercise, he would say: there you are. I see you. You can stay.

The scanning did not leave. The scanning had never been going to leave. But the scanning was no longer being told that it was not welcome. And when something is not being told it is unwelcome, it tends, slowly, to soften. The softening was not silence. It was a different relationship to the noise.

By month four of this new approach, the scanning at 02:47 was still arriving. It was arriving with less argument from him. The argument had been half the weight. Removing the argument did not remove the scanning. It removed the friction between him and the scanning. The friction had been costing him sleep he could now have. The sleep was not a cure. The sleep was just sleep, available to him because he had finally stopped wrestling with the scanning at the bedroom door.

Not resolution. A different relationship to the noise.



## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
Your body doesn't know how to reset. That's the mechanism. It learned escalation so well that the off switch disappeared. I watched mine go—took about six years before I realized the nervous system had become a one-way valve. Activation always possible. Deactivation never arrived.
On shift, that's adaptation. Off shift, it's a problem. You're at a dinner table and your hands shake. You're watching a movie and your heart races. The system learned to stay ready, and it never learned to stop. This isn't weakness. It's what happens when you teach the nervous system that danger arrives without warning.
---

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
I watched a medic's system lock itself into permanent vigilance. She couldn't sit still, couldn't sleep past four a.m., couldn't be in a room without mapping exits. The protective mechanism was working exactly as designed. Keep you alive. Stay alert. Never fully relax.
What nobody tells you is that this protection becomes the prison. Your nervous system is trying to save you from something that isn't there anymore. The call ended. The shift's over. But the mechanism doesn't believe in clock time. It just keeps running.
---

## REFLECTION v03
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
You don't watch yourself become hypervigilant. It creeps in gradually. One day you notice you're tense at your kid's soccer game. Then you can't sit on the couch without analyzing the room. Then you're waking at three a.m. on your days off because the old dispatch tone is still running in your head.
The invisibility is that it feels normal to you. Everyone at the station is the same way. It's just how responders are built. Until you step outside and realize most people don't live at a 7 or 8 on the activation scale as their baseline.
---

## REFLECTION v04
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
The automatic mechanism treats every slight stimulus as a potential threat. A car backfire. A siren across town. A phone notification. Your nervous system snaps to attention every single time. The amygdala has become the default mode.
This is what the job trains into you. Millisecond responsiveness. The capacity to go from idle to full activation in seconds. Off the job, that same automatic response is still running. You're still milliseconds away from panic.
---

## REFLECTION v05
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
I'm thinking about what constant activation does to a nervous system that never learned to recover. The protective mechanism decided to keep all the alarms on. In the house. At the station. At home. Every room illuminated and waiting.
The cost of this constant illumination is that you forget what rest feels like. Forget what genuine safety feels like. Forget that a quiet room isn't a setup for danger. You exist in perpetual readiness that was supposed to protect you.
---

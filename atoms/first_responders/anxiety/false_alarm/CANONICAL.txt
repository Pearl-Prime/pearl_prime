Brianna is at home on a Thursday night. A car alarm goes off outside her apartment. Her body doesn't wait for her mind. Her heart is already hammering. She's at the window before she thinks. Her nervous system knows the sound. That's the dispatch tone. That's the signal. Her body is reaching for the gear that isn't there. She takes a breath. It's just a car alarm. A neighbor's car with a dying battery. But her hands are shaking. She's had twenty-six years to learn what a real alarm sounds like. But on her days off, every alarm is real for just a second. She sits back down. Her body won't settle for another hour.

Brianna stops avoiding. She goes to the grocery store deliberately, knowing an alarm might happen. She prepares herself: if an alarm goes off, my body will react. That's not an emergency. Nowak hears a tone drop at the station and his body launches before the dispatcher finishes the address. Full sympathetic activation. Heart rate spikes. Hands grip the rail. The call is cancelled thirty seconds later — wrong address, no emergency. His body does not stand down. The adrenaline has nowhere to go. His chest pounds for twenty more minutes.

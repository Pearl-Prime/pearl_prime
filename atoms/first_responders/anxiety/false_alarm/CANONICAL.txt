Brianna is at home on a Thursday night. A car alarm goes off outside her apartment. Her body doesn't wait for her mind. Her heart is already hammering. She's at the window before she thinks. Her nervous system knows the sound. That's the dispatch tone. That's the signal. Her body is reaching for the gear that isn't there. She takes a breath. It's just a car alarm. A neighbor's car with a dying battery. But her hands are shaking. She's had twenty-six years to learn what a real alarm sounds like. But on her days off, every alarm is real for just a second. She sits back down. Her body won't settle for another hour.

Jake is driving home from the precinct. Another car honks. His entire body flinches. His hands grip the wheel. His chest compresses. In that fraction of a second, he's back at the domestic call. He's scanning for threat. The car beside him just wanted to switch lanes. His body doesn't care about the reason. It cares about the signal. He's been conditioned by the job to treat alerts as real. Every alert. All the time. Now his nervous system is reading civilian alerts as job alerts. He drives home shaking.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02

COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 4
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization

Dex's baseline arousal is climbing. The grocery store alarm. The phone buzzing. The neighbor's dog. All false alarms. All triggering the full system response. At the station, he's solid. On shift, he responds to real alarms fine. But off-shift, his body has learned to treat the world as a potential call. He's jumpy around his family. His partner comments that he's always in ready-mode. He doesn't know how to explain that his nervous system doesn't understand the difference between his house and the station. The false alarms have created a constant state of low activation.

---
Kyle is calling out more shifts. Not officially. But he's taking days off because the false alarms are exhausting. A shift with real calls is easier than the constant anticipation of calls. At least on-shift, the body is being used correctly. Off-shift, his nervous system is in a permanent state of scanning for the alarm that won't come. He's starting to prefer being at the station. The false alarms make more sense there because they're actually approaching something real. He doesn't tell anyone. He just starts taking more days. His supervisor notices. Asks if he's okay. Kyle says he's fine. But the false alarms have made him not fine.

## TURNING_POINT v01
---
path: story_atoms/first_responders/anchored/anxiety/false_alarm/turning_point/micro/v01.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 4
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Brianna stops avoiding. She goes to the grocery store deliberately, knowing an alarm might happen. She prepares herself: if an alarm goes off, my body will react. That's not an emergency. That's just my nervous system being practiced. When the fire alarm does go off, her body responds. Heart up. Hands ready. This time, she doesn't fight it. She watches it happen. She observes: the activation is real, the threat is not. After two minutes, the alarm stops and she's still standing in the cereal aisle. She finishes shopping. The false alarm is still a false alarm, but she's no longer running from them.

## TURNING_POINT v03

MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Renata brings her wife into the conversation. She says: my body is on-call all the time. Even off-duty. My wife says: what do you need? Renata realizes she's never been asked that. She thinks about it. She says: I need to know I can stand down. It's not something to fix. It's something to witness. So when Renata comes home and her body is in ready-mode, her wife says: I see you. You can land when you're ready. The landing doesn't happen instantly. But being seen changes the quality of the false alarm state. It's less lonely.



## RECOGNITION v01

COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: pre_awareness
---
Marcus runs calls from 8am to 2am. Four bodies. Three overdoses. One elderly person who fell and couldn't get up. His chest is tight by shift end. His throat won't open. He's not panicking about any single call. It's the stack. The cumulative weight. He sits in the ambulance after the last run and his body just—stops. His hands won't move. His mind won't focus. The overwhelm isn't about today. It's about today plus yesterday plus the last month. His hands shake on the steering wheel. He needs to go home. He can't drive.

Sofia is isolating now. She goes to work. She comes home. She doesn't call friends. She doesn't call her wife. She's afraid that if she opens her mouth, everything will spill out and it will be too much. So she doesn't open her mouth. She eats. She sleeps. She waits for her next shift. ## TURNING_POINT v01
---
path: story_atoms/first_responders/anchored/anxiety/overwhelm/turning_point/micro/v01.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 4
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Marcus stops trying to manage the overwhelm alone. He goes to the department counselor. He says: I'm full. There's no space. The counselor listens. Doesn't try to fix. Marcus realizes: the overwhelm is the point. He's not supposed to be fine with this much. His body is telling him the truth. The truth is: I'm running more calls than I have capacity for. The first shift after counseling, Marcus works with the knowledge that his overwhelm is valid. He still takes the calls. But he's no longer fighting the overwhelm. He's working with it.

## TURNING_POINT v03
---
path: story_atoms/first_responders/anchored/anxiety/overwhelm/turning_point/micro/v03.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Jake stops trying to hold all the calls in his head. He starts writing them down. Just the calls. Who. What happened. How it ended. He writes for an hour. And something happens. By externalizing the calls, his head has a little space. The overwhelm is still real. But his mind isn't the only container anymore. He can think about something else for a moment. Just a moment. But it's different. He does this every few days. The overwhelm doesn't disappear. But it becomes manageable when he's not trying to store it all internally.

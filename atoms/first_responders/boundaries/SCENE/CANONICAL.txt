

## SCENE v01
---

---
The restaurant. You've finally got a table. Your partner is looking at you. The phone buzzes. Shift coverage needed. The other person is calling. You look at the menu. Your hand reaches for the phone. The food gets cold.
---

## SCENE v02
---

---
Home on your scheduled day off. The rain is heavy. You're supposed to be here. The station calls. No one's available for the mutual aid request. Your partner sighs. You're already getting dressed. Rain means accidents. Accidents mean calls.
---

## SCENE v03
---

---
The station at two PM. Not your shift. You came in to do something. The captain asks if you can cover the late shift. You don't even think about it. You've already changed into your gear.
---

## SCENE v04
---

---
Your kid's soccer game at three on Saturday. You marked the calendar. You're in the bleachers. The phone. Dispatch. Critical incident downtown. Two blocks from the station. You stand up. Your kid watches you leave.
---

## SCENE v05
---

---
The phone at midnight. Your friend is in crisis. They need to talk. You're exhausted from the shift. You stay on the line for an hour anyway. You're the one who listens. You're the one people call at midnight.
---

## SCENE v06
---

---
The station house during a quiet afternoon shift. No calls pending. Your body isn't resting. Your mind isn't resting. You clean. You organize. You find things to do. Stopping means you might have to go home and you've forgotten how to be home.
---

## SCENE v07
---

---
Your partner's work event. You promised to go. You promised weeks ago. The phone buzzes at six PM. Someone needs something. You're texting apologies while your partner is introducing you to their boss. You're not really there.
---

## SCENE v08
---

---
The station. An overtime shift is available. The money would be good. You think about what you had planned with your family. Thirty seconds. That's how long you think about it. Then you volunteer.
---

## SCENE v09
---

---
Sitting at the table at dinner. The whole family is there. Nobody's talking about work. Your phone lights up. A text from someone at the station. Not an emergency. Just a question. You answer immediately. Your family is used to this.
---

## SCENE v10
---

---
The bar with the crew. You're supposed to be off-duty. Everyone's talking about covering shifts, about calls, about the schedule. The station is where you're comfortable. You stay until late because the station feels more like home than home does.
---

## SCENE v11
---

---
Early morning before your shift. You're meeting a friend for coffee. They're telling you about something difficult. You're listening but your brain is already at the station, already preparing for the shift. You leave early. They notice. You both pretend they didn't.
---

## SCENE v12
---

---
The family planning a vacation. Everyone's excited except you're already running scenarios. You're thinking about coverage. You're thinking about who might need what while you're gone. You're thinking about your job when you should be thinking about your family.
---

## SCENE v13
---

---
Standing in your house on a morning off. You can't remember the last time you felt truly at home. The station knows you. Your crew knows you. Your family is here but you don't know how to be present anymore.
---

## SCENE v14
---

---
The locker room. A junior responder is asking you to mentor them. You say yes immediately. Another commitment. Another way you've made yourself necessary. You don't know how to say no to anyone.
---

## SCENE v15
---

---
Home office work you were supposed to finish. It's been pushed back three times for calls and shifts. Your partner is frustrated. You're frustrated. But this is just what it is. The job comes first. That's the deal.
---

## SCENE v16
---

---
Your old crew member texts. They're struggling. They need someone to talk to. You're off-duty. You're tired. You call them back. You're always calling back. You're the one they trust.
---

## SCENE v17
---

---
Bed next to your sleeping partner. Three AM. Your phone buzzes. Some news. Some question from the station. Some small thing that could wait. You respond anyway. Your partner wakes up. Doesn't say anything. Just rolls over.
---

## SCENE v18
---

---
The station locker room. You're changing out of your gear after a brutal shift. Someone asks if you can take another call tomorrow morning instead of going home to sleep. You're already agreeing. Your body is already adjusting to another shift.
---

## SCENE v19
---

---
The phone at dinner. Not an emergency. Just availability check. They're wondering if you might be free to pick up a shift next week. You're already committing in your head. Your family continues eating. This is normal.
---

## SCENE v20
---

---
You're at home on a weekend. The weather is clear. You should be relaxing. You're thinking about who's on duty today. You're thinking about the calls they might get. You're thinking about being there instead of here.
---

## SCENE v21
---

---
The porch at dusk. Your kid wants to teach you something they learned. You're half paying attention. Your body is still at the station. Even though you physically left, you didn't really leave.
---

## SCENE v22
---

---
Saying no to plans because you picked up a shift. Saying no to rest because the crew needs you. Saying no to your own life because saying yes to the job feels like your only option.
---

## SCENE v23
---

---
The grocery store. You run into someone from work. They ask about coverage. You're already mentally rearranging your schedule to see if you can pick up what they're asking. Your shopping list gets abandoned.
---

## SCENE v24
---

---
Your therapist asks about boundaries. You don't have language for it. The job is who you are. Setting boundaries feels like abandoning the crew.
---

## SCENE v25
---

---
The evening. You're supposed to be present. Instead you're checking the station schedule, checking the roster, running numbers in your head about who's working and who might need help.
---

## SCENE v26
---

---
Standing at the door saying goodbye to a family function early because there might be a call. Might. Not definitely. But might is enough.
---

## SCENE v27
---

---
Your partner asks when you'll set limits. You don't know how. The boundaries don't exist anymore. There's only duty and everything else is secondary.
---

## SCENE v28
---

---
The phone buzzes at the moment you finally sit down to rest. You don't even debate whether to answer. You just answer. That's what you do. That's who you are.
---

## SCENE v29
---

---
Work seeping into home. Home seeping into work. The station is your family. Your family is secondary. That's the order you've established and now you're living in it.
---

## SCENE v30
---

---
The moment you realized you don't know how to say no anymore. It could have been last week or last year. You can't pinpoint it. You just know that yes is the only word you speak now.
---

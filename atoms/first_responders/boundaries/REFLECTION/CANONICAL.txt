

## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
The job teaches you that boundaries are a luxury you don't get. That if someone calls, you answer. If someone needs, you go. If something happens, you're on it. That's not altruism. That's survival.
Other people's emergencies become your emergencies. Not because you're a good person. Because on the job, if you don't treat other people's emergencies as your emergencies, people die.
So you learn to obliterate the line between your time and their time. Between your responsibility and shared responsibility. Between what you owe and what you're obligated to give.
I didn't realize I'd done this until I was off the job. Until I couldn't say no to anything. Until I was saying yes to every request, every favor, every crisis that someone else needed me to solve. Not consciously choosing. Automatically. The mechanism was running on its own.
The problem is that the nervous system doesn't distinguish between on-shift time and off-shift time. The mechanism doesn't respect the clock. It just knows: someone needs something, you provide it. That's the code.

---

I knew a firefighter who couldn't say no to his family. Not because he was a pushover. But because the job had taught him that saying no could kill someone.
On the job, every moment you delayed response was a moment someone was in danger. So you learned to say yes immediately. To mobilize instantly. To treat every request like it could be the difference between life and death.
Off the job, that same automatic response got applied to every family member, every friend, every person who made a request. His daughter would ask him to fix something at midnight and he'd be up. His ex-wife would call with an emergency and he'd drop everything. His friend would need help moving and he'd cancel his own plans.
The protective mechanism is about keeping people safe. It's about treating every request as if it matters that much. And the cost is that you never get to have boundaries. You never get to say "I'm not available." You never get to protect your own time and energy.

The mechanism is called obligation creep. It starts with the legitimate obligations of the job. You have to be available during your shift. You have to respond when you're called.
Then it expands. You're available off-shift because the station might need you. You're on call for mutual aid. You're available to mentee the younger guys. You're available to cover for someone who's sick.
Then it expands further. You're available to friends who need help. Family who need advice. People in your community who know what you do and think you're the person to solve their problem.
The mechanism is automatic. You don't consciously decide to have no boundaries. You just automatically say yes. The job trained you to say yes. To treat every need like it's urgent. To put other people's emergencies before your own rest.
And once the mechanism is in place, it's hard to stop. Your nervous system is geared toward response. Toward availability. Toward making sure everyone else's need gets met.

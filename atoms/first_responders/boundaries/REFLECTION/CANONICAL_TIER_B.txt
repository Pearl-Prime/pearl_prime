

## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
The job doesn't teach you how to have boundaries. It teaches you to ignore them. Someone needs something? You respond. Even off shift. Especially off shift. Because the mechanism learned that your availability is the measure of your worth.
I didn't realize I'd become completely permeable until I looked at a calendar and couldn't find a single day that was actually mine. Every moment was allocated to someone else's need. The automatic mechanism had learned to sacrifice anything for responsiveness.
---

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
The protective mechanism is keeping other people safe by making yourself completely unavailable to yourself. This sounds noble until you realize that your nervous system has learned to treat other people's emergencies as more important than your own survival.
I watched someone I care about disappear into this. By the time they noticed, they'd said yes to everything. They couldn't say no if they wanted to. The automatic response had replaced actual choice.
---

## REFLECTION v03
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
Your boundaries dissolve so gradually you don't notice. First you're staying late. Then checking messages on your day off. Then responding to every request. The invisibility is that by the time you realize what's happened, you can't remember the last time you said no to anything.
You've become completely accessible. Completely obligated. Completely serving everyone's need but your own.
---

## REFLECTION v04
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
The automatic boundary-erasing mechanism starts with the job. You have to be available during shift. You have to respond to every call. Then it expands. You're on call off shift. You're covering for others. You're mentoring.
Off the job, the mechanism keeps running. You can't distinguish between things you should do and things you want to do. Both get the same automatic yes.
---

## REFLECTION v05
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
I've been thinking about what it means to have your nervous system configured to never refuse. The protective mechanism is trying to keep you relevant. Useful. Needed. Because if you're not needed, the underlying fear says, you might be abandoned.
So you become indispensable. The cost is the last boundary—the one between you and everyone else's needs.
---

## REFLECTION v06
---
family: F1
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
The job teaches you that boundaries are a luxury you don't get. That if someone calls, you answer. That if someone needs, you go.
The automatic response becomes: someone needs something, I provide it. No hesitation. No evaluation. Off the job, the mechanism doesn't stop running. You're still automatically saying yes.
---

## REFLECTION v07
---
family: F2
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
I didn't realize my boundary problem until I went to therapy and couldn't remember the last time I said no. The invisibility was that the job never taught me it was okay to have boundaries.
Just taught me that I say yes. I respond. I'm available. Off the job, I'm still operating under that code.

## REFLECTION v09
---

voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---

You can't turn it off by will. Off the job, there's nothing to interrupt the code.

## REFLECTION v10
---
family: F5
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---

Thought I was just being helpful. Didn't realize I'd lost the capacity to say no.

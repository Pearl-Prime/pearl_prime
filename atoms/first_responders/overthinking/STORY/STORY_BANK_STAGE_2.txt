Dani is at the kitchen table at 23:14, replaying the call from 19:42. The replay has been running since approximately 21:00. She has not yet found the version of the call that would let her stop replaying it.

The call had been a pediatric. The pediatric had survived. The transport to the children's hospital had been textbook. The handoff at the ED had been clean. There was nothing about the call that, by any external measure, required this much processing.

The body did not consult external measures. The body had a piece of the call it had not finished processing in the moment, and the body was finishing the processing now. The piece was a moment, about minute four of the call, when she had to make a decision about positioning the patient. The decision had been correct. She knew this. Her partner had agreed at the time, and the ED had agreed in the handoff, and a flight medic she had texted later that night had agreed when she described it.

But the decision had taken three seconds. In those three seconds, she had been alone in her own head, weighing two options against each other, with a pediatric in front of her and a parent crying in the next room. The three seconds had been the loudest three seconds of the shift. The three seconds had been the part of the call the body had not processed in real time, because the body had been busy with the procedural work that had to happen at the same time as the deciding.

So the body was processing the three seconds now, at the kitchen table, four hours later. The processing did not need a different answer. The answer had been right. The processing needed something else — an acknowledgment that the three seconds had cost her something, that she had been alone in those seconds in a way the report did not capture, that the cost of being alone in those seconds was a kind of weight the body was now asking her to feel.

Dani recognized this, in part, because she had done some reading. She had read about how the autonomic nervous system handles high-stakes decisions in the field, and how the cognitive metabolism of those decisions is deferred to the post-shift period, and how the deferred metabolism can feel like overthinking when it is really integration happening on a delayed schedule.

The reading did not make the replay stop. The reading gave her a frame. The frame let her sit at the kitchen table with the replay running, and instead of arguing with the replay or trying to suppress the replay, she could say to herself: this is integration. This is the body finishing the call. This is the work that the shift did not have time for.

By 00:14 the replay had begun to shift its texture. It was no longer running the call for an answer. It was running the call for an acknowledgment. She gave the acknowledgment, inwardly: I was alone in those three seconds. The work was hard. I made the call. The patient survived. The cost was real.

The replay did not end. The replay quieted by some specific amount, and the quieting was enough that she could go to bed and sleep, and the sleep was not deep but it was sleep, and the next morning the call had been filed somewhere the body had needed it to be filed, and the next shift could begin on a body that had finished metabolizing yesterday's work.

This is what awareness looks like at year eight. Not the absence of the replay. A different relationship to the replay. The replay is recognized as the work the body has been deferred from doing. The acknowledgment, given inwardly at the kitchen table, is what the replay had been requesting. The acknowledgment, once given, is what allows the replay to release.

Reuben has a call from year eight that he has been running in his head, at intervals, for eight years.

The call was a structure fire. Residential. Two-story. A family was inside. The family got out. By the time the apparatus arrived, the family was on the front lawn, accounted for. The fire was knocked down in twenty-six minutes. There were no civilian injuries and one minor injury to a firefighter on his crew, who returned to duty the next shift.

By every external measure, the call was a win. The training reports cited it as a clean operation. The chief mentioned it in a department-wide email about a year later, in the context of a different fire, as an example of how things should go. Reuben had been on the nozzle. Reuben had not made the email. The email had cited the captain, who had run command, and the engineer, who had positioned the apparatus.

What Reuben has been running in his head for eight years is not the call. The call was fine. What he has been running is one specific moment, on the second floor, when he was advancing the line and saw, in the periphery of his vision, a stuffed animal on the floor of what turned out to be the older child's bedroom. The stuffed animal was a worn brown rabbit. The rabbit had been in the bedroom before the fire. The rabbit was now partially burned. Reuben had stepped over the rabbit and continued the advance.

The moment had been less than two seconds. Reuben had not stopped. He had not paused. He had done what he was supposed to do, which was advance the line. The rabbit was not his job. The rabbit was a household item, like a lamp, like a chair. The work was the fire.

But the rabbit has been in Reuben's head for eight years. Not constantly. At intervals. Usually after other calls that have nothing in common with the structure fire from year eight, except that they have, in some way, involved a child. The rabbit comes up. The rabbit gets considered. The rabbit gets put back.

What he has come to understand, only in the last year, is that the rabbit is not the call. The rabbit is the thing he uses to handle the call. The call had been about a family that almost lost two children. The call had been about a near-catastrophe his body had walked into and walked out of and that had been, in the moment, too large to feel. The rabbit was the symbol the body assigned to the unfeeling. The rabbit is portable. The rabbit can be brought up at any quiet hour. The rabbit can be examined without examining the whole call.

The rabbit is a manageable proxy for an unmanageable feeling.

He understands this. The understanding has not made the rabbit go away. The rabbit will probably come up at intervals for the rest of his life. What the understanding has done is something else. The understanding has given the rabbit a name. The name is: the part of the call I was not able to feel at the time.

Once the rabbit has a name, the rabbit takes up less room. The room is not zero. The rabbit is still there. But the rabbit is now an item on a shelf in his memory, not an item lying on the floor of his attention. The shelf has a label. The label is the work of sixteen years.

This is what confronting looks like at year sixteen. Not absolution. A label on a shelf. A symbol that the body offered him for handling something the body could not face directly, and a willingness, now, to name the symbol for what it is. The naming does not solve the call. The naming gives the body a way to put the call down at the end of each visit, instead of leaving it lying on the floor of his attention.

The rabbit is on the shelf. The rabbit can come down when the body needs to look at it. The rabbit goes back on the shelf afterward. This is the arrangement he has finally arrived at. It took eight years.

Marcus is in the shower. The shower has been running for nineteen minutes. He has been mostly stationary for the last fourteen.

He is replaying a conversation he had with his partner on the rig today. The conversation had been about a call last week. His partner had said something. Marcus had said something back. The conversation had taken about ninety seconds. Both of them had then moved on to the next call.

In the shower, fourteen minutes into the standing-still phase, Marcus is on the third loop of the ninety-second conversation. Each loop, he is examining a different sentence. The sentence he is currently examining is a sentence his partner had said about the way Marcus had handled a refusal. The sentence had not been negative. The sentence had been neutral. The sentence had also been, in some way Marcus cannot yet decode, slightly off the expected tone.

The "slightly off the expected tone" is what is keeping him in the shower. The body has decided that the slight tone variation needs to be processed. The body is processing it. The processing is taking the form of running the conversation again and listening for the tone, again, in case the tone was something other than what he heard the first time.

What Marcus does not yet know — what he will not know until a counselor he has not yet met says it plainly in about eight years — is that the conversation does not actually need to be processed. The conversation was fine. The slight tone variation, if it existed at all, was about something between his partner and the call, not about Marcus. The processing is not about the conversation.

The processing is about a category. The category is: my partner's regard for me. The category is one his body has assigned a high priority to, because his partner is the person his life depends on during calls, and his body cannot afford ambiguity in his partner's regard, and any small variation in his partner's tone gets recruited into a re-examination of his partner's regard for him as a whole.

This is happening below the level of his awareness. He thinks he is examining a sentence. The body is examining a category. The category is not, in any sense, in danger. The partner's regard is fine. They have worked together for four years and have full operational trust.

The body does not know that. The body knows only that one sentence had a slightly off tone, and the body is reviewing the regard the way the body reviews equipment after a shift — checking for damage, ensuring readiness, doing maintenance.

Marcus does not call this overthinking. He calls this normal. He has been doing it for so long that the doing is invisible to him. He gets out of the shower at minute twenty-two. The hot water has run out. The conversation has been examined for the fifth time. The conversation will be examined again, probably tomorrow, probably during a slow moment on shift, probably without him knowing he is doing it.

His wife, in the bedroom, hears him get out of the shower. She does not know about the conversation. She does not know about the regard. She knows that he showers for a long time after most shifts and that he is quieter than he used to be in the evenings. She has stopped asking why. She has not stopped noticing.

The cost of this rumination is paid, partly, by Marcus, in the time he loses to the showers and the slow evenings. The cost is paid, partly, by his wife, in the quietness she has stopped asking about. The cost is invisible from the outside. The cost is also visible, to anyone who has ever lived with someone who has been doing this work for twelve years and has not yet been told that the showers are a venue.

He puts on pajamas. He gets into bed. He kisses his wife on the cheek. He does not mention the conversation. His wife asks how the day was. He says it was fine. He is, for the most part, telling the truth.

The garage is where he keeps his tools. He has, over the years, accumulated a workbench, a small drill press, a collection of clamps and hand tools, a set of saw horses, and a chair that he found on the curb six years ago and refinished. The chair is in the corner of the garage, between the workbench and the door to the backyard. The chair has a small table next to it, on which he keeps a coffee mug, a notebook, and a pen.

The notebook is the heart of the practice. The notebook is a college-rule lined notebook, the kind kids use for school, that he bought at a drugstore at the start of the year. The notebook has, by now, fifty pages of writing. Each Sunday, he writes for about ten minutes.

What he writes is not a journal in the usual sense. He writes, in short sentences, the calls of the previous week that the body is still holding. He does not analyze them. He does not look for patterns. He does not advise himself. He writes down what the body has not yet released, and he leaves it on the page.

The first few weeks of the practice, the writing was difficult. He did not know what to write. He sat with the notebook open and stared at it. Eventually, after about three weeks, the body started to dictate. The body would offer a sentence — a phrase about a call from Tuesday, a name from a domestic on Friday, a smell from a scene he had cleared on Wednesday — and he would write it down.

The body, given a venue, became less reluctant. By week six, the body had a list of items it wanted to mention each Sunday. By week ten, the list had stabilized. By week twenty, the list had begun to thin.

The thinning was the surprise. He had expected the list to grow. He had expected the practice to be a kind of accumulation. Instead, the practice had become a kind of release. The body, knowing that the Sunday afternoon venue existed, had stopped bringing up items at other times. The items came up on Sunday. The items got written down. The items, having been written down, stopped recurring at the gym on Wednesday, in the car on Thursday, at the dinner table on Saturday.

By week forty, his sleep had measurably improved. He had not changed any other habit. He had not started a medication. He had not stopped drinking. The only change had been the notebook in the garage.

His wife had noticed. She had asked what he was doing in the garage on Sundays. He had told her, roughly. She had not asked for more detail. She had nodded. She had begun, on her own, to leave him alone for those thirty minutes without comment.

This was a kind of marital cooperation he had not asked for and did not entirely know how to be grateful for. He understood that she had been waiting, for nineteen years, for him to find something like this. She had not pushed. She had not suggested. She had simply made the room available when he had finally found his own way to the garage.

He has, by now, two notebooks. The first one is full. The second one is about a third full. He keeps them on a shelf above the workbench. They are between a manual for a circular saw he no longer owns and a small box of grade-2 carriage bolts. The notebooks are not labeled. He does not need them to be labeled. He knows what they are.

Not resolution. A repeatable Sunday afternoon practice. A garage. A chair. A notebook. The body, finally, has a venue it can count on. The venue does not undo the nineteen years. The venue does change what the next year, and the year after that, will cost. The cost is being paid in thirty-minute installments, on a schedule the body has agreed to, in a room of the house that is his.

This is what integrating looks like, at year nineteen, after eighteen years of not having a venue. It looks like a college-ruled notebook on a shelf above a workbench. It looks like Sunday afternoon. It looks like a chair the body finally found.

What she had begun to notice, around year nine, was that the discipline was costing more than it was protecting. The not-talking meant that the calls were going somewhere, and the somewhere was the kitchen at 02:47, the shower at 22:00, the long drive to her parents' house. The calls were getting processed alone, in venues her partner could not enter. The processing was using up resources that, if she had been spending them on her partnership, would have been resources the partnership could have used.

So, six months ago, she had started a small experiment. She had picked a call that had been bothering her — a call from three weeks earlier, a transport with an outcome that had been ambiguous in a way she did not have language for — and she had told her partner about it. Not in the bay. In the kitchen, on a Saturday afternoon, when her partner had asked how the work had been lately.

Her partner had listened. Her partner had said, when Yvette finished, "do you want me to ask questions or just hear it?" Yvette had said: just hear it. Her partner had said: okay. Her partner had registered the call as something Yvette had brought home, and her partner had received it.

The outcome was still what it had been. In the six months since, she has continued the experiment. Not every call. Most calls, she still keeps to herself. A small number, the ones the body has been turning over for more than a week, she brings to the kitchen on a Saturday afternoon. The bringing is brief. The receiving is reliable. The body, after the receiving, releases that particular call.

She does not yet have a name for this practice. It is too new. Her partner, six months in, has begun to recognize the shape of these conversations and has begun to offer the silence that makes them possible. The silence is part of the practice. The silence is what allows the calls to be told without being immediately recruited into something else — advice, sympathy, processing on a different register.

This is what experimentation looks like at year eleven. Not a transformation. A small calibration in what gets brought home and to whom. The amendment is changing, in small ways, what the partnership can hold and what the body has to carry alone.

The body has begun, in the months since they started, to spend fewer hours on the kitchen at 02:47. The body is using the bedroom for sleep, more often than it had been. The body has found, with her partner's help, a venue it had been needing for years and had not believed it was allowed to want.

Naomi has, in the last year, begun to confront a specific cognitive habit she had not previously named. The habit is the construction of alternative versions of calls in which she did better.

She had not known she was doing this until a friend, also in the work, mentioned, in passing, at a barbecue, that she had stopped doing it about two years ago. The friend had said: I used to run the better version of every call. I stopped. It saves a lot of energy.

Naomi had nodded. She had not yet, in that moment, understood that she had been running the better version of every call for ten years. The understanding came later, in the car on the way home, when she ran through the four calls of her most recent shift and noticed that she had, for each of them, a slightly improved version available. The improved version was always operational — a better hand position, a clearer instruction to the patient, a more decisive call to dispatch. The improved versions were small. The improved versions were all real, in the sense that they were achievable. The improved versions were also, in aggregate, an enormous cognitive load.

She had been carrying that load for ten years.

Once she had named the habit, she could see it everywhere. It was running while she did the dishes. It was running while she read. It was running while she stood in line at the post office. The habit was, in some sense, the texture of her interior life. She had assumed, for ten years, that the texture was thinking. The texture was, in fact, a specific kind of thinking that had a name. The name was: counterfactual rehearsal in service of an impossible standard.

The standard was that every call could be done better, given another chance. The standard was correct, in a narrow technical sense — every operation can be improved on. The standard was also incorrect, in a broader sense, because she did not have another chance, and the call had been done at the standard available to her in the moment, and the improved version was not, in any usable way, a thing she could apply.

She had begun, after the barbecue, to interrupt the habit when she noticed it. The interruption was small. She would say, inwardly: the call is done. The better version is not a thing. She would then turn her attention to whatever she had been doing before the habit started running — the dishes, the line at the post office, the book.

The interruption did not, at first, change much. The habit was deeply grooved. It came back. She interrupted it again. By month four, she could feel the habit losing some of its volume. The habit still ran. The habit was now competing with the interruption, and the interruption was getting heard about a third of the time.

By month ten, the habit had thinned. Not disappeared. Thinned. She could go entire afternoons without the habit running on a call from the previous shift. The afternoons that were free of the habit were, by a small measure, different afternoons. She read more. She slept better on the nights of those afternoons. She had longer phone calls with her sister.

What this means, what she has begun to understand, is that the habit was not a feature of her professionalism. The habit had presented itself as professionalism — as commitment to improvement, as taking the work seriously — but the habit had been, in its actual function, a cognitive tax on a person who was already paying the operational tax of the work.

She did not need both taxes. The operational tax was the cost of the work. The cognitive tax of counterfactual rehearsal was a cost she had been paying voluntarily, without recognizing that she had been paying it.

The interruption is the practice. The practice is not transformation. The practice is a small daily decision, available to her now that the habit has been named, to spend the cognitive resources somewhere other than the better version of last shift.

She is using the resources differently. She is using them on the present shift, on her partnership, on the slow phone calls with her sister, on a book she has been reading for three months and is finally, in the last month, finishing. The book is about something other than the work. The book is the kind of thing she had stopped having time for. She has, by interrupting the habit, made time available. The time was, all along, available. The time had been spoken for by a habit she had not known was running.

Caleb has been replaying, for the last four days, a call from Tuesday. The call had been a single-vehicle accident with two occupants. The driver had not survived. The passenger had been transported in stable condition. The call had been Caleb's first significant trauma. He had done what he was told. He had not made any errors that the captain had named. The captain had, in fact, pulled him aside at the end of shift and told him that he had done well.

The "done well" had landed weirdly in his chest. The captain was not someone who threw praise around. The praise had been specific. Caleb had registered the praise as data — the captain thinks I did well — but the data had not produced the relief he had expected. The data had, instead, produced a new question: what does "well" mean for a call where someone died.

The question had been with him for four days.

He had not asked anyone about it. He had not asked the captain to elaborate. He had not asked the other firefighters on the shift, both of whom had been to many of these calls before. He had not asked the chaplain, who had checked in once briefly. He had not asked his girlfriend, who knew he had been on a call but did not know any specifics and would not ask unless he offered.

He had taken the question into his own head and had begun, on his own, to try to answer it. The answering had taken the form of running the call again, looking for moments that would satisfy the "well" — moments where he had done something specifically correct, moments where his presence on the call had been a positive contribution, moments where the outcome would have been worse without him.

The answering had not produced the moments he was looking for. The moments he found were technical. They were small. They were correct. They did not feel, in his chest, like they added up to "well." The math of the call was that someone had died, and his small correct moments did not balance against the death, and the imbalance was the thing the body could not stop processing.

What he did not yet know, and what nobody had told him, is that "well" in this work does not mean "the outcome was good." "Well" means "you held your position, you did the work, you did not compromise the call." The captain's praise had been about Caleb's professional competence. The captain had not been making a metaphysical claim about the call. The captain had been doing a small specific act of mentoring, and the mentoring had been about Caleb's behavior, not about the call's outcome.

If someone had told Caleb this on Tuesday, the replay might have completed by Wednesday. The replay would not have ended — the call would still be a call he would carry — but the replay would have been able to file the "well" in the right folder. The folder is: things the captain said about my work. Not the folder: things the captain said about the meaning of the call.

Without that instruction, Caleb is on day four. The replay is still running. He has gotten about four hours of sleep across the last three nights. He has been quiet at home. His girlfriend has noticed but has not asked. He has been quiet on shift. The captain has noticed but has not asked.

This is what the work does to rookies who do not have the language yet for what is happening. The body does the work. The body asks for help. The help does not arrive, because the culture has not told anyone, in advance, what kind of help to ask for or to offer.

By day five, Caleb will probably be okay. The intensity of the replay will, on its own, begin to decrease. The body's metabolic limits are real. By day seven, the call will have settled into a more chronic background presence. By month two, the call will come up at intervals. By year five, the call will be one of perhaps a dozen calls that have settled into the long-term substrate of his nervous system.

None of this is necessary. With one conversation, on Tuesday, with someone who knew the language, the call would have been able to land in the right folder. The conversation did not happen. Caleb is doing the integration alone. The integration is slower and costlier than it had to be.

What this means is that the system has a gap in it. The gap is not in Caleb. The gap is in the absence of a structured peer-support conversation that the culture has not built in. Caleb's body is, in this sense, an indictment of a structural absence, not a personal failure. He does not know that yet. He will, if he stays long enough, probably figure it out, and probably try to be the person who has the conversation with the next rookie. That is how it propagates. Slowly. In whatever rookies survive long enough to become the senior person with the language.

Tomás has begun, in the last six months, to keep a list of the calls he is still replaying. The list is in the notes app on his phone. The list has a heading he typed once and has not edited since: "calls the loop is still running."

The list, six months in, has fourteen entries. Each entry is a short line — a date, a brief identifier, and a one-word state. The state is "active" if the loop is still running it. The state is "quieted" if the loop has settled. The state is "filed" if the loop has stopped bringing it up.

He had started the list because of a podcast he heard. The podcast had been an interview with a former Navy corpsman who had described his own list, kept for similar reasons, over a long career. The corpsman had said that the list was not a cure. The list was a venue. The venue was the smallest possible thing the corpsman had been able to build for himself, and the list had been the venue, and the venue had been adequate.

Tomás had thought about the podcast for two weeks. Then he had made the list.

The list has changed his relationship to the loops in a way he did not expect. The loops are still running. The loops are still arriving at the kitchen, the shower, the drive home. But when a loop arrives, Tomás can now do something specific. He can open the list. He can check whether the call is on it. If the call is on it, he can confirm that the call has been logged. If the call is not on it, he can add the call.

The act of opening the list, even when the call is already on it, does something the body finds useful. The body has been heard. The body has been registered. The body knows that there is a record. The record does not solve the call. The record changes the body's relationship to the call.

The loop, by month six, had stopped running on this call. The call had been filed in the body's archives. The call was still in the list, marked "filed." The list now contained, in its short notation, a record of a body's quiet processing over two years.

The list has thirteen other entries. Some will move to "quieted" eventually. Some will not. The list is not an algorithm. The list is a venue. The venue accepts what the body brings to it. The body brings what the body brings.

Tomás is four years in. He is going to be at this for a long time. The list will probably outlive his current phone. He will export it when he gets a new phone. The list will accumulate. The list will, over time, become a kind of record of his work — not the work that goes on the chart, but the work that goes on the body, in a register the chart has no field for.

This is what awareness looks like at year four. Not a cure. A list. A venue. A small daily practice with no name. The body, finally, with somewhere to put what the body had been carrying alone.

Aisha has been in therapy for fourteen months. She did not tell anyone at work. She still does not tell anyone at work. She tells some people in her personal life — her sister, her best friend from college, the partner she has been with for six years — but at work, the therapy is not on the record.

The therapy is once a week, at 17:30 on Tuesdays, in an office in a small medical building about twelve minutes from her precinct. The therapist is a woman in her late forties who specializes in first-responder populations. The therapist had been recommended by a peer support officer Aisha had met at a regional conference. The recommendation had been verbal, off the books, in a hallway, between sessions.

Aisha had thought about the recommendation for nine months before she had made the call. The call had taken her three tries to complete. The first two times, she had gotten to the phone number and had not pressed dial. The third time, she had pressed dial, and the therapist's intake voicemail had answered, and Aisha had left a brief message.

The therapist had called back the next day. They had scheduled an intake.

The intake had been awkward. Aisha had not known what to say. She had said the obvious things — thirteen years on the job, sergeant track, here because someone recommended you, not sure what to expect, not sure if this is for me. The therapist had been patient. The therapist had not pressed. The therapist had asked one question that Aisha had not been ready for: what does the body want you to come here for.

The body had answered. Aisha had not. The body had produced a small specific sensation in the chest, a tightening that she had recognized as the same tightening she had been getting at random hours for years. The therapist had said: that. That sensation. Let's start there.

They had started there. Fourteen months in, they were still, in some sense, there. The work had developed in directions Aisha had not anticipated. Some of the work had been about specific calls. Some of the work had been about her father, who had been a corrections officer and had not done his own work and had died at fifty-eight of a heart attack and had left behind a family that had not known how much of him had been on shift even at the dinner table. Some of the work had been about her own choice to go into the work, which she had thought of, for years, as a calling, and which she had begun to understand, with the therapist's help, as also having been a kind of inheritance.

The work in therapy had not made her job easier. The job was the same. The conditions were the same. The work in therapy had made the rumination, the replay, the cognitive overhead of carrying the calls home, somewhat lighter. The lightness was not measurable. The lightness was real.

What this means is that the rumination she had spent thirteen years carrying alone had not, in fact, needed to be carried alone. The carrying-alone had been a feature of the culture, not a feature of the work. The work itself produced calls that needed processing. The culture, separately, had decided that the processing was a private matter. The two were different. Aisha, by going to therapy, had not betrayed the work. She had separated the work from the culture, and had used a venue the culture did not provide, and had done so privately because the culture was not yet ready to be told about it.

She will, perhaps, in the next few years, become a sergeant. If she does, she will be a sergeant who has been in therapy for the better part of three years. She will not lead with that. She will not announce it. She will, when she is in a position to make small structural changes, suggest things that would help — a contracted peer support program, a list of vetted therapists, an annual confidential check-in for senior officers. She will suggest these things because she will know, from her own work, that the alternative is what she has been doing alone for thirteen years, and what other officers around her are still doing alone, on their own kitchens, in their own quiet hours.

She is not yet at the position where the suggestions can be made. She is, for now, a sergeant-track officer who goes to therapy on Tuesdays at 17:30 and who does not tell anyone at work. The not-telling is, for now, the price of having a venue. She is willing to pay the price. She is also keeping a small, private list of the structural changes she would like, eventually, to be in a position to advocate for. The list is in the notes app on her phone, next to the list of calls the body is still working on. The two lists are related. The relation is not yet operational. The relation is, at the level of her body, fully understood.

This is what self-claim looks like at year thirteen. Not a public statement. A private commitment. A weekly appointment. A therapist whose name is in a contact list that does not have a label. A practice that is changing, in slow and small ways, the work the body has to do alone. The aloneness is decreasing. The decreasing is the point.

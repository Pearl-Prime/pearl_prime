He was on the third loop of the same call by 23:14. The call had cleared at 19:42. The intervening three and a half hours had been the dinner that he did not taste, the conversation with his daughter that he did not fully attend, and a shower he could not remember the temperature of. All of it had been the substrate for the replay, which was running in the background of every other task.

The replay was, in his head, a debrief. He was telling himself this was professional review. He was being responsible. He was being thorough. Each loop he tried to extract a lesson from the call so the next call would go better. The lesson kept not arriving, because the lesson he was looking for was not actually about the call. The lesson he was looking for was an absolution the call could not provide.

The call had gone clean by every external measure. The patient had been transported. The vitals were stable on arrival at the ED. The partner had said, on the way back to the station, that the run had been textbook. The captain had nodded at intake. There was no incident report to write because no incident had occurred. By every available metric, the call was finished.

The body did not have a metric. The body had its experience of the eleven minutes of the call, and the body's experience did not match the metrics. There had been a moment, around minute six, when his read of the scene had not aligned with his partner's read, and he had deferred, and the deferral had been correct in retrospect, and the deferral was now what the loop kept circling. Not because the deferral was wrong. Because the deferral had been a moment where his judgment and his partner's had pulled in different directions, and the body did not know how to file that without a longer review than the debrief had allowed.

By 02:47 he was on the kitchen floor doing pull-ups on the doorframe. Not because he was trying to exercise. Because his body needed to do something with the energy the loop had generated and was not discharging. The pull-ups were a substitute for the somatic release the loop had been requiring for hours. The pull-ups would not solve the loop. The pull-ups would buy him forty minutes of being too tired to run the loop, and forty minutes of not-loop was the first installment of recovery the night had on offer.

## STORY v22

She had been on the same call for eight years. Not literally. Operationally, on other shifts, the calls had been different. But there was one call that the body had not let go of, and the loop for that call had been running, on and off, for eight years, in the background of every other call she had run since.

The call was not even the worst call she had run. It was a call from her first year on the rig, when she was twenty-three and new and trying very hard to do the work correctly. The call had not gone wrong. The call had gone almost-right. There had been a small thing she had not done — a phrase she had not said to the patient's husband, a hand she had not put on the husband's shoulder, a moment where she had been so focused on the technical work that the relational work had been less than the call had asked for.

Nobody had told her that the relational work was insufficient. Nobody had needed to. The husband had thanked her. The patient had been stable on transport. The call had been a win by the official scoring. But she had walked away from the call with a small unfinished feeling in her chest, and the small unfinished feeling had taken up residence and had been there ever since.

For eight years, every time she ran a call where the relational work was the thing, the loop from the original call would activate. She would run the new call cleanly — the technical was never the problem — and underneath the new call the original call would be running, asking again what she could have done differently with the husband, asking again if her presence had been less than what the family had needed.

The point is that the loop was not pathology. The loop was a system trying to teach her something she had not been able to learn on the original scene, because the original scene had not allowed the learning. The learning required time, reflection, and a level of metacognition that the rookie-year nervous system had not yet developed. The learning had been deferred to a later date that the deferral had never quite arrived at.

In year nine, she sat at a conference and a senior medic she did not know described, from the panel, a call almost exactly like hers. The senior medic described the same small unfinished feeling. The senior medic was at year twenty-four. She listened and felt something in her chest soften by a small amount. Not because the loop had ended. Because the loop had been heard. The hearing was not from her. The hearing was from a stranger who had named the loop's existence in public, and the public naming had given the loop somewhere to land that was not just her own kitchen.

The loop did not stop after the conference. The loop kept running, on its own schedule, for several more years. But the loop had less weight in it after the conference. The weight had been the aloneness. The aloneness had been broken by a senior medic she would never meet again. That breaking was not a fix. It was a kind of company. The company was what the loop had needed for the eight years it had been running.

## STORY v23

He had begun keeping a list. Not in his head. On a piece of paper. On the side of the refrigerator. The list had a heading he had written in pencil: calls the loop is running. Underneath the heading, he had been writing, over the past eleven months, a one-line entry for each call the loop kept circling.

The list had thirty-four entries by month eleven. Some entries were old. The oldest was from his second year on the job. Some entries were recent. The most recent was from last Tuesday. Each entry had a date and a one-line description and nothing else. He was not writing the calls out in detail. He was not analyzing them. He was just naming them.

The naming was the practice. He had figured out, by the third month of the list, that the loop ran harder on the calls that did not have names. The unnamed calls had a kind of formless weight that could attach to any quiet hour. The named calls had borders. The borders were the list.

By month eleven, the loop was still running. The list had not made the loop quit. What the list had done was something subtler. The list had given the loop an address. When the loop arrived in the kitchen at 02:47, he could now go to the refrigerator, look at the list, and see whether the call the loop was running was already on the list. If it was, he could let the loop run for a few minutes and then say, inwardly: yes, that call is on the list. We have its address. We can stop running it in this hour and pick it up again on the next debrief day.

The naming did not solve the loop. The naming changed the loop's relationship to time. The loop had been treating every 02:47 as if it were the only available venue for the work. The list told the loop that there were other venues — daylight, structured time, a notebook, a conversation with the chaplain who came through the station on Thursdays. The loop, slowly, accepted the rerouting. Not for every call. Not on every night. But often enough that he was now getting three to five hours of unbroken sleep on nights he had been getting one or two for years.

This is what practice looks like after a year of practice. Not a quiet mind. A mind that knows where to put the loop when the loop arrives. The list is on the refrigerator. The loop has an address. The address is not silence. It is somewhere the loop can go that is not the entire kitchen at 02:47.

Not resolution. A different relationship to the noise.

## INTEGRATION v01
---
---
Tomorrow, don't try to overhaul how present you are for twelve hours — that's not a real target and you'll feel like you failed by 10 a.m. Pick one single scene-sized cue instead: a hand on the rig door, one breath, the word "here" said silently, whatever fits your hands and your unit. Use it at every single call you run, not just the hard ones, not just when you remember — every one, so it has a chance to become automatic instead of one more thing to remember under pressure. Some calls it'll feel pointless, redundant, a beat wasted when you're already behind. Do it anyway, especially there, because those are exactly the calls where you're most likely to be somewhere else. By the end of the shift you won't feel like a different responder. You're not trying to. You're just trying to bank a few dozen small returns, each one cheap, each one real, so that a week from now the return costs you less than it does today.
---

## INTEGRATION v02
---
---
Carry this into your next shift as a single, boring habit: attach a "here now" beat to something you already do at every call, no exceptions — gloving up, a radio check, the rig door closing. Not a new task, just a half-second of attention riding on a motion you were making anyway. When you're behind, when the calls are stacked, when a family's waiting for an update — especially then — don't skip it to save time, because the two seconds it costs you is smaller than the time you lose later reconstructing a conversation you weren't present for the first time. Expect it to feel mechanical at first, maybe even silly. That's fine.
---

## INTEGRATION v03
---
---
Take one thing into tomorrow's shift: when you catch your mind three calls away, don't spend energy being annoyed at yourself for it. Just come back — one breath, one look at what's actually in front of you — and count it as a successful rep, not a failure you need to explain. You will do this many times. Possibly dozens. That's not a sign the practice isn't working; dozens of returns is exactly what a full, chaotic shift looks like when you're actually doing the work instead of drifting unnoticed. Pick one recurring moment as your anchor point if it helps — maybe every time you glove up, or every time the radio squawks — and use it as a built-in prompt to check where your attention actually is.
---

## INTEGRATION v04
---
---
Going into your next shift, drop the idea that you need a lighter call volume or a calmer unit before presence becomes possible — that day isn't coming, and waiting for it just means never starting. Instead, pick the single hardest call type for you, the one where you know you drift the most (a post-code debrief, a difficult family), and put your return cue there deliberately, on purpose, before you even step out of the rig. Name the last call you were on, out loud in your head, then step onto the scene with your attention actually reset. If the loop comes back mid-call — the supervisor's comment, the last patient's face — don't fight it. Park it, plainly: "that's real, I'll deal with it later," and redirect back to the person in front of you.
---

## INTEGRATION v05
---
---
Try this tomorrow: at the top of every hour, wherever you are and whatever you're mid-task on, ask yourself silently, "am I actually here, or three calls over?" Don't judge the answer. Most hours, honestly, the answer will be the second one, and that's simply accurate, not a failure. The value isn't in getting a good answer — it's in the asking itself, which functions as a return point even on hours where you were fully checked out until that exact second. When the honest answer is "I'm not here," use whatever's physically nearest as your landing spot — the patient's hand, the sound in the rig, the weight of your radio — and let that be enough to bring you back into the actual minute you're in.
---

## INTEGRATION v06
---
---
Bring one small adjustment into your next stretch of shifts: whenever a call follows you off scene — a patient's face, an unanswered question, a bad number on the monitor — give it an explicit placeholder instead of either fighting it or letting it run silently underneath everything else. Something as plain as, mentally, "finishing that report at the top of the hour" is enough. This isn't about resolving the thing right now, which usually isn't possible. It's about telling the part of you that's still standing on that street that it's been heard and it can stand down until the time you named.
---

## INTEGRATION v07
---
---
Take this into tomorrow specifically for your hardest interactions — the direct, vulnerable questions patients or families ask when they actually want to know something, not just make conversation. "Am I going to be okay?" "What does this mean?" "Why is this taking so long?" In exactly those moments, build in a half-second stop before you answer: stop your hands, stop your feet, let the question actually land before the automatic reply comes out. You will be tempted to answer fast, on reflex, especially when you're behind — that's precisely when the automatic answer is most likely to miss what's actually being asked.
---

## INTEGRATION v08
---
---
For your next shift, borrow a version of the "okay, here" habit: pick a short, silent phrase and say it to yourself at every scene, before you step out of the rig, no matter how routine the call feels. It will feel strange and a little performative the first dozen times — say it anyway. The point isn't the words. It's giving your attention a consistent, tiny signal that this call is starting now, distinct from whatever call you just left. Don't reserve it for the calls that feel important; use it for the routine transport too, because those are exactly the calls where autopilot is most likely to take over completely.
---

## INTEGRATION v09
---
---
Bring this into a shift where you know call volume will be heavy: instead of trying to hold all your calls in your head simultaneously — which is what the anxious version of "staying on top of things" looks like — practice closing each call fully before opening the next, even under pressure, even for ten seconds after you clear. That means, half a step past the rig door, pausing to silently finish one sentence about what actually just happened on that call, before letting your mind move to the next task on your list.
---

## INTEGRATION v10
---
---
Take one experiment into your next rough patch of shifts: stop aiming to feel calm, and aim instead for accuracy — attention pointed at what's actually real right now, stress and all. Each time you step onto a scene, ask yourself only this: is my attention actually on what's in front of me, or is it on a call that already happened or one that hasn't happened yet? If it's elsewhere, point it here, without waiting to feel less stressed first, because that feeling may not arrive all shift.
---

## INTEGRATION v11
---
---
For the next week, run a small, low-effort experiment: on your worst shift, instead of tracking how present you felt, just count your returns — literally tally, even just in your head, every time you notice you'd drifted and brought yourself back. Don't try to reduce the number. A high number on a hard shift isn't failure; it's what active practice looks like under real load, as opposed to a low number that might just mean you didn't notice most of your drifting at all.
---

## INTEGRATION v12
---
---
Carry one plain instruction into tomorrow: whatever cue you pick — a breath, a word, a hand on the rig door — use it at every threshold, every single call, all shift, without exception, even the calls that feel too routine or too rushed to bother. The exceptions are exactly where the habit fails to form, because the mind that skips the boring calls is the same mind that will find an excuse on the hard ones too. You don't need to feel different by lunch. You're trying to rack up a large, boring number of tiny, real returns.

## STORY v01
---
---
Marcus has been a medic for nine years and he still remembers the shift he figured out what "here" actually meant. It was a slow Tuesday, a routine transport, nothing critical, and he caught himself sitting beside a patient with his hand on her wrist for a pulse check, mentally already at the hospital rehearsing the handoff report. The patient, an older woman named Ruth, said his name twice before he heard her. "You're not here," she said, not unkindly, just noticing. He apologized, finished the check, and it needled him the rest of the shift — not because anything went wrong, but because he couldn't say what she'd actually said to him, whether she'd seemed scared. He'd been present in body for four minutes and absent for all of it. That shift he started doing something small: before touching a patient, he'd name one thing in the rig — the color of the blanket, the sound of the engine — just to plant himself there for a second before his hands started working. It didn't fix his call volume. It didn't make the shift shorter. But months later he noticed he could describe his patients again, actual people instead of task lists, and that felt like getting something back he hadn't known he'd lost.

## STORY v02
---
---
Denise almost missed it, the morning she grabbed the wrong med from the bag. Not an error that reached the patient — she caught it before it went anywhere — just a half-second where her hands moved on autopilot while her mind was still finishing a fight she'd had with her spouse that morning. She stood there, blinking, actually seeing the bag for the first time since she'd opened it. She told her partner about it at the station, expecting sympathy. Instead her partner said something that stuck: "Your hands don't need your mind to work. That's the problem. You can do this whole job without ever showing up for it." Denise sat with that for weeks. She started using the back doors of the rig as a kind of trigger — one breath, hand on the handle, before she climbed in. Not a ritual, not slow, just a beat where she let the last call actually end before the next one began. She never caught another near-miss like it, not because she got more careful about meds, but because she stopped arriving three seconds after her own hands did.

## STORY v03
---
---
Priya used to think being a good medic meant carrying every call with her all shift — the cardiac arrest from Tuesday, the family's grief, the call from last month that didn't go the way anyone wanted. She wore it like proof she cared. What she noticed, eventually, was that carrying all of it meant she wasn't fully arriving for any single patient; she was doing five half-jobs instead of one whole job, five times. The turn came during a normal shift, documenting at the station, when a rookie asked her a simple question and she had to ask him to repeat it twice. He wasn't annoyed. He just looked at her funny. That look was the thing that landed, more than any lecture would have. She started testing something almost embarrassingly small: at the start of each call, she'd silently name where she'd just been — "that was the arrest on Fifth" — as a way of closing the door behind her before opening the next one. It wasn't dramatic. Nobody else noticed. But patients started saying "you seem present today" more, and she realized she'd been quietly leaving the scene even when her boots said she was in it.

## STORY v04
---
---
Kimberly had a family member ask her, gently, "Are you okay?" in the middle of an update about her father's condition. She wasn't visibly distracted — she was standing still, making eye contact, saying the right words — but something in her voice must have been thin, because the daughter picked up on the gap between the words and the person saying them. Kimberly went home that shift turning it over. She realized she'd given that family her sentences but not her attention; she was already halfway to the next dispatch in her head while her mouth finished the update. She started, the next week, doing one thing differently before family updates: she'd physically stop walking, plant her feet, for the two or three seconds before she opened her mouth. It sounds like nothing. It changed how the conversations felt to her, though — less like a task she was executing and more like a person she was actually talking to. Families stopped asking if she was okay. Not because she'd fixed her call volume, but because she'd stopped trying to be in two places while occupying one.

## STORY v05
---
---
Yolanda counted it once, out of curiosity — how many times a shift her mind left a call before her body did. She stopped counting around forty. It wasn't a crisis, just a pattern: turn to leave the scene, already planning the next call, gone before the doors closed. The moment that changed things wasn't dramatic. She was leaving a call mid-thought about the next dispatch, and the patient said, quietly, "thank you for today," and Yolanda realized she almost hadn't heard it — one more sentence lost to a mind that had already left. She started practicing a strange little habit: touching the rig door on the way out of every call, just a light two fingers, a signal to herself that this call was actually over now. It wasn't about slowing down. She still moved at the same pace. But she started catching the last sentence in every call instead of losing it to the next one, and something in her felt less scraped-out by the end of shifts, even the bad ones.

## STORY v06
---
---
Rosa's captain used to say something that annoyed her: "wherever your feet are." Rosa thought it was a poster-quote, empty advice for people who'd never worked a real shift. Then she had a call where she gave the handoff report on a patient and couldn't remember, ten minutes later, whether she'd mentioned the medication given — because she'd given that report while already mentally restocking the next call's supplies. She had to go back and check her own run sheet to be sure. That gap scared her more than any code ever had. She started an experiment: for one full shift, every time she caught her mind three calls away, she just noted it — not judged it, just noted "there it goes" — and brought her attention back to her actual hands, her actual patient, her actual task. By call four she noticed the noting itself took less effort; the return was getting quicker, almost reflexive. The captain's line stopped sounding like a poster. It started sounding like the whole job.

## STORY v07
---
---
Beatriz used to pride herself on multitasking — three trains of thought running during a call, catching details from the radio at the same time. She thought this was skill. Then a field training officer watching her shadow pointed out that she'd repeated a vital sign wrong during handoff, not because she didn't know it, but because she'd been listening to the ER nurse while simultaneously composing her own opening line for the next section of the report. She'd been performing attention, not paying it. It stung enough that she tried something new: during handoff, she put her pen down between sections for one breath before picking it back up to write the next part. Just a pause. The information she retained afterward — genuinely retained, not just transcribed — went up enough that she noticed it within a week. She hadn't gotten smarter. She'd stopped asking her mind to be in two places during the two minutes when it needed to be in one.

## STORY v08
---
---
Naomi worked a stretch of nights so packed that she started bragging, a little, about how much she could hold at once — six calls, a trainee to supervise, the chief texting about scheduling. Then a patient asked her a direct question, "Do you think I'm going to be okay?", and Naomi answered too fast, on reflex, the way you answer when you weren't really listening to the question underneath the question. The patient's face changed, just slightly, and Naomi knew she'd been caught being somewhere else. She didn't overhaul her whole night. She just started, in that specific kind of moment — a direct question, a vulnerable one — physically stopping what her hands were doing before she answered. Two seconds. It gave her just enough room to actually hear the question instead of pattern-matching to the nearest reply. She started catching those moments more, not fewer of them coming, just fewer of them sliding past her unnoticed.

## STORY v09
---
---
Corinne had a bad call on her shift that didn't involve her unit, technically — she was two blocks away, uninvolved, safe. But for the rest of that shift she kept finding herself at scenes with her hands doing the motions and her mind still on that street. She didn't say anything to anyone; it felt indulgent to be shaken by something that wasn't even hers. At the end of the shift, driving home, she realized she couldn't remember details of the last two calls she'd run — she'd been present enough to not make an error, absent enough to lose the people. The next shift, she tried something on purpose: whenever she noticed she was still standing on that street in her head, she named it, silently — "that already happened" — and named one true thing about the call she was actually on. It took repeating it more times than she expected. But by the end of the week the street let go of her faster each time, not because the call stopped mattering, but because she'd given her mind a place to put it down.

## STORY v10
---
---
Tanya's kids used to complain that she was "there but not there" on her days off, and she brushed it off as normal tiredness. Then a coworker mentioned, offhand, that Tanya had walked past her twice in the station without seeing her, mid-shift, eyes open, clearly elsewhere. Tanya laughed it off at work and thought about it more than she let on. She started running a small check for herself: at the top of every hour, whatever she was doing, she'd ask silently, "am I actually here right now, or three calls over?" Most of the time the honest answer was the second one. She didn't try to force herself present all day — that felt impossible with her call volume. She just used the hourly check as a return point, a chance to come back for the next call even if she'd drifted for the last one. Her kids started saying she seemed more herself at home, which surprised her, since nothing about her schedule had changed.

## STORY v11
---
---
Angela used to think presence was something you either had or didn't, a personality trait some medics were born calm enough to hold and others weren't. She'd watched a colleague named Frank move through a chaotic shift looking completely unbothered and assumed he was just wired differently. She asked him once, half-joking, what his secret was. He said, "I lose it all the time. I just come back faster than I used to." That answer annoyed her because it was so unglamorous. But she tried it: instead of aiming to stay focused all shift, which felt impossible, she just aimed to notice faster when she'd left, and return faster when she did. The first week it felt like no improvement at all — she still drifted constantly. By the third week, the drifting hadn't stopped, but the noticing had gotten almost instant, and the return took a breath instead of a whole call. She stopped judging the drifting. She started timing the return.

## STORY v12
---
---
Marcus's field training officer once told him something during orientation that he didn't understand until years later: "You will never stop your mind from wandering to the next call. Don't try. Just get good at coming back." At the time it sounded like a consolation prize, an admission that the job would always feel scattered. Years in, working a bad stretch of back-to-back calls, he found himself testing it without meaning to — catching himself gone, and instead of spiraling about how gone he'd been, just turning back to the patient in front of him, again, and again, sometimes eleven times in one call. What surprised him was that the eleventh return still worked. It wasn't a skill that wore out. Each time he came back, the patient in front of him got the same fresh attention, no matter how many times he'd left. He stopped feeling like a failure for wandering. He started feeling like someone building a rep count.

## STORY v13
---
---
Yolanda's shift got a mass-casualty call at the same time as a routine transport was trying to wrap, and for ten minutes she was pulled so many directions that she felt, physically, like she was standing in four places and none of them. A newer medic asked her, wide-eyed, "how do you not lose your mind doing this?" Yolanda almost gave a canned answer, then actually thought about it and said, "I don't not lose it. I just have a way of finding it again fast." She showed the newer medic the thing she does — one hand flat on the rig door, one breath, name the call out loud in her head. The newer medic tried it that same shift, reported back that it felt silly and small. Yolanda told her that was the point; it wasn't supposed to feel like a big fix, because it wasn't fixing the chaos. It was just giving her a two-second place to land in the middle of it.

## STORY v14
---
---
Denise once worked with a medic who never seemed rattled, and for years she assumed the woman simply had fewer calls or an easier shift. It wasn't until they worked a brutal night side by side that Denise saw the truth: same chaos, same radio traffic, same short staffing — the difference was that the woman kept catching herself and coming back, out loud sometimes, muttering "okay, here" under her breath before every call. It looked strange from outside. It worked. Denise adopted the mutter herself, feeling foolish the first few times, saying "okay, here" to an empty rig before rolling on the next call. Within a couple of weeks the phrase had worn a groove into her habits, faster than any of her other efforts to "be more mindful" had ever managed, mostly because it cost nothing and took no extra time — she said it while she was already moving.

## STORY v15
---
---
Beatriz's turning point had nothing to do with a patient. It was a lieutenant's comment, three words long, tossed off during a debrief — "this took forever" — that lodged in her and played on a loop for the rest of the shift, showing up every time she stood still for more than a few seconds. She caught herself, hours later, replaying it during a call instead of listening to her patient describe new chest pain, and the mismatch scared her more than the lieutenant's comment ever could have. She started, after that shift, treating the looping comment like a radio call she couldn't answer yet — acknowledging it was there, telling herself she'd deal with it later, and turning back to the patient. It took returning to that same patient, that same conversation, six or seven times in ten minutes before the loop finally quieted. She didn't resolve the comment. She just stopped letting it stand at the bedside instead of her.

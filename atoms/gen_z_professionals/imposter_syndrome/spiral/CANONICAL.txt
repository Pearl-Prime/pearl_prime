Alex sits in the standup, and someone asks a technical question. Alex doesn't know the answer. The chest gets tight immediately—not from the question, but from the knowing that not knowing was just observed. Seven people in the room. Four of them definitely know the answer. One of them is the VP. Alex offers a half-answer and moves forward. The question sits in the body for the rest of the meeting. During lunch, Alex pulls up documentation, finds the answer in sixty seconds. It was simple. The thing about the spiral is it starts with just this: the small gap between the knowledge and the moment it was exposed. Alex wasn't supposed to have that gap. By this point in the career, that gap shouldn't exist. The chest stays tight through the afternoon.

Alex does the work anyway. The spiral is running, the thought patterns are catastrophizing, but the sprint has tickets that need closing. Alex works through the anxiety. The code gets written. The code gets reviewed. The reviewer approves it. Then approves the next thing. Then the next. Three weeks of approval. Nothing breaks. No one says: we hired you by accident.

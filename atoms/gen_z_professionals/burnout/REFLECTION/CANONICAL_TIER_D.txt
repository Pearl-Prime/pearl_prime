## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
There's a threshold you cross in burnout where you realize that no optimization will help. You'd need to change the entire structure. And once you know that, you can't unknow it. But you stay anyway. The mechanism keeping me here isn't ignorance. It's paralysis. It's the clarity of the situation combined with the weight of my own practical constraints. I see the cliff I'm walking toward. That doesn't seem to stop my feet.
---

## REFLECTION v03
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
What strikes me is how much of my bandwidth goes to noticing the problem instead of changing it. I've become fluent in my own breakdown. I can identify which defense I'm using before it fully activates. I can name the psychological principle. And somehow this has become another form of perfectionism. I'm analyzing my own analysis. I'm treating awareness itself as another benchmark where I might fail. There's something almost comedic about it.
---

## REFLECTION v04
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: inherited
---
I inherited a consciousness from the internet where I'm always partly watching myself perform. There's a version of me living the moment and a version curating it, analyzing it, processing how it appears to invisible audiences. This split doesn't feel like a disorder anymore. It feels like baseline consciousness. But there's a cost. The part of me that's watching never fully arrives anywhere. I'm always maintaining distance.
---

## REFLECTION v05
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
My generation is the first to experience anxiety while fully understanding what anxiety is. We know the mechanism.
---

## REFLECTION v06
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
The burnout started small. Missed deadline. Project that took longer. Expectation reset. And then the system just kept going. Things stacked. Demands accumulated. At some point I passed a threshold where recovery is no longer possible within the existing structure. I would need to fundamentally change how I work, what I do, where I do it. Once you know you've crossed that threshold, you can't unknow it. Everything changes. And yet I stay.
---

## REFLECTION v07
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
I'm operating in a different mode now. It's not that I'm doing less work. If anything, I'm doing more. But I'm doing it mechanically, like I've stepped outside myself and I'm watching someone else go through the motions while I narrate from a distance. This is what burnout doesn't look like in the brochures. It doesn't always look like collapse. Sometimes it looks like optimization. Like finally getting your systems down so well that you can run on fumes indefinitely.
---

## REFLECTION v08
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: inherited
---
What strikes me is how the system is designed to reward the very behaviors that deplete you. Go faster. Care harder. Be more. And if you can't sustain it, the problem isn't the system—it's your resilience, your boundaries, your ability to optimize yourself further. The mechanism here is almost elegant in its cruelty: you're not breaking because you're doing too much. You're breaking because you're not doing it right. I've internalized this completely. My depletion feels like personal failing.
---

## REFLECTION v09
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
The burnout has a rhythm now. I can predict when it hits. Mid-week. Late afternoon. Sunday evening. Times when the gap between what I'm capable of and what's being asked of me becomes impossible to ignore. In those moments I understand with absolute clarity that nothing I do will fix this. I would need to change everything. But then the moment passes. Something shifts. And I'm back to believing it's manageable. The mechanism is episodic burnout awareness.
---

## REFLECTION v10
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
I think about the people who left. Who actually set a boundary. Who actually stopped. And I'm genuinely uncertain whether that took courage or something else. Whether they figured something out that I haven't. Or whether they were just less able to dissociate from their own depletion. I'm good at not feeling things. I'm good at compartmentalizing. I'm good at converting exhaustion into productivity. These skills served me well in school. They're probably killing me now.
---

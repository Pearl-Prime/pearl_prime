

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v01
---
mode: SECULAR_BRIDGE
doctrine_source: somatic
mechanism: false_alarm
weight: standard
carry_line: "Burnout is the alarm system stuck on. Cortisol that should reset between Mondays no longer resets at all."
---
Here is the mechanism. Your stress response is not designed to run continuously. It is designed to fire, do its job, and reset. The reset is supposed to happen on the weekend, between projects, on the walk home. When the reset stops happening, what was a useful alarm becomes a stuck signal. The body keeps producing the chemicals it would produce in front of an actual threat, only now the threat is a Monday standup, then a Tuesday standup, then a Wednesday OKR check-in, then another Monday. The alarm system does not care that none of these are emergencies. It only knows it has not been allowed to come down. This is what burnout looks like before it has a name. You are still hitting your deliverables. You are still showing up to the 1:1. But the recovery curve that used to bring you back to baseline by Sunday night does not bring you back anymore. Naming this matters because the response is different. A stuck alarm does not need more effort. It needs the conditions that allow a reset, which is closer to nothing than to more.

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v02
---
mode: SECULAR_BRIDGE
doctrine_source: somatic
mechanism: spiral
weight: standard
carry_line: "The depletion-prediction loop runs forward and finds you already empty. The forecast borrows fatigue you have not earned yet."
---
Here is the mechanism. When the system is depleted, the prediction loop changes shape. Instead of forecasting outcomes, it starts forecasting your capacity to meet them. You see the calendar for next week and the loop runs: five meetings on Tuesday, the deck due Wednesday, the OKR check-in Thursday, and the loop arrives at next Friday already exhausted. You have not done any of it yet. The exhaustion is borrowed from a future you have not lived. What makes this loop dangerous is that the borrowed fatigue is indistinguishable from real fatigue once it lands in your body. You feel tired now, on Sunday night, for work you have not done yet. The tiredness becomes evidence that you cannot handle next week, which feeds the loop, which produces more borrowed fatigue. The exit is not motivation. The exit is recognizing the loop is running, and noticing that you are not actually doing Tuesday's meetings on Sunday night. You are doing Sunday night. The work next week will get its own response when it arrives.

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v03
---
mode: SECULAR_BRIDGE
doctrine_source: somatic
mechanism: comparison
weight: standard
carry_line: "Measuring your output against a LinkedIn feed is comparing your interior to other people's edited highlights."
---
Here is the mechanism. The comparison engine, in burnout conditions, gets cruel. It does not just track relative position. It uses other people's visible output as a measure of your invisible interior. You scroll LinkedIn at 11 pm and see a former classmate posting about shipping a major launch, three weeks of late nights, the team that pulled it off. You read this through the filter of your own current state, which is a depleted nervous system that has not recovered from last quarter. The engine does the subtraction. They did the late nights. You can barely do your normal hours. Therefore something is wrong with you. What the engine is missing is that you cannot see their nervous system, only their post. You cannot see what they are paying for the launch behind the announcement, the weekend they spent flat on the couch, the conversation with their partner that did not make it to the feed. The comparison takes their output as the full picture and your fatigue as a personal verdict. Both readings are wrong. Recognizing the asymmetry is the first cut against the engine.

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v04
---
mode: SECULAR_BRIDGE
doctrine_source: somatic
mechanism: overwhelm
weight: standard
carry_line: "The context-switch tax across eight Slack channels is the part of your day no one is paying you for."
---
Here is the mechanism. Every time your attention moves from one channel to another, there is a tax. The brain does not switch cleanly. It carries residue from the last context into the next one, and uses cycles to drop the residue and load the new context. In a quiet job, the tax is invisible. In a job with eight active Slack channels, two threads in the engineering room, a Notion comment, a 1:1 in twenty minutes, and a deck due Friday, the tax compounds across hundreds of switches a day. By 4 pm you have done less actual work than the calendar suggests, and you are more tired than the work explains, because most of the day went to the switching. This is not laziness or distraction. It is the predictable cost of an attention architecture that did not evolve for this volume of inputs. Burnout, in this register, is partly the unpaid bill on the context-switch tax. Seeing the tax for what it is gives you something to defend, which is the difference between an inbox-shaped day and a deep-work-shaped day.

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v05
---
mode: SECULAR_BRIDGE
doctrine_source: somatic
mechanism: shame
weight: standard
carry_line: "The secret belief is that you are the only one who cannot handle the job. The secrecy is what makes the belief survive."
---
Here is the mechanism. Burnout shame has a specific shape. It is the conviction that other people in your role are handling the same workload without coming apart, and that your inability to keep up is a private deficiency you must hide. So you hide it. You decline the question "how are you really" with a fine. You schedule a vacation but treat it as a performance issue you are taking time to fix. The hiding is the part that closes the loop. Because no one in your cohort sees how depleted you are, you also do not see how depleted they are. Each of you, separately, concludes that you are the only one. Each of you, separately, performs okay-ness for the others. Shame survives in the gap that hiding builds. Naming the mechanism does not make the workload sustainable, but it does interrupt the part where you treat the depletion as evidence of your defect rather than as a predictable response to a job that is asking more of nervous systems than nervous systems can give. The depletion is data. The hiding is what makes the data feel like shame.

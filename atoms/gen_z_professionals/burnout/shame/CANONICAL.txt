Alex opens the Slack message at 11 PM. Not urgent. Just a request about tomorrow's sprint planning. The shoulders tense immediately — not because the message is heavy, but because it's another thing, and the capacity counter is already at zero. Alex reads it three times. Each read tightens the chest a little more. The thought arrives unbidden: everyone else is managing this. They're handling the same sprint, same requests, same pace. The difference is Alex is here falling apart at the sight of a message while they're apparently functioning. The shame arrives clean and immediate — not about the burnout itself, but about the reaction to it. Too fragile. Too sensitive. Everyone else takes it in stride. This is what weakness looks like from the inside.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v01
---
path: story_atoms/gen_z_professionals/anchored/burnout/shame/mechanism_proof/micro/v01.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Alex notices the pattern across three months of Slack messages. Every request makes the shoulders tense. Every ping makes the hands cold. But when Alex actually works through the requests, they're manageable. The threat response is firing constantly. The chest is tight about things that aren't actually threats. This is the moment the shame mechanism clarifies: the burnout itself isn't the shameful part — it's the visible signs of burnout that feel shameful. Alex is ashamed of being the person who reacts this intensely. Ashamed of the shoulders tensing. Ashamed of being seen as the person who can't handle the normal pace that everyone else handles. The mechanism works like this: burnout creates a nervous system that's exhausted and overactive simultaneously. That overactivity produces visible signs — tension, reactivity, emotional lability. Those signs are exposed to colleagues. The exposure triggers shame. The shame makes Alex hide the burnout deeper, which means not asking for support, not pacing differently, not changing anything. The burnout stays constant but now it's paired with the exhaustion of hiding it. Double load. The shame isn't a response to burnout — it's a mechanism that keeps the burnout locked in place.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02
---

BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal

---
Priya understands now why she told the supervisor she's fine. Why she clocked back in after fifteen minutes in the supply closet. Why she comes back tomorrow despite hands that don't work reliably. The mechanism is: burnout makes the body visible. Shaking hands are visible. Tears are visible. Not finishing tasks on time is visible. The visibility of burnout becomes evidence of failure. Priya could say to anyone at the clinic "the pace is unsustainable" and they'd likely agree. It's a structural problem. But Priya can't say it because saying it means admitting to being the person who's visibly failing under conditions that others manage. So instead Priya hides the shaking hands, wipes away the tears, and tries to move faster despite the body literally unable to move faster. The shame is about being seen as the weak link. Not about the actual unsustainability of the job — about being the visible evidence that some people can't hack it. The mechanism locks in: keep hiding the collapse until the collapse is complete and irreversible. Then maybe no one will blame you for failing, because the failure will look sudden instead of gradual.

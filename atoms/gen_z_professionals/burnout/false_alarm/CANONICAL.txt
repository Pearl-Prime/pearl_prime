Sam gets an email from the client. Neutral subject line. Neutral content. A question about timeline for the next deliverable. The heart rate spikes. The chest tightens. The hands go cold. For a moment Sam's nervous system is firing a full threat response — danger, exposure, failure. Then the rational mind catches up: this is a normal email. The question is routine. Nothing is actually wrong. But Sam has already spent energy overriding the alarm response, talking the body down from a threat that doesn't exist. By the time Sam responds to the email calmly, Sam is exhausted. The pattern is becoming familiar. Normal things trigger emergency responses. Sam has to work twice as hard as normal work because half the energy is spent convincing the body that it's safe. The freelance pace is already demanding — juggling multiple clients, uncertain income. Adding the constant false alarms on top of it makes every day feel like it's at maximum intensity even on days that are objectively manageable.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v01

BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Sam starts to understand the mechanism. When there's enough demand on the system — enough uncertainty, enough pressure, enough ambiguity — the nervous system stops distinguishing between real threats and potential threats. It defaults to alarm. The freelance work is objectively uncertain. Income is variable. Client needs shift. There are real reasons to stay alert. But the nervous system has amplified the alertness to the point where even routine communication triggers the full alarm response. The mechanism is self-reinforcing: uncertainty → nervous system stays activated → every small thing registers as emergency → person has to constantly work to calm the alarm → calming the alarm requires energy that's not available because it's being used to deliver the work itself → person becomes more depleted → with more depletion, the nervous system sensitivity increases → less signal is needed to trigger the alarm. The false alarms aren't random. They're the nervous system's best guess about what a survival-level threat might look like in a situation that's perpetually uncertain. The mechanism doesn't need Sam's rational mind to agree. It just keeps firing.

## TURNING_POINT v01
---
path: story_atoms/gen_z_professionals/anchored/burnout/false_alarm/turning_point/micro/v01.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Sam talks to another freelancer who's experienced something similar. The freelancer says: "I stopped checking email in the evening. I set a window. If something is actually urgent, the client will call." Sam tries this. The first day is hard. The nervous system is convinced that something is being missed. But nothing is missed. The client doesn't call. The work carries on. Day three, Sam notices the evening doesn't immediately trigger alarm the moment the email app is opened. There's no spike. The body is starting to recalibrate to: "evening time is rest time." It's a small shift. But it's evidence that the alarm response is trainable. The false alarms aren't inevitable. They're the nervous system's response to the pattern. If the pattern changes, the response can change. Sam doesn't have to fix the uncertainty in the freelance work. But Sam can change the relationship to the uncertainty by creating reliable periods of non-monitoring. The turning point is realizing: I don't have to be available all the time for the body to stop firing false alarms. I just have to create times that are reliably safe.

The nervous system is learning that false alarms are survivable. That the outcome of a false alarm is usually just relief.

## INTEGRATION v01
---
---
Tomorrow, before you open the task you're already dreading, do one thing first: find the smallest true sentence you can already say about it, and write that down before anything else. Not the polished version — the rough, half-said thing you'd tell a coworker in passing if they asked "how's it going." If you already texted someone a status update this week, go find that message and use it as your first line, verbatim. The goal isn't to finish the task tomorrow. It's to replace the blank-page moment with a running-start moment, just once, on purpose, and notice what changes when the first sentence is already sitting there waiting instead of needing to be invented from nothing. You'll probably still feel the pull toward Slack, or email, or some other bounded, obvious task — that's fine, that's salience doing what it does, not a failure. The only job tomorrow is to give the important task a door before you judge yourself for not walking through a wall. If it works even partially — if you get two sentences further than you would have — that's the whole experiment succeeding, not a partial failure. Write down which sentence worked as a starting point. You'll want it again next week, because this isn't a one-time trick, it's a lever you're allowed to pull every single time the blank page shows up.

## INTEGRATION v02
---
---
Tomorrow, pick one task you know you'll stall on, and before you touch it, write down one sentence describing exactly what "done" looks like — not the whole finished project, just the next visible edge. "Done = the doc has three bullet points under Q3 goals." "Done = the email is sent, doesn't have to be good." Put that sentence somewhere you'll actually see it — a sticky note, the top of the doc, a pinned message to yourself. Then notice, honestly, whether your attention moves toward the task differently once it has a visible edge instead of an open field. You're not trying to finish the whole thing in one sitting. You're testing a specific claim: that flat, edgeless tasks lose to Slack and email not because they matter less, but because they don't show you where the end is. Give one task an edge tomorrow and watch what happens to how long you can stay in it. If the itch to check something else shows up anyway, that's fine — just notice whether it shows up later than usual, or feels weaker than usual. That shift, even a small one, is the actual signal that the lever works, and it's worth using again on whatever you're dreading next.

## INTEGRATION v03
---
---
Tomorrow morning, before your first meeting or 1:1, take thirty seconds and write three words on an actual piece of paper, or a sticky note, or the notes app — not a plan, not an agenda, just three concrete things you want this specific interaction to land: a fact, a question, a name to listen for. Keep it physically visible during the call, not buried in a tab. The point isn't to perform preparedness. It's to give your working memory a place to put things that isn't your own head, so that when something relevant gets said, you have somewhere to check it against instead of relying on catching it live and holding onto it unaided. You might not use all three. That's fine — this isn't a script, it's a shelf. Notice, afterward, whether you retained more of what happened, or felt less scrambled trying to formulate a response in the moment. If it helps even slightly, use it again for the next meeting, and the one after that, until writing three words down before anything with stakes becomes as automatic as opening your calendar.

## INTEGRATION v04
---
---
Tomorrow, find the one task you've renamed in your head as "big" — the one that's been sitting in a doc or a ticket for days under a vague title — and physically rename it, in the actual title field, with its real constraints spelled out: the due date, how rough it's allowed to be, who's actually going to look at it closely. Not "Q3 planning." "Q3 planning — rough bullets, due Friday, internal eyes only." Takes fifteen seconds. Then close the tab and come back to it later in the day, and notice, specifically, whether opening it feels different than it did this morning. You're testing whether the size of the dread was attached to the task or to the label, and for most avoided tasks, it's the label. If the rename doesn't change anything, that's useful information too — it means the block is somewhere else, and you can go looking with the other levers instead. But try this one first, because it's the cheapest, and it's suspicious how often it's the whole fix.

## INTEGRATION v05
---
---
Tomorrow, notice the first moment you catch yourself doing something unusually thorough that isn't the actual task — a very complete Slack reorg, an unusually deep folder clean, a comment thread you're reading for the fourth time. Don't stop and scold yourself. Just say, out loud if you're alone, "what is this standing in for?" and let yourself actually answer it, even just in your head. Nine times out of ten you'll already know. Then go do one small, literal, physical version of the real task — not the whole thing, just the first concrete motor action, the one sentence, the one line — before the decoy can fully reclaim your attention. You're not trying to never get decoyed again; that's not realistic and it's not the goal. You're building the habit of catching it mid-loop and naming it, because naming a decoy breaks its disguise far faster than willing yourself away from it does. Do this once tomorrow, deliberately, even if it feels artificial, and see if the decoy loses some of its pull once it's been said out loud.

## INTEGRATION v06
---
---
Tomorrow, before any task with a real deadline, set a visible countdown timer for a checkpoint ten minutes before the actual due time — not the deadline itself, a warning shot ahead of it. Let it be loud enough or visible enough that it actually interrupts whatever you're doing, rather than something you'd have to remember to check. The point isn't to create more anxiety about time. It's to stop asking your internal clock to do a job it's demonstrably bad at, and hand that job to something external and reliable instead. When the timer goes off, don't judge whether you feel "ready" to wrap up — that feeling was never a trustworthy narrator here. Just take it as the signal, full stop, the way you'd take a real alarm. Notice, afterward, whether the ten-minute warning caught you somewhere you wouldn't have caught yourself, and if it did, that's the whole point proven in one try. Keep doing it for anything with a deadline going forward, not just tomorrow — this one compounds.

## INTEGRATION v07
---
---
Tomorrow, the next time a message lands in your inbox or Slack that doesn't state its own scope — "got a sec," "quick question," "when you get a chance" — resist the urge to either answer instantly out of anxiety or let it sit gray out of dread. Instead, send one line back: "sure — what's this about, so I know what to bring?" Then notice what happens to the message in your head once it has an actual answer instead of an open shape. You're not being difficult or making extra work for the other person; almost everyone answers this kind of question without blinking. What you're doing is refusing to carry an ambiguous object around all day when one sentence could turn it into a known one. Try this on the very next vague message you get, today or tomorrow, and pay attention to whether it stops occupying background space in your head once its shape is settled. If it works, make it the default response to every scope-less message from here on — it costs you almost nothing and it's the cheapest attention tax refund available.

## INTEGRATION v08
---
---
Tomorrow, pick the flattest, most finish-line-less task on your list — something like "update the copy" or "review the doc" with no natural sense of progress — and before you start, write down one literal sentence describing what "finished" will look like in concrete terms. Not "the copy is better." "The copy says 'confirm' instead of 'submit' on line 42, full stop, done." Put that sentence somewhere visible while you work. You're manufacturing, on purpose, the same kind of visible edge that a scrollbar or a progress bar gives you for free elsewhere — because your attention isn't failing to prioritize the important task, it's correctly noticing that task has no edge to move toward. Give it one, artificially, and notice whether you finish it faster or stay in it longer than you expected. This is a small thing to test once, but if it works, it's worth doing for every flat task from now on, since almost all of your real work is exactly this shape.

## INTEGRATION v10
---
---
Tomorrow, when you catch yourself frozen in front of something, run the quick check before doing anything else: is this missing a handle, a visible ending, an external clock, or somewhere to put what I'm holding in my head? Don't try to fix all four. Just name which one is actually missing for this specific task, out loud or on paper, and build only that one small piece — a sticky note with three words, a one-line "done" statement, a ten-minute-warning timer, a scrap of paper to track what's said in a meeting. You're not solving ADHD tomorrow. You're testing a diagnostic that reframes "I can't just do this" into "this specific piece is missing," which is a completely different, far less demoralizing problem to have. Notice whether naming the missing piece and building just that one thing gets you moving faster than trying to push through the freeze the way you usually do. If it does, this becomes the thing you reach for first, before the self-criticism gets a chance to set in.

If it's vague — "notes," "follow up," "planning" — rewrite it on the spot with the real constraints baked in: what rough looks like, when it's due, who's actually judging it closely, if anyone. When something matching that line gets said, notice whether it actually lands differently than the surrounding conversation did. You're testing whether giving your attention one concrete thing to check against, instead of asking it to track everything equally, changes what you retain. If it works even once — if you catch the one thing that mattered instead of missing it the way you usually would — that's worth repeating for every call going forward, because it costs nothing and the payoff compounds every time you use it.

## COMPRESSION v01
---
---
The mechanism: intention and initiation are separate systems, and ADHD widens the gap between them. Caring about the task doesn't produce the motor act of starting it — a second process has to fire, and it responds to concrete handles, not to reasons, however true. The fix: shrink the task until it's a literal first sentence or motor action small enough that the gap has nowhere left to hide. Find something true you already said today and start there instead of from nothing.
---

## COMPRESSION v02
---
---
The mechanism: attention tracks salience, not importance — loud, novel, immediate things win over quiet, important ones by default, every time, regardless of what you actually care about. The fix: don't try to want the real task more. Make it louder instead — mute the competing notifications, put one physical object on your desk that represents only the task, and let the room, not your willpower, decide what wins your attention next.
---

## COMPRESSION v03
---
---
The mechanism: your internal sense of elapsed time runs on an unreliable clock, and it won't warn you before it's wrong — "I have plenty of time" can be false by an hour with no felt signal. The fix: stop trusting the felt sense entirely. Set a visible countdown for a checkpoint before the real deadline, and let the external timer interrupt you, since your own clock was never built to.
---

## COMPRESSION v04
---
---
The mechanism: working memory has a small, fixed capacity, and ADHD runs it thinner — a thought can fall out of your hands mid-task, gone before you can use it. The fix: build an external shelf before you need it. Three words on paper before a meeting — a name, a topic, a date — hold what your head can't, and let you check against it instead of trying to catch everything live and unaided.
---

## COMPRESSION v05
---
---
The mechanism: task size in your head tracks bundled ambiguity, not actual difficulty — "write the section" hides thirty undefined decisions inside one sentence, and your brain correctly senses the whole pile before you've resolved a single one. The fix: unbundle one decision in advance, in writing, from a calmer moment, and hand it to your future self as a single door instead of a wall of thirty.
---

## COMPRESSION v06
---
---
The mechanism: decoy tasks are adjacent, legitimate-looking, endless, low-decision substitutes your brain grabs because they have a handle and the real task doesn't — the thoroughness is a direct measure of avoidance, not diligence. The fix: notice unusual, unscheduled thoroughness as a signal, ask out loud "what is this standing in for," and do one small literal piece of the real task before the decoy reclaims you.
---

## COMPRESSION v07
---
---
The mechanism: re-reading a message repeatedly can be a comprehension loop standing in for an emotional block that has nothing to do with understanding — the words were never the obstacle, the feeling attached to responding was. The fix: stop rereading after the second pass and write the reply immediately, badly, before a third read can start the loop over. The loop only breaks from outside itself.
---

## COMPRESSION v08
---
---
The mechanism: vague task titles invite your brain to assume the largest, most demanding version of themselves by default, since ambiguity defaults to worst-case under any pressure at all. The fix: rename the task on the spot with its real constraints spelled out — due date, acceptable roughness, actual audience — since your brain reacts to the label before it reads a word of content.
---

## COMPRESSION v09
---
---
The mechanism: an unscoped request forces your attention to hold multiple possible meanings open at once, and holding open branches costs more than a clear, even unwelcome, answer would. The fix: collapse the ambiguity yourself with one direct question — "what's this about?" — and let the reply convert a fog your attention keeps circling into a bounded object it can finally file.
---

## COMPRESSION v10
---
---
The mechanism: tasks with a visible finish line — a scrollbar, a countdown, a progress bar — pull attention harder than flat, edgeless tasks, even smaller and easier ones, because attention tracks movement toward an end. The fix: manufacture a visible edge before starting flat work — one concrete sentence describing exactly what "done" looks like — so your attention has somewhere to move toward instead of an open field to wander in.
---

## COMPRESSION v11
---
---
The mechanism: the hardest part of starting is often generating language from nothing, not the task itself — a blank page demands you invent structure, tone, and content all at once, cold. The fix: raid your own sent messages from earlier today for a plain, already-true sentence about the thing you're avoiding, and use it as the literal first line instead of inventing one under pressure.
---

## COMPRESSION v12
---
---
The mechanism: freeze and difficulty are weakly correlated — forty minutes stuck on a ten-minute task usually means a missing handle, ending, clock, or memory slot, not secret difficulty, and treating it as a willpower problem fails because it was never a willpower problem. The fix: run the four-part check — handle, ending, clock, or external memory — name the one that's actually missing, and build only that single piece before touching the task again.
---

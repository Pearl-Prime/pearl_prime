Jordan sits in the office breakroom while three coworkers laugh about their weekend plans. A hiking trip. Someone mentions the views were unreal. Jordan hears the word "unreal" and something pinches in his chest. He watched them walk out Friday with their bodies relaxed, their faces arranged in anticipation. He watched himself leave too, but from a distance — his body moving but unplugged. The weekend happened. Objectively. He was there. He did things. But he observed all of it through a pane of glass that wouldn't let him through. Now these three are alive with the memory of being alive. Their eyes are bright. They're animated. He can feel the difference between their presence and his watching. His jaw tightens. The coffee in his hand is still warm but he doesn't taste it.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v01
---

BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Jordan begins to notice the pattern. It happens every time someone describes an experience with genuine pleasure. The pattern: they'll mention something good. He'll feel his internal flatness. He'll think about the gap between their aliveness and his absence. And then something shifts. The gap becomes evidence. Not of their good luck or skill or circumstance. Evidence of him. Evidence that he's broken in a specific way that other people aren't broken. The cost becomes clear: he can't just not feel things. He has to not feel things while watching everyone else feel them. His body understands this before his mind does. His shoulders round inward. His breath becomes shallower. His stomach develops a persistent knot. The comparison isn't just comparison anymore. It's a mechanism that keeps turning — feeds him evidence about his broken-ness, his absence, his distance from the world. And the evidence is always convincing because everyone else really is on the other side of the glass. Or so it seems when you're standing here.

Maya's phone becomes a mechanism for feeding the evidence. Each scroll is a small test. Will this person feel like they're on the other side? Yes. Always yes. The evidence accumulates. She stops scrolling eventually because it doesn't feel sad — it feels like documentation. Like she's collecting proof of something she already suspects. Kai begins to build a narrative. The evidence is so consistent. She's in meetings and she's not present. She's in her apartment and she's not present. If she can't be present, maybe she can be competent enough that no one notices. Maybe she can engineer her way out of this. Her shoulders stay tight. Her breath stays high and shallow. Her body is braced for the moment when everyone realizes she's not really here.

Jordan is having coffee with a friend when the friend mentions feeling disconnected from their own family during a recent visit. The friend says it out loud. "I was there but not there, you know? Like I was watching myself be there." Jordan's body goes very still. He's hearing evidence that the other side of the glass might also have glass. Not everyone. Not always. But the friend is describing something that sounds like Jordan's experience. Not exactly. But similar enough that the gap Jordan mapped between himself and everyone else develops a crack.

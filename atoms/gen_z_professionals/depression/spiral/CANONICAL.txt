Priya sits in her apartment on a Thursday evening and realizes she hasn't called anyone in two weeks. The realization isn't sharp. It's flat. Like a fact about the weather. She used to call people. Used to want to. Now the thought of calling requires an energy that she doesn't have. Not the energy to dial. She could do that. But the energy to be present on the phone while she's this tired. To sound normal. To pretend. Her body knows something — knows that nothing sounds good right now. Coffee sounded good once. Books sounded good. She tried a book yesterday and read the same paragraph four times. Her eyes moved across the words but nothing landed. Her chest feels compressed. Her jaw is set in a way that means she's bracing against something. It's been a few weeks of this. She thinks maybe it will pass. Maybe she's just tired from work. But underneath the thought, something is gathering. A heaviness that doesn't have a name yet.

Priya begins to notice the spiral. It's not a line downward. It's circular. The thought appears: nothing sounds good. The thought becomes evidence: if nothing sounds good, then maybe nothing will ever sound good again. The evidence becomes prediction: I'm broken then. If she used to enjoy things and now she doesn't, something is wrong. The prediction loops back to the beginning: if something is wrong with me, then nothing will sound good. The spiral isn't fast. It's slow and heavy. Each loop adds weight. She's starting to believe the spiral is true. Not just temporary. True. Her body tenses against the spiral even while the spiral is running. She's hyperaware of her own lack of wanting. That awareness becomes part of the spiral. She's not broken because she can't want things. She's broken because she can see clearly that she can't want things and can't do anything about it. Her stomach develops a knot that doesn't release. Her sleep is worse now — not insomnia but a restless shallow sleep where dreams are always slightly unpleasant.



## INTEGRATION v01
---
mode: BODY_LANDED
reframe_type: BODY_FACT
weight: standard
carry_line: "Your shoulders know where you end."
---
The message came in at 10:47 PM. Not urgent. Just a question that could have waited. You answered it anyway. Your fingers moved before your mind could register that this was the fourth interruption since dinner. Your body kept score though — you felt the tightness move from your shoulders up into your jaw. That tension is not weakness. It's your nervous system registering the boundary that has been crossed.
Notice where your shoulders are right now. Where your jaw sits. These are not failures of discipline. They are the physical record of what happens when the boundary erodes repeatedly.
The message was one small thing. The pattern is not.
Your shoulders know where you end.

## INTEGRATION v02
---
mode: COST_VISIBLE
reframe_type: COST_UNCOUNTED
weight: standard
carry_line: "Each response cost something invisible."
---
Count the messages you answered after hours this week. Not because they were emergencies. Because saying no felt risky. Because "I'm not available" sounds like "I'm not committed." Because the person who doesn't respond might be the person who isn't relied on.
Three messages. Five. Seven. Each one cost something. Not visible in a performance review. The wind-down that didn't happen. The conversation with someone else that you shortened. The thought that arrived at midnight and you needed to track it instead of sleep it away.
These costs don't compound equally across everyone. You know that. The boundary looks different depending on who is asking and who is saying no. That unfairness is real.
Each response cost something invisible.

## INTEGRATION v03
---
mode: STILL_HERE
reframe_type: TIME_SHIFT
weight: light
carry_line: "The boundary holds. It bends first."
note: STILL-HERE mode — final chapter only, one per book
---
You've drawn this line before. Weeks where you held it and weeks where it dissolved. The pattern isn't linear. Some weeks you could say no and it felt possible. Other weeks the word caught in your throat. That's not inconsistency. That's what happens to a boundary when you live inside a system that tests it daily.
What changed from the weeks you held it is not some internal resolve. It's the weight of smaller permissions. Each time you said yes to something that mattered less, you made the larger no more difficult. The boundary didn't get weaker. Your leverage against the system did.
You're not at the beginning. You've seen what the cost is. That's information you carry now.
The boundary holds. It bends first.

## INTEGRATION v04
---
mode: QUESTION_OPEN
reframe_type: QUESTION_REFRAME
weight: standard
carry_line: "What does the yes protect you from."
---
The real question isn't whether you should answer that message. It's why not answering feels dangerous. Something about the no makes you unsafe. Maybe it's the evaluation. Maybe it's becoming invisible to the people who determine what happens to you. Maybe it's something older than this job — a time when not performing eagerness meant being forgotten.
The boundary isn't really about the messages. It's about what the system has promised to do to you if you stop.
You don't have to solve that tonight. Just let the question sit. What does saying yes protect you from.
Notice where you're holding that question in your body. Chest. Throat. Somewhere your breath changed shape.
What does the yes protect you from.

## INTEGRATION v05
---
mode: SOMEONE_ELSE
reframe_type: OTHER_PERSON
weight: heavy
carry_line: "They're answering at 10 PM too."
---
Someone in your organization has better boundaries than you do. You know because you see them leaving at five. You see them not responding to Slack after hours. Something in you interprets this as either confidence or carelessness — the belief that they don't have as much to prove.
The other possibility: they decided something about their survival that you haven't decided yet. Or they have the structural safety to decide it — different demographics, different proximity to consequences, different read on what the system will do if they're unavailable.
Someone else is answering the 10 PM messages too. You just don't see that.
They're answering at 10 PM too.

## INTEGRATION v07
---
mode: COST_VISIBLE
reframe_type: COST_UNCOUNTED
weight: heavy
carry_line: "It has cost you sleep for months."
---
Map out what always-on has taken. Not just time. Sleep that stopped coming easy. Relationships that atrophied because you couldn't show up present. The parts of your week that belonged to you and now belong to the notification timer. The cognitive load of holding work inside your personal hours. The version of yourself that existed before you were always available.
This isn't guilt. This is seeing the trade. You made it because the alternative felt more threatening. The cost and the reasoning are both true at the same time.
It has cost you sleep for months.

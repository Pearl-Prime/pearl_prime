Marcus gets a Slack from Derek asking if he can take on client communication while his manager is out. Marcus's immediate thought is yes—say yes—but he's already drowning. He has three projects in flight, a presentation due Friday, and hasn't seen his therapist in four weeks. The shame arrives before the refusal: I shouldn't have to say no. I should be able to handle this. Other people handle this. The thought of saying "I can't" makes him feel small. Not like he's protecting himself. Like he's failing. Like he's admitting he's not built for this job. The shame is already there before he even opens his mouth to decline. It's in the space before the boundary. It's the feeling that having needs is weakness.

BAND: 3

COST_INTENSITY: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Marcus is in a session and his therapist asks: "Whose voice is the shame?" Marcus sits with it and realizes—it's his parents. The message from childhood: good kids don't have needs. Don't burden people with your limitations. Be fine so others don't have to manage you. Marcus didn't make that rule. His family did. And it's been running his nervous system ever since. The shame becomes something Marcus can observe instead of something Marcus is.



## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v01
---
mode: SECULAR_BRIDGE
doctrine_source: somatic
mechanism: false_alarm
weight: standard
carry_line: "Your alarm system is firing on a Slack ping, not on a real fire. The body cannot tell the difference until you teach it."
---
Here is the mechanism. Your body has an alarm system that is older than your job. It evolved to fire fast and ask questions later, because in the environments it was built for, the cost of missing a real threat was higher than the cost of a few false positives. The system does not read content. It reads pattern. A Slack notification at 9:47 pm from your manager is, to the alarm system, the same shape as a footstep behind you on a dark street. Same chest tightness, same shallow breath, same scan for what went wrong. The alarm did not get the memo that you live in an apartment and your manager is in another timezone asking about a Notion link. It is doing exactly what it was built to do, in a context that was never anticipated. What changes when you see this clearly is small but real. The ping still arrives. The chest still tightens. But you start to recognize the tightness as an alarm, not as evidence. The alarm becomes information about your nervous system, not information about your job. That gap is where everything else gets built.

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v02
---
mode: SECULAR_BRIDGE
doctrine_source: somatic
mechanism: spiral
weight: standard
carry_line: "The prediction loop runs faster than thought. Naming the chain is how you slow it down enough to step out."
---
Here is the mechanism. The mind you have is, among other things, a prediction loop. It takes a small piece of input and runs it forward, link by link, building the next thirty seconds of plausible future. This is useful when you are crossing a street. It is less useful when your manager schedules a 1:1 titled "checking in" and your prediction loop runs from "checking in" to "performance concerns" to "PIP" to "termination" to "rent" in under four seconds. The loop is not lying to you. It is doing what it does. The problem is that it does not know when to stop, and it treats each link as evidence for the next one, even though every link past the first is invented. What helps is noticing the chain itself. Not arguing with it. Just naming the links as they appear. "Checking in. Performance concerns. PIP." When you can see the chain in slow motion, you also see that it is a chain, not a fact. The 1:1 still happens. The prediction still runs. But you stop borrowing emotion from a future that has not arrived and may never arrive.

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v03
---
mode: SECULAR_BRIDGE
doctrine_source: somatic
mechanism: comparison
weight: standard
carry_line: "Your nervous system reads a peer's promotion as a threat. The comparison engine is doing math that does not apply to your life."
---
Here is the mechanism. The comparison engine in you is older than LinkedIn, older than salary bands, older than performance reviews. It evolved to track relative status inside a small group, because in those environments your access to mates and resources depended on it. It did not evolve for a feed of two thousand people you barely know, half of whom are posting their best news. When a college peer announces a Senior Manager promotion in a Discord, the engine does not run a sophisticated analysis of their hours, their luck, their company's growth curve. It runs a fast, dumb subtraction. They went up. You did not. Threat. The chest tightening you feel is not a verdict on your career. It is the engine doing the only thing it knows how to do, with input it was never designed to handle. What changes when you see this is that the comparison still arrives. The chest still tightens. But you start to recognize the tightening as your nervous system mistaking a feed for a tribe, and the verdict it delivered is not data about your life. It is exhaust from a system running on the wrong fuel.

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v04
---
mode: SECULAR_BRIDGE
doctrine_source: somatic
mechanism: overwhelm
weight: standard
carry_line: "Eight open Slack channels is not eight tasks. It is eight predicted threats your nervous system has not closed."
---
Here is the mechanism. Your working memory has a small budget. Maybe four things at once, maybe five on a good day. Slack channels do not respect that budget. Neither does a Notion task list rolling over from yesterday, nor a calendar with five meetings stacked between standup and the end of day. Each open channel, each unread badge, each pending thread is something the nervous system has flagged and not yet closed. It is not eight tasks waiting. It is eight predicted threats holding partial attention, draining the same supply you need for the work in front of you. This is not a discipline failure. It is a capacity question. The channels are doing exactly what they were built to do, which is to demand attention. Your nervous system is doing exactly what it was built to do, which is to hold the demand open until it is resolved. The friction you feel by 3 pm is the gap between an attention budget that has not changed in fifty thousand years and a context-switching load that did not exist five years ago. Naming this is the start of choosing what actually closes.

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v05
---
mode: SECULAR_BRIDGE
doctrine_source: somatic
mechanism: shame
weight: standard
carry_line: "The belief that other 27-year-olds are not this anxious is itself a symptom of the thing you are hiding."
---
Here is the mechanism. Shame, in this context, is the quiet conviction that something is wrong with you specifically, and that if anyone knew, you would lose standing. It runs underneath the visible anxiety. It is what makes you draft the Slack message three times before sending. It is what keeps you from telling your 1:1 that you have been waking up at 4 am. The shame whispers that other people in your cohort are handling this fine, that your college roommate is sleeping through the night, that the version of you on LinkedIn is doing better than the version inside your apartment. None of this is verifiable. The shame does not need verification. It only needs the gap between the inside and the outside, and our culture has built a thousand surfaces for that gap to widen against. What changes when you see shame as a mechanism, not a fact, is that the gap stays but the verdict softens. The other 27-year-olds are mostly running their own version of this loop, mostly hiding it from each other, mostly believing they are alone. The shame is the symptom. The hiding is what keeps it alive.

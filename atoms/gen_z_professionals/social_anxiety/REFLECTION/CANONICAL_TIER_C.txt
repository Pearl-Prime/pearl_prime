

## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
There's a moment right before I have to enter a social situation where I feel this activation. My heart rate increases. My thinking becomes scattered. I'm running through possible interactions, possible failures, possible ways this could go wrong. The mechanism here is called pre-social threat appraisal—the scanning for danger before social engagement. My nervous system is interpreting social interaction as a potential threat.
---

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
What I've noticed is that my social anxiety often has nothing to do with the actual people. It's about the feeling of being watched. Of being evaluated. Of potentially being judged. And in a digital-native world where everything is recordable, this feeling is constant. The mechanism is called social monitoring—the sense that I'm always being observed and evaluated.
---

## REFLECTION v03
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
I construct elaborate narratives about what people think of me. I imagine their judgments. I replay conversations and generate interpretations. And these narratives feel very real even though they're entirely generated by my mind. The mechanism is called social narrative construction—the creation of stories about what others think about you.
---

## REFLECTION v04
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: inherited
---
There's a specific kind of anxiety that happens in group settings. I feel like I need to contribute but also like anything I say might be stupid. So I stay quiet. But then the quiet becomes its own kind of judgment. Like people are noticing that I'm not participating. The mechanism is called conversational paradox—the simultaneous fear of speaking and of being silent.
---

## REFLECTION v05
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I've noticed that my social anxiety increases with visibility. The more people in the room, the more anxious I become. Like there's something specific about being one person in a large group that activates my threat detection system. The mechanism is called exposure anxiety—the fear triggered by being visible to many people.
---

## REFLECTION v06
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
What strikes me is how my social anxiety often shows up as physical symptoms. I have trouble maintaining eye contact. My voice gets quieter. I feel heat in my face. And these physical responses reinforce the anxiety because I can feel myself showing signs of being nervous. The mechanism is called symptom amplification—physical anxiety symptoms creating more anxiety.
---

## REFLECTION v07
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
I've started to notice that I spend a lot of mental energy managing how I appear to others. I'm thinking about my body language. Monitoring my expression. Evaluating whether I look anxious. And this self-monitoring takes up so much bandwidth that I'm not actually present for the interaction. The mechanism is called impression management.
---

## REFLECTION v08
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: inherited
---
There's a part of my social anxiety that's connected to the knowledge that I'm being compared. There are people who are more charismatic. More confident. More socially skilled. And somehow I'm competing in a space where I feel like I'm losing. The mechanism is social comparison anxiety.
---

## REFLECTION v09
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
What I've realized is that my social anxiety sometimes prevents me from having the experiences that would make me less anxious. If I avoid social situations, I don't get to practice. I don't get to learn that the feared outcomes don't happen. So the anxiety persists. The mechanism is called avoidance-maintained anxiety.
---

## REFLECTION v10
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
I notice that my social anxiety has a specific flavor around authenticity. Like people will discover that I'm not who I present myself to be. That underneath the version of me I show is someone fundamentally different or fundamentally inadequate. The mechanism is called authenticity anxiety—the fear that genuine self is unacceptable.
---

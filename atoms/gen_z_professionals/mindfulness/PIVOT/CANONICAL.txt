## PIVOT v01
---
---
You think you need to focus harder and never drift. Try this instead: notice the drift, then return once. Presence is the return, not the absence of leaving.
---

## PIVOT v02
---
---
You think being distracted mid-meeting means you're bad at this job. Try this instead: name the drift silently, ground into your hands or feet, and look at the actual person talking. That's the whole skill, repeated.
---

## PIVOT v03
---
---
You think the goal is an unbroken hour of attention. Try this instead: aim for the shortest possible gap between leaving and noticing you've left. Speed of return, not length of stay.
---

## PIVOT v04
---
---
You think catching yourself drifting proves you're failing at presence. Try this instead: treat the catch itself as the win — it's the only part of the process you actually control.
---

## PIVOT v05
---
---
You think you should suppress the urge to check Slack mid-meeting. Try this instead: let the pull happen, notice it by name, and choose not to follow it, once. That's the rep.
---

## PIVOT v06
---
---
You think one perfect meeting will fix the pattern. Try this instead: collect ten small returns across ten different meetings. The pattern shifts through repetition, not through one clean win.
---

## PIVOT v07
---
---
You think being "engaged-looking" (nodding, camera on) means you were actually present. Try this instead: check for a specific memory afterward. If you can't name one real detail, you were somewhere else.
---

## PIVOT v08
---
---
You think the rehearsed reply in your head is protecting you. Try this instead: notice it's usually defending against criticism that was never made, then return to the actual conversation happening now.
---

## PIVOT v09
---
---
You think you have to fix this across every meeting today. Try this instead: pick one meeting. One catch. One return. Let that be the entire assignment.
---

## PIVOT v10
---
---
You think drifting mid-meeting is a personal flaw. Try this instead: recognize it as what unattended attention always does. The variable you control is how fast you notice.
---

## PIVOT v11
---
---
You think willpower is the tool for staying present. Try this instead: use a physical anchor — hands, feet, the chair under you — as the trigger for returning, every single time.
---

## PIVOT v12
---
---
You think presence has to feel effortless to count. Try this instead: let it feel small and deliberate. A clunky, on-purpose return still counts as the whole rep.
---

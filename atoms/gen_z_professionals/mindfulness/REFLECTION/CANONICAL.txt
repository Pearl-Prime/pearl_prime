## REFLECTION v01
---
---
Here's the part nobody puts in the onboarding deck: half-presence has a cost, and the cost isn't obvious because it doesn't show up as a mistake in the moment. It shows up later, as a gap. You "attend" the meeting — camera on, nodding, even answering when called on — but an hour afterward you can't reconstruct what was actually said. Not because you weren't smart enough to follow it. Because you weren't there to encode it in the first place. Attention is what turns an event into a memory. Skip the attention, and the event still happens to your body, but it never quite happens to you.

The instinct, once you notice this, is to treat it like a discipline problem — try harder, focus more, will yourself into staying. That's the wrong lever, and it's worth understanding why. Presence isn't a state you achieve and then hold, like balancing on one foot until you get good enough to never wobble. It's not a switch you flip to "on" and leave there for a 30-minute stand-up. It's closer to breathing, or walking — a continuous small correction. You don't walk in a straight line by locking your legs; you walk by drifting slightly and correcting, over and over, so fast it looks like a straight line from the outside. Presence works the same way. The mind wanders — to the Slack thread, to the rehearsed reply, to the comment your manager made yesterday that you're still relitigating — and presence isn't the absence of that wandering. Presence is the return.

This matters practically because it changes what you're actually training. You are not training yourself to never leave the room. Nobody does that; every brain drifts, constantly, that's just what attention does left alone. You're training the return trip — noticing you've left, and coming back, faster and more often, the way a rubber band gets better at snapping back with practice, not by never stretching in the first place. Every time you catch the drift and come back — hands on the desk, eyes on the actual face on screen, one breath — you're not failing at presence. You're doing presence, the only way it's actually done.

So the lever isn't "focus harder." It's noticing the exact moment you've left, without punishing yourself for having left, and walking back. Ten small returns in one meeting beats one long stretch of forced, white-knuckled attention that snaps by minute six. The return is repeatable. The return is the skill.

## REFLECTION v02

Nobody warns you about this in the offer letter: the job doesn't just want your hours, it wants your attention, and those turn out to be two very different currencies. You can be fully clocked in — laptop open, badge green, camera on — and still be almost entirely absent, because your attention left for a Slack thread ten minutes ago and never filed a return ticket. The cost of this isn't visible in the moment. It shows up later, when you go to recall what happened in a meeting you technically attended and find nothing there. A blank spot shaped like an hour.

Here's the mechanism worth understanding: your brain doesn't experience "the meeting" and "the Slack thread" as two channels playing at once, like a TV with picture-in-picture. It switches between them, fully, each time — more like changing the channel than splitting the screen. Every switch has a cost, a few seconds of re-orientation, and if you're switching every ninety seconds for a thirty-minute call, you're spending a shocking percentage of that call just re-locating yourself, not actually receiving new information. That's the hidden tax. It's not that half your attention is on the meeting and half is elsewhere. It's that almost none of your attention is anywhere long enough to actually land.

The good news buried in this: you don't fix it by trying to eliminate the switching. You can't; the wandering is automatic, involuntary, baked into how minds work under mild boredom or mild threat (and a meeting after a weird Slack exchange qualifies as both). What you can train is the noticing — catching the switch earlier each time, so the gap between "I left" and "I'm back" shrinks. That gap is the actual skill. It's trainable the same way anything repetitive is trainable: by doing the small rep over and over, not by hoping for one big permanent fix.

Concretely: the next time you notice you've been nodding at a meeting for ninety seconds without absorbing a word, don't spiral about it. Just do the smallest physical anchor available — feel your hands on the desk, or your feet on the floor — and look at the actual face talking, not your own thumbnail. That's the whole rep. It's not dramatic. It's also the only thing that has ever actually worked.

## REFLECTION v03

---
There's a version of "paying attention" that gets taught wrong, and it's worth naming because it sets you up to fail before you start. The wrong version treats presence like a destination — something you arrive at and then simply stay, the way you might land in a chair and remain seated. Under that model, every time your mind drifts to the rehearsed Slack reply or the comment your manager made that's still bothering you, it feels like failure, like you did the thing wrong and need to start over from zero.

The actual mechanism is different, and once you see it, it's a relief. Attention isn't a place you stay. It's a place you keep returning to. Neuroscientists who study this call the wandering itself the default — the brain left alone will always drift, toward threat-scanning, toward unfinished business, toward whatever thread feels most urgent even when it isn't. A Slack ping about "got a sec?" registers, to an underslept nervous system in a hybrid job, roughly the same as a rustle in the grass registered to an underslept ancestor. Of course you check it. Of course your mind stays there rehearsing outcomes. That's not a character flaw. That's the system working as designed, just aimed at the wrong century's threats.

What actually builds attention, then, isn't preventing the drift — that's not on the table. It's the rep of noticing you've drifted and walking back, which is a completely different skill than "never leaving." Every return is a tiny act of training, like a single rep in a gym that doesn't look like much on its own but adds up to something real over weeks. The person who's "good at presence" isn't the person who never drifts. It's the person whose drift-to-return time has gotten short — seconds instead of minutes, a whole meeting's worth of small corrections instead of one long blank patch.

So the concrete lever: stop treating the drift as the problem. Start treating the length of the drift as the thing you're improving. Notice you've left. Don't narrate why or feel bad about it. Just come back — to the actual voice talking, the actual document on screen — and count that as the whole win, every single time you do it.

Let's name the mechanism precisely, because vague advice like "be present" doesn't survive contact with an actual back-to-back Tuesday. What's happening when your mind leaves a meeting for a Slack thread isn't weakness — it's a well-documented feature of attention called task-switching, and it has a real cost that nobody bills you for directly but that you pay anyway: every switch requires a re-orientation tax, a few seconds of "wait, where was I" before you're actually receiving information again. If you switch every minute in a thirty-minute meeting, you might spend a third of the meeting just re-locating yourself, not actually processing anything new.

That's the hidden cost of half-presence: it's not that you get half the information. It's that you get much less than half, because the transitions themselves eat the time, invisibly, without a line item.

Here's where it gets useful. If wandering itself were the problem, you'd be stuck — wandering is automatic, an old-brain habit of scanning for whatever feels most urgent (a "got a sec?" DM registers as urgent whether or not it actually is). But wandering isn't the problem. It's the baseline. The actual variable you can move is how long the wander lasts before you notice it and correct — the return time. Someone whose return time is thirty seconds is functionally far more present across a meeting than someone whose return time is eight minutes, even though both of them "wandered."

And return time is trainable in the most literal sense: repetition builds it, the same way any skill builds through reps rather than through one dramatic insight. Every time you catch yourself mid-drift — nodding at a meeting while actually drafting a reply in your head — and you bring your attention back to the actual sentence being spoken, you've just shortened your average return time by one data point. It doesn't feel like much in the moment. It adds up the way reps always add up: invisibly, then suddenly.

The lever, concretely: stop aiming for "never wander." Aim for "notice faster, return quicker." That's the only version of focus that was ever actually buildable, and it's buildable starting in your very next meeting.

Priya has a theory about this. Somewhere between 18 and now, something changed. Maybe it was the pandemic — the world got smaller and more consequential. Maybe it was entering the workforce — suddenly decisions have professional weight. Maybe it's just the accumulation of small failures that taught Priya not to trust her instincts.
Or maybe it's something about becoming aware of how much you don't know.
As a kid, Priya didn't know how many ways a choice could go wrong. So Priya made choices easily. As Priya grew up and learned more, Priya became aware of all the ways things could fail. And awareness became paralysis.
The mechanism might be: greater knowledge produces greater visible risk, which produces greater deliberation, which produces the sense that the version of you who decided easily was actually just ignorant.
So Priya can't go back to trusting her instincts, because Priya now knows too much about what can go wrong.
This is irreversible. You can't unknow something.
Priya's jaw clenches with this recognition. The person who trusted her instincts wasn't wiser or stronger. She was just less informed. Growing up means becoming too aware to move easily.



## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I replay conversations. I analyze interactions. I generate possible interpretations of what someone meant. I construct contingency plans for things that might happen. My mind is constantly running scenarios, evaluating possibilities, processing potential threats. The mechanism is called rumination—the repetitive mental processing that produces no new information but feels productive in the moment. I'm stuck in loops of analysis that don't lead anywhere.
---

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
What strikes me is how overthinking feels like intelligence. Like the more I think about something, the more I understand it. The more I process, the more control I have. But that's not how my mind actually works. Overthinking just generates anxiety without generating solutions. Yet I keep doing it. The mechanism is mistaking process for progress.
---

## REFLECTION v03
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
I can feel my mind activating when I'm supposed to be resting. There's this constant background thinking—analyzing, planning, worrying. And I understand that this is exhausting. I understand that I should be able to quiet my mind. But the overthinking has become so automatic that I barely notice it until I'm three hours deep in a thought spiral. The mechanism is called automatic rumination.
---

## REFLECTION v04
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: inherited
---
My overthinking has a specific target. Social interactions. Did that person think I was weird? Did I say something wrong? Am I reading too much into what they said? And these questions loop. They don't resolve. They just expand. The mechanism here is social rumination—repetitive evaluation of social situations without resolution.
---

## REFLECTION v05
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
There's a part of me that believes if I just think about something long enough, I'll figure it out. That the right answer exists and I just haven't generated it yet. So I keep thinking. Keep analyzing. Keep processing. But some problems aren't solvable through analysis. They just need to be lived through. The mechanism is called overthinking as problem-solving.
---

## REFLECTION v06
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
I notice that overthinking is often trying to solve something that's already happened. I replay a conversation and generate different outcomes. I analyze a situation that's already passed. I'm trying to change something that can't be changed through thought alone. The mechanism is temporal overthinking—rumination directed at events that are no longer changeable.
---

## REFLECTION v07
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
What I've realized is that overthinking is sometimes how my anxiety shows up. Instead of experiencing the anxiety directly, I think about it. I analyze it. I generate narratives about it. But the thinking doesn't resolve the underlying anxiety. It just gives it a sense of intellectual legitimacy. The mechanism is anxiety masquerading as analysis.
---

## REFLECTION v08
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: inherited
---
There's a loop I get stuck in where overthinking creates more anxiety, and more anxiety creates more overthinking. I'm trying to think my way out of a problem that's being created by thinking. The mechanism is recursive anxiety—each loop feeding the next.
---

## REFLECTION v09
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I've started to notice that my overthinking often happens when I'm trying to avoid something else. When I have something uncomfortable to do, my mind suddenly becomes very interested in analyzing some past situation. The mechanism is avoidance through rumination—using thinking as a way to escape other feelings.
---

## REFLECTION v10
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
What I'm realizing is that my mind has become trained to expect problems. So it spends a lot of time generating them. Looking for them. Analyzing possible threats. This constant vigilance was probably adaptive at some point. But now it's just exhausting. The mechanism is called anxious rumination—thinking that's designed to protect but ends up depleting.
---

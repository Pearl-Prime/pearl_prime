

## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
My body remembers things my mind has decided not to think about. There's tightness in my chest that corresponds to words I never said. There's tension in my shoulders that's been there for so long I almost don't notice it unless I specifically check. There's a way my stomach knots before difficult conversations that happens automatically, before my conscious mind even registers the anxiety. The mechanism here is called somatic memory, and it's describing something that seems obvious once you notice it: the body holds what the mind tries to forget. The nervous system records everything, even if the conscious mind decides not to process it. And it lives there, in muscles and breath and posture. But here's what I'm sitting with: acknowledging that the body held this doesn't immediately release it. Noticing the tension doesn't make it go away. And the question becomes what to do with the awareness of what's been stored.
I'm trying to listen to my body but my body's language is unfamiliar.
---

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
I've started to realize that a lot of the work I did in therapy—processing things mentally, understanding them, making meaning out of them—didn't actually change the way my body responds. My mind is convinced I'm safe. My body still flinches. There's a disconnect here between intellectual understanding and somatic reality. My nervous system didn't get the memo that I've processed the trauma. It's still running the same defensive patterns, still responding to threats that aren't there, still living in the story that my body learned a long time ago. And there's something almost humbling about recognizing that the mind can do a lot of work and the body remains completely unchanged. The somatic piece is its own thing. It's not dependent on my understanding.
The mechanism here is called somatic dissociation—the disconnect between mind and body.
---

## REFLECTION v03
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
What I'm noticing is that my body has its own knowledge. When I push myself too hard, there's a pain that shows up. When I'm in a situation that's not safe, there's a tightness in my throat. When I'm about to overcommit, there's an exhaustion that arrives before I've consciously realized I'm tired. The body is communicating constantly. It's the messenger, and for a long time I just didn't know how to listen. I was trained to override it, to ignore it, to push past what it was telling me in service of something else. To achieve. To help. To be strong. But now I'm trying to listen, and the problem is that listening to the body's signals means sometimes not doing the things I think I should do, and that feels impossible.
I wonder what would change if I actually trusted the signals my body was sending.
---

## REFLECTION v04
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: inherited
---
There's a holding pattern in my body that I don't think I chose. A way of bracing myself. A pattern of breath that's shallow. A default state of mild tension. And I don't actually remember when this became the baseline. I don't remember learning it. It's just been there, the way my body is. And the strange thing is that bringing awareness to it doesn't automatically change it. I can notice: there it is, the tightness. I can notice: there it is, the shallow breath. But changing these patterns seems to require something that's not just willpower or intention. It requires meeting the body in a different way, but I'm not entirely sure what that means or how to do it. The mechanism here is something like somatic homeostasis—the body maintaining a familiar state even when that state is causing pain, because familiar is predictable and safe.
But trusting them would mean changing my behavior. And I'm not sure I'm ready for that.

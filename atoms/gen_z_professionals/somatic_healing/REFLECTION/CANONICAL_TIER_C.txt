

## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I've started to notice my body again after spending so long dissociated from it. There's this feeling of disconnection—like I'm in my head observing my body rather than inhabiting it. And naming this disconnection is the first step, I guess. But there's no resolution yet. Just the awareness that I'm not fully here. The mechanism is called dissociative awareness—the beginning of noticing disconnection without yet knowing how to reconnect.
---

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
My body carries tension that I've stopped noticing. I find myself holding my shoulders up toward my ears. My jaw is clenched. My hands are in fists. And when I notice these things, I can release them temporarily. But the tension returns almost immediately. The mechanism is called somatic holding—the body maintaining tension patterns that have become automatic.
---

## REFLECTION v03
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
There's something about being asked to feel my body that creates anxiety. Because my body is where the burnout lives. Where the anxiety lives. Where all the things I've been managing through dissociation are actually located. So grounding back into my body feels dangerous. The mechanism is called body-based threat—the sense that full embodiment will mean feeling things that are currently being avoided.
---

## REFLECTION v04
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: inherited
---
I'm learning that my nervous system has been dysregulated for so long that I don't actually remember what regulation feels like. So when someone talks about feeling calm or grounded, I don't have a reference point. I don't know what that means in my body.
---

## REFLECTION v05
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
What I've noticed is that my breath is shallow. Almost like I'm constantly in a state of mild panic. And when I try to breathe deeply, it feels unfamiliar. Wrong somehow. My nervous system doesn't trust deep breath. The mechanism is called nervous system learned response—the body training itself to stay in a heightened state.
---

## REFLECTION v06
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
There's a specific kind of pain in my chest and shoulders that doesn't have a clear physical cause. It's been there so long that I've stopped noticing it. But when I pause and feel it, it's intense. And I don't know what to do with that.
---

## REFLECTION v07
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
I'm starting to understand that my body has been trying to tell me something for a long time. That the physical sensations I've been dismissing are actually important information. But I don't know how to listen to my body in a way that leads anywhere.
---

## REFLECTION v08
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: inherited
---
What strikes me is how unfamiliar my own body feels. Like I'm reconnecting with a stranger. There's this process of relearning what's happening in here. But it's slow. And there's no clear endpoint.
---

## REFLECTION v09
---

voice_mode: guide

---

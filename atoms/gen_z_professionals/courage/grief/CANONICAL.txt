Alex sits with the pitch deck and it's not anxiety. It's something heavier. It's the grief of realizing that the version of Alex who was going to be fearless by now, who was supposed to have figured out how to move in the world without this constant friction—that version isn't coming. There was a timeline where by 24, Alex would have moved through enough risk and discomfort to be comfortable with both. Where the pitch would feel like information delivery instead of like standing naked in front of strangers. The grief settles in the chest. Not the sharp kind. The sitting kind. The kind that makes movement feel pointless because the movement would have happened in a different body, the body of the Alex who was supposed to exist. The chest is heavy. The shoulders are already down. Not from effort but from the weight of carrying the future that was supposed to come and didn't.

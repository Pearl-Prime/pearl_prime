---
Alex sends an email to the manager asking for feedback on a proposal. Three hours pass. No response. Alex's body knows what this means: the manager hated it. The proposal was so bad that there's nothing even worth saying. Alex is not trusted to do this kind of work.
By hour six, Alex has already started rewriting the entire thing.
By hour eight, the manager's response arrives: "Great. Moving forward with this." A thumbs-up emoji. Just that.
Alex's body doesn't relax. The response was good, but the delayed response proved the silence meant disapproval. The thumbs-up is probably just... politeness. The manager is probably going to quietly hand this to someone else.
Alex's hands are still cold.
This is the pattern: absence of communication reads as negative communication. Silence becomes a verdict.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v01
---
path: story_atoms/gen_z_professionals/anchored/self_worth/false_alarm/mechanism_proof/micro/v01.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Alex has identified the mechanism: professional communication isn't designed to provide regular positive feedback. It's designed to flag problems. So silence actually means "no problems," which is as good as it gets.
But Alex's nervous system wasn't built for a communication system that operates this way. Alex's nervous system evolved in a context where regular validation was expected. Where the absence of explicit encouragement meant something was wrong.
In the modern workplace, explicit encouragement is vanishingly rare. Silence is the norm. So Alex's alarm system is constantly firing because it's tuned to a baseline (explicit regular validation) that doesn't exist in this environment.
The mechanism is: the alarm is recalibrating to an impossible standard. Alex is waiting for a type of feedback that the environment isn't designed to provide.
So Alex is always in false alarm. Always convinced that the silence means disapproval, when actually the silence means nothing — it just means the system doesn't work that way.

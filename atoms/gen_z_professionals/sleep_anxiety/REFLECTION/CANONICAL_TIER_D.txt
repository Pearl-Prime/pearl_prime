

## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
Bedtime has become something I dread. Not because I don't need sleep, but because as soon as I lie down, my mind activates. All the thoughts I've been avoiding all day suddenly demand attention. And then I'm lying there, trying not to think, which makes me think about not thinking. The mechanism here is called pre-sleep anxiety—the activation that happens when you stop being distracted. My mind interprets rest as threat.
---

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
I check the time constantly. How many hours until I need to wake up. How many hours I've already lost to not sleeping. And this constant calculation creates more anxiety. Because I can feel myself getting more exhausted and more awake simultaneously. The mechanism is called sleep vigilance—the monitoring of sleep itself, which prevents sleep.
---

## REFLECTION v03
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
There's a specific kind of dread that comes on Sunday nights. Or the night before something important. My body knows that stress is coming and it prepares by making sleep impossible. So I go into whatever I'm facing already depleted. The mechanism here is anticipatory insomnia—anxiety about future events preventing present sleep.
---

## REFLECTION v04
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: inherited
---
I've noticed that my sleep anxiety has a very specific narrative. Something will go wrong tomorrow. I'll be too tired to handle it. And this worry will keep me awake, making it more likely that I'll actually be too tired. So the mechanism creates the condition it's afraid of. I'm generating the problem I'm trying to prevent.
---

## REFLECTION v05
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
My sleep has become fragmented. I fall asleep okay but then wake up at 2 or 3am with my mind already spinning. And then I lie there for hours, trying to get back to sleep, growing increasingly anxious about how tired I'll be. The mechanism is called middle-of-the-night anxiety—the specific panic of knowing you're not going back to sleep.
---

## REFLECTION v06
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
What strikes me is how much I know about sleep. I know about sleep hygiene. I know about cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia. I know the neurobiology of sleep. And somehow this knowledge hasn't helped. It just gives me more things to worry about. Like I'm supposed to be able to think myself into sleep when the problem is that I'm thinking too much. The mechanism is knowledge creating more anxiety.
---

## REFLECTION v07
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
I've started to resent the concept of sleep. Like sleep has become an adversary. Something I want but can't have. And this resentment creates more tension around bedtime. The thing I need most has become the thing I'm most resistant to. The mechanism is called adversarial sleep—treating sleep as an opponent rather than a resource.
---

## REFLECTION v08
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: inherited
---
There's a part of my sleep anxiety that's connected to the knowledge that sleep is non-negotiable. I have to get up at a certain time. I have to be functional. And if I don't sleep, I'll fail. This knowledge creates pressure around sleep. And pressure prevents sleep.
---

## REFLECTION v09
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I've noticed that my sleep anxiety often includes catastrophizing about the next day. If I don't sleep, I'll be unable to think clearly. I'll make mistakes. People will notice. The consequences will escalate. And this spiral of possible catastrophe keeps me awake. The mechanism is called sleep-related catastrophizing.
---

## REFLECTION v10
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
What I'm realizing is that my sleep anxiety might be connected to something deeper. The feeling that if I stop moving, if I stop being productive, something important will be lost. So sleep becomes a loss of control. And my anxiety is trying to keep me vigilant. The mechanism is harder to name. But it's real.
---

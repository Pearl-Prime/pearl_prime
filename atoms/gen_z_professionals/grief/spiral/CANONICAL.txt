Jordan's mind is very active. The loss happened two months ago. Jordan should be moving past it by now. Probably. That's what the culture says anyway. Two months is long enough to do the initial grief and then start functioning again. But Jordan is still crying sometimes. In the shower. In the car. The crying is fine—grief is fine—but the thinking about the crying is taking up more space now. Jordan is in the spiral: I'm still not over it. It's been two months. I should be over it by now. But I'm not. So something is wrong with me. Or I'm being dramatic. Or—wait, are other people over things faster? Is this a me problem? The jaw starts to tighten. The thinking is very loud. Jordan is working very hard on the grief in the head. The body is still holding the sadness. The two aren't connecting.

---
Jordan's therapist asks: What would happen if you just felt sad without thinking about being sad? Jordan doesn't understand at first. But then the therapist suggests: next time you cry, don't analyze it, just cry. Jordan tries this. It's awkward. The thinking wants to come in and create a narrative about the crying.

Diane keeps a notepad by the bed now. Not for dreams. For the things that surface at 3am — her mother's cardiologist appointment, the insurance form she keeps forgetting, her daughter's lease expiring in June. She writes them down so her brain will stop rehearsing them. It doesn't stop. She fills half a page and lies there watching the ceiling, already calculating how tired she'll be tomorrow, already failing at the meeting she hasn't attended yet.

She told her husband she was fine. She told her doctor she was a little stressed. She did not tell anyone that she'd been waking at 3am for four months with her chest tight, her thoughts already three catastrophes ahead. She's fifty-one. She's held jobs, raised children, buried a father. She doesn't have anxiety — anxious people fall apart and she has never fallen apart. She kept that story for a long time before she noticed she'd stopped accepting invitations, stopped returning calls, stopped sitting still without something to do.

Her doctor mentioned perimenopause and shifting cortisol patterns and Diane nodded like she understood, then drove home and cried in the driveway for ten minutes. Not because it was bad news. Because it had a name. Because her body wasn't betraying her — it was doing something that made sense, that had a mechanism, that she wasn't just failing to control. She sat in the car for a long time before she went inside. She had been so sure it was her fault.

Diane was the person who handled things. The family organized itself around that fact. Her mother called her first. Her children assumed she had capacity. Her employer knew she'd absorb the extra project. When the anxiety started she tried to handle it the way she handled everything — efficiently, privately, by sheer will. It took eighteen months before she understood that the anxiety wasn't a problem to be managed. It was the whole system telling her it needed something different.

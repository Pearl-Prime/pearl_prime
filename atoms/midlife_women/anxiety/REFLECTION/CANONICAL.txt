
## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic

What I notice is that the anxiety no longer requires a reason. It used to. Twenty years ago there was always a specific trigger I could identify: the difficult conversation ahead, the deadline, the uncertainty about a particular outcome. I could name the source and, in naming it, locate it outside myself. Now the mechanism runs independent of event. I wake at 3am and the cortisol is already there, sharp and purposeful, organizing my body for a threat that isn't in the room. My children are asleep down the hall. My husband is beside me. Nothing has happened. And yet the system is fully activated, running its scan, searching for the reason it knows must exist.

What I have come to understand is that perimenopause is not the source of the anxiety. It is the amplifier of an anxiety that was always running beneath the surface, at a level I could manage, a level that looked like conscientiousness and productivity and high standards. The hormonal fluctuations dropped the suppression threshold. The mechanism that was always there became louder. The anxiety that I spent decades channeling into achievement is now audible as itself.

The automatic quality is what I am examining. The nervous system does not ask permission. It does not wait for my rational assessment of whether the threat is real. It fires, and the firing has a physical signature: the chest tightening, the shallow breath, the particular alertness behind the eyes that means sleep is over. By the time I notice the anxiety, it has already organized my body's response. I am already in the preparatory state for something that isn't coming.

What I am learning is that this is not a disorder of perception. The mechanism is operating exactly as it was designed. A nervous system that learned, somewhere early on, that vigilance was the appropriate baseline — that rest was a vulnerability and preparation was the only safety — does not easily surrender that learning. Perimenopause stripped away the hormonal buffer that had been muffling the alarm. What remains is the mechanism itself, visible now in a way it was not before, asking to finally be seen.

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F2
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: cost

The cost is the years of sleep. Not the acute insomnia of crisis, but the accumulated sleep debt of a nervous system that treated nighttime as a continuation of daytime vigilance. I wake not because something is wrong but because my body does not fully believe in the safety of the dark. The scanning continues through sleep. The dreams have an administrative quality — problems to be solved, conversations to be managed, things I have forgotten to do. Even rest becomes a form of work.

The cost is also the relationship with my own body, which I stopped trusting sometime in my late thirties when the anxiety began manifesting physically and I kept attributing physical symptoms to the anxiety rather than the anxiety to the physical state. The tight chest I dismissed. The heart palpitations I filed under stress. The jaw tension I discovered only when my dentist asked about grinding. I had been dissociating from the body's signals in the service of keeping everything moving.

What I lost was the simple animal knowledge of when I was afraid and when I was safe. The mechanism had run so long on automatic that I could not easily feel the difference. I was always at some level of alert. The alert felt normal. The absence of alert, when it occasionally arrived, felt strange, even suspect — as if calm was something I had not yet earned. The cost of the anxiety was not just the wasted nights and the tight shoulders and the sense of dread that arrived without occasion. The cost was the long disconnection from my own interior state, the years of functioning on a fogged-over version of my own experience.

## REFLECTION v03
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: automatic

There is a version of the anxiety I recognize as inheritance. My mother paced. Her mother, from what I was told, woke early and sat alone with her worry in the kitchen before anyone else was up. The women in my family have always been awake before they needed to be, alert before the situation required alertness, prepared for a difficulty that had not yet arrived. I understood this as a character trait before I understood it as a nervous system pattern. Responsible. Careful. On top of things.

What I am examining now is how completely this automatic vigilance shaped my sense of self. I did not experience myself as anxious. I experienced myself as someone who handled things. The anxiety was the engine; the competence was the output. I kept the two separate because the competence was acceptable and the anxiety was not. The anxiety was a weakness. The competence was what the family had always needed.

The mechanism that interests me is the way the anxiety and the identity became indistinguishable. To quiet the anxiety felt like becoming someone who didn't care, someone who let things fall. The alarm and the sense of responsibility had fused. Dismantling one felt like dismantling the other. This is what I mean when I say that perimenopause did not create a new problem. It revealed the architecture that had always been holding the structure of my competent, capable, managing self. What I am left with at 51 is the question: what is beneath the vigilance, and is it enough to build on.

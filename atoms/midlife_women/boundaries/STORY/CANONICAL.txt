## STORY v01
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 1
IDENTITY_STAGE: pre_awareness
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 1
---
Diane's mother calls at 8am. Diane answers. The call takes forty minutes and ends with her mother still dissatisfied and Diane already behind. This happens Monday through Friday. On weekends it happens at 9am. Diane has never mentioned the timing because her mother is seventy-four and lonely and what kind of daughter would that make her. She keeps answering. The resentment she feels during the calls she files under "guilt" and tries to ignore.

## STORY v02
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 2
---
She was forty-seven when she realized she didn't know what she wanted for dinner. It came up because her daughter asked, genuinely curious, and Diane opened her mouth to say something and found nothing there. She had spent so long tracking what everyone else wanted — her mother's preferences, her children's allergies, her husband's moods — that her own appetites had gone quiet. She ordered what she always ordered. Later she sat with that moment, turning it over. It was not about dinner.

## STORY v03
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 2
---
She said no to the committee and felt the room change. The pause before her colleague responded. The slight tilt in her supervisor's attention. She said no clearly and kindly and professionally and spent the next three days certain she had made a catastrophic mistake. The no was reasonable. The aftermath in her body was not. She understood then how many years she had been managing not just her own discomfort but other people's comfort with her compliance.

## STORY v04
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
COST_TYPE: opportunity
COST_INTENSITY: 3
---
Diane started small. She stopped volunteering first. Just watched the ask hang in the air and let someone else pick it up. Her hands shook the first few times. Slowly she moved to harder things: she told her mother she couldn't talk on Wednesdays because she had calls. She told her son she needed twenty-four hours' notice for favors. She told her employer she wouldn't answer email after 7pm. Each one felt enormous. None of them broke anything. She had believed they would. She had been wrong about that for a very long time.

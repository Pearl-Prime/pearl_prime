Her mother calls her by her sister's name now. Then she corrects herself. Then she calls her by her sister's name again. Diane has learned not to correct it. She has learned to just answer, to be the daughter her mother needs in the moment, whatever the moment requires. She manages this gracefully. She manages it so gracefully that her siblings don't realize she goes home afterward and lies on the bathroom floor for twenty minutes, which is the only private space in her life, and lets herself feel it.

She found a photo from fifteen years ago and didn't recognize her own face immediately — not because she looked different but because the expression was different. The woman in the photo looked like she knew what she wanted. Diane stood in the kitchen holding her phone and thought: I know what happened to that woman. I made choices. The choices were reasonable. Some of them were the right choices. And still she looked at that face and felt something she didn't have a clean word for.

The divorce was the event. The grief was not. The grief was the slow understanding of everything she had been telling herself for years — that they were working through something, that it was a phase, that the partnership she'd imagined was still possible if they just had more time. The divorce named what she had been losing for a decade. She was supposed to be relieved. She was also devastated by losses she'd already been living through without calling them that.

## STORY v04
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
COST_TYPE: opportunity
COST_INTENSITY: 2
---
At forty-eight she started noticing the griefs she hadn't named. The body before the pregnancies. The career pivot she hadn't taken. The friendships that had gone quiet under the weight of everyone's full lives. The version of herself who used to travel alone, who made decisions without consulting anyone, who had a life that was less organized around other people's needs. She wasn't sure if this was grief or inventory. Her therapist said it could be both. She said that noticing was the beginning of choosing, and that choosing was still possible. Diane didn't quite believe this yet. She was keeping the idea close.

Diane has been thinking about leaving the firm for three years. She has not told her husband. She has not told her colleagues. She runs the numbers sometimes at night, after everyone is asleep, and the numbers could work, and then she closes the spreadsheet and goes back to bed. The plan exists in a folder on her laptop labeled with a name no one would recognize as significant. She is not ready. She might be ready. She doesn't look too closely.

She finally said it. After eleven years of circling around the same conversation, she sat across from her husband at the kitchen table after the kids were in bed and she said the thing. Not all of it — but the essential, unsayable thing. He was quiet for a long time. Then he said he knew. They talked until 2am. Nothing was resolved that night. But something shifted — some long-held tension she had been carrying in her jaw and her shoulders released slightly. She drove to work the next day feeling like a door had opened somewhere.

She submitted the application on a Tuesday morning before she could talk herself out of it. The position was a lateral move on paper and a complete reinvention underneath — different field, different culture, less money for the first two years, more of what she'd wanted for longer than she'd admitted wanting it. She did not tell anyone until she had a second interview. She was afraid of their practicality, their reasonable concerns, their love dressed up as caution.

Diane got the offer and sat with it for forty-eight hours. Her mother thought she was crazy. Her husband was worried. Her financial spreadsheet was ambivalent. At fifty-one she had survived enough to know that the things she most regretted were not the risks she'd taken but the ones she hadn't. She accepted on Thursday morning. Her hands shook when she hit send. She went to a meeting immediately after and said nothing to anyone. She sat in the meeting feeling like a person who had finally decided something important about her own life.

## STORY v01
---

IDENTITY_STAGE: pre_awareness
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 1
---
Diane checks the retirement account on the first of the month. She has been doing this for two years with the same feeling each time: the number is not enough. She closes the app and returns to what she was doing. She does not make a plan because she doesn't know how to start a plan from behind where she should be, and starting from behind is so disorienting that not starting feels easier. The first of the month comes again.

## STORY v02
---

IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 2
---
She had always believed that if she worked hard and made responsible choices she would be fine. She did work hard. She made responsible choices — including the choice to take leave when her children were small, which had felt responsible at the time and now looked like a $200,000 decision she hadn't known she was making. The financial anxiety isn't just about money. It's about having believed that the math would add up and discovering in her fifties that it hadn't, and not knowing whom to be angry at.

## STORY v03
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
COST_TYPE: opportunity
COST_INTENSITY: 3
---
She was supporting her mother's assisted living, co-signing her son's lease, and still paying down the home equity loan from the renovation. Her own retirement contributions were at the minimum. She tracked all of it in a spreadsheet that she kept color-coded so she could see at a glance that everything was technically fine and simultaneously feel, looking at it, that nothing about it was fine. She kept telling herself she would deal with it next year. This had been true for six years.

## STORY v04
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 2
---
She finally told her financial advisor the whole picture — all of it, including the money she'd lent her son, the care costs she was absorbing, the career gap that cost her the best years of compounding. She expected judgment. Her advisor was calm and specific and said there were paths forward. Diane cried, which embarrassed her. Her advisor said she saw it often: women who had been financially capable their whole lives coming in with the face of someone expecting to be failed. The shame had been compounding too.

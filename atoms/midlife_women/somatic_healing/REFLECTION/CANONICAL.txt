
## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic

The body kept the record I was not keeping consciously. The tight shoulders I had for a decade and attributed to sitting at a desk. The chronic tension in my jaw that my dentist found before I did. The low-grade nausea that appeared before difficult conversations and that I learned to override so automatically I stopped registering it as information. The body was speaking in a continuous language that I had been trained to treat as noise, as inconvenience, as the physical cost of a demanding life rather than as the record of what the demanding life had required of me.

The mechanism I am examining is the dissociation that felt like competence. To function at the level I was functioning, to manage the volume and complexity of my life, required a particular relationship with the body's signals: they needed to be deferred. Not ignored entirely — I responded to acute symptoms when I had to — but the chronic signals, the ones that said slow down, this is too much, something here needs attention, these I became expert at converting into the background. The body's discomfort was the cost of operations. The cost of operations was acceptable. The body would be addressed later. Later was always later.

What I am finding now, in the process of trying to reverse this, is that the body did not simply accumulate stress like water in a container. What accumulated is more complex than that. Specific tensions are located in specific places, and the places are not random. The places correspond, in ways I am slowly learning to read, to the specific demands and suppressions of the years when those tensions were deposited. The shoulder that carries the particular tightness of held responsibility. The place in the chest that holds the unexpressed grief. The body was not just stressed. It was specific.

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F2
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: cost

The cost of the decades of postponed body attention is not primarily the physical symptoms, which are real. The cost is the lost access to a source of information I needed. The body knows things before the mind does. It registers threat before the thinking can name it. It knows when a relationship is wrong, when a choice is not aligned, when the direction is off. I had this resource and I systematically trained myself not to use it, because using it would have slowed me down, would have required me to stop and attend to something that the demands of my life did not have space for.

The cost is also the interrupted relationship between my physical self and my sense of being at home in the world. The body is the instrument through which everything is experienced. When I am not in relationship with my body — when I am managing it rather than inhabiting it — the experience of being alive becomes more abstract, more cognitive, less sensory. I lived in my head for a long time. The body was transport. This is not a neutral arrangement. It is a poverty of experience that I did not recognize as poverty because the cognitive mode was so richly furnished.

The work I am doing now, the slow work of attending to the body's accumulated record, has a quality I did not expect: it is both harder and more rewarding than I anticipated. Harder because the body's record includes material that the mind managed to keep at a manageable distance. The grief that could not be acknowledged has been living somewhere. Meeting it through the body is more direct than meeting it through language. Rewarding because the access, once opened, leads to a quality of presence I had not had for a long time — the felt sense of inhabiting my own life rather than administering it.

## REFLECTION v03
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: automatic

There is a particular quality to what I am learning to call the body's needs — as distinct from the body's demands. The demands are what I was always responsive to: illness, exhaustion past a certain threshold, the injuries that required treatment. Those I addressed. The needs are different. The needs are what the body requires not when it reaches crisis but in the ordinary course of a life: rest that is genuinely restful, movement that is pleasurable rather than compensatory, touch that is not transactional, stillness that is allowed to be complete rather than interrupted by the next obligation.

The automatic quality I am examining is the way the body's needs were systematically deprioritized not by any single decision but by the accumulated default of a life organized around provision. The body got what was left over. The body got the exercise that fit the schedule, the sleep that fit the calendar, the care that could be managed around everyone else's care. The body was served last and this was not experienced as neglect because the body was not, after all, in crisis. The threshold for attending to the body had been set high enough that ordinary need did not register as need at all.

The somatic healing that I am engaged in now is not dramatic. I am not doing anything that looks, from the outside, like an intensive intervention. I am learning to notice the body before it reaches the threshold that demands response. I am learning to receive physical care — the massage, the rest, the stillness — without converting it immediately into guilt or restlessness. I am learning that the body's needs are not interruptions of the real work. They are what makes the real work possible. This is knowledge I had intellectually for years. The project now is installing it somewhere that the body itself can recognize.

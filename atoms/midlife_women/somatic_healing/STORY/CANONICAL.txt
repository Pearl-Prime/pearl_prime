## STORY v01
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 1
IDENTITY_STAGE: pre_awareness
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 1
---
Diane's shoulders are always tight. This has been true for so long she doesn't think of it as tight anymore, just as her shoulders. Her jaw clenches at night; she wears a guard. Her low back aches on Tuesdays after the Monday calls with her mother. She takes ibuprofen and keeps moving. The body is a logistical issue to be managed, not a conversation to be had. She is efficient at not noticing what it costs her to be efficient.

## STORY v02
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 2
---
She started the yoga class because her doctor suggested it and she expected to be bad at lying still. She was bad at lying still. The instructor guided them through a body scan — ten minutes of just noticing — and Diane got to her chest and felt something she couldn't name: a constriction that wasn't physical pain but was something, had been something for a long time. She cried briefly during savasana and was embarrassed and then was not. Something had been waiting for her to stop moving long enough to acknowledge it.

## STORY v03
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 2
---
Her therapist suggested somatic work and Diane had been skeptical because she was practical and evidence-based and this was not how she thought about her problems. She went anyway. The somatic therapist asked her to notice what happened in her body when she thought about her mother. Diane said: I don't know, I feel tense. The therapist said: where. Diane sat with the question and found the answer in her throat and chest, specific and located, and was surprised to discover that her body had been tracking something her mind had been explaining away.

## STORY v04
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
COST_TYPE: opportunity
COST_INTENSITY: 1
---
She started giving her body twenty minutes a day. Not exercise — she already had exercise. This was different: she would lie on the floor and breathe and pay attention. The first few weeks felt like wasted time. The second month she started noticing patterns: she held her breath on work calls. Her stomach contracted when certain names appeared on her phone. Her shoulders descended two inches when she left the city for the weekend. The body was keeping records. She had just started reading them.

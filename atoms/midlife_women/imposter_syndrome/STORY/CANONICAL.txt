Diane has been preparing the same way for twenty years. Every presentation: two extra rehearsals, every backup scenario mapped, every potential question anticipated. She is never underprepared. Her colleague Marcus prepares for thirty minutes and speaks with the same confidence she projects after three days of prep. She knows the disparity. She has never told anyone about the gap between her internal experience and the competence she displays.

She was passed over for director in 2019. Her male colleague got it; she was told she needed more executive presence. She had been managing his team's dysfunction informally for two years. She took the feedback and worked on executive presence and got promoted in 2021 and has been one of the best directors they have and still, still, in certain rooms, she feels the old contraction — the slight shrinking, the voice taking on a smaller register, the instinct to make herself easier to dismiss before they dismiss her.

She didn't apply for the VP role the first time it opened. She looked at the job description and found the three things she didn't have and focused there, which was not how she evaluated her male colleagues for the same opportunity. She noticed this two years later when she was watching a colleague she had mentored prepare his application for a role he was equally unqualified for. He applied anyway. He got the role. She is still his senior by every measure that matters.

She submitted the VP application on the deadline day, having rewritten the personal statement four times. She was objectively one of the strongest candidates and she knew this and it still took everything she had to send it. When she got the offer she sat with it for a day before she felt anything other than the waiting for the catch. Then her daughter asked how she felt. Diane thought about it and said: like I've been trying to prove something for twenty years and I'm finally tired of trying and I'm just going to do the job. Her daughter said that sounded right.

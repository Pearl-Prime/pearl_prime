Diane doesn't call it depression. She calls it tired, stretched, going through a lot. She gets through the days. She shows up for everything. She laughs at the right moments. She is functional, present, capable. It is only late at night, when the house is quiet and there's nothing left to manage, that she notices the flatness underneath everything — the sense that she is moving through her own life at a slight remove, watching it happen rather than living it.

COST_INTENSITY: 2

She used to know what she liked. Wine with specific opinions about it. The particular pleasure of a long run on a quiet morning. Strong feelings about novels. At forty-eight she noticed those preferences had gone quiet — not lost, exactly, just inaccessible. She kept buying wine and running and reading, going through the motions of the person she used to be, but the feeling of it had flattened. She mentioned this to her doctor as a side note. Her doctor wanted to talk about it more. Diane said she was probably just busy.

## STORY v03
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 2
---
Her performance reviews were excellent. She was managing her mother's care, her children's crises, her household, her team. From any external measure she was doing well. The depression looked like nothing from the outside because she never let it slow her down. She just felt it in everything she did — the extra effort it took to initiate, to feel interested, to believe that what she was doing mattered. Functioning is not the same as living. She had stopped being able to tell the difference.

## STORY v04
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
COST_TYPE: opportunity
COST_INTENSITY: 3
---
Her psychiatrist explained the neurobiology of perimenopause-adjacent depression and Diane sat there and felt simultaneously educated and furious. Years of this, she thought. Years of this and she'd spent them believing it was a character issue, a resilience deficit, a failure to be grateful enough. She had deprioritized herself so thoroughly that her own brain had started working against her and she hadn't recognized it as medical. She started medication with a level of resistance that surprised the doctor. She didn't feel she deserved to be better.

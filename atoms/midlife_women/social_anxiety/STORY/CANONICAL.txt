She canceled the dinner, then the book club, then the birthday party. Each time she had a reason that was true: she was tired, she had a lot going on, she needed an early night. She started staying home more. The staying home felt like failure and also like the only thing she could bear. She made the excuses and felt guilty about the excuses and then felt relieved to be at home, alone, with nothing left to perform for anyone.

People described Diane as warm and socially confident and she had been, for a long time, both of those things without qualification. She still behaved as both. But the experience of it had changed. Parties felt effortful in a way they hadn't before. The drive home after a work function left her depleted in a way that used to take a full conference, not a cocktail hour. She didn't talk about it because the description sounded like something was wrong with her, and she was fine, and the last thing she needed was one more thing to be dealing with.

She was the kind of person who collected people. Large social world, easy in groups, genuinely enjoyed the noise and energy of a full table. She had built her identity partly on being that person — the connector, the hostess, the one whose house was always open. The contraction surprised her. She didn't know who she was if she was not also that. She started declining slowly and felt the shape of her social world changing and wasn't sure yet whether she was losing something or finding something out.

She started accepting only invitations she was genuinely glad to have. The list was shorter. She arrived at dinner with a different quality of presence — less performance, more actual. Her old friend said over the main course: you seem different. Diane said she was figuring out what she could sustain. Her friend said she seemed more like herself, actually. Diane turned that over for a long time. She had been maintaining an older version of herself, a version built for a different bandwidth. The smaller, genuine version turned out to have its own vitality.

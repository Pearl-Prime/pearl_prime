Diane's Tuesday calendar: 7am call with her mother about the medications. 9am school meeting for Marcus. 11am client presentation. 2pm follow up with her father's home care coordinator. 4pm performance review prep. 7pm her daughter needed her to proofread the college essay. She got through all of it. She always got through all of it. She just couldn't remember what she'd eaten or whether she'd liked any of it.

## STORY v02
---

IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
COST_TYPE: identity
COST_INTENSITY: 2
---
She used to love her job. She knew that because she could remember loving it — the problem-solving, the people, the days that went fast because the work was interesting. Now the days go fast because there are too many of them and she can't locate the part of herself that used to find them interesting. She keeps performing engagement because she is professional and her team depends on her. But she is performing it. That is the thing she hasn't told anyone.

## STORY v03
---

IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 2
---
Her friend called it the sandwich and Diane laughed because what else do you do. Her mother's memory was going. Her son was in crisis. Her daughter was applying to colleges that cost more than her first house. Her marriage was functioning at a maintenance level — two people managing a household, not a couple. The job kept demanding more. She was managing six lives simultaneously and she was doing it, she was doing it, she just couldn't find herself in any of them.

## STORY v04
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
COST_TYPE: opportunity
COST_INTENSITY: 3
---
Diane took three days off and spent them catching up. She couldn't not. The inbox followed her, the calls followed her, the invisible list ran in the back of her head the whole time. When she returned to work she was not rested. She was exactly as tired, just three days more behind. She sat at her desk on Monday morning and understood something she had been avoiding: rest would never happen in the gaps. She would have to make a different arrangement entirely. She had no idea how.

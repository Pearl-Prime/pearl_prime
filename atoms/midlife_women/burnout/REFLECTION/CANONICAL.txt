
## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic

The depletion math is not complicated once I write it down, but for years I refused to do the arithmetic. Here are the simultaneous demands that were running in the year I finally stopped being able to pretend I was fine: my mother's increasing confusion and the administrative load of her care. My father's health crisis and the hospital visits and the phone calls from doctors who expected me to translate between the medical system and the family. My sixteen-year-old's mental health struggles and the daily labor of holding that space without alarming my partner or the younger children. My job, which required full presence five days a week and invisible labor most evenings. The household, which did not manage itself. The friendships I was not maintaining but not releasing, which accumulated as a background guilt. And the marriage, which needed things from me that I was not capable of providing because I was empty by the time I reached it.

The mechanism of burnout is not mysterious. It is a depletion calculation that goes negative and stays there. The inputs fell below the outputs. For long enough. The nervous system, which had been maintaining itself through adrenaline and willpower and the particular dopamine hit of being needed, could no longer sustain the override. The system began failing in small ways that I rationalized. The crying in the car before I walked into the office. The inability to make simple decisions. The flat quality that descended on the things that used to give me pleasure.

What I am examining is not why I burned out — the arithmetic is obvious — but why I could not see it while it was happening. The mechanism that drove the burnout was the same mechanism that prevented me from recognizing it. I had learned to read my own exhaustion as a signal to try harder. When the system was failing, I interpreted the failure as a performance problem rather than a supply problem. I needed to manage better, organize better, be more efficient. The idea that the problem was structural — that no one could have sustained this load — did not arrive until the system had been failing for eighteen months.

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F2
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: cost

What the burnout cost was the ability to feel genuine care. Not the performance of care — that held for longer than it should have, because the performance was so automatic I could produce it even when nothing underneath was generating it. But the real thing, the felt sense of caring about the people in front of me, the quality of presence that is the only thing that makes the caregiving worth anything — that went first. I was dispensing the appropriate responses, showing up in the right places, saying the things I knew were required. The interior was dim.

The cost I am still counting is the compound interest of the years I spent operating on empty. The decisions I made during that period carry the quality of decisions made in fog. The friendships I let lapse because maintaining them required more than I had. The professional opportunities I declined because I had no bandwidth for new demands. The conversations with my children that were responses rather than genuine meetings — I was there, I was present in the technical sense, but I was not fully in the room.

The deepest cost is harder to name. It is something like the loss of my own interior as a place I could access. Burnout hollows. It does not take things you can see from the outside. It removes access to your own experience. I could tell you what everyone else needed. I could not tell you what I was feeling, because there was a layer of exhaustion so thick between me and the feeling that reaching through it was no longer possible. I didn't know that was what was happening. I thought I was just tired.

## REFLECTION v03
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: automatic

The thing about being the person everyone needs is that the role compounds over time. Once you have demonstrated a capacity for management, for reliable presence, for holding the complicated together without complaint, the assignment becomes permanent. I did not resent this when I accepted it. I accepted it because it was true — I was capable, I was organized, I cared enough to do it well. But the role did not have an end date. The expectation that I would continue performing it did not account for the cost of the performance.

The automatic quality of the over-functioning is what I examine now. I did not decide to become the family's administrative center, the emotional hub, the person through whom all the scheduling and planning and medical coordination and emotional processing flowed. I became it incrementally, through a series of small choices that were each individually rational. Each time I stepped in, the step was justified. The cumulative pattern was not visible from inside any single step.

What I notice, looking back, is that I never built in a review mechanism. I never asked whether the distribution was sustainable, whether the roles had calcified in a way that had stopped reflecting anyone's actual capacity. The distribution that had made sense in 2012 was still running in 2021 without modification, absorbing a decade's worth of additional complexity — aging parents, adolescent crises, career evolution — without any corresponding adjustment in who carried what. The burnout was the system's forced review. I did not schedule the review. It arrived on its own terms, and by that point it had opinions.

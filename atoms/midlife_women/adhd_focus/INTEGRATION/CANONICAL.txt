## INTEGRATION v01
---
---
Tomorrow, before you touch the task you've been circling, do one thing: shrink it until it's almost embarrassingly small. Not "clean the office," but "move one pile six inches." Not "call the doctor," but "find the phone number and put it in a note titled TOMORROW." Write the tiny version down somewhere you'll see it in the morning — the bathroom mirror, the coffee maker, the lock screen — so you don't have to remember to shrink it again, you just execute the shrunk version that's waiting for you. Do only that. Do not let yourself keep going and finish the whole task in a burst of momentum guilt — that's a different day's work, and turning the small step into a springboard for the whole task retrains your brain to fear small steps, because it learns "small steps turn into big obligations." Let the small step be the whole win, closed and complete, even if the folder is still on the table. Tomorrow evening, notice only whether the small thing got easier to start than the big thing was — that's the only data point that matters, and it's the one that will tell you whether to keep shrinking your tasks this way or adjust the size.

## INTEGRATION v02
---
---
Tomorrow, pick the one task on your list that has gone gray — the one you've looked at so many times it no longer registers as anything. Don't try to feel more motivated about it; that lever is broken and pulling it again will just waste the day. Instead, change something about how it looks. Move it to a different surface. Write one word on a bright sticky note and place it somewhere new. Put the folder in the passenger seat instead of the counter. The goal isn't to make the task more urgent — it's to make it visually unfamiliar again for a few seconds, long enough for your attention to snag on it the way it would on something new. Notice, tomorrow, the first moment your eye catches on it differently than it has all week. That flicker of "oh, that" is the whole mechanism working. You don't have to act on it immediately. You just have to let yourself notice that the gray thing briefly wasn't gray, and trust that a little more of that, repeated, is how importance becomes visible to a brain that runs on salience instead of shoulds.

## INTEGRATION v03
---
---
Tomorrow morning, before the day fills in around you, take the one task with a deadline that still feels far away and give it a physical presence three days early. Not a calendar reminder — those get swiped. An actual object, in your actual path: the form on the driver's seat, the empty container on the counter, the shoes by the door that mean "this errand." Let the object do the work your internal clock won't do on its own, which is make a distant date feel close before it's actually close. You are not trying to feel the urgency in advance — you likely can't, and that's fine, that's not a moral failure, it's just how your particular clock runs. You're trying to build a physical tripwire so that when the felt sense of "now" finally does arrive, as it always eventually does, you're not starting from zero. Notice tomorrow evening whether the object in your path changed anything about how many times you thought about the task, even if you didn't act yet. Early awareness, even without action, is the object doing its job.

## INTEGRATION v04
---
---
Tomorrow, before you start the task you've been avoiding, write down its steps — not in your head, on an actual piece of paper or the notes app, in front of you, three to five short lines. Then, and this is the part that matters, keep the list visible the entire time you work, the way a person standing next to you would keep the structure visible without you having to hold it yourself. You are building your own body double out of paper. When you lose the thread mid-task, which you will, look at the list instead of trying to reconstruct the plan from memory — that reconstruction is exactly the tax your working memory can't afford right now, and the list exists so you never have to pay it. This is not a lesser way of working. It is not "cheating" toward a version of productivity that doesn't need scaffolding. Notice tomorrow whether having the steps outside your head, instead of inside it, changes how far you get before you stall.

## INTEGRATION v05
---
---
Tomorrow, when you catch yourself circling a task with that specific flavor of low-grade dread — the kind that's been going on for days without the task actually growing — stop and say out loud, to yourself or to anyone nearby, exactly what the task requires in real minutes. Not "I have to deal with the email." Say: "This is answering one question in three sentences." Notice the gap between how heavy the task has felt and how light it sounds once you name it in plain, literal terms. That gap is the interest your avoidance has been quietly charging you, and naming the task accurately, out loud, is how you stop the meter. You don't need to do the task the moment you name it, though you might find you already have the momentum. The point tomorrow is just to practice catching the inflation before it compounds another day, because the earlier you catch it, the smaller the gap is between how it feels and what it actually is.

## INTEGRATION v06
---
---
Tomorrow, notice the next time you feel dread about contacting an institution — a school, a clinic, an insurance line, anything with hold music in your memory. Before you let the dread decide the task is hard, ask yourself one specific question: what exactly am I picturing right now, and when did I last confirm it's still accurate? Most of these mental pictures are years old, built from a bad experience that may no longer apply — the portal that now has a two-click reset, the office that now takes texts, the form that's now online. You're not being asked to override the dread through bravery. You're being asked to fact-check it, the way you'd fact-check an old rumor before repeating it. If the picture turns out to be accurate, fine — at least now you know what you're actually dealing with. If it's outdated, which it often is, the task usually turns out to be smaller than the dread it's been generating.

## INTEGRATION v07
---
---
Tomorrow, take the private plan you've been quietly renegotiating with yourself for days — the errand, the project, the phone call you keep meaning to get to — and make it social in one small, low-stakes way. Text someone a specific time. Tell your spouse out loud what you're starting and when. Put it on a calendar someone else actually glances at. You're not doing this because you can't be trusted alone with your own intentions — you're doing this because a plan that exists only in your head has no friction keeping it in place, and you already know from experience that your private deadlines move whenever you're tired, which is often. Let one other person, even peripherally, become a witness to the plan. Notice tomorrow whether knowing someone else has a rough idea of your timeline changes how easily you let yourself push it.

## INTEGRATION v08
---
---
Tomorrow, before you try to tackle the pile that's been sitting untouched — the inbox, the counter, the tabs — don't start working yet. First, just count it. Say the number out loud or write it down: "six things." Then do the smallest one and recount: "five things." Let the shrinking number be the motivator instead of the vague sense that you "should" deal with the whole pile. You are not tricking your brain, you are feeding it the one kind of evidence it actually responds to: a visible, finite, dropping count instead of an abstract, open-ended obligation. Notice tomorrow at what number, if any, you start to feel a pull to keep going rather than stop — that number is useful information about how your particular brain likes its piles portioned.

## INTEGRATION v09
---
---
Tomorrow, notice the task you've been waiting for "the right moment" to do — the paperwork, the errand, the phone call you keep deferring until things are calmer. Before you defer it again, ask plainly: does this actually require what I think it requires, or have I just always pictured it that way? Try doing it in the least ideal setting available — standing in the kitchen, in the car before you turn the key, on your phone in a waiting room. You may find the task fits into conditions you'd never have chosen, because the "right moment" was a story about how the task should be done, not a real requirement of the task itself. Notice tomorrow how much smaller and more portable the task turns out to be once you strip away the imagined staging you'd built around it.

## INTEGRATION v10
---
---
Tomorrow, if you have something due in a few days that you already suspect won't get real attention until the deadline is close, stop pretending otherwise. Open your calendar right now and block the actual evening you predict you'll do it — not an aspirational earlier date, the honest one, the night before or the night urgency will hit. Protect that block on purpose, tell your family it's happening, treat it as the real plan rather than the backup plan. You are not giving up on becoming an early starter. You are building a system for the brain you actually have, the one that ignites on urgency, so that when the predictable crunch arrives, you have a protected hour waiting instead of a wasted evening on top of it. Notice tomorrow whether naming the honest plan in advance, instead of the aspirational one, reduces how much you dread the days leading up to it.

## INTEGRATION v11
---
---
Tomorrow, take the task that's been stuck in your head as a heavy, vague category — "deal with the finances," "sort out mom's stuff," "figure out the resume" — and say it out loud to another person, even someone with no stake in it. Ask them nothing. Just describe it, as if you had to make it make sense to a stranger. Listen to what comes out of your own mouth: you'll likely hear yourself land on two or three concrete steps you hadn't consciously identified while it was just swirling silently in your head. Write those steps down the moment you hear yourself say them, before they dissolve back into vagueness. Notice tomorrow how different the spoken version of the task feels from the version that's been living, unexamined, in your head all week.

## INTEGRATION v12
---
---
Tomorrow, pick the task you've opened and closed without acting on more than twice this week, and instead of berating yourself about it, make a small, neutral tally of it — a mark on a sticky note each time you open it and don't act. Don't attach a lecture to the marks. Just count. Notice, as the marks accumulate over the day, whether there's a point where the sheer visible pattern of your own avoidance strikes you as almost funny rather than shameful — that shift, from verdict to data, is the whole mechanism, and it tends to arrive faster than any amount of self-scolding ever did. If you dial, call, or click through before the day's done, good. If you don't, keep the tally anyway; it's useful information either way about exactly where the snag is.

## STORY v01
---
---
Marisol's tax folder finally moves on a Tuesday, not because of willpower but because her son needs the printer, and the folder is in the way of the printer, and moving a folder out of the way of a printer is not the same task, in her brain, as "do the taxes." She sets it on the table to get it out of the printer's path. It lands open. A single receipt slides out — the one from the dentist, the big one, the one she's needed all along. She looks at it. She looks at the laptop, already open for something else entirely. Fourteen minutes later, without ever having decided to "start the taxes," she has entered three numbers into the form. The folder is still messy. The receipt is filed. Marisol learns that she doesn't do the big scary task — she does the tiny adjacent thing that happens to be sitting right in front of her.

## STORY v02
---
---
Denise doesn't close the volunteer tab. Instead, one night, scrolling for nothing, she notices the tab's favicon has changed color — the fair's website updated its branding. The small visual shift catches her eye in a way the text never did. She clicks over out of pure curiosity about the redesign, not obligation. While she's there, glancing at the new layout, she sees her own name still sitting in a "returning volunteers" list from last year. Something about seeing her name already there, already claimed, makes the form feel less like a decision and more like a formality. She fills in the three new fields in ninety seconds. Denise learns that six days of guilt did nothing; one accidental glance, driven by novelty instead of duty, did everything.

## STORY v03
---
---
Her resume sits unfinished until her neighbor mentions, over the fence, that her company is hiring for something adjacent to what Priya used to do. Priya doesn't go inside and open the file. She stands at the fence and, out loud, tells her neighbor what she used to do all day — the systems she managed, the team she ran. Her neighbor says, "Wait, say that first part again, that's exactly what they need." Priya goes inside, still hearing her own voice in her head, and for the first time in two weeks, types instead of formats. She learns that the file was never the problem. The silence around the file was — she couldn't start writing about herself into a blank document, but she could talk about herself to a person, and then just transcribe the talking.

## STORY v04
---
---
Jean's mother's pill organizer gets filled at 6:40 on a Sunday morning, not the night before as planned, because Jean wakes up at 6:38 with the specific, bright, adrenaline-lit thought that today is the day her mother runs out. The dread she felt all week did not move her. The sudden, close deadline did. She drives over in pajama pants. Her mother, unbothered, is already up making tea. Jean fills the organizer in four minutes flat while the kettle boils, then sits down and has tea she didn't expect to have. Jean learns that her brain will not respond to "this matters." It will respond to "this is happening in the next hour," and there is no use being ashamed of which lever actually works.

## STORY v05
---
---
Angela's insurance folder finally gets used when her daughter, home from college, opens the laptop to print a boarding pass and the folder icon is right there on the desktop, in her way. "What's this?" her daughter asks. Angela explains the claim in one sentence. Her daughter, twenty and impatient with paperwork in the way she is impatient with all inefficiency, says, "Just upload all four now, I'll watch you." Angela does it while her daughter watches, narrates, mildly bored. It takes six minutes. Angela has needed a witness, not a reminder, the entire time. She learns that her stuck tasks move fastest when someone else is simply present and expects to see them move — not nagging, just watching.

## STORY v06
---
---
Renee's five-minute-favor email gets answered because she's cleaning out her inbox for an unrelated reason — trying to find a warranty receipt — and the email resurfaces in the search results. Seeing it out of context, stripped of the three days of guilt she'd built around it, it reads simply as three sentences requesting a phone number. She answers it in the same breath as finding the receipt, task-switched without noticing she'd switched. Renee learns that her avoidance had inflated the email into something large and consequential in her mind, but the actual task, encountered sideways, was almost nothing.

## STORY v07
---
---
Terry's financial aid portal login is recovered not through the school's IT line, which she dreaded calling, but because her daughter, venting about the same portal for an unrelated reason, says "oh just use the forgot-password thing, it's instant." Terry hadn't considered this because in her head "login problem" meant "phone call to bureaucracy," a category of task her brain had filed as unbearable. Removing the imagined phone call removed the entire block. She resets it in ninety seconds while her daughter is still talking. Terry learns to ask, before assuming a task is hard: what specifically am I picturing, and is that picture even accurate?

## STORY v08
---
---
Kathy's garage sale stickers get used the week she almost throws out the whole box during a fit of decluttering — and stops herself only because throwing away unused stickers feels, absurdly, more wasteful than the sale never happening. So she picks one Saturday, texts three neighbors "doing a driveway sale Saturday, want in," and the shared date, set by committee instead of by her alone, holds in a way her private plan never did. She learns that a plan that lives only in her own head has no friction to keep it real; a plan witnessed by other people, even loosely, becomes load-bearing.

## STORY v09
---
---
Fran's six tabs collapse into action when her phone buzzes with a reminder for something unrelated — a haircut — and in the two seconds before she dismisses it, she notices the tab bar behind the notification, all those small silent flags. Seeing them all at once, as a row instead of one at a time, she feels the size of the pile rather than any single item's weight. She picks the shortest one — the return label — because it's visibly the fastest, closes it, and the row is now five instead of six. That tiny visual shrink is satisfying enough that she does the mammogram scheduling next. Fran learns that her tabs weren't a to-do list to her brain; they were wallpaper, until she saw them as a shrinking row instead of static clutter.

## STORY v10
---
---
Lisa's permission slip finally gets signed in the school parking lot, at pickup, because another mother pulls out a pen to sign her own copy on the hood of her car and asks, "you got yours too, right?" Lisa has hers, damp and folded, in her purse from four days of migration. She signs it on the same car hood, borrowed pen, no table, no quiet moment, none of the conditions she'd been unconsciously waiting for. She learns that she'd been waiting for a "good moment to sit down with it," a moment that was never coming and was never actually required.

## STORY v11
---
---
Monica's self-review gets written at 11pm the night before it's due, not because she finally disciplined herself, but because the deadline crossed from "abstract future thing" into "tomorrow," and her brain, which had been utterly unmoved by the bullet points all week, suddenly treated the task as if it were on fire. She writes the whole thing in forty minutes, tears in her eyes at how easy it turns out to be. She learns, with some frustration, that her brain was never going to do this early no matter what she promised herself, and that the honest move is to schedule the real work for the night the deadline turns hot, instead of shaming herself for missing the calm-week window that was never going to work.

## STORY v12
---
---
Barb's rollover envelope gets opened the day her husband, unprompted, moves it off the piano to dust underneath, and says, mildly, "what's this been doing here?" Barb, caught without a ready deflection, actually explains what's inside it — and hearing herself describe it out loud, the task shrinks from "handle my retirement" to "sign three lines and mail one envelope." She does it that afternoon. Barb learns that saying a task out loud, to someone who has no stake in her shame about it, does more diagnostic work than any amount of private planning.

## STORY v13
---
---
Sandra's cardiologist call finally happens because she starts, on a whim, tracking every time she opens and closes that contact without calling — a tally on a sticky note, mostly out of dark curiosity about her own behavior. By the fourth tally mark that day, the sheer visible absurdity of it — four open-closes, zero calls — makes her laugh once, short and surprised, and dial on the fifth. The call takes ninety seconds and the receptionist is kind. Sandra learns that shame never got her to dial, but a small, almost funny data point about her own avoidance did.

## STORY v14
---
---
The thank-you note gets written when her son, home from college for the weekend, asks whether she ever finished the cards, and offers, "want me to just write the words and you copy them, that's basically what I do." Sandra — no, this is a different one, Aunt Carol's card — sits with her son at the kitchen table while he drafts two sentences out loud and she writes them in her own handwriting on the card. It takes four minutes. She learns that the blank card wasn't the obstacle. The invented pressure to compose something meaningful from scratch, alone, was — and that borrowing someone else's first draft doesn't make the sentiment less hers.

## STORY v15
---
---
The mammogram gets scheduled not on the day Fran means to do it but on the day her friend, texting about an unrelated lunch plan, mentions she just went for hers. Fran replies, half-joking, "ugh I still haven't," and her friend, still in the text thread, says, "call now, I'll wait." Fran calls with her friend's messages sitting open beside her, an audience of one who isn't even in the room, and books the appointment in the time it takes her friend to reply "see?? told you." Fran learns that a witness doesn't have to be present to work — just present enough in her mind to make the task real instead of hypothetical.

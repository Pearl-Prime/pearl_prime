## COMPRESSION v01
---
---
The intention-action gap: knowing and doing run on separate wiring, and thinking about a task repeatedly doesn't bridge them. Willpower operates on the "should" side; the gap is on the "do" side. Fix: shrink the next physical step until it's small enough that starting doesn't register as a decision — move the folder, open the file, dial the first three digits. You're not building discipline. You're routing around a wiring gap that discipline was never going to close.
---

## COMPRESSION v02
---
---
Attention runs on salience, not importance. A task that looks identical every day goes invisible regardless of how much it matters, while a novel, visually different version of the same task suddenly gets noticed. Fix: manufacture salience on purpose — move the object, change its color, place it somewhere unfamiliar. Don't wait to feel more motivated about something important; make it look new instead, because your attention system responds to novelty, not to shoulds.
---

## COMPRESSION v03
---
---
Time blindness isn't misjudging duration — it's the absence of a felt sense of proximity until a deadline is suddenly, completely now. Dread doesn't fix this, because dread tracks importance, not nearness. Fix: convert time into space. Put the deadline's object in your physical path, days early, so proximity becomes visible instead of relying on an internal clock that won't generate the warning on its own.
---

## COMPRESSION v04
---
---
Working memory has a fixed capacity, and a household's worth of open loops keeps it near empty. Multi-step tasks stall not from laziness but from nowhere to hold "step one, then two, then three" while also executing. Fix: externalize the steps — on paper, in front of you, kept visible throughout — or borrow another person's presence as a temporary structure-holder. Either way, you stop trying to hold the plan and yourself at once.
---

## COMPRESSION v05
---
---
Avoidance inflates task size. Each day a task sits untouched, the guilt of avoiding it gets mistaken for evidence of difficulty, until a three-sentence email feels like a genuine burden. Fix: name the literal, stripped-down size out loud before trusting the dread — "this is three sentences" — and let the plain fact deflate the accumulated interest avoidance has been charging.
---

## COMPRESSION v06
---
---
Stuck tasks often trace to an outdated mental picture, not the current reality. A category like "call the school" keeps its old dread-tag long after the underlying task changed to a two-click reset. Fix: before deciding a task is hard, ask what you're specifically picturing and when you last confirmed it's accurate — you're fact-checking your dread, not overriding it with bravery.
---

## COMPRESSION v07
---
---
A private plan has no friction holding it in place — you're a lenient judge of your own postponements, and "this weekend" can silently become "next weekend" with nobody noticing. Fix: make the plan social on purpose. Text a person a date, say a start time out loud, put it somewhere someone else glances at. Borrowed external structure isn't a weakness; it's the tool that reliably works when private resolve doesn't.
---

## COMPRESSION v08
---
---
An unbounded pile reads as infinite and is therefore unstartable; a visibly bounded, shrinking pile has an ending your brain can compute. Motivation from "this matters" is unreliable; motivation from a dropping number is concrete and immediate. Fix: count the pile out loud before starting, then recount after each item, and let the shrinking number do the motivating instead of waiting to feel ready.
---

## COMPRESSION v09
---
---
Many stalled tasks are stalled on invisible, self-imposed prerequisites — a table, a pen, a calm mind — that the task doesn't actually require. The "right moment" is often a staging story, not a real condition. Fix: interrogate the imagined requirement directly, then attempt the task in the least ideal available setting. Most paperwork survives being signed standing up.
---

## COMPRESSION v10
---
---
For a brain that ignites on urgency, the calm early window for starting a task essentially doesn't exist, no matter how many times you promise yourself otherwise. Shaming yourself for missing it just adds a second failure on top of the first. Fix: schedule and protect the honest night — the one closest to the deadline — as the actual plan, not the backup plan, and build the system around the brain you have.
---

## COMPRESSION v11
---
---
Tasks held only in your head tend to balloon, because your brain fills ambiguous gaps with worst-case texture; spoken language forces vagueness into specifics because you can't say "it's just big and scary" to a real listener without eventually naming actual steps. Fix: narrate the stuck task out loud to anyone, with no stake required, and write down the concrete steps the moment you hear yourself say them.
---

## COMPRESSION v12
---
---
Shame produces a verdict ("you're bad at this"), and verdicts make you flinch away from evidence instead of examining it; a neutral tally produces a fact, and facts are easier to act on because there's nothing to defend against. Fix: track your own avoidance lightly and visibly — a tally mark each time you open and don't act — and let the almost-funny pattern break the freeze that shame never could.
---

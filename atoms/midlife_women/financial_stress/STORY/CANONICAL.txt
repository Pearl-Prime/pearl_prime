Diane keeps two spreadsheets. One for her household. One for her mother's care costs. She doesn't let herself look at them at the same time. Looking at them at the same time makes the numbers do something to her chest that she can't describe. She manages each one separately, efficiently, with precision. The spreadsheets are never wrong. She is always slightly short by the time the month ends, and she moves money around from accounts she has named things like "emergency" and "future" and she doesn't think about what that means.

She took the career break when her children were small and took it again when her father was dying and the compounding she lost during those years is not theoretical. She has calculated it: roughly $200,000 in lifetime earnings, less in her 401(k) at peak compounding years, a career trajectory that had to restart twice. She doesn't regret the choices. She regrets that no one told her the cost in advance. She regrets that the cost was hers alone to absorb.

---
Her siblings assume she's handling the parents' finances because she's always handled things, because she lives closest, because she didn't make a fuss when it started. They send Christmas cards and check in occasionally and ask how things are going, meaning: are the parents okay. Not meaning: how much is this costing you, is it sustainable, do you need us to do anything. Diane says it's fine. It costs her approximately $1,200 a month above what the parents' own funds cover. She has not told them this.

She called her brother on a Sunday and told him the actual number. She had been working up to this for two years. He was quiet. Then he said he hadn't realized. She almost said it's fine. She didn't. She said she needed help with it. He asked what he could do. She had a list ready because she had been making it, secretly, for months — specific amounts, specific responsibilities, specific months where the gap was biggest. He agreed to three of the five items. She hung up and shook for ten minutes. She had not known how much the silence had been costing her too.

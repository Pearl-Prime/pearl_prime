Diane keeps notes on her phone now from the 3am wake. The notes are practical — things she would forget by morning, appointments to make, calls to return. She has been doing this for eight months. The notes have accumulated into a kind of diary of her insomnia. Reading back through them she can see what she thinks about at 3am: her mother's decline, her son's semester, the retirement account, whether the marriage is okay. The same concerns, cycling, night after night, unresolved.

## STORY v02

COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 2
---
She has read about sleep hygiene. She does most of it. The bedroom is cool. She doesn't look at her phone until the morning. She has tried magnesium and melatonin and going to bed earlier and going to bed later. The sleep problem is not a discipline problem. She knows this, technically. But at 2am, when she's been awake for an hour, she still feels like she is failing at sleep, like the inability to sleep is a character judgment, like she should be able to will herself into unconsciousness if she were stronger or calmer or had handled things better.

## STORY v03
---

IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
COST_TYPE: opportunity
COST_INTENSITY: 2
---
The exhaustion was cumulative and then it was chronic and then she stopped remembering what not-tired felt like. She was managing on six interrupted hours and caffeine and the sheer discipline of having no alternative. Her doctor asked about her sleep as a routine question and Diane started to give the routine answer and then suddenly couldn't. She said: I haven't slept through the night in two years. Her doctor asked follow-up questions. They talked for fifteen minutes. No one had asked before.

## STORY v04
---
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 1
---
Her doctor prescribed low-dose progesterone and said it helped many patients with perimenopause-related sleep disruption and Diane filled the prescription and didn't take it for three weeks because she kept thinking she should be able to handle this naturally. The night she took it she slept six uninterrupted hours. She lay there the next morning in the specific astonishment of the rested. She had been so committed to handling things herself that she had been refusing help in the place she needed it most.

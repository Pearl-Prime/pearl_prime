## STORY v01
---
---
Marisol used to think being a good employee meant being everywhere the job needed her, all the time, including in her own head during meetings that didn't need her full attention yet, and in her kid's world during meetings that did. The Tuesday it broke her was ordinary: client deck, VP mouth moving, daycare deadline ticking in the back of her skull like a second clock nobody else in the room could hear. She missed the VP's actual question. She asked him to repeat it. Not once — twice. In the elevator afterward, her coworker Dana said, not unkindly, "You looked like you were somewhere else in there." Marisol almost laughed. She'd been in four places at once and fully in none of them. That night, after pickup, after the daycare teacher's raised eyebrow, after her daughter fell asleep mid-sentence in the car seat, Marisol sat in the driveway for one extra minute before going inside. She didn't fix anything. She just noticed: the meeting got half of her, the worry got half of her, and nobody, including her daughter now asleep in the back seat, ever got the whole woman. She started small — one meeting a day, phone face-down, no lap open. Not perfect. Returning, badly, repeatedly. It was enough to notice the difference.

## STORY v02
---
---
Dana ran her calendar like an air-traffic controller and was proud of it, right up until the day her director asked a direct question in the all-hands and Dana's answer came out one beat late, slightly wrong, because her mind had been in the parking lot arguing with a text her mother sent an hour ago. Nobody else noticed. Dana noticed. She'd trained herself to be present at exactly nothing, sliding her attention across six surfaces at once — Slack, deck, kid's school portal, the meeting itself — believing this was efficiency. What she actually felt, most days, was a low hum of having been nowhere fully. She started testing something a friend mentioned offhand: at the top of each hour, for ten seconds, name where she physically was. "I am in the third-floor conference room. My feet are on the carpet." It felt absurd the first four times. The fifth time, she caught herself mid-drift during a client call and pulled back before she missed anything. She still checked her phone too much. She still worried about her mother's texts. But she started noticing the gap between being in a room and being in a room, and noticing it, it turned out, was the whole beginning of closing it.

## STORY v03
---
---
Mei's performance review was going well, objectively — her manager used the word "exceptional" — and Mei spent the last four minutes of it doing math about pickup instead of hearing the word land. She got in her car afterward and could not remember, twenty minutes later, what specifically her manager had praised. Just "exceptional," floating, unattached to any actual sentence. That bothered her more than a bad review would have. She'd earned something good and hadn't been present enough to receive it. The following week she tried an experiment: in her next 1:1, when she felt her mind start drafting the reply-all email three topics ahead, she wrote one word on her notepad — "here" — not as a command, just a flag planted. She drifted nine more times in that meeting. Each time, the word was still on the page, and she came back to it. She left that 1:1 able to repeat, almost verbatim, what her manager had actually said. Not because she'd tried harder. Because she'd built a small, dumb, physical way to notice the leaving and come back from it, over and over, without judging the drift itself.

## STORY v04
---
---
Renee had a theory that multitasking during low-stakes meetings was smart resource allocation — draft the email, half-listen, contribute when needed. The theory held until the day she surfaced from an email draft to hear her own name, and a silence, and five people waiting for an answer to a question she'd missed entirely. She covered. Badly. Afterward, her colleague Farah, who'd noticed, said something Renee turned over for days: "You can multitask what you do. You can't multitask where you are." Renee didn't overhaul her life. She picked one thing: her hands. In meetings, she kept one hand flat on the table, no phone, no second screen. When her mind wandered — and it did, constantly, to the same client email — the flat hand was still there when she noticed, a small anchor she'd agreed with herself in advance to come back to. She missed less. Not everything. Less. The client email still got written, just after the meeting, with her whole attention instead of a fifth of it borrowed from somewhere else.

## STORY v05
---
---
Farah trained herself years ago to nod at the right intervals in meetings while thinking about something else entirely, a skill she considered a career asset until she realized she couldn't say what her own team had actually decided in yesterday's planning session. She'd been in the room. She had the notes. She had no memory of arriving at the decision, only of leaving it. Her therapist, unprompted, asked her to try naming three things she could physically sense whenever she noticed she'd drifted — the chair, her feet, the temperature of the room. Farah thought it sounded flimsy. She tried it anyway in the next status meeting, silently, three times in forty minutes. Each time it took four or five seconds. Each time, she came back slightly more anchored than before she'd left. By the end of the meeting she could recount, actually recount, what had been decided and why. Nothing about the meeting had changed. What changed was how many times, small and unannounced, she noticed she'd left and walked herself back.

## STORY v06
---
---
Kendra treated notifications like fires — every one needed to be seen the second it arrived, especially during client calls, where she prided herself on catching problems early. What she didn't clock, for a long time, was that catching problems early meant leaving the client conversation early too, dozens of times a call, in tiny increments that added up to her missing half of what the client actually said. A client repeated himself, gently, twice, before she caught it. That was the moment. She didn't quit Slack. She put her phone screen-down, on silent, in a drawer, for the duration of client calls only — one category, not her whole life. The itch to check it didn't disappear. It came in waves, strong at first. Each wave, she let it pass and brought her attention back to the actual person on the actual call. Three weeks in, a client told her, unprompted, that she was the easiest person to talk to on the account team. Kendra hadn't gotten smarter. She'd just started being where she already was, more of the time.

## STORY v07
---
---
Alexis got the "you seem like you have it together" comment so often she'd started saying thank you on reflex, without registering it, the same way she attended most of her days — competently, automatically, absent. The day it cracked open was small: her daughter asked her, at dinner, "Mom, are you here?" Not rhetorical. A real question, because Alexis's face had been at the table while the rest of her was still in the four o'clock meeting. She didn't have an answer that didn't sting. She started a rule for herself: one transition a day — walking from the parking garage to the office — done with her hands empty, no phone, no earbuds, just noticing four things she could see. It felt like nothing, most days. But it was one clean return, once a day, and slowly her daughter's question stopped landing like an accusation, because more often, when asked, Alexis actually was here.

## STORY v08
---
---
Jess believed her attention was a fixed resource she just needed to manage better — more caffeine, better lists, faster typing — until a coach pointed out that the problem wasn't the amount of her attention but how rarely she noticed it had already left. In one client call, she counted: her mind wandered off eleven times in fifty minutes, to Slack, to an invoice, to a comment a coworker made in passing. Eleven times wasn't the failure, the coach told her. Eleven returns would have been the win, and she'd only caught three of them. Jess started keeping a small tally, literally tick marks on a sticky note, not to shame herself but to practice the return itself as a rep, like a stretch. The tally climbed the first week — not because she wandered more, but because she started catching it. By the second week, the number of catches started mattering less than how quickly, once caught, she came back. That was the actual skill. Not staying. Returning, faster, more often.

## STORY v09
---
---
Toni could recite, in order, every reason her constant mental rehearsal of hard conversations was useful — she was preparing, she was being thorough, she was protecting the relationship. What it actually did, at the client dinner where she rehearsed a text to her sister through the entire appetizer course, was rob the actual dinner of a woman who was only sitting at it in body. Her sister sent the real text the next morning: "You've seemed far away lately." Toni sat with that sentence longer than she wanted to. She picked one boundary: during meals with people, phone in the other room, not silenced, physically elsewhere. The rehearsals in her head didn't stop. But without the phone as a runway, she started catching herself drift and pulling back into the actual conversation in front of her, again and again, imperfectly, until one dinner she realized she'd laughed at something without meaning to perform it.

## STORY v10
---
---
Naomi did the daycare math in every meeting that ran near five, an automatic subroutine she couldn't turn off and had stopped noticing she was running. During one quarterly review, her CFO asked her opinion directly and she answered from the wrong meeting, referencing numbers from a call two days prior. It wasn't a disaster. It was embarrassing enough to notice. She talked to a friend who suggested something oddly specific: put the actual pickup deadline on a sticky note on the table, visible, instead of carrying it as a background calculation. It sounds like it would be more distracting. It was the opposite — the worry, written down and visible, stopped needing to be silently rehearsed every ninety seconds, and Naomi found she could look at the note once, trust it, and come back to the meeting instead of running the math again and again in the dark.

## STORY v11
---
---
Simone had replayed the same ninety seconds of a colleague's sideways look so many times she could have performed it as a one-woman show, and she did this, mostly, during meetings that had nothing to do with the colleague at all. A mentor asked her once, casually, "How many times today do you think you've relived that moment?" Simone guessed four. It was closer to fifteen. The mentor didn't tell her to stop — she said replaying it wasn't the problem, replaying it silently and unnoticed thirty times a day was. Simone started just counting, a tally on her phone's notes app, each time she caught the replay start. The count itself changed nothing at first. But by the end of the week, the counting had become its own small interruption — noticing the replay begin was often enough to let it go, the way naming a knock at the door is different from just hearing footsteps circle the house all night.

## STORY v12
---
---
Marisol's second attempt at this, months after the daycare pickup meeting, looked different from her first. She wasn't trying to never wander — she'd given that up as a losing game. She picked one signal: the moment she noticed her hand was on her phone without her having decided to pick it up. That was it. Not an app, not a rule about screens, just that one specific noticing. The first week, she caught it maybe twice a day. By the third week, she caught it enough times that the gap between wandering and noticing had visibly shrunk — from a full derailed conversation down to a sentence, sometimes half a sentence. Her daughter's daycare teacher stopped raising an eyebrow at pickup. Not because Marisol was suddenly perfect. Because more of her, more often, showed up to the actual thing in front of her, one small catch at a time.

## STORY v13
---
---
Whitney carried her director's offhand comment about "restructuring conversations" into every meeting for six days, present in body, absent in every way that mattered, replaying a sentence that hadn't even been about her specifically. A peer noticed and said something plain: "You've been in the room but not in the room all week." Whitney didn't get reassurance from HR, and she didn't need it to fix this particular problem — she needed a way to stop the sentence from running on a loop underneath every meeting. She tried writing it down once, fully, in a note to herself, with the worst-case scenario spelled out. Oddly, writing the fear all the way to its end gave it a container. It didn't need to run in the background anymore because it had already been let out, once, on paper. She still worried. But the meetings underneath the worry started to actually reach her again.

## STORY v14
---
---
Layla's nervous system had a standing subscription to her nanny cam app, renewing itself every time a notification arrived mid-meeting, regardless of what the notification actually said. She was proud of not looking at her phone. She'd never noticed that not looking wasn't the same as not leaving. A colleague, watching her stare through a slide deck one Tuesday, asked gently if she was okay. Layla realized she couldn't say what slide they were on. She started a small practice: when the ping came, she let herself take one full breath before deciding whether to worry, a pause built to interrupt the automatic flight of attention rather than white-knuckle through it. Most pings, it turned out, weren't emergencies. The breath gave her enough room to notice that before her mind had already sprinted home.

## STORY v15
---
---
Coretta had built a reputation for composure under her skip-level's direct questions, and privately she knew the composure was a kind of ventriloquism — competent words coming out while she watched herself from a few feet outside her body, disconnected from the performance. It worked, technically. It also left her exhausted in a way that had nothing to do with hours worked. A peer mentor asked her once what she actually felt in her hands during those meetings. Coretta didn't know. She'd never checked. She started, oddly, with exactly that: once per meeting, feeling her feet on the floor before answering a hard question, one deliberate second of arriving in her body before her mouth started moving. The answers didn't get smarter. But for the first time in a long time, the woman answering and the woman standing outside watching were the same person again, at least for that one second, and then a little longer, and then, some days, for most of the meeting.

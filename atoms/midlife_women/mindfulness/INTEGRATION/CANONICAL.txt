## INTEGRATION v01
---
---
Tomorrow, before your first meeting that matters — the client call, the 1:1, whatever's on the calendar by 10 a.m. — do one thing before you sit down: put your phone somewhere you'd have to stand up to reach, and pick one physical anchor you'll return to when you notice your mind has left, whether that's the weight of your feet on the floor or your hand flat on the table. You will still drift. That's not the part to fix. The part to fix is what happens in the three seconds after you notice — instead of judging the drift or trying to force it to stop happening again, just come back to the anchor, once, and let the meeting flow back in. Do this even if you only catch yourself twice. Two catches, two returns, is the whole rep. It won't feel like much tomorrow. It's not supposed to. It's supposed to be small enough that you can actually do it inside a real meeting, with real stakes, without turning it into one more thing you're failing at. Notice, return, move on — no scorecard, no story about what it means that you drifted in the first place. By the end of the day, you'll have practiced the only skill that actually compounds here: not staying, but coming back, slightly faster than you did last time.

## INTEGRATION v02
---
---
Carry this into tomorrow's calendar as a single, boring experiment: pick the one meeting most likely to pull your mind toward daycare, pickup, or whatever logistical deadline usually hijacks your attention, and before it starts, write that exact deadline down on paper where you can glance at it — one line, no elaboration. Then let the meeting happen. When the worry surfaces mid-meeting, as it will, don't run the math again in your head. Look at the line, confirm nothing's changed, and bring your attention back to the room. You're not trying to stop caring about pickup. You're giving the worry a fixed address outside your skull so it stops needing to be recalculated every ninety seconds as a background process. If you catch yourself glancing at the note three times during one meeting, that's not a sign the practice failed — that's three successful returns to a room you'd otherwise have half-left without noticing. The note holds the worry. You do the returning. Tomorrow, that's the whole job.

## INTEGRATION v03
---
---
Tomorrow, choose one transition in your day — the walk from your car to the building, the first sixty seconds after you walk through your own front door — and give it your full, undivided attention on purpose, no phone, no rehearsing what you'll say next, just noticing what's actually there: the temperature, a sound, the specific weight of your bag on your shoulder. This isn't meant to fix your whole day. It's meant to be one clean, chosen return, small enough to actually complete, in a day that will otherwise be full of half-presence you don't have full control over yet. If your mind wanders during that sixty seconds anyway — and it might — just notice it happened and come back to the walk, the doorway, the room. You're not aiming for a perfect transition. You're aiming for one where you catch the wandering and return at least once. That single rep, repeated daily, is what eventually makes "are you here?" a question people stop needing to ask you, not because you never leave, but because you come back quickly enough that it stops being noticeable that you left at all.

## INTEGRATION v04
---
---
Tomorrow, in the meeting where you're most likely to run a background calculation instead of actually listening — the one where a deadline, a worry, or a replayed comment usually takes over — try naming, silently, one specific thing you can physically sense the instant you notice you've drifted: the chair under you, the mug in your hand, the sound of the HVAC. Not as a grand mindfulness practice. As a five-second interruption you can run without anyone in the room knowing you're running it. You'll probably need it more than once. That's fine — use it every single time you catch the drift, treating each use as a single successful rep rather than a sign you should have needed it less. By the end of the day, count, loosely, how many times you caught yourself and came back. That number, not zero drifting, is the actual measure of whether today went well. Small, repeatable, unglamorous — that's the whole design, and it's supposed to fit inside a day that's already full.

## INTEGRATION v05
---
---
Take one specific boundary into tomorrow: pick a single category of interaction — client calls, the family dinner table, pickup time at daycare — and make it the one place your phone is physically elsewhere, not silenced on the table, not face-down within reach, actually elsewhere. Inside that one boundary, when your mind wanders anyway, toward Slack, toward an email, toward whatever your particular loop is, notice it and bring yourself back to the actual person in front of you. You're not trying to become a phone-free person everywhere tomorrow. You're testing whether one bounded, specific space can hold your full attention if you remove the easiest exit ramp from it. Expect to want to check it. Expect the wanting to pass, in waves, if you let it. By the end of that one call, that one dinner, that one pickup, you'll have a data point you didn't have this morning: whether presence gets easier when the exit is simply farther away.

## INTEGRATION v06
---
---
Tomorrow, when a notification arrives during something that actually requires your attention — a meeting, a conversation, a moment with your kid — try inserting exactly one full breath before you decide anything about it. Not ignoring it forever. Not springing toward it either. One breath, in that gap between the ping landing and your attention automatically sprinting after it, long enough to ask whether this genuinely needs you right now or whether it can wait until the thing in front of you is done. Most days, most notifications, the answer will be that it can wait. You're not testing your willpower against the phone. You're testing whether a single breath is enough space to catch the automatic flight response before it fully takes you out of the room. Some days it won't be enough, and you'll leave anyway — that's not a failure of the practice, that's data. Try it again next time. The breath is the whole intervention. It's supposed to be almost too small to notice you're doing it.

## INTEGRATION v07
---
---
Carry one specific fear or replayed comment into tomorrow with a plan already made: the next time a sentence from a meeting, a review, or a passing comment starts running on a loop underneath everything else you're trying to do, give it fifteen minutes, in writing, before the day is over — not a quick note, the actual worst-case scenario, followed all the way to its end, on paper. This will feel counterintuitive; writing the fear out seems like it should make it louder. It usually does the opposite, because a fear that's been fully aired once needs to run in the background far less than one that's only ever been half-acknowledged between meetings. Once it's written, you have something concrete to return to when it resurfaces: "already written." Not resolved — written. That distinction is enough, most days, to let the actual room back in without the sentence competing for space underneath it.

## INTEGRATION v08
---
---
Tomorrow, run a small experiment on your own replaying habit: keep a literal tally — tick marks on paper, a running count in your notes app — every single time you catch your mind mid-replay of some comment, some look, some moment that's already over and that you can't do anything more about right now. Don't try to make the number small. Let it climb, because a climbing number means you're catching the replay, not that you're replaying more than usual — you were always replaying this much, you just weren't counting it before. The counting itself, oddly, tends to interrupt the loop faster than trying to argue yourself out of it in silence. By evening, look at the tally not as evidence of how distracted you were, but as a log of how many times you brought yourself back today. That's the actual number worth caring about tomorrow.

## INTEGRATION v09
---
---
Take this into tomorrow's toughest meeting: before your skip-level, your director, or whoever's going to ask you a direct hard question, decide in advance that you'll take one deliberate second — feeling your feet on the floor, or your hands in your lap — before you answer. Not to stall. To arrive, briefly, in your actual body before the competent, practiced version of you starts talking. It will feel like an unnecessary pause the first few times. Do it anyway. You're not trying to change what you say. You're closing, even briefly, the gap between the woman answering and the woman watching herself answer from a few feet outside her own body — a gap that's been costing you energy you couldn't name until now. One second, once, in one meeting, is the whole assignment tomorrow.

## INTEGRATION v10
---
---
Tomorrow, in the meeting most likely to pull you into eleven silent departures over fifty minutes, try tracking just the catches, not the drifts. Every time you notice your mind has left — for Slack, for an old comment, for a worry — put a small mark somewhere private: the corner of your notebook, a tally on your phone. You're not counting how many times you failed to focus. You're counting how many times you succeeded at noticing, which is the actual rare skill most people never build because they're only ever taught to feel bad about the wandering itself. At the end of the meeting, look at your marks and let them be proof of something real: not a perfect meeting, but an actively practiced one. Tomorrow's win isn't zero wandering. It's a higher catch count than you'd have logged if you weren't counting at all.

---
Tomorrow, choose the one meal or meeting where you know a specific unsent text, unresolved conversation, or half-drafted email tends to hijack your attention, and give it one deliberate minute beforehand — actually draft the text, actually think the hard thought through, on your own terms, before you sit down. You don't have to send it. You're not resolving the relationship tonight. When it tries to surface anyway during the meal, notice it, and come back to the actual course being served, the actual person talking. You'll probably need to come back more than once. That's the practice working, not failing.

## INTEGRATION v12
---
---
Take one plain question into tomorrow and ask it of yourself, silently, at some point mid-meeting: "Where am I right now — actually, physically?" Not as a test you're supposed to pass. Just a checkpoint. If the honest answer is "in the parking lot, arguing with a text," or "at my kid's school portal," or "replaying something my coworker said Tuesday," that's useful information, not a verdict on your character. Use whatever anchor is closest — your feet, your hands, the surface in front of you — to walk yourself back into the actual room, once. You might need to ask the question three or four times across one meeting. Each time you ask it and answer honestly, you've done the entire practice correctly, regardless of how many times the honest answer was "not here yet."

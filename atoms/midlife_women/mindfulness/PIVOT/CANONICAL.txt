## PIVOT v01
---
---
You think being present means never letting your mind wander during the client call. Try this instead: notice the wander, and return to one physical anchor — your feet, your hands — as many times as it takes. The return is the skill, not the staying.
---

## PIVOT v02
---
---
You think multitasking through the meeting is efficient because you're covering more ground. Try this instead: pick the one thing in front of you, let the rest wait, and notice how much less you have to redo afterward. Presence isn't slower. Half-presence just hides its cost.
---

## PIVOT v03
---
---
You think the worry needs to keep running in the background so you don't forget it. Try this instead: write the one line down, once, where you can see it. A worry with a fixed address stops needing to be recalculated every ninety seconds.
---

## PIVOT v04
---
---
You think catching your mind wandering fifteen times in a meeting means you're bad at focusing. Try this instead: count the catches, not the drifts. Fifteen catches is fifteen reps of the only skill that actually compounds — coming back.
---

## PIVOT v05
---
---
You think looking composed while your mind is elsewhere is the same as being present. Try this instead: feel your feet on the floor for one second before you answer. The performance and the person get to be the same woman again, even briefly.
---

## PIVOT v06
---
---
You think the notification needs an answer the instant it arrives. Try this instead: take one full breath before deciding anything. Most pings can survive one breath's delay, and that breath is often the whole difference between a drift you catch and one you don't.
---

## PIVOT v07
---
---
You think you have to stop caring about the thing pulling your attention away in order to be present for the thing in front of you. Try this instead: give the worry fifteen minutes on paper before the meeting. A fear that's been fully aired once runs quieter in the background than one that's only half-acknowledged.
---

## PIVOT v08
---
---
You think presence means your mind stops wandering entirely. Try this instead: shrink the gap between leaving and noticing. That gap, not the wandering itself, is the only number actually worth tracking.
---

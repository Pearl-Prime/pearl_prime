## COMPRESSION v01
---
---
Half-presence costs more than either full presence or a real break, because you pay the attention-tax twice — once for a room you're not absorbing, once for a worry you can't act on yet — and collect the benefit of neither. Attention doesn't split; it alternates, fast, with a re-entry cost each switch. Presence isn't a state achieved and held — it's a return, repeated, usually late, usually imperfect. The fix isn't preventing the wander. It's one small physical anchor — a hand on the table, feet on the floor — you return to the instant you notice you've left. Ten drifts caught is ten reps of the only skill that compounds.
---

## COMPRESSION v02
---
---
An unresolved worry behaves like an open tab your mind won't let you forget, regardless of whether the room you're in can do anything about it. It gets louder in low-stakes meetings, not quieter, because those are exactly the moments your mind has bandwidth to go chew on unfinished business. Suppressing it backfires, the way not-thinking-about-a-knock backfires. What works: give the worry a fixed, visible home outside your head — a written line, a filed thought — so it stops needing silent recalculation every ninety seconds, and your attention can return to the actual room in front of you.
---

## COMPRESSION v03
---
---
Competence and presence run on different systems — you can nod, ask good questions, and perform full engagement while your actual attention sits somewhere else entirely, and nobody flags the gap because the performance holds. This is why half-presence survives years at work undetected. What closes the gap isn't trying to be present all day — too big a target. It's one chosen transition, done fully, daily: a walk, a doorway, sixty seconds, no phone, no rehearsal. Small, repeated returns are what eventually make the felt experience of "being somewhere" catch up to the appearance of it.
---

## COMPRESSION v04
---
---
The number worth tracking was never "how many times did my mind wander" — every mind wanders, in every meeting, always. The number that actually matters is the gap between leaving and noticing. A mind that drifts eleven times and catches all eleven is functioning better than one that drifts three times and catches none, because unrecovered drift compounds silently while caught drift gets corrected in seconds. Track catches, not drifts, with a simple tally. A rising number means you're noticing more, not failing more — that's the whole reframe.



## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: inherited

I want to name something about the grief that happens in nursing and is never processed because it is not named as grief.

A patient dies. The death is recorded. The next patient arrives. The shift continues. And the grief is not named. There is no moment of acknowledgment. There is no space for processing. The death is just the next event.

The mechanism is the normalization of loss. When loss happens repeatedly and is treated as part of the workflow, the loss stops being named as loss. It becomes just what happens. The patient died. Document it. Move on.

But the grief is still there. It is just unnamed. It is just incorporated into the rest of the emotional work of the shift. And because it is unnamed, it is not processed. It accumulates.

What I have noticed is that the accumulated unnamed grief becomes part of the baseline emotional state. You are functioning but underneath is the weight of all the losses that were never named.

The mechanism is the way repeated loss without ceremony becomes incorporated trauma.

A patient dies at 40. A patient dies at 50. A patient dies at 30. The death is premature. The death is wrong. The death is not the natural order.

The mechanism is the wrongness of premature death. The person should have more time. The person should have more life. And they do not. And you have to witness the wrongness.

What I have noticed is that this particular form of grief is not always acknowledged. The death happens and it is treated as a medical outcome. But underneath is the grief that the person did not have a full life. That the death was premature.

The mechanism is the grief of witnessing lives cut short.

I want to name something about the grief that accumulates because there is no ritual for acknowledging loss in nursing.

In other contexts, there is a funeral. There is a gathering. There is a ritual that marks the loss and permits grieving. In nursing, there is no ritual. The patient dies and the next patient arrives.

The mechanism is the absence of ritual for acknowledging loss. Without the ritual, the grief has no container.

What I have noticed is that this can leave nurses in a state of perpetual incomplete grieving. The losses accumulate but are never marked.

The mechanism is the way absence of ritual prevents grieving completion.

## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic

I want to name something about courage in nursing that is specific and different from what people usually mean by courage.

Courage in most contexts means doing something difficult or dangerous despite fear. Running into a burning building. Standing up to an oppressor. Taking on an obvious enemy. The stories we tell about courage are stories about people overcoming external threats.

But the courage required in nursing is often quieter and more confusing because the threat is structural and often invisible. The courage required is to speak truth to power when the power structure has made it clear that truth is not welcome.

The mechanism is the collision between what you observe and what the hierarchy tells you to believe. You see something that is wrong. You know it is wrong. But the person in power is saying it is right. And the courage is in keeping your own perception even when the hierarchy tells you it is wrong.

I have watched nurses build the quiet courage to trust their own observations. To look at a patient and say "that is wrong" even when the physician says it is fine. To see a protocol and say "that will harm people" even when administration says it is standard. To witness a system failure and say "this is the problem" even when everyone else is saying we just need to work harder.

The mechanism is the protection of your own perception against the institution's version of reality. And this is more difficult than it sounds. Institutions are powerful. They provide your paycheck. They employ your colleagues. They have more authority than you do. And they are telling you that your perception is wrong.

The courage is in saying: my perception is legitimate. What I see is real. The system does not get to tell me I did not see what I saw.

Nora considers applying for the clinical educator position. The job posting is open. She's qualified. She's been doing informal education on the unit for years. The consideration lasts about ten seconds before her body intervenes: heart rate climbs, stomach tightens, a flush of heat across her neck. The body's response is identical to the response she'd have if someone pointed a weapon at her. Nobody is pointing anything at her. She's reading a job posting on a screen. The posting is an opportunity. Her body is treating it as a threat. The opportunity and the threat share the same screen and the same moment and nothing else.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v01

MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Nora maps the alarm's relationship to courage specifically. She sets IVs in difficult veins without alarm. She manages acute decompensations without alarm. She navigates hostile family members without alarm. The alarm doesn't fire on clinical risk. The alarm fires on identity risk—the risk of stepping outside the role she currently occupies. Clinical courage is inside her role. Career courage is outside it. The alarm protects the perimeter of her current identity. Anything inside the perimeter—no matter how clinically dangerous—passes through. Anything outside the perimeter—no matter how professionally beneficial—triggers the full response.

Aisha identifies that the alarm's intensity correlates with the hierarchy gap. Speaking to a peer: minimal alarm. Speaking to a charge nurse: moderate. Speaking to a manager: high. Speaking to a department head: maximum. The gap between her position and the recipient's position is the alarm's primary input. The clinical validity of the concern is irrelevant to the calculation. An unsafe staffing ratio reported to a peer produces mild alarm. The same unsafe staffing ratio reported upward produces a full threat response. The alarm is measuring power differential, not message importance. The most important messages need to travel upward. The alarm makes upward travel the most expensive.

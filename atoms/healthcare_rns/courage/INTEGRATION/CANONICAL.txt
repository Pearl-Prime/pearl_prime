

## INTEGRATION v01
---
mode: BODY_LANDED
reframe_type: BODY_FACT
weight: standard
carry_line: "You spoke. Your hands stayed steady."
---
The attending was making the call wrong. The patient was deteriorating differently than what the attending projected. You had three seconds in the hallway and your body was screaming: don't say it, hierarchy exists, consequences exist. Your chest was tight. Your throat was dry. You said it anyway. Not loudly. Not confrontationally. Just the fact: "The lactate is rising. I think we're reading this differently." The attending heard you. Considered. Changed the order. Your body is here now, post-moment. Still here. The courage wasn't the absence of alarm. It was moving through the alarm to say what needed saying. Feel your feet. Your breath is back to normal. The moment is held.
---

## INTEGRATION v02
---
mode: COST_VISIBLE
reframe_type: COST_UNCOUNTED
weight: standard
carry_line: "Speaking up cost you the quiet."
---
The easy path was silence. Let the attending lead. Follow the order. Chart the outcome. The cost of speaking up is that you're now the one who pushed back. The attending might resent it. The team might see you as difficult. The hierarchy remembers. You paid with social capital you can't easily recover. This is why most nurses don't speak. Not because they lack courage. Because courage costs and the cost is distributed disproportionately toward those with less power. You spent it anyway because the patient needed it. That's the real cost of speaking: not the moment of saying it, but the days after when the system reminds you that challenging authority carries consequences.
---

## INTEGRATION v03
---
mode: STILL_HERE
reframe_type: TIME_SHIFT
weight: light
carry_line: "You found your voice over years of silence."
note: STILL-HERE mode — final chapter only, one per book
---
You remember when you wouldn't have said anything. Year one, year two, year three. The hierarchy was absolute then. You trusted the system more than you trusted your own read. Now you trust your clinical judgment. Now you speak. That change didn't come from getting braver. It came from knowing enough to understand what you know. It came from watching enough outcomes to recognize patterns. It came from the accumulated evidence that your voice has value. The silence is still there as an option. You choose differently now. That's not transformation. That's learning.
---

## INTEGRATION v04
---
mode: QUESTION_OPEN
reframe_type: QUESTION_REFRAME
weight: standard
carry_line: "How much does staying quiet cost."
---
The real question isn't whether you have the courage to speak. You do. The question is how much staying silent would cost the patient, the team, yourself. What happens in the spaces where you don't speak because the hierarchy exists. How many times the read was right and the order was wrong and nobody said the thing. The courage question is actually a cost question. Not "am I brave enough" but "can I afford the silence."
---

## INTEGRATION v05
---
mode: SOMEONE_ELSE
reframe_type: OTHER_PERSON
weight: heavy
carry_line: "He's still waiting for permission to speak."
---
The new grad is holding the information. The patient assessment is clear. The attending's read is different. He knows he's right. He doesn't know yet that speaking up is his responsibility, not the attending's prerogative. That the hierarchy exists to organize work, not to own truth. He figures it out. Or he doesn't. His career is shaped by how many times he swallows the thing he knows. You remember that version of yourself.

## INTEGRATION v07
---
mode: COST_VISIBLE
reframe_type: COST_UNCOUNTED
weight: heavy
carry_line: "Courage costs visibility and sometimes consequences."
---
Speaking up marks you. The system doesn't reward it evenly. Some attendings appreciate it. Others resent it. The evaluation might reflect it. The assignment might shift. You're no longer the silent nurse who blends. You're the one who questions. The cost isn't the moment of speaking. It's the organizational memory that follows it. Not all cultures are ready for voices from below. That's not your failure. That's the cost of working in hierarchies that aren't built for partnership.



## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
Bedtime on my days off is supposed to be restorative. But instead there's this anxiety about whether I'll be able to sleep. Because I've had so much sleep disruption from shift work that I'm not sure my body remembers how to sleep normally anymore. The mechanism is called sleep anxiety secondary to shift work—the worry about whether my dysregulated sleep pattern will allow restful sleep.
---

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
I've noticed that I sleep differently depending on whether I'm working nights or days. And the transition between them is always difficult. There's this period where my body is confused about what time it is. And my mind becomes hyperactive. The mechanism is called circadian rhythm disruption anxiety—the worry that accompanies the dysynchronization between internal clock and external schedule.
---

## REFLECTION v03
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
What strikes me is that even on my nights off, I often wake up at the time I would normally need to wake up for work. My body has learned a rhythm that it can't seem to unlearn. And when I wake up at that time on a day off, there's this moment of disorientation that creates anxiety. The mechanism is called conditioned waking anxiety.
---

## REFLECTION v04
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: inherited
---
There's a specific kind of anxiety that comes late in a shift when I know I'm going home to try to sleep during the day. Because daytime sleep is never quite restful. There's always ambient noise. The light is wrong. My body doesn't fully relax. And I know this. So there's anxiety about getting insufficient sleep before my next night shift.
---

## REFLECTION v05
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I've noticed that I catastrophize about the sleep I'm not getting. If I don't sleep well during the day, how will I function at night? If I function poorly, what if I make a clinical error? What if someone gets hurt because I was too tired? This cascade of worry keeps me awake. The mechanism is sleep-related catastrophizing connected to professional responsibility.
---

## REFLECTION v06
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
What I'm realizing is that some of my sleep anxiety is connected to the loss of control that comes with shift work. I can't choose to sleep when my body wants to. I have to sleep when the schedule allows. And this loss of control creates anxiety. The mechanism is called scheduled sleep anxiety—the anxiety about being forced to sleep at times that don't feel natural.
---

## REFLECTION v07
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
There's a particular loneliness to sleep disruption in healthcare work. I can't complain too much because everyone is dealing with it. But that doesn't make it easier. And the isolation of insomnia can intensify the anxiety. The mechanism is called isolated sleep anxiety—experiencing the disruption alone without adequate support.
---

## REFLECTION v08
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: inherited
---
I've learned that my sleep anxiety is sometimes about more than just sleep. It's about the larger reality of working shifts that your body isn't designed for. It's about the knowledge that this level of sleep disruption has health consequences. And it's about the inability to change the fundamental situation. The anxiety is actually quite rational.
---

## REFLECTION v09
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
What strikes me is how sleep anxiety has become a conditioned response. The moment I realize I need to sleep, the anxiety activates. Because I've had so much sleep disruption that my nervous system has learned to associate bedtime with stress. The mechanism is called conditioned sleep anxiety.
---

## REFLECTION v10
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
There's something about night shift that seems designed to prevent sleep during the day. My nervous system is activated by the work. My circadian rhythm is disrupted. My environment is wrong. And then when I do try to sleep, I'm anxious about whether I'll manage it. The mechanism is called compound sleep anxiety—multiple factors creating anxiety simultaneously.
---

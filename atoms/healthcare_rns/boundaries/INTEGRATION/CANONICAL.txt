

## INTEGRATION v01
---
mode: BODY_LANDED
reframe_type: BODY_FACT
weight: standard
carry_line: "Your body knows the cost of yes."
---
You feel it in your chest. The tightness that appears when you're asked to pick up the shift. When the unit is short-staffed and the guilt is heavy and the person asking already knows your answer. Your breathing gets shallow. Your shoulders round forward. This isn't anxiety about the work. This is your nervous system registering the cost: another twelve hours subtracted from the recovery time your system needs. The boundary isn't something you say. It's something you feel first. Your body speaks before your voice does. The words come after. "I can't." "Not this week." "I need to say no." Your body is giving you information before the yes becomes automatic. Feel that. That tightness is data.
---

## INTEGRATION v02
---
mode: COST_VISIBLE
reframe_type: COST_UNCOUNTED
weight: standard
carry_line: "Each yes costs days you don't get back."
---
The math is simple. One extra shift costs recovery. Two extra shifts cost sleep. Three shifts costs clarity. The exhaustion compounds like debt. You work the extra night. You work the next scheduled shift tired. You work the next one more tired. By day four, you're not functioning at baseline anymore. You're functioning at reduced capacity while running at full speed. The system doesn't measure this. It measures coverage. Your body measures it. The immune system reads the sleep deprivation. The decision-making slows. The patience thins. You say yes to protect the unit and pay the cost in your physical reserve. The institution counts the staff hours covered. Your nervous system counts the nights not recovered.
---

## INTEGRATION v03
---
mode: STILL_HERE
reframe_type: TIME_SHIFT
weight: light
carry_line: "You learned no after years of proving yes."
note: STILL-HERE mode — final chapter only, one per book
---
When you started, you said yes to everything. The good nurse was the one who covered, who stayed late, who didn't mind the weekend call. You proved yourself through availability. Five years later, you learned something: the institution has infinite yes. It will take every hour you offer. It has structural reasons to ask. You have structural reasons to give. And somewhere between year one and now, you learned that the boundary saves both the work and you. Not because saying no is selfish. Because the work you do after three weeks of extra shifts is not the work you want to do. Your patients deserve you restored, not depleted. That's not selfishness. That's competence.
---

## INTEGRATION v04
---
mode: QUESTION_OPEN
reframe_type: QUESTION_REFRAME
weight: standard
carry_line: "Who would you be with your time back."
---
The question underneath every boundary isn't "Can I say no." It's what you'd do if you could stop. Who would you be with twelve hours unaccounted for. What would your hands do if they weren't moving through another patient's chart. What would your mind think about if it wasn't running night shift calculations. You don't have to answer this. The question lives in the space before you say yes. It sits there as a cost assessment. Not guilting you into refusal. Just making clear what you're trading. The boundary is the moment you acknowledge the question and decide the answer matters.
---

## INTEGRATION v05
---
mode: SOMEONE_ELSE
reframe_type: OTHER_PERSON
weight: heavy
carry_line: "She's still saying yes to everything."
---
She's been here two years. She picks up the extra shifts every week. The staffing crisis is real. The guilt is real. She doesn't yet know that the yes will compound into exhaustion that becomes her baseline. That one day she'll realize she hasn't had a full day off in eight weeks and the thought doesn't even register as a problem anymore. You could tell her: this is temporary. The staffing crisis isn't. But the depletion will be if you keep running at this pace. She's not ready. She's still in the phase where the yes feels like loyalty. You're in the phase where you understand it as a cost.
---

## INTEGRATION v07
---
mode: COST_VISIBLE
reframe_type: COST_UNCOUNTED
weight: heavy
carry_line: "The cost is invisible until collapse."
---
The institution measures staffing ratios. It doesn't measure the nurse carrying those ratios beyond capacity. It doesn't track the accumulated exhaustion, the mental health decline, the second career nurses leave because the yes became unsustainable. The cost is distributed across individual bodies. Your body. Hers. The one who called in sick after three weeks of double shifts. The cost is real even when the institution can't see it.
---

Maria watches Chen at the nurses station after a twelve-hour shift. Chen's charting is meticulous, practiced, the pen moving steadily through documentation. When the charge nurse calls asking if anyone can stay late, Chen glances up briefly, then returns to work. Maria's throat tightens. She's already heard the ask three times. She hasn't declined once. Chen declines without explanation. The fluorescent lights hum overhead. Other nurses rustle papers, shuffle toward the time clock. Maria sees Chen standing, gathering her bag. No apology. No justification. Just the simple movement of leaving. The weight of Maria's own undeclared yes presses into her shoulders, heavy and unexamined.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v01

MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Chen's refusal to stay late triggers a fear in Maria that feels almost instinctive. If Chen says no and others don't question her commitment, then Maria's constant yes must mean something else. Either Maria is choosing self-sacrifice, or Chen is choosing to be a worse nurse, a less dedicated teammate. Maria watches Chen's patients. Their outcomes are identical. The care is sound. Yet the fear persists that boundary-setting equals abandonment. This fear is the mechanism: if I limit myself, I abandon my patients and my team. The proof appears to contradict it, but Maria's mind reframes. Chen just hasn't been tested by the right crisis yet.

Alicia catches Maria in the break room looking at her phone. It's seven forty-five p.m. She should have left forty-five minutes ago. The charge nurse is still asking for coverage for an admission.

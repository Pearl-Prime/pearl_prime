

## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: protective
tier_variant: D

I want to name something that was done to nurses in their training that is so foundational it often goes unnamed: the systematic removal of the right to say no. In school and in clinical training, you learn that saying no to a patient, to a family member, to a physician, to an admission, to another task is a form of abandonment. The patient needs care and you are trained to provide it.

The boundary becomes impossible because the choice was never real.

family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: protective
tier_variant: D

I want to name something about the way boundaries collapse around time in nursing. Your shift is twelve hours. But the shift does not end at the twelve-hour mark. It ends when everything is done. And everything is never done. So your boundary around when you work is not actually a boundary. It is a target that is frequently exceeded. The mechanism is the frame of "the work is not finished" as the justification for extending the boundary indefinitely.

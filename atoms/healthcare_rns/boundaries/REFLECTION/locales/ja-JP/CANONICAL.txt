I want to name something that was done to nurses in their training that is so foundational it often goes unnamed: the systematic removal of the right to say no.

In school and in clinical training, you learn that saying no to a patient, to a family member, to a physician, to an admission, to another task is a form of abandonment. The patient needs care and you are trained to provide it. The instruction is clear: the patient's need supersedes your limit. You say no and you are not being a good nurse.

The mechanism is simple and devastating: the conflation of the boundary-setting self with the selfish self. If you set a limit, you are choosing yourself over the patient. If you refuse a task, you are being unwilling to help. The training creates a false binary where the only option is to erase yourself in service of the patient's need.

Over years, this becomes so internalized that you do not notice you are doing it anymore. Someone asks you to stay late and your body says no but your mouth says yes, and you go home not understanding why you are so depleted. The family member asks for something unreasonable and you feel obligated to try to do it. The assignment is unsafe and you tell yourself you will just manage it.

What has been removed is not just the right to say no. What has been removed is the feedback loop that would tell you that you are being asked to do too much. When you always say yes, nobody learns that your capacity is limited. The system does not adjust. And you do not know where your boundary is because you have never been permitted to find it.

The mechanism was not malicious. It was presented as virtue. You learned that good nurses sacrifice themselves. And now you have no idea where you end and the job begins.

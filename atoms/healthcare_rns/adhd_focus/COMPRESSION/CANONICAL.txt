## COMPRESSION v01
---
---
Mechanism: intention and initiation are separate systems; an ADHD brain doesn't bridge them automatically just because a task matters. Knowing something needs doing produces no motion on its own. Fix: shrink the entry point to the literal first physical motion — type one line, click one button — and let momentum carry the rest. The task's size never changes. The doorway does.
---

## COMPRESSION v02
---
---
Mechanism: attention runs on salience — novelty, urgency, social presence — not on a rational ranking of importance. A code blue wins instantly; a compliance module loses for six weeks, because only one is loud. Fix: manufacture salience artificially — a sticky note in your eyeline, a person checking in by name, a visible timer — instead of trying to feel more strongly about a task that will never out-shout its surroundings on its own.
---

## COMPRESSION v03
---
---
Mechanism: time-blindness means duration isn't tracked reliably from the inside; you get "now" and "not now," with no accurate sense of how much "not now" elapsed. Sixteen minutes vanish between seeing the whiteboard and acting on it, not from negligence but from a perception gap. Fix: externalize the clock — a repeating silent alarm every thirty minutes — so you stop depending on an internal sense that was never going to reliably fire.
---

## COMPRESSION v04
---
---
Mechanism: working memory has a hard ceiling, and rapid six-patient report at ninety seconds a head exceeds it for everyone, ADHD or not — retaining two of six isn't a competence failure. Fix: treat an external notepad, an index card in shorthand, as the primary tool, not a backup — the same way pilots use checklists, not as an admission anything is broken.
---

## COMPRESSION v05
---
---
Mechanism: a visible pile of tasks triggers freeze based on aggregate size, not on any individual item's actual difficulty — five sheets look five times harder as a stack even though no single sheet changed. Fix: physically hide everything except the one item in front of you; momentum handles the rest once the pile itself is out of view.
---

## COMPRESSION v06
---
---
Mechanism: silent reading can visually continue — eyes tracking left to right — after executive attention has already disengaged, producing the illusion of having read something you never actually processed. Fix: read aloud, even under your breath, the moment a line needs a third pass; the auditory-motor checkpoint catches what silent scanning let slip through.
---

## COMPRESSION v07
---
---
Mechanism: chaotic, high-stakes memories (a code, a rapid incident) resist being organized in your head before writing — you're asking working memory to build the container and fill it simultaneously, which is exactly what it's worst at. Fix: borrow an existing external structure — the form's own field order, read aloud by someone else — and fill it one line at a time in that order, not the order it happened.
---

## COMPRESSION v08
---
---
Mechanism: dread scales with how unclear a task's size is, not with its actual size — an unopened form accumulates imagined weight the longer it sits, regardless of its true content. Fix: give yourself thirty seconds to open and glance at a dreaded task purely to learn its real size, with zero commitment yet to finishing it.
---

## COMPRESSION v09
---
---
Mechanism: floating intentions recur and recede without triggering action because they have no physical anchor to catch on when they resurface — "I need to call her back" can be thought five times and acted on zero. Fix: the moment an intention repeats itself for the second time, give it a body immediately — a sticky note in your visual path, an object moved somewhere you'll physically encounter it.
---

## COMPRESSION v10
---
---
Mechanism: a task done alone for an abstract future record competes poorly against anything immediate; the same task attached to a real person, present, right now, becomes far more salient without changing in actual importance. Fix: manufacture that person deliberately — explain the material to a coworker, answer a question that sends you back to the source — rather than waiting for guilt to eventually work, which it won't.
---

## COMPRESSION v11
---
---
Mechanism: a commitment to only "touch" a task for two minutes bypasses the resistance triggered by committing to "finish" it; once started, stopping often feels worse than continuing, so momentum finishes what the timer only promised to start. Fix: set a short timer with permission to stop when it buzzes, and trust that starting, not the timer's length, is doing the actual work.
---

## COMPRESSION v12
---
---
Mechanism: every stall in this bank is a bridging problem — full understanding, zero motion — not a caring problem, and treating it like the latter (more guilt, more reminders) misses the mechanism and adds shame without adding movement. Fix: keep a short list of concrete bridges — timer, sticky note, coworker's name, "just the first line" — and when frozen, ask which bridge you haven't tried yet instead of asking why you can't just do it.

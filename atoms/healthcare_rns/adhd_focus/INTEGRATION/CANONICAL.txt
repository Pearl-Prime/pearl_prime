## INTEGRATION v01
---
---
Tomorrow, before your shift starts, pick one task you already know will stall — charting, an in-service module, a callback you've been putting off — and write down its literal first physical motion: "open the tab," "type one line," "pick up the phone." Tape it somewhere you'll see it, your badge reel or the corner of your monitor. When the freeze hits today's version of that task, you won't be deciding what to do. You'll just be doing the one small thing you already wrote down, and the rest will very likely follow on its own once you're moving.
---

## INTEGRATION v02
---
---
Carry this into tomorrow: set a silent repeating alarm for every thirty minutes of your shift, no exceptions, and treat each buzz as a two-second reality check against the clock rather than your gut sense of time. You'll catch at least one moment where you're further behind than you felt — the turn that was due at 1500, the med pass drifting — and you'll catch it while it's still a two-minute fix instead of a sixteen-minute one. This isn't about getting better at sensing time. It's about no longer needing to.
---

## INTEGRATION v03
---
---
Tomorrow, when report gets handed to you fast and you know you've only caught half of it, don't perform confidence you don't have. Pull out an index card, or open a blank note, and ask for it again, slower, in your own shorthand. It'll feel exposing the first time. It buys you a physical object you can check all shift instead of guessing from a memory that was never built to hold six patients at ninety-second speed. Keep the card in your pocket, not your head, and pull it out every time the fog rolls in.
---

## INTEGRATION v04
---
---
Carry this forward: the next time you're facing a pile — five discharge sheets, ten charts, an inbox — physically hide everything except the one item you're working on right now. Close the other tabs. Turn the rest of the stack face down. Minimize every window but one. You don't need the whole pile to feel smaller; you need it to disappear from view long enough for your hands to start on the one thing in front of you. Momentum will very likely carry you into the next item without needing the same push twice.
---

## INTEGRATION v05
---
---
Tomorrow, notice the first task you catch yourself feeling guilty about instead of starting. Don't add more guilt to it. Instead, ask one coworker to check in with you by name at a specific time — "ask me at 1500 if I finished the module" — or say out loud what you intend to do and when. You're not building discipline. You're giving a quiet, important task the social presence it needs to compete with everything louder on your unit, and that presence will move you faster than another hour of feeling bad ever could.
---

## INTEGRATION v06
---
---
Carry this into your next shift: the moment you notice yourself thinking "I need to do X" for the second time without having done it, stop trusting the third repetition to work better. Give it a body immediately — a sticky note on your badge reel, an alarm on your phone, the object itself moved somewhere you'll physically walk past. The thought was never the problem; it was going to keep recurring and receding forever without an anchor. One small physical object ends the loop in a way another mental note never will.
---

## INTEGRATION v07
---
---
Tomorrow, when a task feels tangled — an incident report, a complicated handoff, something with a dozen details in no clear order — don't try to sort it out in your head before you start writing. Find the external structure that already exists: the form's own field order, a template, a colleague reading you the sequence out loud. Fill it one line at a time in that order, even if it's not the order things happened. You don't need to untangle the memory first. You need a container that isn't your own head to pour it into.
---

## INTEGRATION v08
---
---
Carry this forward: the next time you catch yourself dreading an unopened task — an email, a form, a message you haven't read — notice whether you actually know its size or you're just assuming. Give yourself thirty seconds to open it purely to check, with zero commitment to finishing. Most of the dread you're carrying around today is about not knowing how big something is, and that kind of dread evaporates the instant you actually look, often faster than you expect.
---

## INTEGRATION v09
---
---
Tomorrow, when you catch yourself rereading the same line of a policy or module transcript for the third time without landing it, stop pushing on the same channel. Read it out loud, even under your breath, or switch to skimming a transcript instead of watching passively. You're not failing to focus harder — reading and comprehending briefly decoupled, and the fix is rerouting through a different sense, not gritting through the same one.
---

## INTEGRATION v10
---
---
Carry this into tomorrow: find one task you've been circling for days without starting, and manufacture a reason to explain it to someone else — walk a new hire through it, answer a coworker's question that sends you back to the source, or ask someone to quiz you on it in ten minutes. The task itself won't have changed size or importance. But giving it a person and a moment attached to it will very likely get you moving in minutes on something guilt alone couldn't touch in weeks.
---

## INTEGRATION v11
---
---
Tomorrow, when you feel yourself staring at a task with your hands ready and nothing happening, resist the urge to wait for motivation. Ask instead: what's the smallest possible first move here — not the task, the literal doorway into it? Type one line. Click one button. Pick up the phone. The freeze isn't about the size of the task. It's about the size of the entry point, and shrinking that one thing is usually the entire fix.
---

## INTEGRATION v12
---
---
Carry this forward: keep a short list of your go-to bridges somewhere you'll actually see it — your badge reel, your phone lock screen, a sticky note at the station. A timer. A coworker's name. "Just the first line." A physical object moved into your path. When a freeze hits tomorrow, skip the question "why can't I just do this" and ask instead "which bridge haven't I tried yet." That question has a real, usable answer every time your shift throws this at you again.

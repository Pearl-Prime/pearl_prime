

## INTEGRATION v01
---
mode: BODY_LANDED
reframe_type: BODY_FACT
weight: standard
carry_line: "The flatness is not emptiness."
---
You moved through the shift and charted everything and said the right things and the flatness was there the whole time. Not sadness. Not overwhelm. Just a glass wall between you and the feeling. You can see the patient suffering and know you should feel something and the feeling doesn't arrive. That's not burnout in the early stage. That's compassion fatigue. The empathy tank running dry on the same schedule as the night shifts. Your body's way of saying: you can't pour from an empty cup because you've been running the pour at maximum for too long. Feel your hands holding the chart. Your shoulders are down. You're present. The flatness sits on top of presence like a layer of clear glass. That's not failure. That's survival.
---

## INTEGRATION v02
---
mode: COST_VISIBLE
reframe_type: COST_UNCOUNTED
weight: standard
carry_line: "The cost is what you stop feeling."
---
You used to cry at home after hard shifts. The boundary between work and self existed. Now the flatness comes home with you and you can't quite access the sadness on either side. The empathy that was your calling now feels like a resource you're rationing. You have enough for the chart, enough for the assessment, not enough for the person. The institution doesn't measure this. It measures patient satisfaction scores and clinical outcomes. It doesn't measure the nurse who's present but not there. The cost is that you're delivering care from behind glass. The patients get what they need. You get less of yourself.
---

## INTEGRATION v03
---
mode: STILL_HERE
reframe_type: TIME_SHIFT
weight: light
carry_line: "You stayed when feeling broke."
note: STILL-HERE mode — final chapter only, one per book
---
Year one, you felt everything. The patient deaths landed hard. The family grief was yours too. Year two, it started shifting. Year three, the glass wall appeared and you couldn't figure out when or how. Now you walk into the room and you're competent and empty at the same time. You didn't break. You adapted. That's what the profession requires. The cost is that adaptation came as anesthesia. You're functioning. The feeling is just quieter now. You're still here, doing the work, and the work is no longer the thing that makes you feel alive.
---

## INTEGRATION v04
---
mode: QUESTION_OPEN
reframe_type: QUESTION_REFRAME
weight: standard
carry_line: "Can you get the feeling back without leaving."
---
The real question isn't whether you're burnt out. You are. The real question is whether the compassion fatigue is temporary depletion or permanent shift. Whether there's a way to reconnect with the meaning of the work without first stepping away from it. Whether the glass wall is protective or terminal. You don't have the answer. The question sits in the charting, in the moment before entering a patient's room when the flatness is already there. You're competent enough to keep going. The question is whether going is still what you want.
---

## INTEGRATION v05
---
mode: SOMEONE_ELSE
reframe_type: OTHER_PERSON
weight: heavy
carry_line: "She still believes the feeling will come back."
---
The new nurse cries after shifts. She's exhausted and moving through her own grief. She thinks the tears mean she's fragile. You could tell her: the tears are not fragility. They're the evidence that the feeling is still there. Hold onto that. The day it stops is not the day you heal. It's the day the glass wall appears. She'll keep working after it appears. The patients won't know. But she'll know. The cost of staying is learning to function without feeling.
---

## INTEGRATION v07
---
mode: COST_VISIBLE
reframe_type: COST_UNCOUNTED
weight: heavy
carry_line: "The cost is becoming a different nurse."
---
You chose this profession because you felt things deeply. The empathy was a superpower. The cost of years of high-frequency emotional labor is that the superpower becomes a liability. The profession requires protection. Protection comes as distance. Distance comes as flatness. You're a better nurse now — more resilient, more stable, less reactive. But you're also a different nurse. That shift is not a failure. It's the cost of survival in a system that demands everything.

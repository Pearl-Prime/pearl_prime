

## INTEGRATION v01
---
mode: BODY_LANDED
reframe_type: BODY_FACT
weight: standard
carry_line: "The alarm is data, not the emergency."
---
Your shoulders are high. Your jaw is locked. The heart rate monitor you're not hooked to is running at 110. This is not failure. This is the system reading the room correctly — the complexity, the variables, the moment before entering that patient's room when everything narrows. Your nervous system is doing its job. The alarm fires because the work demands it. The cost comes later, when the shift ends and your body is still running threat response. Feel your feet on the linoleum. Your hands aren't shaking. Your voice still works. The body knows the difference between danger and high stakes. The alarm fires. You enter the room.

You carry it without measuring it.

## INTEGRATION v04
---
mode: QUESTION_OPEN
reframe_type: QUESTION_REFRAME
weight: standard
carry_line: "What would steady care actually cost to sustain."
---
You've learned to interpret the alarm as information. Threat, urgency, complexity, change. The body is fluent in clinical language. But something else lives underneath: the question of whether a nervous system built for occasional crisis can sustain constant high-stakes decision-making. Not whether your alarm is justified. It is. But whether running this way indefinitely is sustainable or whether the cost is being paid in ways the body will eventually reveal. You don't have the answer. The question sits in the quiet after the shift, in the parking garage, in the shower where nobody sees your hands still slightly trembling. The real question isn't whether you can handle the anxiety. It's whether handling it this way is the only option available to you.

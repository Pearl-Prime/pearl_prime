

## INTEGRATION v01
---
mode: BODY_LANDED
reframe_type: BODY_FACT
weight: standard
carry_line: "The alarm is data, not the emergency."
---
Your shoulders are high. Your jaw is locked. The heart rate monitor you're not hooked to is running at 110. This is not failure. This is the system reading the room correctly — the complexity, the variables, the moment before entering that patient's room when everything narrows. Your nervous system is doing its job. The alarm fires because the work demands it. The cost comes later, when the shift ends and your body is still running threat response. Feel your feet on the linoleum. Your hands aren't shaking. Your voice still works. The body knows the difference between danger and high stakes. The alarm fires. You enter the room.
---

## INTEGRATION v02
---
mode: COST_VISIBLE
reframe_type: COST_UNCOUNTED
weight: standard
carry_line: "Nobody counts the shifts you gave."
---
Twelve hours of living in the upper register. The cortisol that soaked every tissue. The adrenaline that made you sharp and then left you hollow. Charting says you covered six patients. The body ledger says something else. It says: early morning wake-ups that aren't scheduled. It says: the way you jump when the monitor alarms at home. It says: the restlessness that becomes a permanent feature, not a temporary response. You log your hours. The system logs your productivity. Nobody logs the wear. The cost of clinical readiness is a nervous system that never fully rests. That's not fragility. That's the price of precision in high-stakes care.
---

## INTEGRATION v03
---
mode: STILL_HERE
reframe_type: TIME_SHIFT
weight: light
carry_line: "You made it through what broke you."
note: STILL-HERE mode — final chapter only, one per book
---
You remember the first time the alarm fired like this. Your hands were shaking so badly you had to step away from the bedside. You thought you weren't made for this. That the anxiety meant something was wrong with you, not the work. Now the alarm fires the same way. Your hands are steady. Your voice is steady. You enter the room and do the thing. The tremor moved from your hands to the underside of your skin, where nobody can see it. That's not healing. That's acclimation. That's the nervous system learning to run high-intensity operations day after day while pretending it's normal. You are still here. The cost of that presence is distributed across your entire body. You carry it without measuring it.
---

## INTEGRATION v04
---
mode: QUESTION_OPEN
reframe_type: QUESTION_REFRAME
weight: standard
carry_line: "What would steady care actually cost to sustain."
---
You've learned to interpret the alarm as information. Threat, urgency, complexity, change. The body is fluent in clinical language. But something else lives underneath: the question of whether a nervous system built for occasional crisis can sustain constant high-stakes decision-making. Not whether your alarm is justified. It is. But whether running this way indefinitely is sustainable or whether the cost is being paid in ways the body will eventually reveal. You don't have the answer. The question sits in the quiet after the shift, in the parking garage, in the shower where nobody sees your hands still slightly trembling. The real question isn't whether you can handle the anxiety. It's whether handling it this way is the only option available to you.
---

## INTEGRATION v05
---
mode: SOMEONE_ELSE
reframe_type: OTHER_PERSON
weight: heavy
carry_line: "Someone else runs the same alarm."
---
In a hospital across the city, another RN is standing in a doorway. The alarm fires. Shoulders high. Breath shallow. The system reads the stakes. She's not new. She's not fragile. Her nervous system is doing exactly what it's trained to do. She doesn't know the pattern has a cost. Not yet. Not for years. You could tell her: the alarm is legitimate and the price is real. That both things are true at once. That the work demands this and the work extracts something for it. She's walking into that room now, same as you did. The professional face resets. The alarm fires. The system works.
---

## INTEGRATION v06
---
mode: BODY_LANDED
reframe_type: BODY_FACT
weight: interrupt_landing
carry_line: "You are here. Your hands work."
note: interrupt_landing — use after The Interrupt story, quietest atom in the book
---
The alarm fired. The pause opened. There was no moment to recalibrate. You entered the room and your hands knew what to do. That's not because you aren't anxious. It's because the anxiety doesn't stop the work. The body can run the alarm and still place the line, still assess, still respond. The two things exist in the same moment. There is no quiet version of you that does this job. There is only you, alarm and all, moving through the work that needs moving through.

## INTEGRATION v07
---
mode: COST_VISIBLE
reframe_type: COST_UNCOUNTED
weight: heavy
carry_line: "The cost compounds with every shift."
---
This isn't one night. It's three hundred and sixty-five nights. The nervous system toll compounds. The muscle tension that becomes chronic pain. The blood pressure that creeps up. The insomnia that feels normal until you can't remember what sleep felt like. The body keeps a ledger the system doesn't see. Productivity metrics don't account for the cellular cost of sustained threat response. Your patients will never know how much of this alarm is spent on their behalf. That's not martyrdom. That's the actual contract of clinical care.
---

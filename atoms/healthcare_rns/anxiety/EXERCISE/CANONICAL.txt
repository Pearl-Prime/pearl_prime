## EXERCISE v01
---

---
You're in fight-or-flight mode between patients.
Find a quiet spot, even a supply closet.
Press your feet hard into the floor.
Hold that pressure for five full counts.
Release. Notice what changed in your body.
Place one hand on your heart.
Feel it working. Feel it pumping.
Your body is alive. It's doing its job.
Now breathe in for four counts.
Out for six counts. Repeat three times.
---

## EXERCISE v02
---

---
Stand with your back against a wall.
Feel the wall supporting your entire back.
You don't have to hold yourself upright alone.
Let the wall help you for one minute.
Notice your shoulders dropping slightly.
Now name one patient you helped today.
One actual person. What did you do for them?
You handled that. You can handle this moment.
Feel your feet grounding into the floor.
You're here. You're safe. You're trained.
---

## EXERCISE v03
---

---
Splash cold water on your face and wrists.
Not to wake up. To reset your nervous system.
The cold interrupts the anxiety spiral.
Breathe in through your mouth. Out through your nose.
Reverse breathing calms your parasympathetic nervous system.
Do this five times in a row.
Your body will shift from threat to safe.
Check in: what's actually happening right now?
Not what might happen. What is happening?
You're here. You're breathing. You're okay.
---

## EXERCISE v04
---

---
Find the closest sink. Wash your hands the way you wash them between patients — soap, count, rinse. While you wash, name out loud or in your head what you are doing. "Soap. Lathering. Rinse." Do not skip the count. The hands have a script and the script is calming. When the hands are dry, take one slow breath in through the nose, out through the mouth. The hand-washing is also a transition. You did just finish something. The wash marks the end. Now you are between things, and the body can rest in the between for fifteen seconds before the next call light.
---

## EXERCISE v05
---

---
At the change-of-shift desk, before report. Put both hands flat on the counter. Press down. Feel the counter pushing back. The counter is solid. The shift has been less solid than the counter all day. Press for ten counts. Release. Press again. Ten counts. Release. Now look at the wall clock. Note the actual time. Out loud or in your head, say: "It is [time]. The shift is [over / not over yet]." The body sometimes needs the time stated explicitly to believe it. Then give or take report.
---

## EXERCISE v06
---

---
In the car after shift. Engine on or off, your choice. Both hands on the wheel. Push the seat back if you can. Press your back into the seat. Squeeze the steering wheel for a count of eight. Release. Squeeze for eight. Release. Squeeze for eight. Release. Now let the hands fall into your lap. Breathe in through the nose for four. Out through the mouth for six. Five cycles. The car is the airlock. Do not drive yet. The body needs the airlock to remember that the unit is back there and you are out here.
---

## EXERCISE v07
---

---
Standing at the bedside between tasks. Without the patient noticing, feel both your feet inside your shoes. Notice which one is bearing more weight. Shift to the other foot. Notice. Shift back. Notice. The micro-shift discharges nervous-system tension through the legs and the patient sees only a nurse standing there competently. Two cycles. Then breathe in through the nose. Then exhale slow. The grounding is invisible. The reset is real.
---

## EXERCISE v08
---

---
In a supply closet or any space with a door. Close the door. Lean your back against it. Slide down until you are squatting. Or just lean. Whichever the legs want. Close your eyes. Breathe in for four. Hold for two. Out for six. Repeat for ten breaths. Do not check the phone. Do not run the patient list. The ten breaths are not optional and not productive. They are maintenance. The supply closet does not know you are doing maintenance. That is fine. After ten breaths, stand up, open the door, and re-enter the floor. The reset has happened. Nobody needs to know.
---

## EXERCISE v09
---

---
Home, after shift, scrubs off. Sit in any chair. Put both hands palm-up on your thighs. Look at the palms. The palms held charts and IV bags and the hands of three patients today. The palms did real work. Without thanking them in any specific way, just look at them. Breathe in. Breathe out. The looking is enough. The hands have been seen. Repeat once a day, for as many days as it takes for the hands to know they are not on the clock right now.

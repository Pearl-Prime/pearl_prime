

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v01
---
mode: SECULAR_BRIDGE
doctrine_source: somatic
mechanism: bedside_vigilance
weight: standard
carry_line: "The watching is the job. The job stops at clock-out. The watching does not. The body never agreed to leave it at the unit."
---
Here is the mechanism. Your nervous system was not built to watch human bodies in failure for twelve hours straight, then twelve more, then four more on overtime. It was built to track one or two human bodies at a time, in stable conditions, with long stretches of nothing in between. The job inverts every parameter. Four patients. Six on a bad night. Each one with a vital-sign trajectory the body is silently tracking even when you are not at the bedside. Each one with a chart you are mentally re-checking against the last MAR pass. Each one with a family in the chair that is also being tracked, because their version of the story will arrive at the desk by morning. The watching is heavy. It does not stop because the shift ends. The body learned to watch. It does not know how to un-watch on cue. So you come home and your kid is fine and you are still scanning for color, for breath sound, for the small thing wrong. That is not a flaw in you. That is the cost of twelve hours of trained watching, paid back to the body in the only currency the body knows. What changes when you see this clearly is that the watching keeps happening, but you start to recognize it for what it is. The vigilance becomes information about your nervous system, not a verdict on the kid in the chair eating cereal. That gap is where the rest of your life gets built.

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v02
---
mode: SECULAR_BRIDGE
doctrine_source: somatic
mechanism: moral_injury
weight: standard
carry_line: "You did the right thing the wrong system would let you do. The gap between what you could give and what the patient needed is not your failure. It is the system's, and the body is paying its rent anyway."
---
Here is the mechanism. Moral injury is the word that arrived after the word burnout stopped fitting. Burnout suggests exhaustion. Moral injury names something else — the specific pain that comes from being placed, repeatedly, in situations where you cannot do what you know is right. You know what the patient needs. You also know your ratio. You know the next ICU bed is four hours away and the family is asking, and your hands are full, and the call light is on, and the order has been pending in the system for forty minutes because pharmacy is short-staffed. You do the thing you can do, which is not the thing you would do if the system were what it should be. You walk away knowing the difference. The body knows the difference too. It logs each gap. It does not log them as "system failures." It logs them as something more personal, because the body that walked away is the one logging. That is not pathology. That is what happens when a system places good people in conditions designed to grind down their judgment, and the judgment, being trained, keeps issuing verdicts on a self that did the only thing the conditions allowed. What changes when you see this is that the verdicts do not stop, but you can start to recognize them as verdicts on the gap, not verdicts on you. Naming the gap aloud, even in the shower, even just once, is part of how the body learns to file the gap somewhere other than the self.

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v03
---
mode: SECULAR_BRIDGE
doctrine_source: somatic
mechanism: cumulative_witnessing
weight: standard
carry_line: "You have seen more deaths in a year than most civilians will see in a lifetime. The body counts what the mind tries to stop counting. The count is real."
---
Here is the mechanism. Civilians have, on average, a small number of bedside deaths across an entire life. A grandparent. A parent. Eventually a peer. You have more than that in a week, in some units. The mind tries to file each one — make sense of it, attach a context, find the meaning — and after a while the filing system runs out of folders. The body keeps counting whether the mind files or not. It registers each one in tissue. It accumulates. Twenty years in, the accumulation is real, even if you cannot list the names. The anxiety that arrives on the drive in, the dread that lands at the time clock, the chest tightness when the call bell rings — these are not separate events. They are the count surfacing in the only language the body has. Naming this is not the same as solving it. You cannot un-count. But you can stop pretending the count is not there, which is part of what costs the most. The pretending is what makes the body have to work harder to be heard. What changes when you see this is that the count keeps being a count, but you stop demanding that the body act like the count is not happening. The bedside still asks for what it asks for. The body, off shift, gets permission to be a body that has counted that much.

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v04
---
mode: SECULAR_BRIDGE
doctrine_source: somatic
mechanism: twelve_hour_circadian
weight: standard
carry_line: "Twelve hours on the floor, then thirty-minute lunch, then twelve more — your hormones did not consent to this schedule. The anxiety at hour ten is partly the cortisol curve, not just the day."
---
Here is the mechanism. Your body runs on a daily hormone cycle that was designed for hunting and gathering, for sleep at dark and activity at light, for meals at predictable intervals. The twelve-hour shift breaks every assumption that cycle was built on. Cortisol, which should peak in the morning and decline through the day, gets called back up at hour eight because the call light went on. Insulin, which should respond to a planned meal, gets a granola bar at the desk at 1500. Melatonin, which should rise after dark, gets canceled by fluorescent lights at 0200. The body adapts as much as it can. It cannot adapt all the way. The anxiety that arrives around hour ten, the irritability at hour eleven, the dread of the next admission at hour eleven and a half — these are not character failings. They are the predictable behavior of a hormone system that has been asked to run a marathon under conditions designed for a sprint. What changes when you see this is that the anxiety still arrives at hour ten. But you stop reading it as a verdict on whether you are cut out for the job, and start reading it as the predictable price of asking the body to do twelve hours of vigilance on broken sleep and a granola bar. The body is doing what it can. The schedule is what it is.

## TEACHER_DOCTRINE v05
---
mode: SECULAR_BRIDGE
doctrine_source: somatic
mechanism: caregiver_depletion
weight: standard
carry_line: "You gave compassion to thirty bodies today. The well is not infinite. The anxiety in the parking lot is the empty well telling you it needs filling, and you are not built to fill it from your own bucket."
---
Here is the mechanism. Compassion is real work. It uses real attention, real presence, real warmth. The brain that gives compassion is doing measurable work, the same way the legs that walk a hallway are doing measurable work. Twelve hours of compassion, distributed across four to six patients, plus families, plus the call light, plus the new admission at hour ten, draws down a finite reserve. The reserve does not refill on shift. It refills off shift, in sleep, in stillness, in the company of people who are giving you compassion instead of asking for it. The anxiety in the parking lot at end of shift, the dread of the drive home, the irritation when your own family asks for one more thing at the door — these are not personality failures. They are an empty reserve telling you it is empty, in the only language an empty reserve can speak. What changes when you see this is that the depletion is still real. You cannot will the reserve back up. But you can recognize the signals as signals, not as evidence that you are no longer capable of caring. The caring is still there. The well is just empty. Wells refill on their own time, not on demand. Naming this is part of giving them the time they need.

## PERMISSION_GRANT v01
---

---
The job comes with a posture. The competent nurse who handles it. The one who stays late. The one who does the extra chart. The one who does not bring it home. That posture has saved careers. It has also covered up a body that is not, in fact, fine. You are allowed to set the posture down for an hour.

You are allowed to be tired in a way the break room cannot fix. The eleven-minute lunch is not rest. The bag of chips is not food. The body has been on its feet for ten hours and it is not done. The tiredness is not a character problem. It is the predictable outcome of the math. You are allowed to feel it without explaining it.

You are allowed to need silence after the shift. The drive home does not have to be a podcast. The car does not have to be filled with input. The body has been in input for twelve hours. You are allowed to drive the long way home through the silence, with the radio off, and let the silence be a kind of medicine.

You are allowed to grieve a patient who died on your shift. Not later. Now. In the bathroom. In the car. In the parking lot. The grief does not need a ritual. It does not need anyone's permission to start. The grief is the right response to losing a person, even one you did not know last week. You are allowed to be wet-faced and still capable.

You are allowed to be angry at the system. Not at the patient. Not at the family. At the system. The understaffing. The chart that does not let you chart what you saw. The MAR that runs you in circles. The administrator who has not been on the floor in a decade. The anger is appropriate. The anger does not make you a bad nurse. It makes you a nurse who has been paying attention.

You are allowed to leave the unit at the unit. The change of shift report is the official handoff. There is also an unofficial handoff, the one where you let your body know that the bedside is no longer yours for twelve hours. You are allowed to perform that handoff out loud, in your car, to yourself, in a sentence. "I am off shift. The unit has it from here."

You are allowed to be the consciousness watching the anxiety, not the anxiety itself. The chest tightens at the time clock. The dread arrives at change of shift. And somewhere underneath, awareness keeps watching. The awareness is older than the badge. It is still there. It is the part of you the job has never been able to deplete.

These are not affirmations. They are descriptions of the rights a body comes with, before the unit, during the unit, and after. Read them once a shift. Let the body work them in.

## PERMISSION_GRANT v02
---

---
Anxiety, on the unit, looks like competence. The constant scanning. The mental running of the MAR. The fifth check of the IV line. Underneath the competence is a nervous system that has been asked to track too much for too long. Set the scanning down for the next hour.

You are allowed to not know everything about your patients all the time. You can look at the chart. The chart is a tool. The mind running parallel to the chart, holding all the same data in tissue, is doing labor that is not paid for and not asked for. You are allowed to let the chart hold what the chart was built to hold.

You are allowed to ask for help with a task. The team is a team. The hierarchy of who-asks-whom is not a verdict on your competence. It is a workflow. You are allowed to use the workflow without translating the ask into evidence that you are failing.

You are allowed to not be the strong one tonight. The role of "the one who handles it" is allowed to be empty for one shift, or one hour, or one bathroom break. Someone else in the unit can be that role tonight. The patients will be cared for. The shift will run. You are allowed to be the one who needs a minute.

You are allowed to be honest with the patient when honest is the right thing. You do not have to manage every family interaction at the level of a press briefing. Some families need the truth. Some need the gentle version. You are allowed to read the room and choose, and to also be wrong sometimes about which they needed.

You are allowed to take the bathroom break before the call light is answered. The bladder is real. The lunch is real. The water bottle by the desk is real. The unit was running before you put on the badge and it will run if you are gone for four minutes. You are allowed to take the four minutes.

You are allowed to be a nurse and a person at the same time. Not a nurse who happens to have a body. A person who is also a nurse. The two are not in a fixed ratio. The ratio shifts. You are allowed to let it shift toward person at the end of the shift, without feeling like you have abandoned the role.

These are the standing permissions. Read them slow. The body knows what to do with them. The mind catches up later.

## PERMISSION_GRANT v03
---

---
The unit teaches you to read rooms. To read patients. To read families. To read charts. The reading is constant. The reading is also a drain. You are allowed to not read every room you walk into for the rest of your life.

You are allowed to be in a restaurant without assessing the elderly woman two tables over. The assessment will start anyway. The training is real. You are allowed to notice the assessment, complete one pass, and then let it go. You are not on the clock at the restaurant. The training does not need to bill the meal.

You are allowed to not give medical advice at the family gathering. The brother-in-law's question about his back can wait for an appointment. You are allowed to say "I can't really tell you over dinner — see your GP." You are allowed to mean it. The training does not have to perform for relatives.

You are allowed to forget what unit you were on last week. Twelve years in, the units blur. The patients blur. The names go. The faces go. Some of the keeping is the body's mercy. You are allowed to let it forget. The keeping that matters keeps itself.

You are allowed to want out without it meaning failure. Many nurses leave. Some come back. Some do not. The wanting-out is real data. It is not a character verdict. It is the body telling you something about the trajectory. You are allowed to listen to the data without committing to act on it tonight.

You are allowed to be proud of the work without performing the pride. The work was hard. You did it. The doing is enough. You do not have to make it a story for the public, or post about it, or accept the thank-you-for-your-service script. The work was the work. The body that did it knows.

You are allowed to age inside the body. The varicose veins are real. The plantar fasciitis is real. The back that does not like the night-shift bed is real. You are allowed to have the body the job has built, and to also love that body, and to also ask less of it as the years stack.

You are allowed to be the consciousness watching the nurse, not the nurse itself. The role is a role. The wearer of the role is older. The wearer is the part of you that the badge was never large enough to define.

These are not affirmations. They are accurate descriptions of the rights a healthcare worker comes with. Read them. The body will know.

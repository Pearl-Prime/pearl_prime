Priya has, in the last two years, instituted a practice she calls — to herself, only — the doorway minute.

The doorway minute is what she does after she signs out at the end of shift, but before she leaves the floor. Specifically, she walks to the doorway of the unit — the actual doorway between the unit's secure entry and the hallway that leads to the elevators — and she stops there for sixty seconds.

In the sixty seconds, she does three things. She names, inwardly, three patients she had on her assignment that shift, by their first names. She names, inwardly, one thing she did for each patient that did not appear in the chart. And she puts her hand briefly on the doorframe and says, inwardly, the same sentence she has said every shift for two years: I was here. I am leaving. The unit continues.

The practice had emerged about three years into her career as a result of a specific incident she does not, at this point, remember the full details of. She remembers that there had been a patient who had died on her shift. She remembers that she had stayed an extra ninety minutes finishing the paperwork. She remembers that she had walked off the unit and had not come back for the next three days because she had been sick — or had been telling herself she was sick — and that when she had come back, she had felt, for about four weeks, like she had not entirely returned.

The not-entirely-returning had been the problem the doorway minute had been built to solve. She had developed, on her own, with no instruction from anyone, the recognition that she needed to mark the transition. The chart did not mark the transition. The clock did not mark the transition. The transition needed to be marked by her body, with intention, at the threshold, every shift.

For the first year of the practice, she had not been able to do it consistently. Some shifts ended too chaotically. Some shifts she forgot. Some shifts she did the practice and felt nothing in particular. She had kept doing it anyway. The keeping-doing-it had been the discipline.

By year two of the practice, the doorway minute had become reliable. The body had come to expect it. On shifts when she skipped it — usually because she was running late for something — the body would protest. The protest took the form of a low-grade unease for the rest of the evening, a feeling that something had been left undone.

By year fifteen, the doorway minute is one of the few things she does at the hospital that has not been measured, evaluated, or written into any policy. The doorway minute is hers. The doorway minute is the body's transition. The doorway minute is what allows her to take the work home in a way that does not consume the home.

She does it tonight. The shift had been hard but not catastrophic. She had cared for seven patients. Two of them had been challenging in different ways. She had spent extra time with one family. She had given a small medication that another nurse had questioned, gotten the resident to confirm, and the medication had done what it was supposed to do.

She walks to the doorway. She stops. She names three patients. Mrs. R, the post-op from this morning. Mr. K, the family conversation. Mrs. T, the question about the medication. For Mr. K, she had stayed for the conversation with his son when she could have stepped out. For Mrs. T, she had pushed the medication question with the resident when the easier thing would have been to wait until morning.

The building had taken time. The result is one minute, at one doorway, with one sentence. The minute does the work. The minute is enough.

Mila has begun, in the last four months, to read fiction again.

She had stopped reading fiction around year three of her psych nursing career. She had not stopped on purpose. She had stopped because the fiction had begun to feel insufficient — too neat, too narratively resolved, too unlike the actual texture of the work. The patients on her unit did not have arcs. The patients on her unit had episodes. The fiction was about characters who changed. The patients on her unit, mostly, did not change. They cycled. They came in, they stabilized, they were discharged, they came back. The arc-shaped narratives had begun to feel like a kind of consumer product she could not afford to believe in.

For five years she had read only nonfiction. Mostly memoirs by other clinicians. Sometimes books about the history of psychiatry. Once or twice, books about the philosophy of mental illness. The nonfiction had felt usable. The nonfiction had not asked her to believe in arcs.

Four months ago, a colleague had given her a novel for her birthday. The colleague was a social worker on her unit who knew Mila and liked her and did not know that Mila had stopped reading fiction. The novel was a literary novel about a woman in her forties dealing with the aftermath of her mother's death. Mila had not asked for the novel. The novel had sat on her shelf for three weeks.

One Saturday afternoon, with no particular intention, Mila had picked it up. She had read the first chapter. The first chapter had not been about arcs. The first chapter had been about a woman in a kitchen, holding her dead mother's coffee mug, having a small private moment of incomprehension. The incomprehension had been precise. The incomprehension had been recognizable.

Mila had kept reading. By the end of the day she had read a hundred and forty pages. The novel had not delivered an arc. The novel had delivered, in close and careful detail, a series of small private moments of incomprehension. The protagonist had not changed across the moments. The protagonist had been, in the novel's terms, exactly the same person at the end as at the beginning, only the person had now been seen — by the novel, by herself, by the reader — in a way she had not been seen before.

Mila had finished the novel in two days. She had then ordered three more by the same author. She had read all three over the next month.

What this had unlocked, for her, was the recognition that fiction did not have to be about arcs. Fiction could be about presence. Fiction could be about the close and careful attention to small private moments of incomprehension, which was, in fact, what her patients lived in, and what the psychiatric literature could not describe with the same precision the novels could.

The fiction had become, in some quiet way, a kind of training. Not training in technique. Training in attention. The novels were doing a kind of close attention to interior states that her unit, on its best day, also did. The novels were better at it, because the novels had more time. The unit had to do the close attention in fifteen-minute increments, between rounds, between med passes, between admissions. The novels did it for three hundred pages without interruption.

She had begun to read the novels at intervals during her week. Not on shift. On her days off, in the morning, before the day's errands. The reading had become a small practice she had not planned and had not yet named.

What she has begun to understand, four months in, is that the reading is doing for her what the unit cannot — sustained, slow, uninterrupted attention to interior states, performed at the level of detail her training had taught her to value but that the institutional structure of her work did not allow her to perform.

The reading is a kind of skill maintenance. It is also, she suspects, a kind of slow medicine for her own interior, which has been receiving less and less close attention as her career has progressed. The patients have been receiving the attention. She has not. The novels are correcting the balance, in small private morning increments, on her days off, in a kitchen that has begun, in the last four months, to be a different kind of room.

This is what experimentation looks like at year eight. Not a transformation. A novel a colleague gave her. A Saturday afternoon. A pattern of reading that has begun to repair, in slow ways, what the work has been depleting. The unit will continue to deplete. The reading will continue, on her own time, to refill what the unit takes. The balance is not, perhaps, recoverable in full. The reading is making the imbalance survivable.

He has gone to the grocery store eight times, which is more times than the grocery store warrants but is what the apartment allows. He has gone to a doctor once for a routine appointment. He has gone to his mother's house twice, both times for dinner, both times to deflect his mother's questions about whether he was sleeping.

He has been sleeping. He has not been sleeping well. He has been sleeping in installments — three hours, two hours, three more hours — across nights that begin around 22:00 and end around 09:00. The sleep architecture is not, in any reasonable sense, sleep. It is rest distributed across the night, in pieces that the body permits at intervals.

What he has been doing during the waking hours is — and this is the part that is taking him by surprise — nothing in particular. He has watched television. He has scrolled his phone. He has eaten meals that did not interest him. He has taken walks that did not refresh him.

He has not, in three weeks, been able to cry again. The crying had wanted to come up during the procedure. The crying had been ready. The crying had been interrupted by the procedure. The crying had not, since the procedure, found a new venue. He had thought, before the leave started, that the apartment would be the venue. The apartment had not turned out to be the venue. The apartment was too quiet. The apartment had too much space for the crying to organize itself.

What he is beginning to understand, in week three, is that the crying had been about something specific that had happened over the last year, and the crying had been blocked by the something specific not yet having been named to anyone.

The something specific had been an institutional decision, made roughly a year ago, to require all OR nurses to participate in productivity benchmarks that had been calculated in a way that did not include the time needed for genuine pre-op patient conversations. The decision had been justified by the institution as efficiency optimization. The decision had had the effect, over the course of the year, of reducing the pre-op conversation time from an average of four minutes per patient to an average of forty seconds.

Joaquin had been an OR nurse for twelve years. The pre-op conversation had been, in his understanding, one of the irreducible elements of his work. The four minutes had been the time during which the patient — usually anxious, often frightened, sometimes outright terrified — had been able to look him in the face and be acknowledged as a person before they were rolled into the OR. The forty seconds did not allow for that acknowledgment. The forty seconds was a procedural greeting. The forty seconds was hello, my name is Joaquin, I am your nurse, I will be with you the whole time, do you have any questions.

Most patients did not have questions in forty seconds. Most patients had questions that emerged in minute two of the four-minute conversation, which the new structure did not allow them to reach.

Joaquin had been doing the forty-second version for a year. The forty-second version had been eating something in him that he had not been able to name. The crying during the procedure had been the something finally surfacing. The procedure had been a routine procedure for a sixty-three-year-old patient who, in the forty seconds before the procedure, had looked Joaquin in the face and had said, "I am scared," and Joaquin had not had time to respond properly, and the procedure had begun, and the patient had been anesthetized, and Joaquin had stood beside the table and had felt something arrive in his chest that had been arriving in small installments for a year and was now arriving all at once.

This is the thing he has not yet said to anyone. He has not said it to the counselor he has not yet called. He has not said it to his mother during the dinners. He has not said it to himself in words, until this exact moment, in his apartment, in week three of the leave, on a Tuesday afternoon, while sitting on his couch.

He sits on the couch. He says, out loud, to the empty apartment: I have not been allowed to do my job. I have not been allowed to do my job for a year. I have been doing a version of my job that is not my job. The thing the procedure made me cry about is that I have been participating in a structure that took the four minutes away from a patient who was scared, and I did not stop participating.

The saying does not solve anything. The saying does something else. The saying gives the something specific a name. The something specific had been operating in his body for a year without a name. The name, once said, makes a small but real change in the something. The something becomes, in part, a sentence that can be said. The sentence can be said to the counselor, when he calls her. The sentence can be said to his nurse manager, possibly, when he goes back. The sentence can be said to other OR nurses, who are probably carrying versions of the same sentence.

The leave is not over. The leave has, at week three, finally produced something the apartment had been waiting to produce. The apartment had needed three weeks of quiet to allow the sentence to surface. The surface is the work that the leave had been for.

This is what confronting looks like at year twelve, after twelve years of mostly not having to confront. Not a single event. A leave. An apartment. Three weeks. A sentence finally said, out loud, to a room with no one in it. The room is enough. The saying is enough. The next steps will come.

Eun-Ji has, by year twenty-one, arrived at an arrangement with her body that she would not have predicted at year five.

The arrangement is that she no longer fights the body's signals. The signals are, by now, well-known to her. There is the low-grade tightness in the chest before a death visit, which has not gone away in twenty-one years and which she has come to accept as the body's way of preparing her to be useful in a room she is about to enter. There is the longer exhale that arrives after a death visit, which signals that the body has finished its work in the room and is in the process of restoring its baseline. There is the heavy-limbs feeling that arrives, sometimes, in the evenings of days when she has done more than one death visit, which is the body's report that the day has been more than the day's allowance.

She does not fight any of these. She names them. She honors them. She has, over years, built her schedule around them.

The schedule, by year twenty-one, has a shape that took her about ten years to negotiate with her agency. She does not do more than two death visits in a day. She does not do death visits on Mondays — Mondays are for follow-ups with families, for charting, for the slower kinds of work. She does not do back-to-back night calls. She does not take new admissions on the same day as a death.

The schedule is not what the agency would prefer. The agency would prefer that she be more flexible. She has been, in small ways, willing to be flexible at the margins. She is not flexible on the core. The core has been negotiated over years. The agency has, eventually, accepted it, because Eun-Ji is one of the most experienced hospice nurses they have and they would prefer her on the schedule with limits than off the schedule entirely.

She has not lost the work. She has not been pushed out. She has, by year twenty-one, become a kind of unofficial reference for the agency on how a hospice nurse sustains a long career. Younger nurses have begun to ask her, in quiet moments, how she does it. She has, in response, begun to share the arrangement.

The sharing is careful. She does not present the arrangement as universal. She presents it as her arrangement, which had emerged from her body, which had been negotiated with her agency over years, and which would have to be re-negotiated by each nurse with her own body and her own agency, on her own timeline.

The sharing has, in the last three years, begun to produce a small ripple. Two younger nurses have, with her informal mentorship, begun to construct their own arrangements. The arrangements are not the same as hers. The arrangements are theirs. Both have been pleasantly surprised, in different ways, by the agency's willingness to accommodate.

What Eun-Ji has come to understand is that the agency's willingness had been there all along, in some latent form. The agency had not been opposed to nurse-led scheduling. The agency had been waiting for nurses to ask. Most nurses had not asked. The not-asking had been a feature of the culture, not a feature of the agency. The culture had told nurses that asking would be a sign of weakness. The culture had been wrong. Eun-Ji had, over years, discovered the wrongness, and she had begun to slowly, quietly, repair it.

She is at her own kitchen table on a Sunday morning, drinking tea. She has a death visit scheduled for Tuesday. She has charted everything she needed to chart for last week. This is what integrating looks like at year twenty-one. Eun-Ji is, at year twenty-one, one of the people who is making the propagation possible by simply having done the listening and refusing, in small ways over many years, to stop listening when the culture had asked her to.

She was at the front door at 20:14 and the body did not know it was off the unit. The scrubs were still on. The badge was still clipped. The smell of the unit was on the sleeves of the jacket she had put on for the parking lot.

She stood in the entryway for ninety seconds. Not because she was trying to do something. Because the body had not yet been told it could move into the rest of the house. The body was still completing a posture it had been holding for thirteen hours. The posture had a specific shape — head slightly forward, shoulders rounded over the patients she was not currently holding, breath shallow at the clavicle, attentional aperture wide enough to track six rooms simultaneously.

The aperture did not narrow when she crossed the threshold. The aperture stayed wide. Her body was still scanning, but the scan had no patients to find, because the patients were across town in a building she was not in. The scan did not know that. The scan was on automatic, and the automatic was operating on a schedule the parking lot had not interrupted.

By 21:30 she was in pajamas. The body had completed roughly forty percent of the discharge. The scrubs were in the wash. The shoes were by the door. Dinner was something she had eaten without remembering. She was sitting on the couch with the TV on and the TV was something her eyes were aimed at and the eyes were not actually receiving any of it because the aperture was still wide and the aperture was still looking for patients.

At 22:47 her phone buzzed. It was a coworker, asking about a chart from earlier in the shift. The chart was fine. The coworker just had a quick question. The body went on full alert before the message had finished loading, because the body had recognized the cadence of a unit communication, and the body had not been told that it was off duty, and the body, sincerely and faithfully, returned to the unit in the chest.

She answered the question. She put the phone down. The aperture stayed wide. By the time sleep arrived, sometime past 23:30, the aperture had narrowed enough for the body to release its first full breath since morning. The first full breath since morning came thirteen hours and forty-seven minutes after the shift had started. The math of it she would not see clearly until the next morning, in the parking lot, before the next shift.

He had been a nurse for fourteen years. The first six had been the good years. The middle four had been the years he started to notice the body. The last four had been the years the body had stopped letting him not notice it.

The noticing was not a single moment. It was a long accumulation of moments. The morning he had walked to the unit and felt his stomach drop on the elevator, the way a stomach drops on a small descent. The afternoon he had stayed in the bathroom for six minutes after handwashing, not crying, just standing, because the body needed the bathroom to be a room he was alone in. The night he had charted a death and gone to the next room and the body had not allowed his face to fully resume the next-room posture, and the patient in the next room had noticed and had said, gently, are you okay, and he had said yes, because he was working, and the patient had let him be working.

The patient who had noticed had become, in his memory, a kind of marker. The patient had seen something the institution was not equipped to see. The patient had named, with the simplest possible question, the thing the institution had no language for. The patient had been seventy-three. The patient had died eleven days later, on a different shift, not his. He had read about it in the handoff. The body had held a small unbillable grief in the locker room for the patient who had asked.

The point is that the unit produced these moments at a rate the body could not metabolize. Not because the body was insufficient. Because the rate was incompatible with metabolism. The body needed an interval to process each loss, each near-loss, each small dignified moment between him and someone he would not see again. The shift did not provide the interval. The next call light arrived. The next admission arrived. The next family with questions arrived.

By year fourteen, the body had built a system for handling the lack of interval. The system was to hold each loss in a place inside the chest that was not quite available for daily life. The place inside the chest got fuller. The system was working, in the sense that he was still showing up. The system was not working, in the sense that the fullness was beginning to leak — into sleep, into his relationships with people outside the unit, into the part of him that was supposed to have access to humor.

He did not call this anxiety. He called this being a senior nurse. The two words were doing the same work, but only one of them was honest.

She had started doing something new on the drive home. Not every shift. Not as a discipline. As something the body had begun asking for, by way of the small thing it did when she pulled out of the parking lot.

The body had begun, around month four of a new practice she did not have a name for, to want a song. Not music in general. A specific song. The same one. A song from her parents' country that her mother used to hum in the kitchen when she was small. The body would, somewhere between the exit gate and the first stoplight, ask for the song.

She did not know, the first time it happened, that the body was asking. She just turned the song on. The song played. The body, by the second verse, did something it had not done in years. The body released a single tear from the corner of her left eye, and the tear had no thought attached to it. The tear was not for any particular patient. The tear was for the day. The tear was a single drop of a discharge the body had been deferring for the entirety of the shift.

She had let the tear happen. She had not analyzed it. She had not catalogued it as evidence of anything in particular. The song had finished. The drive had continued. She had arrived home with the body slightly different than it had been at the parking lot.

The body had taken note. The body had remembered the sequence. The next shift, on the drive home, the body had asked for the song again. Not in words. In the way the chest had been tight at the exit gate and had loosened by a small amount at the thought of the song. She had played the song. The same thing had happened. Not always one tear. Sometimes no tears. Sometimes a long exhale she had not been planning. Sometimes a small wordless humming, which she had also not been planning, and which she had been embarrassed by, and which the body had ignored her embarrassment about and had hummed anyway.

By month nine of the song-on-the-drive practice, the practice had become the practice. Not a fix. Not a cure. A small ritual the body had instituted, from inside, on its own authority, to do the somatic clean-up the shift had not allowed. The ritual was four minutes long. The four minutes did not undo twelve hours. The four minutes did something the twelve hours had not had the opportunity to do: signal to the body that the shift was, in fact, ending, and that the body was being met at the threshold by something that was hers.

This is what integration looks like in this work. Not a quiet body. A body that has been allowed to develop, on its own quiet schedule, a small repeated act that the shift cannot interrupt. The song is on a playlist. The playlist is in her phone. The phone is in her car. The car is in the parking lot. The parking lot is the threshold the body has come to recognize as the place the song belongs.

Not resolution. A different relationship to the noise. The song is part of the relationship now.

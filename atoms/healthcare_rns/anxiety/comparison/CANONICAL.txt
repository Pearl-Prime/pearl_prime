---
Nora watches Elena take report. Elena writes in a shorthand that fits an entire patient on three lines. Nora's report sheets are dense—full sentences, medication times, lab values, social notes. Elena's shorthand looks like efficiency. Nora's full sentences look like compensation. She doesn't know if Elena's shorthand misses things. She doesn't know if her own full sentences catch more. She knows that Elena looks fast and she looks slow. The comparison runs on visible speed, not on invisible accuracy. The metric she's using isn't the metric that matters. She uses it anyway.

Tomás finishes his charting forty minutes after the senior nurse finishes hers. Same patient acuity. Same documentation requirements. Forty minutes slower. The forty minutes feel like a professional verdict. The senior nurse has been doing this for twelve years. Tomás has been doing it for five. The seven-year gap explains the forty minutes completely. ## MECHANISM_PROOF v01
---
path: story_atoms/healthcare_rns/anchored/anxiety/comparison/mechanism_proof/micro/v01.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Nora maps what the comparison actually measures. She compares herself to Elena's speed, to Raj's calm, to the charge nurse's authority. Each comparison uses a single variable. Speed. Calm. Authority. She's never compared using her own strongest variables: thoroughness, patient education, family communication. The comparison selects the metric where she's weakest relative to the other person's strength. It's comparing her floor to their ceiling. The comparison framework is structurally designed to produce a deficit. Any nurse compared to another nurse's single strongest trait will lose. The comparison guarantees the loss by choosing the metric.
---

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02

BAND: 2
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Tomás discovers the comparison has a direction: he only compares upward. Nurses more experienced, faster, calmer. He never compares to the new graduate who takes ninety minutes to chart what he charts in fifty. He never compares to the traveler who needed three shifts to learn the unit's flow while he learned it in one. The comparison system filters out everyone he's ahead of and retains everyone he's behind. The filter produces a world where he's always last. He's not last. He's in the middle of a distribution that the comparison won't let him see.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v03

BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Aisha realizes the comparison increases during high-stress shifts. When the unit is calm, she works without measuring herself against anyone. When the unit is chaotic, every other nurse becomes a benchmark. The comparison activates under threat—as though high-stress situations require her to prove she belongs. The need to prove belonging is the anxiety. The comparison is the anxiety's measurement tool. It runs when she feels most vulnerable and produces data that increases the vulnerability. The comparison is a stress response disguised as an assessment. It feels like information. It functions like a weapon.

Claire identifies the comparison's effect on her teaching. She precepts new nurses. When a preceptee excels quickly, Claire compares the preceptee's trajectory to her own and finds hers slower. Derek calculates the cognitive cost of comparison. During a twelve-hour shift, he estimates he spends twenty to thirty minutes in active comparison—measuring his speed against the nurse next to him, his confidence against the charge nurse, his clinical knowledge against the attending. ## TURNING_POINT v01

MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Nora uses her own metric. Not Elena's speed, not Raj's calm. Her metric: did the patient and family leave the interaction with more understanding than they entered with. She runs the metric on her last three shifts. Three shifts, fourteen patient-family education sessions, eleven of which produced visible comprehension. The number is hers. The metric measures what she actually does, not what someone else does better. The comparison tries to redirect: but Elena is faster. The metric holds: Elena's speed is Elena's metric. Tomás compares downward for the first time. Not cruelly—accurately. Derek reads his own clinical write-up from six months ago. Then reads his most recent one. The improvement is visible: tighter language, clearer assessments, better narrative structure.

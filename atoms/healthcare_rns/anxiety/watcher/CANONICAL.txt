## MECHANISM_PROOF v01
---
path: story_atoms/healthcare_rns/anchored/anxiety/watcher/mechanism_proof/micro/v01.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Nora identifies the watcher's unique feature in nursing: the stakes justify it. In most professions, self-monitoring is excessive. In nursing, a missed detail can mean a death. The watcher uses this truth to maintain permanent authority. You must watch, because the alternative is negligence. The logic is sound. The application is pathological. She doesn't need to evaluate a successful IV after it's placed. She doesn't need to recheck a correct medication after it's administered. The watcher converts reasonable vigilance into perpetual self-surveillance. The stakes make the surveillance feel mandatory.
---

## MECHANISM_PROOF v02
---
path: story_atoms/healthcare_rns/anchored/anxiety/watcher/mechanism_proof/micro/v02.txt
BAND: 2
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: opportunity
COST_INTENSITY: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Tomás calculates the watcher's bandwidth cost. In any interaction, approximately thirty percent of his attention goes to the watcher—monitoring his own performance, his audience's reactions, his professional presentation. That's thirty percent of cognitive capacity diverted from the actual task. In a twelve-hour shift with continuous patient interactions, the watcher consumes roughly three and a half hours of processing power that could go to clinical reasoning, patient assessment, or just being present. The watcher doesn't improve his nursing. The watcher taxes it.

---
Aisha notices the watcher is strongest during emotional moments. Routine tasks—vitals, medication, charting—the watcher runs at low volume. But the moment a parent cries, or a child is scared, or a conversation requires genuine emotion, the watcher amplifies to maximum. The emotional moments are when she needs to be most present. The watcher is most active during these moments. The mechanism steals presence from the moments that most require it. She performs empathy under surveillance. The performance is convincing. The empathy is diluted by the monitoring.

## MECHANISM_PROOF v04
---
path: story_atoms/healthcare_rns/anchored/anxiety/watcher/mechanism_proof/micro/v04.txt
BAND: 4
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 4
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Claire discovers the watcher has rewritten good outcomes. The delivery went well: "got lucky." The code save worked: "the team carried it." The patient recovered: "it was going to happen anyway." The watcher can't process positive outcomes as evidence of competence because competence would reduce the need for watching. The watcher needs to be necessary. So the watcher reframes every success as either luck, team effort, or inevitability—never as Claire's skill. The reframing keeps the watcher employed. The watcher is protecting its own job at the expense of Claire's confidence.

## TURNING_POINT v02

BAND: 2
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Tomás tells a colleague: "I spend half my brain watching myself during patient interactions." The colleague, a fifteen-year veteran, looks at him for a long time. "I thought that went away with experience. It didn't." The honesty is a relief and a disappointment. The watcher isn't a training wheel that falls off. The watcher is a permanent feature that can be managed but not removed. The managing is the work. The colleague shares one strategy: "I give the watcher one minute after each interaction. Then it's done." The one-minute boundary is the first tool Tomás has been offered.

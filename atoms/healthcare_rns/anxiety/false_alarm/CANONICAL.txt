Nora's pager goes off at 2am. Her body is already moving before her eyes focus on the screen. Heart rate spiked, hands reaching for shoes, adrenaline flooding her system in the time it takes to read: non-urgent lab callback. Non-urgent. Her body prepared for a code. ## MECHANISM_PROOF v01
---
path: story_atoms/healthcare_rns/anchored/anxiety/false_alarm/mechanism_proof/micro/v01.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 2
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Nora tracks her alarm responses over a shift. Forty-seven alarm activations. Three were actual emergencies. Forty-four were false positives: self-correcting equipment, routine adjustments, non-urgent pages. Her body ran a full emergency protocol forty-seven times. Three were warranted. The accuracy rate is 6.4%. If any medical test had a 6.4% accuracy rate, it would be discontinued. Her nervous system's test runs at 6.4% and she can't discontinue it. The three real emergencies keep the alarm funded. The forty-four false positives are the tax.

COST_INTENSITY: 2

Tomás calculates the energy budget. Each false alarm costs approximately ten minutes of elevated cortisol. Forty false alarms per shift: four hundred minutes—nearly seven hours of biochemical emergency state during a twelve-hour shift. The remaining five hours of non-emergency are spent recovering from the previous alarm or waiting for the next one. He has no baseline hours. His shift is either alarm or recovery from alarm. The patient care that happens between alarms is delivered by a body that is never at rest. The care is competent. The body is exhausted.

Aisha discovers the checking has a paradox. Each extra check that confirms stability should reduce the alarm. Instead, each check that confirms stability feeds the alarm: see, you needed to check—what if you hadn't? The checking becomes its own evidence. If she checks and finds stability, the alarm says: good thing you checked. If she checks and finds a change, the alarm says: see, you need to check more. Both outcomes reinforce the checking. The system has no input that reduces its own activity. It's a self-sustaining loop that consumes hours of her shift.

## TURNING_POINT v01
---
path: story_atoms/healthcare_rns/anchored/anxiety/false_alarm/turning_point/micro/v01.txt
BAND: 3
MECHANISM_DEPTH: 3
COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: experimentation
---
Nora starts a practice. When the pager sounds, before she moves, she reads the display. Two seconds. The alarm screams: move now. She reads first. Non-urgent lab callback. She walks instead of running. The walk is excruciating to the alarm—every step at walking pace feels like negligence. She arrives at the phone. Makes the call. The patient outcome is identical to if she'd run. The two-second read and the walk saved her one unnecessary adrenaline spike. One. Multiplied across a shift, the savings compound. The alarm hates the two seconds. The two seconds are her only lever.

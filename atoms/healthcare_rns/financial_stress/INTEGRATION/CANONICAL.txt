

## INTEGRATION v01
---
mode: BODY_LANDED
reframe_type: BODY_FACT
weight: standard
carry_line: "Your shoulders know about the debt."
---
You're standing at the start of another shift and your body is already tired. Not from the work you're about to do but from the work you've already done to cover the hours that make the math work. Your shoulders are tight. Your jaw is clenched. The numbers ran through your head on the drive in. Student loans. Rent. The car needs work. The calculation of how many shifts to break even. This isn't acute stress. This is the baseline. The body lives in the financial alarm all the time. You feel your feet on the floor and underneath that, the weight of debt. This tightness isn't failure. It's accurate information: you're working in an underpaid profession and the cost is distributed through every muscle. That's not dramatic. It's structural.
---

## INTEGRATION v02
---
mode: COST_VISIBLE
reframe_type: COST_UNCOUNTED
weight: standard
carry_line: "The cost is years added to your life."
---
The overtime is necessary. The union negotiated the wage, the institution keeps the wage low, and you pick up the extra shifts to make the salary functional. That's the math every working nurse is doing. The cost is hidden in years. You'll work five extra years to pay off loans before someone else in your cohort takes their first real vacation. You'll pick up shifts when you're tired because the margin is thin. You'll skip the therapy you need because the copay is too much. You'll stay in the job longer than you want because the pension matters. The institution gets your hours. Your life adjusts around the wage. The cost isn't measured in money. It's measured in years.
---

## INTEGRATION v03
---
mode: STILL_HERE
reframe_type: TIME_SHIFT
weight: light
carry_line: "You stayed even when the math didn't work."
note: STILL-HERE mode — final chapter only, one per book
---
You remember when you thought the salary would be enough. Year one, when you were living like a student and the paycheck felt substantial. Year three, when you realized the nursing wage in most systems isn't built for actual living costs. The debt is more visible now. The compromises are more obvious. The other professions your intelligence could have pursued are more visible too. You stayed. Not because the money got better. It didn't. But because you learned how to live on a nurse's salary and you learned which parts of the work matter to you and which parts are just the cost of admission. The financial stress didn't break you. It just became part of what you carry.
---

## INTEGRATION v04
---
mode: QUESTION_OPEN
reframe_type: QUESTION_REFRAME
weight: standard
carry_line: "How long is sustainable without changing the structure."
---
The real question isn't whether you can handle the financial stress. You are handling it. The question is how long the handling is sustainable. How many extra shifts before the health costs exceed the income. How many years of thin margins before something breaks. Not you specifically. The system. The question lives underneath every overtime request: at what point does the personal math require a different choice. You don't know the answer. The question is just there, in the quiet, when you're calculating whether picking up that shift is worth the cost to your body.
---

## INTEGRATION v05
---
mode: SOMEONE_ELSE
reframe_type: OTHER_PERSON
weight: heavy
carry_line: "He's optimistic about the wage still."
---
The new grad thinks the salary will be okay. He hasn't run the numbers against rent yet. He hasn't started the loans. He hasn't experienced the moment when you realize that nursing wages in most systems are built for a co-income household, not for the solo nurse trying to live independently. He will. He'll pick up the extra shifts. He'll adjust the budget. Or he'll leave. His choice will be shaped by whether the financial stress feels like temporary pressure or permanent condition.
---

## INTEGRATION v07
---
mode: COST_VISIBLE
reframe_type: COST_UNCOUNTED
weight: heavy
carry_line: "The profession costs you future security."
---
You're building someone else's retirement through your hours while your own retirement exists in equations with too many variables. The institution determines the wage. The market determines the cost of living. You fill the gap with hours. The cost is that you're trading future freedom for present stability. Most nurses never reach a point where the financial pressure completely releases. That's not failure. That's the structural contract of working in healthcare at this wage level. You know it now.
---

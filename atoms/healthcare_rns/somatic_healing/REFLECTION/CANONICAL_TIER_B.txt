

## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
My body has been through a lot. Years of shift work. Physical exertion. Exposure to illness and trauma. And I've learned to not feel it all that much. To push through. To keep functioning. But lately I'm noticing the cost. The chronic pain. The exhaustion that doesn't respond to rest. The sense that my body is failing me. The mechanism is called late-onset somatic awareness—the realization of physical cost after years of dissociation.
---

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
What I've noticed is that my body holds tension in specific places. My neck and shoulders from leaning over patients. My feet and legs from standing for hours. My hands from repetitive movements. And I can feel this tension. But I've learned to interpret it as normal. As part of the job. Not as something that needs addressing.
---

## REFLECTION v03
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
There's something about being a healthcare worker that makes it hard to listen to my own body. Because I know the language of symptoms. I can name what's wrong. But naming it doesn't seem to lead to healing. So there's this disconnect where I can describe my physical state eloquently and still not address it.
---

## REFLECTION v04
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: inherited
---
I'm learning that my body knows things my mind doesn't. That there's information in the somatic experience that my clinical mind has dismissed. The tightness in my chest isn't just stress. The shakiness in my hands isn't just caffeine. There's something deeper. And it's asking for attention.
---

## REFLECTION v05
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
What strikes me is how much I've been trained to override my body's signals. As a nurse, I learn to work through discomfort. To prioritize the patient's needs over my own physical experience. And this has become such a deep pattern that I don't know how to listen to my body in any other way.
---

## REFLECTION v06
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
There's a specific kind of disconnection that comes from years of clinical work. I can observe my body. I can describe it. But I can't quite feel it from the inside. It's like I'm narrating the experience of having a body rather than actually inhabiting it.
---

## REFLECTION v07
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
I've noticed that when I start to feel my body more fully, there's often pain. Physical pain. But also the pain of realizing how much damage has been done. How many years of pushing too hard. How much my body has sacrificed for the work. The mechanism is called somatic grief.
---

## REFLECTION v08
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: inherited
---
What I'm realizing is that healing my body might require letting go of some of my identity as a resilient healthcare worker. Because some of that resilience has been dissociation. And real healing requires presence.
---

## REFLECTION v09
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
There's something about the practice of slowing down enough to notice my body that feels radical. In a profession built on speed and efficiency, rest and attention feel like betrayals. But my body seems to be demanding it.
---

## REFLECTION v10
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
I'm learning that my body has a wisdom that my clinical mind sometimes dismisses. That the signals it sends are worth listening to. That healing isn't about fixing the problem. It's about creating a different relationship with the body that's held so much.
---

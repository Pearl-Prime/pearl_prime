

## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I know what the chronic pain means. I know what the elevated heart rate means. I know what the disrupted sleep means. I have a clinical frame for all of it. It's all explicable within the medical model. It's all a known response to occupational stress. Which somehow makes it feel more real and also more hopeless. Because I understand exactly what's happening and I'm still living it. The mechanism here is called clinical understanding without clinical resolution—the way knowing what's wrong doesn't actually help you feel better.
I'm trained to read other bodies but not my own.
---

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
My body carries the weight of thirty years in healthcare. The knees. The back. The exhausted adrenals. The dysregulated nervous system. These are not metaphorical. These are physical. And they're a direct consequence of the work. The mechanism is called occupational embodiment—the way your body becomes a record of your work history. And unlike some jobs, you can't just leave the work behind. The work stays in your body. It lives in your tissues. It shapes your posture and your breath and your baseline state of being.
The mechanism is called clinical dissociation—the way medical training creates distance from bodily experience.



## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I've spent a career earning enough to be secure but never enough to feel secure. My salary is reasonable. My benefits are solid. And yet the anxiety about money is constant. I'm running calculations about retirement. I'm wondering if I'm saving enough. I'm assessing the risk of an injury or illness that would prevent me from working. The mechanism here is called financial hypervigilance—this state where you're constantly evaluating whether you're covered, whether you've prepared enough, whether you could survive a disruption. And it's exhausting. Because the answer is always: maybe, but probably not fully.
I've spent my career helping people through medical crises. I know what they cost. And that knowledge is embedded in my financial anxiety.
---

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
What I've noticed is that healthcare workers often have a particular relationship to financial anxiety. We see the cost of medical care firsthand. We know what an unexpected illness can do to someone's finances. We're aware of the precarity of the system we work within. And so the anxiety about money has a specific character—it's not abstract. It's based on real knowledge of real costs. I've seen the medical bankruptcy. I know it could happen to me. And no amount of savings feels like it would protect me from that.
There's a part of me that doesn't believe any amount of money would actually make me feel safe.
---

## REFLECTION v03
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
My financial anxiety shows up most acutely when I think about my future. Will I be able to retire? Will I have enough? Will I have to keep working in this job until my body completely gives out? I'm thirty years into nursing and my body is already telling me it doesn't have another thirty years in it. But I don't know what else to do. The financial anxiety is tied directly to the sense that I'm trapped. That I need this income and I'm not sure how much longer my body can provide it.
I'm saving for retirement but I'm also afraid I won't be able to retire. The mechanism is competing narratives.
---

## REFLECTION v04
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: inherited
---
There's a specific kind of financial anxiety that comes from knowing exactly what my costs are. As a nurse, I've internalized the financial realities of healthcare. I know what hospital care costs. I know what medications cost. I know what would happen if I had to take time off due to illness. And I know that my salary, while reasonable, might not cover those things. The mechanism here is called informed anxiety—anxiety that's based on actual knowledge rather than abstract fear.
Healthcare salaries are reasonable until you realize how much healthcare actually costs.
---

## REFLECTION v05
---

And I'm not sure I've prepared adequately for that possibility.

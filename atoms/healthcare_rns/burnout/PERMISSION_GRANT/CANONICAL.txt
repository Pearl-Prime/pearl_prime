## PERMISSION_GRANT v01
---

---
Nursing burnout arrives wearing a stack of unspoken rules. Take the assignment without complaint. Cover the short staffing. Eat at the desk. Hold the family conversation with composure even when the family is breaking apart in front of you. Carry the patient who died last week to the next code without bringing them with you. Never let the charge see the cost. Never let the new grad see it either. These rules are not policy. They are the floor culture talking to itself in your voice. Set them down.

You are allowed to be affected by the patient who died. Being affected is not a failure of professional distance. It is the body doing what caregiving bodies do when the person they cared for is gone. The chart is closed. The grief is not on the chart. The grief is allowed.

You are allowed to need recovery that the schedule did not build in. Twelve-hour shifts were designed around a different assignment density than the one you walked into today. Your body needing more than the schedule gives is not weakness. It is the bill the schedule never accounted for.

You are allowed to talk about the shift to someone who can hold it. The cultural rule that you do not bring it home, that family will not understand, that nobody outside nursing gets it, leaves you alone with what only your nervous system is carrying. You are allowed to say what happened. You are allowed to say what it cost. You are allowed to need to be heard.

You are allowed to leave the floor for the bathroom. You are allowed to leave the floor for water. You are allowed to take the break the policy says you can take and to take it without checking the call lights one more time. The patients are safer with a nurse who has eaten than with one who has not.

You are allowed to ask the charge for a different assignment when the one you are carrying is not safe. Asking is not weakness. Asking is the practice of patient safety that includes the safety of the nurse providing it.

You are allowed to grieve the version of nursing you went into the profession to do. The gap between what you were trained for and what the floor lets you actually deliver is a real loss, even if the chart does not record it. Grieving the gap is not betraying the patients. It is naming what the system asks the nurses to absorb on the patients' behalf.

You are allowed to be the consciousness watching the burnout, not the burnout itself. The call light hits. The chest tightens. The composure clicks into place. And somewhere behind all of it, awareness keeps watching. That awareness is the seat. That awareness is the part of you that survives the shift, regardless of how the shift goes.

These are not affirmations. They are accurate descriptions of the rights every nursing nervous system comes with. Read them again. Read them slowly. Let the body hear them, one permission at a time.



## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I can diagnosis dehydration on a patient by checking skin turgor and urine color. I can identify sepsis by elevated lactate and subtle changes in mental status. But when I look in the mirror, I become illiterate. I notice the tremor in my hands and think: is this my body failing or is this just what shift work does? I catalog my own symptoms the way I would a patient's differential diagnosis. And then I keep moving because that's what we do. There's a particular blindness that comes with the clinical eye. I can see everything except what's in front of me.
---

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
The problem is that I know exactly what's happening to my body. The cascade. Sustained stress activates the sympathetic nervous system, which floods my bloodstream with cortisol and adrenaline, which suppresses my immune function and dysregulates my sleep. I know this at a cellular level. I've explained this to patients. I've coached others through it. And I keep the same pace. I don't think I'm in denial. It's more that I've learned to read my symptoms as evidence of dedication, as proof that I'm needed. The tachycardia stops being a warning sign and becomes an identity marker.
---

## REFLECTION v03
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
After three decades of nursing, I've developed a sophisticated system for not noticing what I'm experiencing. It's not suppression. It's more like I've categorized my symptoms into a different file system. The fatigue is a professional burden. The emotional flatness is a systemic issue. This lets me function. But it also means I'm never actually treating myself. I can identify the exact moment a patient stops being honest about their symptoms. But when I do it, I don't see it at all.
---

## REFLECTION v04
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: inherited
---
There's a specific kind of anxiety that comes from knowing exactly what my costs are. As a nurse, I've internalized the financial realities of healthcare. I know what hospital care costs. I know what medications cost. I know what would happen if I had to take time off due to illness. And I know that my salary, while reasonable, might not cover those things. The mechanism here is called informed anxiety—anxiety that's based on actual knowledge rather than abstract fear.
---

## REFLECTION v05
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
My heart rate elevation during night shift is just what night shift does to the body. My difficulty eating during trauma codes is the adrenaline response. My insomnia is shift-work sleep disorder. The emotional flatness is compassion fatigue. We talk about it like it's inevitable, like it's the cost of caring. Except it's me. My body. My sleep. My emotional capacity. And the strangest part is how normal this feels. How aligned with my identity as a caregiver.
---

## REFLECTION v06
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
There's a specific mechanism at play when you can name every pathology except the one you're living. I can identify signs of depression in a colleague within minutes. I can see the burnout pattern in my coworkers. But myself? I'm choosing a different interpretation. I'm calling it resilience instead of dissociation, dedication instead of depletion, strength instead of denial. The mechanism here is called reframing, and I'm very good at it.
---

## REFLECTION v07
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
I've learned that I can think my way through my own exhaustion in a way I would never accept from a patient. If a patient came to me and said they had insomnia and tremors and difficulty concentrating, I would immediately investigate. But when it's me? I just accept it as part of the job. The mechanism here is called professional immunity—this sense that the things that would be concerning in a patient are just normal variations for myself.
---

## REFLECTION v08
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: inherited
---
What I'm noticing is that I can't feel my own pain the way I feel other people's pain. When a patient is suffering, I respond with full capacity. My nervous system mobilizes. But when I'm suffering? There's a distance. There's a clinical frame. It's like I have an off-switch for my own pain that I've developed over decades of work. And now I can't seem to turn it back on.
---

## REFLECTION v09
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
There's a moment at the end of most shifts where I feel almost dissociated from my own body. I'm running on stimulation and stress hormones and sheer will to complete the work. I'm no longer actually present in my physical form. This dissociation is probably the thing allowing me to keep going. If I was fully present, I couldn't sustain this. The mechanism is called protective dissociation.
---

## REFLECTION v10
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
I can diagnosis dehydration, sepsis, compensation mechanisms. I'm fluent in the language of pathology. But I become illiterate when I look at myself. The clinical blindness keeps working. I keep moving. I don't remember the last time I felt good about going to work. I remember feeling competent. I remember moments of connection. But good? That's been gone for a long time.

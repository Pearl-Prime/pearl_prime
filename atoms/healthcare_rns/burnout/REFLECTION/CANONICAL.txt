

## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I can diagnosis dehydration on a patient by checking skin turgor and urine color. I can identify sepsis by elevated lactate and subtle changes in mental status. I can name the exact compensation mechanism that's keeping a patient stable before they crash. This is my language. This is how I see the world.
But when I look in the mirror, I become illiterate. I notice the tremor in my hands after a double shift and think: peripheral neuropathy? Poor glycemic control? Something else I should be tracking? I catalog my symptoms the way I would a patient's, creating differential diagnoses for my own collapse. And then I keep moving because that's what we do. We keep moving. There's a particular blindness that comes with the clinical eye. I can see everything except what's in front of me.
I don't remember the last time I felt good about going to work. I remember feeling competent. I remember moments of connection. But good? That's been gone for a long time.
---

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
The problem is that I know exactly what's happening to my body. I know the cascade. Sustained stress activates the sympathetic nervous system, which floods my bloodstream with cortisol and adrenaline, which suppresses my immune function and dysregulates my sleep. I know this. I understand the mechanism at a cellular level. I've explained this to patients. I've coached others through it. And I keep the same pace.
I don't think I'm in denial, exactly. It's more that I've learned to read my own symptoms in a particular way—as evidence of dedication, as proof that I'm needed, as the price of being the one people rely on. The tachycardia stops being a warning sign and becomes an identity marker. It's a special kind of dissociation, available primarily to people who've spent years learning to interpret bodily signals through a lens of medical utility.
The patients don't see the burnout. They see someone who cares and who's there for them. They don't need to know the cost of that care. But I know it. And I'm not sure how much longer I can pay it.
---

## REFLECTION v03
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
After three decades of nursing, I've developed a sophisticated system for not noticing what I'm experiencing. It's not suppression, exactly. It's more like I've categorized my own symptoms into a different file system. When I feel the fatigue, I don't feel it as my fatigue. I feel it as a professional burden, a systemic issue, a collective problem that we're all managing.
This lets me function. But it also means I'm never actually treating myself. I can identify the exact moment a patient stops being honest about their symptoms—that slight hesitation, that reframing. I see it immediately. But when I do the same thing, I don't see it at all. It's like there's a blind spot built into the clinical frame itself.
I think about the quality of care I'm able to provide now compared to the beginning of my career. And I know it's diminished. I'm doing the tasks. But the presence is gone. The time for listening is gone. And that bothers me in a way that nothing else does.

## REFLECTION v04
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: inherited
---
My heart rate elevation during night shift is just what night shift does to the body. My difficulty eating during trauma codes is the adrenaline response—completely normal, totally expected. My insomnia is shift-work sleep disorder, well-documented, a known occupational hazard. The emotional flatness I feel sometimes is compassion fatigue, which is almost a badge of honor in nursing. We talk about it like it's inevitable, like it's the cost of caring.
Except it's me. My body. My sleep. My emotional capacity. I spend my shifts monitoring the vital signs of people I barely know while completely ignoring my own. And the strangest part is how normal this feels. How aligned with my identity as a caregiver. To notice myself is somehow to abandon my role.
The nursing shortage means I can't leave because they need me too much. But the nursing shortage also means I'm being pushed past my capacity. I'm trapped by the very system that's destroying me.
---

## REFLECTION v05
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
There's a specific mechanism at play when you can name every pathology except the one you're living. I can identify signs of depression in a colleague within minutes. I can see the burnout pattern in my coworkers. I can predict which nurses will hit crisis point by watching their coping mechanisms activate and sustain. But myself? I'm a mystery. Or I'm not a mystery at all—I'm just refusing to apply the same diagnostic lens. I'm choosing a different interpretation. I'm calling it resilience instead of dissociation, dedication instead of depletion, strength instead of denial. The mechanism here is called reframing, and I'm very good at it. I've been practicing for years.
I've started to understand my burnout as a kind of moral injury. I'm betraying my own values through the impossible choices I'm making. And that injury lives somewhere deeper than physical exhaustion.
---

## REFLECTION v06
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
I've learned that I can think my way through my own exhaustion in a way I would never accept from a patient. If a patient came to me and said they had insomnia and tremors and difficulty concentrating, I would immediately start investigating. I would look at their schedule, their stress, their sleep environment. I would take it seriously. But when it's me? I just accept it as part of the job.
The mechanism here is called professional immunity—this sense that the things that would be concerning in a patient are just normal variations when it comes to myself. And it's a dangerous mechanism because it lets serious things hide in plain sight. It lets depletion masquerade as professionalism.
I don't remember the last time I felt good about going to work. I remember feeling competent. I remember moments of connection. But good? That's been gone for a long time.

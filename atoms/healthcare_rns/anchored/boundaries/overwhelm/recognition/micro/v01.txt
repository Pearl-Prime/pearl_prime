Maria learns to stop asking herself whether she can finish her shift on time. The answer is no. She always has chart notes still open when she clocks out. Always has one more call light she should have answered. The mechanism is this: she has reframed "on time" from the actual clock to her internal clock. She will stay until she's done. Not until the official end of her shift. Not until her replacement arrives. Until she personally has completed every task. This feels like responsibility. It feels like caring. Her shoulders don't relax anymore. Her chest stays tight through her drive home. She checks her work email on her days off because she worries about what she might have missed. The boundary has been erased so thoroughly that she no longer sees where it was. She tells herself this is how good nurses work. The cost appears later—not as pain, but as absence. She's absent from her partner's stories. Absent from meals. Absent from herself. But the chart notes get finished. The patients get tended. By the mechanism's logic, she is exactly where she should be.

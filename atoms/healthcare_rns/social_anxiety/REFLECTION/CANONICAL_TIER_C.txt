

## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I interact with people all shift long. I'm comfortable with patients. I can connect with families. But somehow when I'm in a social setting outside of work, there's this activation. Like my nervousystem doesn't quite believe that social interaction can be casual or low-stakes. The mechanism is called context-dependent social anxiety—the loss of comfort when the professional frame is removed.
---

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
What I notice is that I'm most anxious in social situations where there's no clear role. At work, I'm the nurse. That role is clear. People know what to expect from me. But in a casual social setting, I'm just a person. And somehow that feels less defined. Less safe. The mechanism is role-dependent anxiety.
---

## REFLECTION v03
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
There's a specific kind of social anxiety that comes from being constantly in a position of authority or expertise. At work, people listen to what I say. They trust my judgment. And then I go into a social situation and I feel invisible. Or I feel like I should be contributing expertise that isn't relevant. The mechanism is called role-determined anxiety—the loss of confidence when the defining role is absent.
---

## REFLECTION v04
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: inherited
---
I've noticed that I'm most anxious in social situations with people who work outside of healthcare. Because I can't easily translate the intensity of my work life into language they understand. So there's this gap. And in that gap, there's anxiety. Like I'm exposing something about my world that others don't share.
---

## REFLECTION v05
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
What strikes me is that my social anxiety often has a specific flavor. It's not about being judged for who I am socially. It's about people discovering how much the work has changed me. How the intensity and exposure has affected my capacity for normal social interaction. The mechanism is called identity-exposure anxiety.
---

## REFLECTION v06
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
There's a part of my social anxiety that's connected to the emotional depletion from work. I come to a social situation already emotionally spent. So the effort to engage socially feels enormous. And I'm anxious about not having enough capacity. The mechanism is emotional resource anxiety.
---

## REFLECTION v07
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
I've noticed that I'm more anxious in social situations that feel frivolous. Like we're discussing trivial things when I've just spent a shift managing life-and-death situations. And I can intellectually understand that social bonding happens around casual conversation. But my nervous system seems skeptical of the value.
---

## REFLECTION v08
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: inherited
---
What I'm realizing is that some of my social anxiety is actually grief about the life outside of work that I've lost. I used to enjoy casual socializing. But the work has changed me enough that I'm not sure I know how to do that anymore. The mechanism is loss-driven anxiety.
---

## REFLECTION v09
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
There's something about the vulnerability required in social connection that feels more threatening than the professional vulnerability I manage at work. At work, I'm vulnerable in specific ways that are defined and contained. In social situations, the vulnerability feels uncontrolled. The mechanism is called uncontrolled vulnerability anxiety.
---

## REFLECTION v10
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
I've learned that my social anxiety decreases when I'm with other healthcare workers. Because there's no translation needed. We speak the same language. We understand the context. But that also means I'm increasingly isolated from people outside of my profession. The mechanism is professional isolation anxiety.
---

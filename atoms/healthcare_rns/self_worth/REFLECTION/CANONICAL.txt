

## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: invisible

I want to name something about the way self-worth in nursing becomes completely entangled with the capacity to help others.

Before nursing, you had a self that was separate from helping. You had worth because you were a person. But nursing teaches you that your worth is specifically located in the helping. Your worth is how much you care. Your worth is how much you sacrifice. Your worth is how much you are needed.

The mechanism is the replacement of intrinsic worth with functional worth. Your worth becomes dependent on your utility. On what you do for others.

What I have noticed is that this starts early. In nursing school, you learn that good nurses care deeply. Good nurses sacrifice. Good nurses put others first. The implication is that not putting others first is not being a good nurse. Which means not putting others first is not being worthy.

So you organize your life around being needed. You become the person who shows up. The person who helps. The person who sacrifices. And your worth becomes completely dependent on the continuation of that role.

The mechanism is the replacement of worth as being with worth as doing.

## REFLECTION v03
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: invisible

I want to name something about the way self-worth becomes lower as the distance from direct care grows.

Bedside nursing is the truest nursing. Administrative nursing is less nursing. Nursing education is less nursing. Other roles are less nursing. The further you get from direct patient care, the less of a "real nurse" you are.

The mechanism is the hierarchy of worth within the profession. The person in direct care is the most worthy. Everyone else is further down.

What I have noticed is that this can trap people in roles that are harming them. A nurse wants to move into a role that would be less physically demanding or emotionally demanding, but she believes she would be less worthy in that role. So she stays.

The mechanism is the way the profession's value hierarchy prevents nurses from protecting themselves.

## REFLECTION v05
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: invisible

I want to name something about the way self-worth becomes dependent on patient outcomes.

A patient recovers and you feel worthy. The patient dies and you feel unworthy. A patient improves and you are a good nurse. A patient declines and you are a bad nurse.

The mechanism is the complete externalization of worth. Your worth is determined by things outside your control. You have made your worth dependent on outcomes you cannot control.

What I have noticed is that this creates vulnerability to outcomes that are not actually your responsibility. A patient dies for reasons beyond your control and you feel like you are a failure.

The mechanism is the way outcome-dependent worth creates false responsibility.

I want to name something about the way self-worth disappears as the role expands.

You started as a nurse. And then you became everything: counselor, advocate, educator, social worker, case manager, chaplain. Your role expanded to fill every need.

The mechanism is the way role expansion is experienced as increasing worth. You are more valuable because you do more. But actually, the self is disappearing into the expanding role.

What I have noticed is that at some point, there is no self apart from the role. You are nothing but the expanded role. And the expanded role requires everything you have and more.

The mechanism is the way role expansion erases the self.

## REFLECTION v09

family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: invisible

I want to name something about the way self-worth becomes unstable as the identity is questioned.

If you are a good nurse, you help people. If you are not helping, are you still a good nurse. If you are less emotionally engaged, are you still worthy. If you are setting boundaries, are you still a good nurse.

The mechanism is the instability of worth constructed entirely on a role. As the role changes or as the way you perform the role changes, the worth becomes unstable.

What I have noticed is that this can happen gradually. The nurse is still caring but with less intensity. Still helping but with more boundary. And she is questioning whether she is still worthy because the performance is changing.

The mechanism is the way role-based worth becomes unstable when the role changes.

I want to name something about the way self-worth can be damaged by being treated as interchangeable.

You are replaceable. If you leave, the unit will hire someone else. If you die, the unit will continue without you. You are not essential. You are interchangeable.

The mechanism is the knowledge of your own replaceability combined with the belief that your worth is in being essential. If you are replaceable, you are not essential. If you are not essential, you are not worthy.

What I have noticed is that this recognition can be quite damaging to self-worth. The person realizes that her worth was illusory. The worth was constructed on being needed but she is not actually needed as an individual.

The mechanism is the way the knowledge of replaceability undermines worth constructed around necessity.

## REFLECTION v19
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: invisible

I want to name something about the way self-worth can be damaged by the awareness of your own limits.

You have a limit. You cannot work beyond this point. You cannot care beyond this point. You have a boundary. And the recognition of this boundary is experienced as the recognition of your own unworthiness.

The mechanism is the way limits are experienced as failures. If you had more capacity, you would not have limits. The limit is evidence of your own inadequacy.

But the mechanism is false. Everyone has limits. The worth is not dependent on limitlessness.

What I have noticed is that nurses often experience their own capacity limits as evidence of unworthiness. And then they push beyond the limits to try to prove their worth.

The mechanism is the way capacity limits are misinterpreted as unworthiness.

I want to name something about the way self-worth becomes unstable as the identity is questioned by aging.

Your body is aging. You can no longer do the physical work as easily. You can no longer work the long shifts without consequence. The identity that was based on physical capacity is threatened.

The mechanism is the way age threatens capacity-based worth.

I want to name something about the way self-worth can begin to recover when the impossibility of the construct is finally acknowledged.

At some point, the person realizes: the worth construction is impossible. I cannot be all things. I cannot help everyone. I cannot sacrifice infinitely. The structure I built is not sustainable.

The mechanism is the recognition of impossible requirement. The recognition that the self-worth structure was built on impossible demands.

What I have noticed is that this recognition can be the beginning of something different. Not a return to the old structure. But a new relationship to worth.

The mechanism is the way impossible requirement recognition begins the reconstruction.

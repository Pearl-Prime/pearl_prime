She was at the kitchen table at 23:14 with the laptop open, signed into the patient portal her hospital let her access from home, scrolling the chart for the patient in room four. The patient was stable. She had signed the chart at 19:21. The handoff at 19:00 had been clean. The night nurse had asked one clarifying question and had not asked any others.

None of that was stopping her from re-reading the chart. The re-reading was not searching for an error. The re-reading was searching for a feeling. The feeling was somewhere in the day's interventions and had not been allowed to surface during the day, because the day had not had a quiet enough hour for surfacing.

The chart did not contain the feeling. The chart contained the meds, the vitals, the assessments, the I&Os, the notes from the resident, the time she had repositioned the patient at 14:30. The chart was a record of the procedural day. The feeling was somewhere underneath the procedural day, and the chart was the only document she had access to from the kitchen, and the body had decided, in the absence of a better tool, that the chart would have to do.

For an hour she scrolled. By 00:14 she closed the laptop. The feeling had not surfaced. The feeling was still down there. The chart had not delivered. The chart could not deliver, because the chart was not what the feeling was about.

She went to bed at 00:30. She woke at 02:47, the way she always woke at 02:47 on the nights after a long shift. The waking was the feeling, finally arriving, in the only opening the body had been able to find for it. The feeling was for the patient in room four — not because the patient was in danger, but because the patient was forty-one and looked like her own brother, and her brother had been on her mind for weeks, and the patient and her brother had collided in some sub-cognitive way during the shift and the body had held the collision until 02:47 when there was finally a quiet hour to feel it.

She lay in the dark and let the feeling be the feeling. She did not run the chart. The chart was closed. The feeling did not need the chart. The feeling needed twenty minutes of darkness and a body that was, finally, available to receive it. By 03:14 the feeling had completed one of its passes. By 04:00 she was asleep again. By 06:00 the alarm went off for the next shift, and she got up, and the body had done a small amount of what it had been needing to do, and the next shift would be a little less heavy than the last one because of it.

He had been working nights for seven years and had developed, by year four, a habit he did not approve of. The habit was that on his days off he would run the unit in his head the way other people would run a movie. Not the work itself. The faces. The conversations. The chart entries he had made and the chart entries he had not made. The handoffs given and the handoffs received.

The habit was loudest on day-two of a four-day stretch off. Day-one he was too tired. Day-three the habit had started to release. Day-four the habit was nearly gone, because the next shift was looming and his body had reoriented to the upcoming work. Day-two was the day the habit was at full volume. He had come to call day-two, inwardly, "review board day."

Review board day had its own rhythms. The morning would start with a low-grade cognitive hum that he could not quite locate. By mid-morning, the hum had organized into specific items — the patient in room nine whose magnesium had been slightly low and whom he was wondering, retroactively, if he had monitored adequately. The family in room two who had asked him a question he had answered hastily because the call light in room seven had been on. The colleague who had given him a look in the med room at 16:30 that he had not yet decoded.

By afternoon, the review board had a full docket. He was making coffee at 14:00 and re-running the conversation with the family in room two. He was at the gym at 15:30 and the magnesium for room nine was coming up between sets. He was at the grocery store at 17:00 and the colleague's look in the med room was being analyzed under the bright produce-aisle light.

What he understood, after years of this, was that the review board was a form of grief he did not have other language for. The day-two unwinding was the body, finally with some bandwidth, running the items the shift had not had time to feel. The items did not lend themselves to feelings he could name. So the body presented them as cognitive items, and the cognitive items occupied the day, and by day-three the items had been turned over enough that the body could release some of them.

He had stopped fighting the habit at year six. He had begun, instead, to schedule day-two more loosely. To not put errands on day-two. To plan a walk for day-two. To call his mother on day-two, because his mother could talk for forty minutes about nothing in particular, and forty minutes of nothing-in-particular was a kind of medicine for review board day.

The review board still convened on day-two. The review board had a slightly shorter docket now, because day-two had more space in it. The habit had not gone away. The habit had been given a venue, and the venue had reduced the cost.

She had instituted, at year nine, a small practice she did not tell anyone about. The practice was that at the end of each shift, before she logged off the workstation, she would close her eyes for thirty seconds and name, inwardly, the three patients she had cared for that day whose names she most wanted the body to know it had seen.

The naming was not a goodbye. It was not a memorial. It was not a religious act, though her mother would have recognized something of it from the village. The naming was a kind of acknowledgment. The body had cared for these patients. The chart had recorded the care. The chart did not have a place for the body's acknowledgment that the patients had been people. The thirty seconds was the body's place.

She had started the practice on a Tuesday in February of her ninth year. She had started it because the rumination at home had gotten so loud that she had to do something at the unit before going home, or the rumination would consume the evening. The thirty seconds was the minimum intervention she could think of that the unit would allow.

The thirty seconds had a small effect, immediately. The drive home was a little quieter. The kitchen was a little less full of unfinished items. The chest still ran, but it ran with a small acknowledgment underneath it, the acknowledgment that the body had been allowed to see who it had been caring for before the workstation closed.

By month four of the practice, the drive home was meaningfully different. The rumination at home had not vanished, but it had a different texture. It had less of the urgent retrospective quality. The patients had been named at the workstation. The naming had done some of the work that the rumination had been trying to do, in advance, on the unit's clock, with the body still in scrubs.

By month eight, she had added a small extension. She would name the three patients, and then she would name, inwardly, one thing the body had done that the chart did not record. The conversation with the daughter in room five. The two minutes she had stayed in the doorway of room seven. The hand on the shoulder of the husband in room one. The body had done these things. The body had not been given a place to register that it had done them. The extension was the place.

The practice had become, by year ten, the thing she could most reliably hand to a new nurse who was struggling with the end-of-shift cognitive load. She did not explain it as a technique. She would say: try closing your eyes for thirty seconds before you log off. Name the three patients you most want your body to know it saw. See what happens.

Most nurses tried it once. Some kept it. The ones who kept it kept it because the body told them to. The body, given a chance to acknowledge its own day, tended to ask for the chance again. The practice was, in this sense, self-sustaining once started.

Not resolution. A different relationship to the noise. The thirty seconds at the workstation was the part of the relationship that belonged to the shift itself, and the rest of the relationship — the drive, the kitchen, the dark hours — had become a little easier to hold because of it.

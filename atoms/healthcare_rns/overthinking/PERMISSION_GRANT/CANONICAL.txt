## PERMISSION_GRANT v01
---

---
The mind learned to track. To verify. To audit. The tracking made you a competent nurse. The tracking off-shift, after midnight, on the third re-reading of the chart in your head, is something else. Set it down for an hour. The shift was already worked. It cannot be worked again.

You are allowed to leave the patient where you left them. The next nurse took the assignment. The next nurse is competent. The next nurse will catch what comes next. You are allowed to trust the handoff, even when the mind keeps offering reasons not to. The handoff is real. The trust does not have to be earned every night anew.

You are allowed to not re-chart the day in your head. The chart you wrote is the chart. The chart in your head is not a more complete version. It is the worry version. The worry version is not more accurate. It is just longer. You are allowed to stop maintaining it.

You are allowed to be uncertain about a small decision. The pulse oximetry at 1830 — you took it or you did not, and the chart says what the chart says. The uncertainty is the body asking you to be perfect. You are not built to be perfect. You are built to be careful, and you were careful, and the rest is what it is.

You are allowed to disagree with the doctor's call and to also let it go. You raised the concern. You documented. The decision was theirs to make. The replay of the conversation at 0230 is not protecting the patient anymore. The patient is already past the moment the conversation was about. You are allowed to let the conversation be over.

You are allowed to not know how it ends. The discharge plan is not yours to track from home. The outcome is not yours to monitor. You were the nurse during the hours you were the nurse. After that, someone else is the nurse. You are allowed to release the patient from your bandwidth, even if the mind has not yet released them.

You are allowed to need a different way of processing than the unit gives you. The shift-end huddle is not enough. The break-room conversation is not enough. The dark joke is not enough. You are allowed to write it down, to tell a therapist, to walk in silence for an hour. The processing has to go somewhere. You are allowed to find your where.

You are allowed to be the consciousness watching the loop, not the loop itself. The replay runs. The audit fires. The chart in your head keeps opening itself. And somewhere underneath, awareness keeps watching. The awareness is older than the training. The training did not build it. The training did not depend on it. It is still yours, intact.

These are not affirmations. They are the standing permissions. Read them slow. The body needs them more than the mind does.

## PERMISSION_GRANT v02
---

---
The training rewards precision. The mind, off-shift, is trying to maintain precision in conditions where precision is not required and not even possible. The mind cannot win that demand. Set the demand down.

You are allowed to forget small details from the shift. The exact wording of the family's question. The exact time the call light came on. The exact PRN order. The chart holds what needs to be held. You do not have to hold all of it twice, in tissue, off the clock.

You are allowed to interrupt your own loop. Stand up. Walk to the kitchen. Pour water. Drink it. The loop does not stop because you understood it. The loop stops because the body moved. You are allowed to use the body when the mind has run too long.

You are allowed to leave a conversation unfinished in your head. The argument with the resident — the words you should have said — does not have to land. Some conversations do not get the closing line. The conversation closed when you walked away. The replay is not closing it differently. You can stop running it.

You are allowed to be wrong sometimes. The math was right but the timing was off. The assessment was correct but you delivered it badly. The chart was thorough but you missed one small note. You are allowed to be wrong in small ways and to not turn each wrong-ness into evidence of incompetence. The competence is established. The small wrongs are a normal cost of doing.

You are allowed to ask a peer "did I handle that right?" and to also live with the answer. The peer might say yes. They might say "I'd have done it differently." Either answer is information. Neither answer is a verdict on the rest of your career. You are allowed to use the information without absorbing it as a defining fact.

You are allowed to stop replaying the bad outcome that was not preventable. Some outcomes were going to happen. The patient came in late. The body had already chosen its trajectory. You did what was possible. The replay that says "if I had only" is the mind looking for control where there was none. You are allowed to set the looking down.

You are allowed to be a person on Sundays. Not a nurse-in-rest. A person. Read a book. Watch a movie. Garden. The mind that audits the shift on Sundays is the mind on overtime. You are allowed to release it from the clock.

These are not affirmations. They are the standing permissions. The body knows what to do with them. The mind catches up later.

## PERMISSION_GRANT v03
---

---
The body that ran the shift is the same body that is lying awake replaying it. The replay is asking the body to work more hours, unpaid, on the same shift. You are allowed to refuse the request. The replay is not the work. The work is done.

You are allowed to take a sleeping aid sometimes. A real one. Not just chamomile. The body is not failing because it cannot sleep through a chart replay. The body is responding to a profession that did not let it stand down. You are allowed to use a tool to get the sleep the body needs.

You are allowed to take a break from the unit and to also come back. Travel nursing. A different unit. A non-clinical role. A leave. Many nurses cycle through these. The cycling is not a betrayal of the profession. It is the way the profession survives a nurse's whole career, instead of burning the nurse out in five years.

You are allowed to be honest with the therapist about what you saw. Not just the headlines. The specifics. The kid on the vent. The family the doctor was rude to. The code you ran alone for three minutes because the team was slow. The specifics matter. The therapist can hold them. You are allowed to put them down there.

You are allowed to grieve a patient who died three weeks ago. The grief does not run on the shift schedule. It surfaces when it surfaces. The Thursday-night grief about Wednesday-of-three-weeks-ago is not late. It is on time, by the body's clock. You are allowed to cry in the shower at 2230 about a patient you did not know two months ago.

You are allowed to not be inspirational. The nurse-as-hero story is a story other people need. It is not always the story you need. You are allowed to be a person who shows up, does the work, and goes home, without performing inspiration for the public or for yourself.

You are allowed to outgrow the version of you that thought the audit was helping. The audit is doing its trained job. The job is partly necessary and partly excess. You are allowed to recognize which is which now, in this body, in this house, on this night.

You are allowed to be the consciousness watching the chart-replay, not the chart-replay itself. The replay runs. The audit fires. And underneath, awareness keeps watching. That awareness was there before the badge and will be there after. It is the part of you the work could never touch.

These are not affirmations. They are the standing rights. Read them once a week. The body will work them in slow.



## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I overthink patient interactions constantly. Did I miss something? Is there a diagnosis I didn't consider? Did I communicate something incorrectly? Did the patient understand what I was trying to explain? The mechanism here is called hypervigilance to clinical error—this constant scanning for what might have gone wrong. And it makes sense in healthcare. The cost of missing something is high. So the overthinking serves a protective function. Except it also means I'm never actually at ease. I'm always reviewing, second-guessing, wondering if I did something wrong. And the obsessive analysis doesn't actually improve my clinical practice. It just consumes my mental energy and increases my baseline anxiety.
As a nurse, overthinking can be protective. It can catch errors. But it's also exhausting.
---

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
What I've noticed is that my overthinking is particularly active after difficult patient interactions or bad outcomes. I'll replay the entire interaction in my mind, analyzing what I could have done differently, what warning signs I missed, what I should have noticed. And the mechanism here is called post-event rumination—the way anxiety loops back over something that already happened, trying to extract lessons or achieve a sense of control over something that's already outside of control. It's not productive. It's just painful.
I'm not sure where the line is between thoughtful clinical judgment and obsessive rumination.

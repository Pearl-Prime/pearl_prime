

## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
I replay clinical encounters. I analyze decisions I made. I construct alternative outcomes to the care I provided. And often these alternative outcomes are worse. So the overthinking becomes a way of reassuring myself: I made the right choice. And yet it's exhausting. The mechanism is called clinical rumination—the repetitive mental review of professional decisions.
---

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
What strikes me is that my overthinking often focuses on the moments where I didn't connect with a patient the way I wanted to. The patient who I was too busy to really listen to. The family I dismissed too quickly. The person who needed more from me than I had available. And these moments loop in my mind. The mechanism is called empathic guilt—rumination focused on unmet relational needs.
---

## REFLECTION v03
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
There's a specific kind of overthinking that comes from having clinical knowledge. I understand the complications that could result from my actions. I understand what could go wrong. So my mind generates a lot of possible negative outcomes. And I can't dismiss them the way someone without medical knowledge might. The mechanism is informed worry—overthinking based on actual knowledge of actual risks.
---

## REFLECTION v04
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: inherited
---
I notice that I'm most likely to overthink when I'm exhausted. When my cognitive resources are depleted, my mind seems to ruminate more. And yet when I'm exhausted is exactly when I should be resting. So the overthinking becomes an obstacle to the recovery I need. The mechanism is called exhaustion-driven rumination.
---

## REFLECTION v05
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
What I've realized is that some of my overthinking might be a way of processing the moral weight of healthcare work. Every shift contains decisions that affect people's lives. So there's a lot to process. And my mind seems to want to process it exhaustively. But exhaustive processing doesn't actually lead anywhere. It just exhausts me more.
---

## REFLECTION v06
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
There's an overthinking pattern that starts with a single clinical decision and spirals into broader questions about my competence, my career choices, my whole professional identity. A single moment becomes evidence of systemic inadequacy. The mechanism is called rumination escalation—starting with a specific thought and spiraling to broader and broader self-doubt.
---

## REFLECTION v07
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
---
I've noticed that I overthink most about the situations where the outcome was ambiguous. Where I don't actually know if what I did was right or wrong. Because the uncertainty leaves room for doubt. And my mind wants to fill that room with analysis. But analysis doesn't resolve ambiguity. It just extends the discomfort.
---

## REFLECTION v08
---
family: F3
voice_mode: mirror
mechanism_emphasis: inherited
---
What strikes me is how my overthinking is sometimes trying to solve a problem that's actually unsolvable. I'll ruminate about how to support a patient whose situation is genuinely terrible and hopeless. And the rumination doesn't change the situation. It just creates additional suffering for me.
---

## REFLECTION v09
---
family: F4
voice_mode: guide
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
---
There's something about the shift-work schedule that seems to feed overthinking. When I can't sleep, my mind becomes very active. And it seems to prefer ruminating about work. So I end up at 3am replaying a patient interaction or analyzing a clinical decision. The mechanism is called insomnia-driven rumination.
---

## REFLECTION v10
---
family: F5
voice_mode: narrator
mechanism_emphasis: protective
---
I've learned that overthinking for me is often a way of trying to maintain control in a situation where I actually have limited control. I ruminate about what I could have done differently. But I can't go back and do it differently. So the rumination is actually an attempt to achieve an impossible outcome. The mechanism is control-seeking rumination.
---

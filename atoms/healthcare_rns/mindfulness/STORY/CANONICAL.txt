## STORY v01
---
---
Marisol has been a nurse for nine years and she still remembers the night she figured out what "here" actually meant. It was a slow Tuesday, four patients, nothing urgent, and she caught herself standing at a bedside with her hand on a patient's wrist for a pulse check, mentally three rooms away rehearsing what she'd tell the doctor about a different chart. The patient, an older man named Walter, said her name twice before she heard him. "You're not here," he said, not unkindly, just noticing. She apologized, finished the check, and it needled her all shift — not because anything went wrong, but because she couldn't say what his room looked like, what his voice sounded like, whether he'd seemed scared. She'd been present in body for four minutes and absent for all of it. That night she started doing something small: before touching a patient, she'd name one thing in the room — the color of the blanket, the sound of the TV, anything — just to plant herself there for a second before her hands started working. It didn't fix her caseload. It didn't make the shift shorter. But six months later she noticed she could describe her patients again, actual faces instead of task lists, and that felt like getting something back she hadn't known she'd lost.

## STORY v02
---
---
Denise almost missed it, the morning she scanned the wrong wristband. Not a wrong med — she caught that before it went anywhere — just a half-second where her hands moved on autopilot toward Room 8 while her mind was still finishing a fight she'd had with her sister that morning. The scanner beeped an error and she stood there, blinking, actually seeing the room for the first time since she'd walked in. She told her charge nurse about it at break, expecting sympathy. Instead her charge nurse said something that stuck: "Your hands don't need your mind to work. That's the problem. You can do this whole job without ever showing up for it." Denise sat with that for weeks. She started using the doorway of every room as a kind of trigger — one breath, hand on the frame, before she crossed in. Not a ritual, not slow, just a beat where she let the last room actually end before the next one began. The scanner never caught another almost-error from her, not because she got more careful about meds, but because she stopped arriving three seconds after her own hands did.

## STORY v03
---
---
Priya used to think being a good nurse meant carrying every patient with her all shift — Room 3's pain score, Room 6's family drama, the code from last week that didn't go the way anyone wanted. She wore it like proof she cared. What she noticed, eventually, was that carrying all of them meant she wasn't fully arriving for any single one of them; she was doing five half-jobs instead of one whole job, five times. The turn came during a normal shift, charting at the desk, when a tech asked her a simple question and she had to ask him to repeat it twice. He wasn't annoyed. He just looked at her funny. That look was the thing that landed, more than any lecture would have. She started testing something almost embarrassingly small: at the start of each patient interaction, she'd silently name where she'd just been — "that was Room 6" — as a way of closing the door behind her before opening the next one. It wasn't dramatic. Nobody else noticed. But she noticed that patients started saying "you seem present today" more, and she realized she'd been quietly leaving the room even when her badge said she was in it.

## STORY v04
---
---
Kimberly had a family member ask her, gently, "Are you okay?" in the middle of an update about her father's labs. She wasn't visibly distracted — she was standing still, making eye contact, saying the right words — but something in her voice must have been thin, because the daughter picked up on the gap between the words and the person saying them. Kimberly went home that night turning it over. She realized she'd given that family her sentences but not her attention; she was already halfway to the med room in her head while her mouth finished the update. She started, the next week, doing one thing differently before family conversations: she'd physically stop walking, plant her feet, for the two or three seconds before she opened her mouth. It sounds like nothing. It changed how the conversations felt to her, though — less like a task she was executing and more like a person she was actually talking to. Families stopped asking if she was okay. Not because she'd fixed her workload, but because she'd stopped trying to be in two places while occupying one.

## STORY v05
---
---
Yolanda counted it once, out of curiosity — how many times a shift her mind left a room before her body did. She stopped counting around forty. It wasn't a crisis, just a pattern: turn to leave, already planning the next task, gone before the door clicked shut. The moment that changed things wasn't dramatic. She was leaving Room 9 mid-thought about Room 14's dressing change, and the patient in 9 said, quietly, "thank you for today," and Yolanda realized she almost hadn't heard it — one more sentence lost to a mind that had already left. She started practicing a strange little habit: touching the doorframe on the way out of every room, just a light two fingers, a signal to herself that this room was actually over now. It wasn't about slowing down. She still moved at the same pace. But she started catching the last sentence in every room instead of losing it to the next one, and something in her felt less scraped-out by the end of shifts, even the bad ones.

## STORY v06
---
---
Rosa's charge nurse used to say something that annoyed her: "Wherever your feet are." Rosa thought it was a poster-quote, empty advice for people who'd never worked a real unit. Then she had a shift where she gave report on a patient and couldn't remember, ten minutes later, whether she'd mentioned the wound vac setting — because she'd given that report while already mentally drawing up the next patient's meds. She had to walk back and check the chart herself to be sure. That gap scared her more than any code had. She started an experiment: for one full week, every time she caught her mind three rooms away, she just noted it — not judged it, just noted "there it goes" — and brought her attention back to her actual hands, her actual patient, her actual task. By day four she noticed the noting itself took less effort; the return was getting quicker, almost reflexive. The charge nurse's line stopped sounding like a poster. It started sounding like the whole job.

## STORY v07
---
---
Beatriz used to pride herself on multitasking — three trains of thought running during report, catching details from four rooms at once. She thought this was skill. Then a preceptor watching her shadow pointed out that she'd repeated a lab value wrong during handoff, not because she didn't know it, but because she'd been listening to the outgoing nurse while simultaneously composing her own opening line for the next section. She'd been performing attention, not paying it. It stung enough that she tried something new: during report, she put her pen down between patients for one breath before picking it back up to write the next name. Just a pause. The information she retained afterward — genuinely retained, not just transcribed — went up enough that she noticed it within a week. She hadn't gotten smarter. She'd stopped asking her mind to be in two rooms during the two minutes when it needed to be in one.

## STORY v08
---
---
Naomi worked a stretch of nights so packed that she started bragging, a little, about how much she could hold at once — six patients, a trainee to supervise, a manager texting about scheduling. Then a patient asked her a direct question, "Do you think I'm going to be okay?", and Naomi answered too fast, on reflex, the way you answer when you weren't really listening to the question underneath the question. The patient's face changed, just slightly, and Naomi knew she'd been caught being somewhere else. She didn't overhaul her whole night. She just started, in that specific kind of moment — a direct question, a vulnerable one — physically stopping what her hands were doing before she answered. Two seconds. It gave her just enough room to actually hear the question instead of pattern-matching to the nearest reply. She started catching those moments more, not fewer of them coming, just fewer of them sliding past her unnoticed.

## STORY v09
---
---
Corinne had a code on her unit that didn't involve her patient, technically — she was two rooms down, uninvolved, safe. But for the rest of that shift she kept finding herself standing at bedsides with her hands doing the motions and her mind still in that doorway. She didn't say anything to anyone; it felt indulgent to be shaken by something that wasn't even hers. At the end of the shift, driving home, she realized she couldn't remember details of the last two patients she'd seen — she'd been present enough to not make an error, absent enough to lose the people. The next shift, she tried something on purpose: whenever she noticed she was still standing in that doorway, she named it, silently — "that already happened" — and named one true thing about the room she was actually in. It took repeating it more times than she expected. But by the end of the week the doorway let go of her faster each time, not because the code stopped mattering, but because she'd given her mind a place to put it down.

## STORY v10
---
---
Tanya's kids used to complain that she was "there but not there" on her days off, and she brushed it off as normal tiredness. Then a coworker mentioned, offhand, that Tanya had walked past her twice in the hallway without seeing her, mid-shift, eyes open, clearly elsewhere. Tanya laughed it off at work and thought about it more than she let on. She started running a small check for herself: at the top of every hour, whatever she was doing, she'd ask silently, "am I actually here right now, or three rooms over?" Most of the time the honest answer was the second one. She didn't try to force herself present all day — that felt impossible with her assignment. She just used the hourly check as a return point, a chance to come back for the next task even if she'd drifted for the last one. Her kids started saying she seemed more herself at home, which surprised her, since nothing about her schedule had changed.

## STORY v11
---
---
Angela used to think presence was something you either had or didn't, a personality trait some nurses were born calm enough to hold and others weren't. She'd watched a colleague named Frank move through a chaotic shift looking completely unbothered and assumed he was just wired differently. She asked him once, half-joking, what his secret was. He said, "I lose it all the time. I just come back faster than I used to." That answer annoyed her because it was so unglamorous. But she tried it: instead of aiming to stay focused all shift, which felt impossible, she just aimed to notice faster when she'd left, and return faster when she did. The first week it felt like no improvement at all — she still drifted constantly. By the third week, the drifting hadn't stopped, but the noticing had gotten almost instant, and the return took a breath instead of a hallway. She stopped judging the drifting. She started timing the return.

## STORY v12
---
---
Marisol's preceptor once told her something during orientation that she didn't understand until years later: "You will never stop your mind from wandering to the next room. Don't try. Just get good at coming back." At the time it sounded like a consolation prize, an admission that the job would always feel scattered. Years in, working a bad stretch of short-staffed shifts, she found herself testing it without meaning to — catching herself gone, and instead of spiraling about how gone she'd been, just turning back to the patient in front of her, again, and again, sometimes eleven times in one conversation. What surprised her was that the eleventh return still worked. It wasn't a skill that wore out. Each time she came back, the patient in front of her got the same fresh attention, no matter how many times she'd left. She stopped feeling like a failure for wandering. She started feeling like someone building a rep count.

## STORY v13
---
---
Yolanda's unit got a new admit at the same time a discharge was trying to leave, and for ten minutes she was pulled so many directions that she felt, physically, like she was standing in four rooms and none of them. A newer nurse asked her, wide-eyed, "how do you not lose your mind doing this?" Yolanda almost gave a canned answer, then actually thought about it and said, "I don't not lose it. I just have a way of finding it again fast." She showed the newer nurse the thing she does — one hand flat on the door frame, one breath, name the room out loud in her head. The newer nurse tried it that same shift, reported back that it felt silly and small. Yolanda told her that was the point; it wasn't supposed to feel like a big fix, because it wasn't fixing the chaos. It was just giving her a two-second place to land in the middle of it.

## STORY v14
---
---
Denise once worked with a nurse who never seemed rattled, and for years she assumed the woman simply had fewer patients or an easier assignment. It wasn't until they worked a brutal shift side by side that Denise saw the truth: same chaos, same call lights, same short staffing — the difference was that the woman kept catching herself and coming back, out loud sometimes, muttering "okay, here" under her breath before every room. It looked strange from outside. It worked. Denise adopted the mutter herself, feeling foolish the first few times, saying "okay, here" to an empty hallway before walking into Room 5. Within a couple of weeks the phrase had worn a groove into her habits, faster than any of her other efforts to "be more mindful" had ever managed, mostly because it cost nothing and took no extra time — she said it while she was already walking.

## STORY v15
---
---
Beatriz's turning point had nothing to do with a patient. It was a doctor's comment, three words long, tossed off during rounds — "this took forever" — that lodged in her and played on a loop for the rest of the shift, showing up every time she stood still for more than a few seconds. She caught herself, hours later, replaying it at a bedside instead of listening to her patient describe new chest tightness, and the mismatch scared her more than the doctor's comment ever could have. She started, after that shift, treating the looping comment like a call light she couldn't answer yet — acknowledging it was there, telling herself she'd deal with it later, and turning back to the room. It took returning to that same room, that same patient, six or seven times in ten minutes before the loop finally quieted. She didn't resolve the comment. She just stopped letting it stand at the bedside instead of her.

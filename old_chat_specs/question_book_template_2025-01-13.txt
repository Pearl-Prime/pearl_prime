NYC Executive Anxiety - Chapter 1: The Sentence
Once I feel certain, then I'll decide.
You've been saying some version of that sentence for years. Maybe not those exact words. Maybe it's "once I have all the data" or "once I'm confident in the analysis" or "once the risk is clear." But the structure is always the same:
Once I feel a certain way, then I'll act.
And here's what that sentence has been doing to you:
It's been running your professional life. Quietly. Automatically. Without you even noticing.
The Moments You've Said It
Think about the last strategic decision you deferred.
You told yourself you needed more information. One more data point. One more stakeholder conversation. One more scenario analysis. Just to be sure.
Or the feedback you softened because you weren't certain it would land right. The insight you held back in the meeting because you hadn't fully pressure-tested it yet. The opportunity you passed on because the upside wasn't guaranteed.
Maybe it was the hire you delayed making. The budget reallocation you kept studying. The restructuring proposal you kept refining. The board presentation you rewrote seventeen times.
Every single time, the sentence was there:
Once I feel certain, then I'll decide.
What You're Not Doing While You Wait
Here's what that sentence costs you:
The decision gets made anyway. Just not by you. The market moves. Competitors act. Your team fills the vacuum with their best guess. The board interprets your silence as agreement.
The insight you held back? Someone else shared a less refined version and got credit for the strategic thinking.
The hire you delayed? They accepted another offer. The project timeline slipped. The team stayed under-resourced for another quarter.
The presentation you kept perfecting? It missed the window when the decision-makers were actually focused on this issue.
You're not making better decisions by waiting for certainty.
You're making fewer decisions. And the ones you're not making are getting made without you.
The Pattern You Haven't Named
You think you're being strategic. Rigorous. Prudent.
And maybe you are. But you're also doing something else:
You're teaching yourself that the version of you who feels uncertain isn't allowed to lead.
That only the certain version—the one who has all the answers, who can defend every angle, who never appears to be guessing—that's the version who gets to act.
And here's the problem with that:
That version doesn't exist.
Not because you're not capable. Not because you're not smart enough or experienced enough or strategic enough.
But because certainty, in the world you actually operate in, is rare.
Most of the decisions you face don't come with certainty. They come with incomplete information, shifting variables, and time pressure. That's not a bug in your role. That's the job.
So while you're waiting to feel certain, you're actually waiting for a condition that isn't coming.
The Question
What if the sentence is the problem?

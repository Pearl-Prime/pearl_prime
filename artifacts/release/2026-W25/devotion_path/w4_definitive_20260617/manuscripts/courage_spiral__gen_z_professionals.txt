earn it — the rest, the belonging, the right to stop bracing — as if grace were a
wage paid only after enough has been suffered, enough proven, enough fixed. So you
keep the door shut, certain you are not yet ready to be helped, and you call the
shut door humility. Sai Maa calls it the misunderstanding at the root of the
exhaustion.

The Mother does not measure you before She loves you. The light does not audit your
record before it falls. In the lineage Sai Maa carries, grace is understood as
ceaseless — already pouring, always, on the deserving and the undeserving alike,
because the very category of deserving is a human invention the divine does not
use. The sun does not decide which fields have earned the morning.

Then why does it seem withheld? Because the channel is closed, not because the
supply is rationed. And the channel is closed precisely by the belief that you must
first become worthy. Worthiness-seeking is a sealed posture. It says: not yet, not
like this, not until I am better. Willingness is the opposite posture. It says:
here, now, as I am, I will let it in.

This is gentler than anything the striving mind proposes, and far harder for it to
accept, because it cannot be achieved — only allowed. You do not have to manufacture
the energy. You do not have to deserve it. You have to stop guarding the one opening
through which it would enter.

So Sai Maa's instruction is almost embarrassingly simple. Be willing. Not perfect.
Not finished. Not healed enough to qualify. Willing. The channel asks nothing of you
but that you cease holding it shut, and the moment you do, you discover what was
always waiting on the other side of a door only you were holding.

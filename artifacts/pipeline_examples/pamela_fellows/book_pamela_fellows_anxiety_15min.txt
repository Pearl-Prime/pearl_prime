WIRED FOR WORRY
Understanding Your Anxiety Response and How to Rewire It

By Pamela Fellows
Somatic Therapy / Polyvagal Theory Tradition
Body Wisdom Press

A NOTE ON THE TEACHINGS

The nervous system does not learn through willpower. It learns through safety.

This is the foundational insight of somatic therapy and polyvagal theory — the understanding that the human body's response to threat is not a choice, not a character flaw, not a failure of discipline or courage. It is a biological response, wired into the oldest structures of the brain, refined over millions of years of evolution, and operating far below the threshold of conscious awareness.

Pamela Fellows teaches from within this tradition that anxiety is not a disorder of the mind. Anxiety is a signal from the body. Specifically, it is a signal from the autonomic nervous system — the system that governs heartbeat, breath, digestion, and the fundamental assessment of safety and danger that happens thousands of times per day without a single conscious thought.

Polyvagal theory, developed by Dr. Stephen Porges, identifies three primary states of the autonomic nervous system: ventral vagal (safe, connected, socially engaged), sympathetic (mobilized, alert, fight-or-flight), and dorsal vagal (collapsed, shut down, immobilized). Anxiety lives in the sympathetic branch. It is the body's mobilization response — the engine revving, the muscles tensing, the heart accelerating — in the absence of an actual physical threat.

The work is not to silence this response. The work is to understand it, to honor its protective intelligence, and to offer the nervous system the specific inputs it needs to return to a state of regulation and safety.

These teachings do not replace the care of a licensed therapist, psychiatrist, or physician. Trauma responses can be complex, and some bodies need professional support to navigate them safely. This book is a companion — a map of the territory, a set of practices for the road.

CHAPTER 1: THE WEIGHT

It starts before the eyes open.

The alarm has not sounded. The room is dark. The body lies in bed, and somewhere in the transition between sleep and waking — in that liminal space where dreams dissolve and reality assembles itself — the chest tightens. The stomach clenches. The jaw, which was loose in sleep, locks. The hands curl into loose fists beneath the pillow.

There is no reason. There is no threat. The room is safe. The door is locked. The morning holds nothing unusual — the same commute, the same desk, the same meetings, the same drive home. And yet the body is already mobilized. The heart beats three, five, eight beats faster than rest. The breath has shifted from the slow, deep rhythm of sleep to the shallow, rapid pattern of readiness. The muscles along the spine — the erector spinae, the trapezius, the muscles that an ancestor would have needed to run from a predator — are awake and tensed.

This is anxiety. Not the idea of anxiety. Not the diagnostic label on a chart. The lived, physical, minute-by-minute experience of a nervous system that has decided, without consulting the rational mind, that something is wrong.

Polyvagal theory names this decision neuroception — the nervous system's below-conscious assessment of safety and danger. Neuroception does not reason. It does not weigh evidence. It does not consider that the mortgage payment went through, that the children are healthy, that the relationship is stable. Neuroception operates on cues — tone of voice, facial expression, ambient sound, the quality of light, the posture of nearby bodies — and it renders a verdict in milliseconds: safe or not safe.

In a body shaped by anxiety, neuroception has become biased toward threat. The threshold has shifted. Cues that a regulated nervous system would read as neutral — a sharp sound, a neutral facial expression, a change in routine — register as dangerous. The sympathetic branch activates. Adrenaline enters the bloodstream. Cortisol rises. The body prepares for a fight that exists nowhere except in the ancient wiring of the brainstem.

Place one hand on the chest and one hand on the belly. Feel the breath. Notice which hand moves more. In an anxious body, the chest hand rises and falls rapidly while the belly hand stays still. The breath has migrated upward, into the emergency zone, away from the deep, slow, diaphragmatic pattern that signals safety to the vagus nerve.

This is the first teaching: anxiety is not a thought problem. It is a body problem. It lives in the muscles, in the breath, in the heart rate, in the gut (which contains more neurons than the spinal cord and maintains its own independent assessment of safety). The racing thoughts — the catastrophic projections, the worst-case scenarios, the mental loops that spin and spin without resolution — are not the cause of anxiety. They are the symptom. They are the mind's attempt to explain the body's alarm. The body sounds the siren first. The mind invents the fire.

This distinction changes everything. Because if anxiety is a body problem, then the body is also where the solution lives. Not in the thoughts. Not in the arguments against catastrophe. Not in the rational refutation of irrational fears. In the body. In the breath. In the hands on the chest and the belly. In the specific, learnable, practicable skill of sending safety signals to a nervous system that has forgotten what safety feels like.

The nervous system can learn this again. It learned danger. It can learn safety. Not through force. Not through willpower. Through repetition. Through practice. Through the patient, consistent delivery of the cues that the vagus nerve needs to shift from sympathetic activation back into ventral vagal regulation.

The body is not broken. The body is doing exactly what it was designed to do in the presence of perceived threat. The work is to change the perception. And that work begins not in the head, but in the hands pressing gently against the chest, feeling the heartbeat, and beginning — one breath at a time — to slow it down.

Notice the hands right now. Are they relaxed or curled. Notice the jaw. Is it clenched or soft. Notice the space between the eyebrows — the corrugator muscles that pull the brow into a frown. Are they contracted. These are not rhetorical observations. These are data points. The body is reporting its autonomic state through the muscles of the face, the hands, the jaw, the shoulders. Every one of these muscles is under the influence of the vagus nerve, and every one of them can be used as an entry point for changing the state.

Unclench the jaw. Not by thinking about it. By physically dropping the lower jaw a fraction of an inch so that the teeth are no longer touching. Feel the masseter muscles — the strongest muscles in the body — release. Now let the tongue drop from the roof of the mouth and rest softly behind the lower teeth. Two changes. Two muscles. The vagus nerve registers both and sends a preliminary signal to the heart: ease. Not full safety. A preliminary report. A first draft.

This is how the rewiring begins. Not with a grand insight. Not with a cathartic breakthrough. With the jaw. With the tongue. With the small, specific, body-level interventions that speak the language the autonomic nervous system actually understands.

CHAPTER 2: THE BLOCKAGE

David teaches seventh-grade history. He is good at it. His students like him. His evaluations are strong. He has taught for fourteen years and he knows the material so thoroughly he could deliver most of his lessons from memory.

And yet. Every morning at 6:47, when the alarm sounds, David's body launches into a state of activation that belongs on a battlefield, not in a suburban bedroom. His heart rate jumps from resting 62 to 94 within seconds of waking. His hands shake as he buttons his shirt. His stomach churns — he cannot eat breakfast, has not eaten breakfast on a school day in six years. On the drive to work, his grip on the steering wheel is so tight that his knuckles ache by the time he parks.

David does not tell anyone about this. He does not tell his wife, who would worry. He does not tell his doctor, who would prescribe medication. He does not tell his friends, who would tell him to relax, to breathe, to stop overthinking, as though the electrical storm in his nervous system could be calmed by a bumper sticker.

David's anxiety has a history. Like all anxiety, it has a body memory — an original event, or series of events, that taught the nervous system its current pattern.

David was eleven. His father, a quiet man who expressed displeasure through silence rather than shouting, came to parent-teacher night. David's teacher — Mrs. Kendrick, he still remembers her name — told David's father that his son was bright but disorganized. That his desk was messy. That he talked too much during group work.

The drive home was silent. David's father said nothing for twenty-two minutes. David sat in the passenger seat and felt his body do something it had never done before: his hands went cold. His vision narrowed. His stomach produced a wave of nausea so sudden and so intense that he pressed his forehead against the window and prayed he would not vomit in the car.

His father said nothing that night. Nothing the next day. The silence lasted three days. And during those three days, David's nervous system encoded a lesson that would shape the next thirty years of his life: evaluation is danger. Being seen is danger. The classroom — any room where performance is observed and judged — is a threat environment.

This is what trauma looks like in the polyvagal framework. Trauma is not necessarily the dramatic, life-threatening event that popular culture imagines. Trauma is any experience that overwhelms the nervous system's capacity to cope — and a child's capacity is small. An eleven-year-old sitting in a silent car beside a disappointed father is a child whose nervous system is being flooded with more cortisol and adrenaline than it can process. The event does not need to be violent. It needs only to be overwhelming. And the silence of a father who does not speak for three days is, to a child's nervous system, overwhelming.

The body stores this lesson in the autonomic nervous system, below the reach of conscious memory. David does not think about Mrs. Kendrick every morning at 6:47. He does not consciously connect his shaking hands to a parent-teacher night in 1993. The connection lives deeper than thought — in the brainstem, in the vagus nerve, in the neuroceptive wiring that scans every classroom, every faculty meeting, every parent conference for the specific constellation of cues that once meant danger: evaluation, silence, the possibility of disappointing an authority figure.

David's window of tolerance — the band of nervous system activation within which a person can function, think clearly, and engage socially — has narrowed. A regulated nervous system has a wide window: it can handle stress, absorb surprise, recover from activation, and return to baseline. David's window is narrow. The distance between his baseline and his activation threshold is so small that ordinary events — an email from the principal, a parent's name on the conference sign-up sheet, a student's bored expression during a lesson — push him into sympathetic overdrive.

He compensates. He over-prepares every lesson. He arrives at school an hour early to organize his desk so that no one can call it messy. He volunteers for extra duties so that no one can call him lazy. He works harder than anyone in his department, driven not by ambition but by the terror — housed in his body, not his mind — that if he stops, if he slows, if he lets a single seam show, the silence will come again.

The compensation works. From the outside, David is a high-performing, dedicated teacher. From the inside, David is a man whose nervous system has been running a marathon every day for fourteen years. The cost accumulates. His blood pressure rises. His sleep deteriorates. He develops a persistent ache between his shoulder blades — the trapezius muscles, locked in a permanent brace, guarding the body against a blow that exists only in the memory of the autonomic nervous system.

David does not know any of this. He knows only that he is tired. That the mornings are hard. That the drive to school fills him with a dread that has no name and no logical explanation. That he loves teaching and simultaneously wishes, with a desperation that shames him, that he never had to walk into a classroom again.

This is the pattern: the nervous system learns a threat response in one context and generalizes it to every similar context for the rest of the body's life. The eleven-year-old boy in the silent car becomes the forty-five-year-old man gripping the steering wheel. The original event is decades old. The body response is happening right now.

Understanding this pattern is the first step. Not because understanding alone heals — it does not — but because understanding changes the relationship to the symptom. David's shaking hands are not weakness. They are the hands of a boy protecting himself in the only way he knew how. The over-preparation is not perfectionism. It is a survival strategy, developed by a nervous system that learned — correctly, at the time — that being seen as disorganized meant being punished with silence.

The strategy worked then. It kept the boy safe. It does not work now. It keeps the man imprisoned. And the work — the slow, patient, body-centered work of somatic therapy — is to honor the strategy, thank the nervous system for its protection, and teach it a new response. Not by arguing with it. Not by shaming it. By offering it, through the body, the experience of safety it has been seeking since 1993.

CHAPTER 3: THE HEART PRACTICE

The practice begins with orientation.

Sit in a chair with both feet flat on the floor. Feel the contact between the soles of the feet and the ground. Press gently — not hard, just enough to activate the nerve endings in the feet that send information to the brain about gravity, stability, position in space. The feet on the floor are the first signal of safety: the body is supported. The body is grounded. The body is not falling.

Now turn the head slowly to the right. Not stretching. Not exercising. Looking. Let the eyes scan the room — the wall, the window, the door, the objects on the shelf. Take time. Let the gaze land on specific things: the color of a book spine, the shape of a lamp, the texture of the curtain. This is orientation — the act of mapping the environment through the visual system and reporting to the brainstem: this space is known. This space is safe.

Turn the head slowly to the left. Same practice. Scan. Land. Notice. The nervous system reads the environment through the eyes and the ears and the skin, and when the environment registers as familiar and unthreatening, the vagus nerve sends a calming signal to the heart. The heart rate slows. Not dramatically. By a beat or two. But in the economy of the autonomic nervous system, a beat or two is a shift in state.

Now bring the attention to the breath. Do not change it. Simply notice it. Feel the inhale. Feel the exhale. Notice the ratio — in most anxious breathing, the inhale is longer than the exhale. The body is taking in more air than it releases, as though preparing for exertion, as though the next moment might require running.

The practice is to reverse this ratio. Slowly. Without force. Inhale for a count of four. Exhale for a count of six. The exhale is the key. The exhale activates the parasympathetic branch of the nervous system — the rest-and-digest system, the ventral vagal pathway, the state of regulation and safety. Every time the exhale extends beyond the inhale, the body receives the message: stand down. The threat is not here. The muscles can release. The heart can slow. The gut can resume its work.

Inhale, four counts. Exhale, six counts. Repeat. Five times. Ten times. Twenty times. The body does not need to believe it is safe. The body needs to receive the physiological inputs that correspond to safety — and an extended exhale is one of the most potent of those inputs. The vagus nerve does not respond to arguments. It responds to breath patterns.

Now add touch. Place one hand on the chest and one hand on the belly. This bilateral hand placement — called a self-hold in somatic therapy — activates the interoceptive system, the body's ability to sense its own internal state. The hands on the torso tell the nervous system: the body is being attended to. The body is not alone. Contact is present.

Press gently. Feel the ribcage expand on the inhale. Feel the belly soften on the exhale. Let the hands ride the wave of breath like buoys on a slow sea. With each cycle, the nervous system recalibrates. The threshold for activation rises — meaning it takes a bigger cue to trigger the alarm. The window of tolerance widens by a millimeter.

A millimeter is enough. A millimeter, practiced daily, becomes a centimeter. A centimeter becomes a life in which the drive to work does not require a death grip on the steering wheel. A life in which an email from the principal does not send the heart rate to 120. A life in which the hands are steady and the stomach is calm and the morning alarm produces not dread but neutrality — which, for a nervous system that has lived in threat for decades, is the same thing as joy.

Now add the most advanced element: the ventral vagal anchor. This is a memory, a sensation, a moment in which the body felt genuinely, completely safe. Not the idea of safety. The felt sense of safety. For David, it is a specific memory: sitting on the back porch with his grandfather, age nine, watching a thunderstorm. The rain hammering the tin roof. The smell of wet grass. His grandfather's hand on his shoulder — warm, heavy, still. No words. No evaluation. No performance. Just two bodies watching the sky.

Call the memory forward. Not as a picture. As a body sensation. Feel the weight of the hand on the shoulder. Feel the wooden chair beneath the legs. Hear the rain on the tin. Let the sensory details fill the body, and notice — notice carefully — what happens in the chest, in the belly, in the hands.

The chest softens. The belly releases. The hands unclench. The shoulders drop — perceptibly, measurably — by half an inch. The breath deepens. The exhale lengthens without effort. The heart slows.

This is the ventral vagal state. This is what regulation feels like. Not excitement. Not happiness, necessarily. Something quieter. Something more fundamental. A settledness. A sense that the body can rest in this moment because this moment is not dangerous.

The body just demonstrated — through direct experience, through sensation, through the involuntary release of muscles and the involuntary slowing of the heart — that it has access to a state other than threat. That state is not gone. It is not broken. It is not permanently lost to the events of 1993 or any other year. It is here. It is accessible. It is separated from the anxious state by one breath, one memory, one pair of hands on the chest.

The practice is repetition. The nervous system does not change its baseline in one session. It changes through accumulated experience — the way a path forms through a forest. The first walk barely bends the grass. The hundredth walk creates a trail. The thousandth walk creates a road. Each time the body enters the ventral vagal state through this practice, the neural pathway strengthens. The road becomes easier to find. The anxious state becomes easier to leave.

David practices in his car before school. Three minutes. Feet flat on the floor. Eyes scanning the parking lot. Exhale longer than the inhale. Hands on the chest. Grandfather's porch. Rain on tin.

By the third week, his resting heart rate on arrival at school drops from 94 to 82. By the sixth week, he eats breakfast. By the tenth week, his wife notices that his shoulders are lower and asks if he has been getting massages. He has not. He has been breathing. He has been placing his hands on his own chest. He has been teaching his nervous system, one exhale at a time, that the classroom is not a battlefield and the morning is not a threat and the silence of 1993 does not have jurisdiction over the body of 2026.

CHAPTER 4: THE OPENING

There is a moment in every nervous system's healing when the body surprises itself.

It does not announce its arrival. It does not come during the practice — not during the counted breaths or the self-hold or the ventral vagal anchoring. It comes in the middle of ordinary life, in a moment so unremarkable that it almost passes unnoticed.

For David, it comes on a Thursday. Third period. Twenty-seven seventh-graders in the room. He is teaching the Constitutional Convention — a lesson he has taught so many times he could deliver it in his sleep. A student in the back row, Marcus, raises his hand and asks a question David has never been asked before.

The question is good. It is genuinely insightful. It catches David off guard. And in the half-second between hearing the question and beginning to formulate an answer, David's nervous system does something it has not done in a classroom in fourteen years.

It stays calm.

The heart does not accelerate. The hands do not shake. The stomach does not clench. The familiar cascade — surprise triggers evaluation triggers threat triggers activation — does not fire. The circuit breaker holds. The signal travels from brainstem to vagus nerve to heart, and the message that arrives is not danger. The message is: this is interesting.

David pauses. He smiles. He says, "That is a genuinely excellent observation. Let me think about that for a second." And he stands in front of twenty-seven students and thinks. In public. Without knowing the answer. Without the armor of over-preparation. Without the shield of expertise. He thinks, openly, vulnerably, the way a person thinks when the body is not screaming at the mind to produce an answer before the silence becomes dangerous.

He finds the answer. It is not perfect. It is interesting and partial and honest, and it leads to a conversation that fills the last twelve minutes of class — a real conversation, unscripted, messy, the kind of teaching moment that textbooks cannot manufacture and over-preparation cannot predict.

The bell rings. The students leave. David stands at his desk and places one hand on his chest. The heartbeat is steady. The breath is full. The trapezius muscles between his shoulder blades — those faithful sentinels that have guarded his back for decades — are soft.

He feels the absence of the old response the way a person feels the absence of a sound after a long noise stops. A hum that has been running so long it became invisible has gone quiet. And in the quiet, David can hear something that the anxiety has been drowning out for thirty years: the actual sound of his own life. Students in the hallway. A bird outside the window. The creak of his chair. The beating of a heart that is, right now, at this moment, resting in ventral vagal regulation — safe, connected, present.

This is the opening. This is what it means for the nervous system to rewire. Not a dramatic transformation. Not the erasure of the past. The eleven-year-old boy is still present. Mrs. Kendrick is still present. The silent car ride is still stored in the body's memory, and it will surface again — in moments of stress, in moments of evaluation, in moments that carry the old cues. But the surface now leads somewhere different. The alarm sounds and the body has a new option. Instead of the old path — activation, mobilization, brace, grip, endure — there is a new path. The new path goes through the feet on the floor and the eyes scanning the room and the exhale that is two counts longer than the inhale and the hands on the chest and the rain on the tin roof of a grandfather's porch.

The new path exists because David walked it. Day after day, breath after breath, three minutes in the car before first period, he walked the path until it became a road. The grass is bent. The trail is clear. The nervous system knows the way.

This is what healing looks like in the polyvagal framework. Not the absence of anxiety. The presence of choice. Not the elimination of the threat response. The expansion of the window of tolerance until the window is wide enough to hold surprise, uncertainty, imperfection, the raised hand of a seventh-grader with a question that has no prepared answer — and to hold all of it without leaving the state of safety.

The body learned this. The body — not the mind, not the will, not the positive affirmation or the cognitive reframe or the motivational poster on the office wall. The body, through the specific, consistent, patient delivery of safety signals to the autonomic nervous system, learned a new response to an old cue.

This is the promise of somatic therapy. This is the teaching that Pamela Fellows returns to again and again: the nervous system is plastic. It can change. It changed once — from safety to threat, in a silent car in 1993. It can change again — from threat to safety, in a classroom in 2026. The mechanism is the same. The nervous system learns through experience. It learned danger through the experience of danger. It learns safety through the experience of safety.

The practice provides the experience. The breath provides the signal. The hands on the chest provide the contact. The ventral vagal anchor provides the memory. And the body — that faithful, intelligent, deeply misunderstood body that has been sounding the alarm for years not out of malice but out of love, out of the fierce determination to keep its owner alive — the body receives the message and, in its own time, in its own way, at its own pace, lays down the weapon it has been carrying since childhood and allows itself, finally, to rest.

Place the feet on the floor. Feel the ground. Scan the room. Extend the exhale. Place the hands on the heart. Find the anchor.

The nervous system is listening. It has always been listening. It does not need to be convinced. It does not need to be argued with. It needs to be shown — through the body, through the breath, through the steady repetition of safety — that this moment, right here, right now, is not the car. Is not the silence. Is not 1993.

This moment is safe. And a safe nervous system is a nervous system that can do anything — think, create, connect, love, teach seventh-grade history to twenty-seven kids, and stand in front of a room without knowing the answer and feel, in the center of the chest, not terror but curiosity.

That is the opening. That is the rewiring. That is what the body has been capable of all along.

David still teaches seventh-grade history. He still arrives early. He still prepares thoroughly — but now the preparation comes from engagement rather than terror. The difference is felt in the body before it is understood by the mind. The grip has loosened. The shoulders have dropped. The trapezius muscles — those old soldiers — have been honorably discharged from a war that ended decades ago but that no one informed them was over.

The boy in the silent car is still part of David. He always will be. The nervous system does not delete its records. But the boy is no longer in command. The adult — the man with the steady heart rate and the full belly and the hands that rest open on the desk instead of gripping the edge — the adult is in command now. And the adult can do something the boy could not do: choose. Choose to stay present instead of bracing. Choose to feel instead of armoring. Choose to stand in front of a room and not know the answer and discover, in that electric, vulnerable, fully alive moment of not-knowing, that the silence is not punishment. The silence is space. And in that space, everything — curiosity, connection, laughter, the raised hand of a twelve-year-old with a question that changes the lesson — everything becomes possible.

WHERE TO GO DEEPER

The practices in this book are an introduction to a vast and rapidly evolving field. Somatic therapy, polyvagal theory, and trauma-informed bodywork offer a rich landscape of tools for nervous system regulation and healing.

For those who wish to continue, begin with a somatic experiencing practitioner — a therapist trained in the body-centered approach to trauma resolution developed by Dr. Peter Levine. Look for clinicians who understand polyvagal theory and who work with the autonomic nervous system directly, not only through cognitive methods.

Study the work of Dr. Stephen Porges, whose polyvagal theory provides the scientific foundation for understanding the autonomic nervous system's role in anxiety, trauma, and social engagement. Explore the work of Deb Dana, whose accessible books translate polyvagal theory into practical, everyday applications.

Practice daily. Three minutes of oriented breathing and self-hold each morning changes the nervous system more profoundly than an hour of analysis each week. Consistency beats intensity. The path through the forest forms through repetition, not force.

The body is not the enemy. The body has been the protector all along. Honor its intelligence. Learn its language. Offer it the one thing it has been seeking since the original wound: the experience of safety in the present moment. The body will do the rest.

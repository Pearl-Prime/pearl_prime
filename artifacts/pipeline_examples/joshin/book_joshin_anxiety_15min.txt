QUIET ENOUGH
A Zen Guide to Calming Anxiety and Finding Presence

Written by Joshin
Still Forest

---

A NOTE ON THE TEACHINGS

Joshin teaches in the tradition of forest Zen. Not the Zen of robes and scrolls. The Zen of moss, of bark, of sitting still long enough to hear rain change direction.

His lineage draws from Soto Zen and the mountain hermit traditions of Japan. Zazen — seated meditation — forms the spine of the practice. But Joshin brings it outdoors. He teaches under canopy. He instructs beside rivers. He holds silence in places where silence already lives.

This is not a book of answers. Joshin does not believe in answers. He believes in looking. He believes the act of looking — steady, patient, undecorated — dissolves most of the problems that answers try to solve.

The Japanese terms in this text arrive as Joshin uses them: naturally, without ceremony. Shoshin means beginner's mind — the willingness to approach a familiar experience as though encountering it for the first time. Ma means the space between things — the pause that holds more than the words surrounding it. Zazen means seated meditation, but Joshin would say it means something closer to "returning home."

If anxiety brought the listener here, good. Anxiety is not the enemy in Joshin's teaching. Anxiety is a signal. It means the nervous system is paying attention. The practice is not to silence that signal. The practice is to sit close enough to hear what it actually says.

Listen as you would in a forest. Without rushing toward the next sound.

CHAPTER 1: THE MIRROR

The breath arrives before the thought. Always. Every single time.

But the anxious mind reverses this order. It insists that the thought came first. It insists the spiral started somewhere in language, somewhere in logic, somewhere a person could trace and fix and prevent.

Joshin sits with this misunderstanding every morning. He sits on a round cushion in a room with one window. The window faces east. The light enters slowly. And the first thing Joshin notices — before his knees settle, before his spine finds its line — is that the breath is already here.

It did not need permission. It did not need a technique. It arrived the way weather arrives. Automatically. Generously. Without narrative.

This is the mirror.

Not a metaphor. Not a symbol Joshin invented to make a point. The mirror is the oldest teaching in his tradition. The mind, in its natural state, reflects everything and holds nothing. Anxiety appears in the mirror. Fear appears. Restlessness appears. They arrive the way clouds arrive in a lake's surface — real, visible, temporarily vivid — and then they move.

The problem is not what appears in the mirror. The problem is when a person mistakes themselves for the image instead of the glass.

A listener hearing this for the first time might feel a small click. Or nothing. Both responses are correct.

Joshin does not push. He presents. He says: the mirror does not flinch when a storm passes over its surface. The mirror does not chase the cloud. The mirror does not say, "That cloud means something is wrong with me."

Anxiety says exactly that. Anxiety says: this feeling means something is broken. This sensation in the chest — this tightness, this heat, this hum — means danger. The body sends a signal and anxiety translates it into catastrophe.

But the body did not say catastrophe. The body said: pay attention. The body said: something shifted. The body said: notice.

The mirror simply reflects.

In Joshin's tradition, shoshin — beginner's mind — is not about ignorance. It is about approaching each moment without the accumulated weight of previous moments. The anxious mind carries every previous moment like stones in a bag. Each new sensation gets measured against old sensations. Each new fear gets layered on top of old fears. The bag grows heavier with every comparison, every memory, every prediction drawn from yesterday's suffering.

Shoshin sets the bag down. Not forever. Not through force. Just for this breath. Just for the length of one honest exhale.

The shoulders drop half an inch. The jaw unclenches by a fraction. The belly, which has been held tight since morning — maybe since last week, maybe since childhood — releases one degree of its grip.

That is the beginning.

Not a cure. Not a transformation. A beginning. The kind of beginning that happens silently, in the body, without announcement or applause.

Joshin would say: the mirror was never broken. It was only covered. Dust settles. Leaves fall on still water. The surface gets obscured. But underneath the debris, the reflective quality remains. It has always remained. It did not go anywhere. It does not require construction. It requires clearing.

The practice is not to build something new. The practice is to clear what has collected.

Breath is the simplest clearing tool. Not special breath. Not counted breath. Not breath shaped by a timer or a technique or an app. Just the breath that is already happening. The breath that arrived before the thought.

Notice it. That is the entire first instruction.

Not improve it. Not deepen it. Not measure it against an ideal breath remembered from a video or a class.

Notice it.

The chest rises. The chest falls. The air touches the nostrils — cool on the way in, warm on the way out. The belly expands. The ribs open like hands uncupping. The exhalation takes slightly longer than the inhalation. Not because of technique. Because the body, when observed, naturally lengthens its release.

This is happening right now. This has never stopped happening. The anxious mind got so loud that the breath became inaudible. But it never stopped.

Joshin says: you are the mirror, not the image. The image changes. The mirror remains.

Sit with that. Not as an idea to be understood. As a sensation to be felt. The next time a wave of anxiety passes through — a tightening in the chest, a lurch in the stomach, a rush of heat in the face — notice it as an image in the mirror. Not as proof of something. Not as a symptom. Not as a sentence. An image. Temporary. Already moving.

CHAPTER 2: THE REFLECTION

Kenji is thirty-four. He designs websites for small businesses in Osaka. He is good at his work. His clients like him. His reviews are strong. His portfolio is clean and modern and reflects the careful attention he brings to every project. None of this information stops the feeling that arrives every Sunday evening at approximately seven o'clock.

It starts in his stomach. A tightening. Not pain — tightening. Like someone slowly turning a dial. His breathing shifts. It moves from his belly to his upper chest. The breaths grow shorter, faster, shallower. His thoughts begin to accelerate. Not with new information. With old information dressed in new urgency.

Monday. The client revision. The email he has not answered. The meeting he does not want to attend. The invoice he forgot to send. The possibility — vivid, specific, and entirely fictional — that he has made a catastrophic error in the website code he delivered on Friday.

He has not made an error. He checked the code three times. He ran the browser tests. He previewed every page on three devices. But the feeling does not consult the evidence. The feeling arrives on its own schedule with its own certainty, indifferent to facts.

Kenji sits at his kitchen table. His tea grows cold. He picks up his phone, sets it down, picks it up again. He opens the email app. No new messages. He closes it. He opens it again. Still nothing. The checking provides thirty seconds of relief. Then the dial tightens again. The relief evaporates faster each time, like a drug that demands a higher dose.

This has been Kenji's Sunday for eleven years.

He does not call it anxiety. He calls it "the Sunday thing." He manages it the way a person manages a limp — by compensating, by planning around it, by never mentioning it in company. He endures it. He waits for Monday morning to arrive so the abstract dread can be replaced by concrete tasks. Concrete tasks he can handle. The problems he can name and solve are tolerable. It is the space between — the ma, the gap, the open air of unstructured time — that floods with noise.

Kenji finds Joshin's teaching through a friend. Not a spiritual friend. A coworker who mentioned a podcast about forest meditation while they waited for a build to compile. Kenji listens to one episode while walking to the train station. He almost turns it off. Joshin speaks too slowly. The pauses feel uncomfortable. The silence between sentences feels like dead air.

But one sentence catches him. Joshin says: "The anxious mind fills every gap with rehearsal. It rehearses conversations that will never happen. It rehearses failures that have no basis. The rehearsal feels productive because it feels like preparation. But preparation requires new information. Rehearsal just replays the old."

Kenji's thumb hovers over the pause button. He does not press it. He walks past his station. He keeps listening.

He starts sitting. Not zazen. Not formally. He sits on his kitchen floor on Sunday evenings at seven o'clock — right when the feeling arrives — and he does nothing about it.

This is harder than it sounds. Enormously harder. The instinct is to fix. The instinct is to check the email, to review the code, to open the project files and verify that the links work and the images load. The instinct is to call someone and confirm that everything is fine. The instinct is to fill the ma with action. Any action. Even useless action feels better than sitting in the gap.

Kenji sits in the gap.

The first Sunday, he lasts four minutes. His heart races. His palms sweat. His right leg bounces without his permission. He stands up and checks his email. No new messages.

The second Sunday, he lasts six minutes. The tightening in his stomach intensifies at minute three — a sharp clenching, almost nauseous — and begins to fade at minute five. Not disappear. Fade. Like a wave that reaches its peak and begins to recede. He notices this. He has never stayed still long enough to notice the fading. In eleven years of the Sunday thing, he has always intervened before the wave could crest and fall on its own.

The third Sunday, something shifts. He is sitting on the floor. The window is dark. The tightening arrives on schedule. The thoughts begin their rehearsal — the client, the code, the meeting, the invoice. And Kenji watches them. Not from a place of calm. Not from a place of wisdom or detachment. From a place of exhaustion. He is tired of running the same program. Tired the way a person gets tired of a song they have heard ten thousand times.

He notices his shoulders. They are up by his ears. He did not put them there. He does not remember lifting them. But here they are — braced, held, defending against a threat that exists only in rehearsal.

He breathes. Not a technique. Just the next breath. The shoulders lower. Not because he forces them. Because he noticed. Noticing is the release mechanism that force can never replicate.

His jaw is clenched. He notices. The jaw softens. His teeth part by a millimeter.

His hands are fists on his knees. He notices. The fingers open slowly, as if they were holding something and discovered it was empty.

The stomach is still tight. He notices. It stays tight. Not everything releases on the first noticing. Some tension has been held for years. It does not let go in a single breath. That is acceptable. Joshin would say: the mirror does not demand that every cloud move at the same speed. Some clouds are wispy and dissolve in seconds. Some are heavy and dark and take hours to pass. Both are weather. Both are temporary.

By Sunday number six, Kenji begins to understand something that no amount of reading or listening could have taught him. The feeling and the story are two separate events. The stomach tightening is one event. The narrative about Monday — the client, the code, the imagined catastrophe — is a second event. They arrive together so consistently that he assumed they were the same thing. He assumed the tightening caused the narrative. Or the narrative caused the tightening. That they were one thing with two faces.

They are not.

The feeling is physical. Brief. Finite. It has a beginning, a peak, and a decline. It lasts, on its own, between ninety seconds and three minutes. He times it, not with a watch, but with his breathing. Ten to twenty breaths from onset to fade.

The story is mental. Elaborate. Self-sustaining. It takes the sensation and builds a tower. Each floor of the tower is a hypothetical. Each hypothetical generates more sensation. More sensation generates more hypothetical. The loop feeds itself. The tower grows. What began as a stomach tightening becomes a four-story catastrophe complete with consequences, regrets, and a vivid imagined scene of professional humiliation.

But the stomach tightening, left alone, lasted ten breaths.

Kenji learns to catch the moment of separation. The sensation arrives. He feels it in his belly. He breathes. He names it — "tightening." He does not name it "disaster" or "failure" or "proof that something is wrong." He names it by its physical quality. Just the quality. Tightening. Heat. Pressure. Hum.

Then he waits. The story approaches. He can feel it gathering, the way a person can feel a sneeze building. The client. The code. The meeting.

He lets the story float past. Not always. Not easily. Sometimes it grabs him and he rides it for twenty minutes before he remembers to look down and notice that his fists are clenched again. But each time he catches it, the gap between sensation and story grows a little wider. A little more spacious. A little more breathable.

This is shoshin. This is beginner's mind applied to one's own inner weather. Each Sunday, Kenji approaches the feeling as if meeting it for the first time. Without the accumulated interpretation of eleven years. Without the biography of anxiety he has written about himself. Just: a tightening. Here. Now. One breath.

The Sunday thing does not vanish. Kenji does not want it to vanish. He wants to sit with it honestly. And honesty — Joshin teaches this — is the only thing the mirror requires.

CHAPTER 3: THE SITTING

This chapter is a practice. The listener does it now. Not later. Not when conditions improve. Not when the room is quieter or the mind is calmer or the day is less demanding. Now. The conditions are already adequate. Zazen does not require perfect conditions. It requires a body and a willingness to remain still.

Find a place to sit. A chair works. The floor works. A park bench works. A train seat works. The surface does not matter. The posture does: upright without rigid. A straight spine. Not military straight — alive straight. The kind of straight that a plant grows toward light. Effortful at the root, easy at the top.

Feet flat on the ground if in a chair. Crossed legs if on the floor. Hands resting on the thighs, palms down. This is not decoration. Palms down settles the nervous system. The contact between palm and thigh creates a circuit — a loop of warmth and weight that signals stability. Palms up opens the system, makes it receptive, invites input. For this practice, settling comes first.

Close the eyes. Or lower them to a soft spot on the ground about three feet ahead. Joshin prefers lowered eyes. Closing shuts the world out entirely. Lowering lets the world stay while attention moves inward. The peripheral vision blurs. The floor becomes a soft, undemanding surface. The eyes rest without closing.

Now. The breath.

Do not change it. Do not improve it. Do not lengthen it or slow it or deepen it. Let it be shallow if it is shallow. Let it be ragged if it is ragged. Let it be fast if it is fast. The breath arriving right now is the honest breath. It reflects the current state of the body — the tension, the activation, the residue of the day. Changing it immediately turns this into a performance. Joshin does not teach performance. Joshin teaches observation.

Notice where the breath moves. It may be high in the chest — that is common with anxiety. The chest rises, the shoulders lift slightly, and the belly stays locked, held tight like a fist. Notice this without correcting it. Let the tight belly be tight. Let the shallow breath be shallow. Observation is enough.

Stay here for ten breaths.

On breath four or five, the mind will produce a thought. A plan. A memory. A worry. A to-do list item. An image from last night's dream. This is guaranteed. It is not a failure. It is not a sign that the practice is not working. It is the mind doing what minds do — generating content. Constantly. Reliably. Without being asked.

Joshin's instruction: notice the thought the way a person sitting on a riverbank notices a leaf floating past. See it. Name its category silently — "planning," "remembering," "worrying," "fantasizing." Then return to the breath. Not forcefully. Not with frustration. Gently. The way a person turns their head back to a conversation after a brief distraction. Oh. Right. The breath. Still here.

Continue.

Now. The body scan.

Start at the crown of the head. Notice any sensation there. Pressure. Warmth. Tingling. Nothing. "Nothing" is a valid report. The absence of sensation is information. Move the attention slowly downward.

The forehead. Is it smooth or furrowed. The muscles around the eyes. Gripping or soft. These tiny muscles work constantly during the day — squinting at screens, furrowing in concentration, tightening during stress. They deserve a moment of attention.

The jaw. This is where most people store the first layer of tension. The jaw clenches to hold back words, to endure discomfort, to brace against anticipated pain. Millions of people walk through their days with the jaw tight enough to crack a walnut and never notice.

Notice the jaw. Breathe into the jaw. This is not mystical — it is anatomical. Directing attention to a tense area increases blood flow to the surrounding muscles and invites release. The jaw may soften. The teeth may part. The lips may separate slightly. Or the jaw may hold. Both are fine. Both are honest reports.

The throat. The muscles of the throat constrict under stress, producing the feeling of something stuck, something unsaid. Notice the throat. Let the swallow happen if it wants to. Let the throat be as it is.

The shoulders. Joshin calls the shoulders "the shelves of obligation." Everything a person carries lands here first. The weight of responsibility. The brace against uncertainty. The physical posture of bearing up. Notice the weight. Notice if the shoulders are lifted toward the ears. If they are, do not push them down. Simply notice the lift. Awareness often accomplishes what force cannot. The shoulders may drop of their own accord, as if they were waiting for permission.

The chest. This is the seat of anxiety for most bodies. A tightness. A pressure. A feeling of constriction, as if the ribs have drawn inward. Sometimes a fluttering — the heart beating harder than usual, announcing itself against the breastbone. Place the attention there. Not to fix. To be present with. The chest is not the enemy. The chest is the messenger. It sends signals about the current state of the nervous system. Listen without editing the message.

Breathe.

The belly. The second center of anxiety. The gut clenches, the appetite vanishes or surges, the stomach churns or tightens or drops. Place the attention there. Let the belly be soft. Not flat, not held, not armored, not sucked in. Soft. This is the most counterintuitive instruction for anxious people. Softening the belly feels like removing armor in a battle. It feels like dropping the guard. But there is no battle. There is only a body sitting in a room, breathing. The belly does not need to protect the organs from the thoughts. The thoughts are not weapons.

The hands. Notice if they are tense. Notice if the fingers are curled. Spread the fingers slightly. Let them rest on the thighs like sleeping animals. Light. Warm. Still.

The legs. The thighs. The knees. The calves. The ankles. Feel the weight of the legs against the chair or the floor. Gravity holds them. They do not need to participate. They can rest.

The feet. Feel the contact with the ground. The soles pressing down. The toes. The arches. This is the anchor. Gravity works. The earth holds. The feet press down and the ground presses back with equal force. This is not philosophy. This is physics. And anxious minds benefit enormously from returning to physics — to the felt, the measurable, the reliable, the actual.

Now. Joshin's core instruction. The mirror practice.

Sit in this posture for five minutes. Set a timer if needed. A gentle tone. Not an alarm. During these five minutes, let everything appear. Thoughts. Feelings. Sensations. Sounds. Smells. The neighbor's dog. The traffic outside. Let them arise, be seen, be reflected, and dissolve. Do not follow any of them. Do not suppress any of them. Do not rank them by importance or urgency.

The practice is to be the mirror, not the image.

A thought about tomorrow appears. The mirror reflects it. It moves on.

A wave of tightness rolls through the chest. The mirror reflects it. It peaks. It fades. It moves on.

A sound from outside breaks the silence. The mirror reflects it. The silence returns.

A feeling of sadness arrives from nowhere — no story attached, no reason offered. The mirror reflects it. The sadness sits for a moment. Then it shifts.

Nothing needs to be done about any of this. Nothing needs to be solved, processed, journaled, analyzed, or understood. The mirror simply reflects. That is its nature. That is the listener's nature too, underneath the layers of reaction and interpretation and narrative.

This is zazen. Not the formal, hour-long, monastery version with the incense and the bell and the teacher walking behind with a stick. The living, breathing, five-minutes-on-a-kitchen-floor version. Joshin insists these are the same practice. The duration is irrelevant. The setting is irrelevant. The quality of attention is everything.

Five minutes. Starting now.

[Silence.]

When the timer sounds, do not rush to open the eyes. Do not grab the phone. Do not stand up immediately. First: notice the state of the body. Compare it — briefly, without analysis, without grading — to the state of the body five minutes ago. The jaw. The shoulders. The chest. The belly. The hands.

Something shifted. It may be subtle. A degree of softness that was not there before. A breath that reaches slightly deeper into the belly than it could at the start. A thought pattern that slowed from a sprint to a walk. A warmth in the hands that was absent before.

This is enough. This is the entire practice. Repeated daily, it rewires the relationship between sensation and story. The body still sends signals. The mind still generates narrative. But the gap between them grows. And in that gap — that ma, that pause, that open air — a different kind of quiet lives.

Not empty quiet. Not forced quiet. Not the quiet of suppression or avoidance. The quiet of a forest at dawn, full of life, full of movement, full of birdsong and insect hum and wind in the canopy — but steady. Grounded. Still enough to hear clearly.

CHAPTER 4: THE RETURN

The listener returns to the day now. The practice ends. The mirror stays.

Joshin does not ask anyone to meditate for hours. He does not ask anyone to retreat from the world, to become calm, to transcend anxiety, to achieve a state of permanent peace. He finds these goals misguided. They treat the human condition as a problem to be solved rather than a reality to be inhabited.

Anxiety is not a disease to be cured. It is a signal to be understood. The nervous system speaks through sensation. It says: something requires attention. Something shifted in the environment. Something needs a response. The practice is to hear the signal clearly — without the distortion of old stories, without the amplification of rehearsal, without the automatic leap from sensation to catastrophe.

The signal, heard clearly, is almost always manageable. The tightening in the stomach says: transition is happening. The racing heart says: energy is available. The shallow breath says: the body is on alert. These are not emergencies. These are reports. Accurate, useful, temporary reports.

The catastrophe comes from the story layered on top of the report. The story says: this tightening means failure. This racing heart means danger. This shallow breath means something is fundamentally wrong. The story is persuasive because it arrives with physical evidence — the body is activated, and activation feels like proof.

But activation is not proof. Activation is activation. It means the system is responding. The mirror reflects whatever is here. The story says the reflection is a verdict. The practice says the reflection is a weather report.

This is a permission. Not a technique. Not a strategy. Not a tool to add to the anxiety-management toolkit.

Permission to feel the feeling without building the tower. Permission to notice the body without immediately trying to change it. Permission to sit in the gap between stimulus and response — that sacred ma — and discover that the gap is survivable. More than survivable. Spacious. Interesting. Alive.

The mirror does not require special conditions. A traffic jam is a mirror. A difficult conversation is a mirror. A sleepless night is a mirror. A crowded train is a mirror. A job interview is a mirror. Every moment that triggers the anxious response is an invitation to practice: am I the image, or am I the glass.

The image panics. The glass observes.

The image rehearses. The glass reflects.

The image says: "This proves I am broken." The glass says nothing. It simply shows what is here. Completely. Impartially. Without judgment.

Kenji still sits on Sunday evenings. The tightening still arrives. It arrives at approximately seven o'clock, faithful as a commuter train. But his relationship to the tightening has changed. It is weather now. Internal weather. It rolls in, it peaks, it passes through, it dissipates. He does not need to fix the weather. He needs to let it move.

Some Sundays are harder than others. Some Sundays the rehearsal grabs him before he catches it and he rides the loop for an hour, arriving at the bottom with clenched fists and cold tea. He does not consider these failures. Joshin would not consider these failures. The mirror does not judge its own reflections. A cloudy mirror is still a mirror. A mirror covered in leaves is still a mirror. The clearing happens again. And again. And again. That is the practice.

There is a phrase in Joshin's tradition: sit like a mountain. Not stiff like a mountain. Not cold like a mountain. Present like a mountain. The mountain does not resist the weather. The mountain does not wish for different weather. The mountain does not compare today's weather to yesterday's weather and conclude that something is wrong. The mountain sits in whatever comes and remains what it is.

This is the teaching in its simplest form. The breath arrives. The body sits. The mind reflects. Everything changes and the mirror holds steady.

The anxious mind fears that stillness means stagnation. It fears that stopping the rehearsal means being unprepared. It fears that softening the belly means becoming vulnerable. That sitting in the gap means missing something important.

But stillness is not stagnation. Stillness is the ground from which clear action arises. The person who sits still for five minutes and then acts does so from a different foundation than the person who spirals for five minutes and then reacts. The actions may look identical from the outside. The internal experience is entirely different. One is chosen. The other is compelled.

From stillness: clarity. From clarity: choice. From choice: action that serves rather than action that soothes. Action that addresses the actual situation rather than the rehearsed catastrophe.

This is not a transformation. This is a return. The mirror was always here. The stillness was always underneath the noise. The breath was always arriving before the thought. The gap was always available.

Nothing new has been added. Something that was in the way has been set down.

The listener carries this into the rest of the day. Not as a philosophy. Not as a belief system. Not as something to explain at dinner. As a physical memory. The body remembers what it felt like to soften the jaw, to lower the shoulders, to let the belly be unarmored. The body remembers because the body did it — not because the mind understood it.

One last instruction from Joshin. Not a technique. An invitation.

Tomorrow, when the feeling comes — the tightening, the acceleration, the rehearsal — pause. One breath. Notice the chest. Notice the belly. Name the sensation by its physical quality. Just the quality. Not the story.

Then act. From the pause, not from the spiral. From the mirror, not from the image.

That is enough. That is the whole practice.

The forest does not hurry. Everything reaches completion in its own season. The mirror does not strain. Everything appears in its own time. The breath does not rush. It arrives, it stays, it leaves, it returns.

So does the listener. Again and again and again.

WHERE TO GO DEEPER

Joshin recommends beginning with five minutes of seated practice each day. Same time. Same place. Same cushion or chair. Consistency matters more than duration. A person who sits for five minutes every morning for a year builds a deeper practice than a person who sits for an hour once and never returns.

For those drawn to zazen, Joshin suggests seeking a local Zen center — not for doctrine, but for community. Sitting with others changes the quality of attention in ways that are difficult to describe and impossible to replicate alone. The silence becomes shared. The stillness becomes collective. The practice gains weight.

For reading, Joshin points toward the work of Shunryu Suzuki, particularly the teachings on shoshin. The phrase "in the beginner's mind there are many possibilities, in the expert's mind there are few" captures the essence of what this practice offers the anxious mind: the freedom to meet each moment without the weight of previous moments.

For body-based practice, forest walking — slow, undirected, without destination or device — is Joshin's most consistent prescription. The Japanese tradition of shinrin-yoku, forest bathing, overlaps deeply with his teaching. The forest does not ask anything of the visitor. It simply presents itself. And in that generous presentation, the nervous system remembers how to settle.

The Still Forest community offers guided practices, seasonal retreats, and written teachings at varying depths. The practice asks nothing of the listener except willingness. The rest unfolds from there.

Sit once. Begin.

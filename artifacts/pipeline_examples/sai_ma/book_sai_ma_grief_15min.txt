STILL HERE WITHOUT YOU
Finding Your Way Through Grief and Heartbreak

By Sai Maa
Vedic Hindu / Divine Healing / Compassion Tradition

A NOTE ON THE TEACHINGS

The Vedic tradition holds that the universe is vibration. Nada Brahma — the universe is sound. Every atom hums. Every cell sings. Every breath carries a frequency that connects the individual soul, the atman, to the vast intelligence of creation. When the body grieves, that vibration changes. The hum dims. The song falters. The breath catches and holds and forgets its rhythm.

Sai Maa teaches from within this tradition that prana — the life force, the vital current that animates all living things — is not a state to be achieved but a dynamic flow to be maintained. Grief interrupts the flow. Loss creates a constriction in the pranic body that radiates outward through the physical, emotional, mental, and spiritual layers of being. The Vedic sciences — Ayurveda, yoga, mantra, pranayama — offer precise tools for restoring this flow.

This book is rooted in svadhyaya, the sacred practice of self-study. It asks the griever not to look away from pain but to look into it — with compassion, with devotion, with the understanding that every human heart carries within it the same divine intelligence that holds the stars in their orbits. Grief does not diminish that intelligence. Grief reveals it.

These teachings are offered as a companion on the path. They do not replace the care of a physician, a therapist, or a trusted community. They walk beside.

CHAPTER 1: THE WEIGHT

The body remembers before the mind does.

On the first morning after a great loss, the eyes open to a world that looks identical to yesterday. The ceiling is the same ceiling. The light through the curtains falls at the same angle. The alarm sounds at the same pitch. Everything is precisely as it was. And everything has changed.

The body registers this change as heaviness. A gravitational pull that begins in the center of the chest and radiates outward — into the shoulders, which curl forward as though shielding the heart. Into the arms, which hang limp and purposeless, unsure what to hold now that the one they held is gone. Into the legs, which carry the weight of a body that has become unfamiliar to itself, heavier than physics can explain, denser than muscle and bone.

In the Vedic understanding, this heaviness has a name: tamas. One of the three gunas, the fundamental qualities of nature, tamas is the force of inertia, darkness, and contraction. It is not evil. It is not punishment. It is the natural response of a system that has been overwhelmed. When prana — the vital life force — encounters a shock too large to process, the system contracts. It conserves. It goes dark, the way a city dims its lights during a storm to protect the grid.

The grieving body is a body in conservation mode. The digestion slows — food becomes tasteless, appetite vanishes, or the opposite occurs, and the body craves dense, heavy food as though trying to replace from the outside the substance it has lost from within. Sleep fragments. The body falls unconscious from exhaustion but wakes at odd hours, the heart racing, the mind cycling through memories and regrets and the terrible arithmetic of all the conversations that will now never happen.

Place one hand on the belly. Not pressing. Resting. Feel the breath move — or fail to move — beneath the palm. In a grieving body, the breath often stops at the ribs. The diaphragm, that great dome of muscle that separates the chest from the abdomen, locks. It holds. It refuses to descend fully, as though a full breath would open a door that the body has decided must stay closed.

This is the first recognition: grief is not merely an emotion. It is a full-body event. It reshapes the posture. It alters the chemistry of the blood. It changes the rhythm of the heart, the pattern of the breath, the quality of the skin. Grief lives in the tissues. It lodges in the fascia. It curls the spine and tightens the throat and turns the hands into fists that grip the bedsheets at three in the morning while the mind replays, for the hundredth time, the last conversation, the last glance, the last ordinary moment that turned out to be final.

Sai Maa teaches that this suffering is not separate from the divine. It is not a detour from the spiritual path. It is the spiritual path. The Vedic tradition does not separate human pain from divine intelligence. It holds that Shakti — the creative force of the universe — moves through joy and through sorrow with equal presence. The love that connected two souls did not originate in either body. It originated in the field of consciousness itself. And that field does not die when a body dies.

This is a difficult teaching for a body in pain. The chest does not care about philosophy. The hands do not want theology. The throat wants to scream, and the belly wants to collapse, and the knees want to buckle against the cold bathroom tile at midnight.

And so the tradition begins not with a concept but with a practice. Not with understanding but with touch. Place both hands on the heart. Feel the heartbeat through the palms. This simple act — skin on skin, warmth on warmth — is the beginning of svadhyaya. Self-study. The willingness to turn attention toward what is actually happening in this body, in this moment, without flinching, without editing, without the desperate hope that it will be different tomorrow.

It might be different tomorrow. It might not. The practice does not promise a timeline. The practice promises presence. And presence — Sai Maa teaches this again and again — presence is the first medicine.

The Vedic sages understood this thousands of years ago. They observed the body with the same precision that modern science now brings to the study of neurology and endocrinology. They mapped the energy channels — the nadis — and identified 72,000 pathways through which prana flows. They observed that grief concentrates its damage at the junctions, the marma points, where multiple nadis converge. The heart center — Anahata — is the greatest of these junctions. It is the crossroads where the ascending current of earth energy meets the descending current of cosmic energy. When this junction blocks, the entire system stalls. Above the heart, the throat closes. Below the heart, the belly hardens. The body fragments into zones that no longer communicate with each other.

This fragmentation is the lived experience of grief. The head says one thing, the heart feels another, the body does a third. The grieving person walks through the world in pieces, performing competence while dissolving internally, smiling at colleagues while the chest collapses behind the smile. The body becomes a collection of contradictions held together by habit and will.

Svadhyaya — self-study — begins the process of re-integration. Not by forcing the pieces together. By turning the gaze inward and witnessing, without judgment, the exact configuration of the fragmentation. Where does the numbness live. Where does the pain concentrate. Where does the body hold and where does it release. This witnessing is itself a form of prana. Attention is energy. And energy, directed with compassion, begins the slow work of restoration.

CHAPTER 2: THE BLOCKAGE

Ananya sits on the edge of her bed at dawn. The room is still dark. She has not slept — not truly — in eleven days. Eleven days since the phone call. Eleven days since the word "gone" entered her vocabulary as a permanent resident rather than a visitor.

Her husband of nineteen years. A Tuesday morning. A commute he had made four thousand times. A intersection he had crossed without incident for a decade. And then the call.

Ananya's body responded before her mind could organize a thought. Her legs gave way. Her vision narrowed to a tunnel. A sound left her throat — not a word, not a cry, something more ancient than language, a sound that belongs to every woman who has ever received that call in every century of human existence. She crumpled against the kitchen counter. The phone fell. His voice was replaced by the voice of a stranger saying words that had no meaning: impact, hospital, time of death, next of kin.

Eleven days later, the ceremonies complete, the relatives returned to their cities, the refrigerator still full of food that neighbors brought, Ananya sits on the edge of the bed and feels nothing.

This is the cruelest phase. The numbness. The place beyond tears, beyond anger, beyond the dramatic expressions of loss that others can witness and respond to. The numbness is invisible. It looks like composure. Colleagues say she is handling it well. Her mother-in-law says she is being strong for the children. Ananya nods. She accepts the praise for a strength she does not possess. She is not strong. She is frozen.

In the Vedic model of the pranic body, this freezing corresponds to a blockage in Anahata — the heart chakra, the fourth energy center, the seat of love, compassion, and connection. When the heart receives a blow of this magnitude, Anahata does not simply close. It armors. It builds a wall of dense, contracted energy that prevents the free flow of prana through the entire system.

The effects cascade. The throat chakra, Vishuddha, constricts — Ananya cannot speak about her husband without her voice flattening into a monotone that sounds nothing like her own. The solar plexus, Manipura, the center of personal power and will, goes dark — decision-making becomes impossible, and the simplest choices (what to eat, what to wear, whether to answer the phone) feel like acts of enormous, exhausting effort. The sacral chakra, Svadhisthana, the center of feeling and flow, dries — pleasure disappears, and the body that once responded to music, to sunlight, to the touch of another hand becomes numb to all sensation.

Ananya notices this in specific, concrete ways. She stands in the shower and cannot feel the water. She knows it is warm — the steam fogs the glass — but the sensation does not register on her skin. She eats a mango, her favorite fruit, and tastes nothing. She picks up her daughter from school and watches the girl run toward her across the playground, and the sight — which once flooded her chest with a warmth so intense it bordered on pain — produces nothing. Flat. Empty. The screen has gone dark.

The blockage is doing its work. It is protecting Ananya from a grief so vast that her nervous system has determined it would be dangerous to process all at once. This is not malfunction. This is intelligence. The body is wise. It doses the pain. It releases it in amounts the system can handle — a flash of memory here, a pang of loneliness there, a sudden tightness in the chest at the sound of his ringtone, which she has not changed and cannot change and does not know if she will ever change.

Svadhyaya — self-study — begins with noticing this pattern without trying to fix it. Ananya starts to observe. She notices that the numbness is not uniform. It has edges. It has moments of porousness, usually late at night when the defenses are down, when the body is too tired to maintain the armor. In those moments, a feeling seeps through — not the full force of the grief, but a tendril of it. A single thread of sadness that winds through the numbness and touches something alive in the center of her chest.

She notices that this tendril arrives most often with sound. The specific sound of his keys in the door — a sound she will never hear again, and yet one that her body listens for every evening at six-fifteen, tensing with an anticipation that has no resolution. The sound of a cricket match on a neighbor's television. The sound of his voice in a video on her phone — a casual recording from a holiday, unimportant at the time, irreplaceable now.

Nada Brahma — the universe is sound. The Vedic tradition understands that sound is not merely heard. Sound is felt. Sound vibrates in the bones, in the fluid of the inner ear, in the cavity of the chest. Sound penetrates the blockage in ways that thought cannot. This is the reason music makes the grieving person cry when nothing else can. Sound bypasses the armor. It finds the cracks. It enters directly.

Ananya discovers this one evening when her daughter plays a recording of a bhajan — a devotional song — that used to play in the kitchen on Sunday mornings. The sound enters the room and Ananya's body responds before her mind can intervene. Her hands begin to tremble. Her breath catches. And then — from deep beneath the numbness, from a place she thought had died when he died — a wave of feeling rises. It is hot. It is enormous. It is terrifying.

She lets it come. She does not know where she finds the courage, but she lets it come. The wave breaks over her and she bends forward on the sofa, her face in her hands, and she sobs. Her daughter, twelve years old and wise beyond reason, sits beside her and places one hand on her back and says nothing. There is nothing to say. There is only the sound of a mother's grief and the warmth of a daughter's hand and the bhajan still playing in the background, filling the room with a frequency that has carried human sorrow for three thousand years.

The blockage does not dissolve in this moment. But it cracks. Like a dam that has held too much water for too long, it cracks, and through the crack flows the first honest movement of prana that Ananya's heart has felt in eleven days.

She will need to crack it again. And again. And again. Grief does not open once and stay open. It opens and closes, opens and closes, like a door in a strong wind. But each opening widens the passage. Each flood of feeling carries more of the stagnant energy out of the body. Each crack allows more light to enter the heart.

And light — in the Vedic tradition — is another word for grace.

The crack does not close again. Not fully. Once the pranic body has experienced movement after stagnation, once the tears have carved a channel through the numbness, the channel remains. It may narrow in the days that follow. The armor may attempt to rebuild. But the body has tasted flow, and it remembers. The memory lives in the tissues, in the fascia, in the cells of the heart muscle itself. And each time Ananya returns to the practice — each time she sits, each time she breathes, each time she allows the sound of a bhajan or the smell of sesame oil or the weight of her daughter's hand on her back to penetrate the armor — the crack widens. The channel deepens. The flow strengthens.

This is the nature of pranic healing. It is cumulative. Every session adds to every previous session. The body does not forget its openings. The heart does not forget its moments of thaw. The path forward is not linear — there will be mornings of numbness, afternoons of rage, evenings of a loneliness so profound it seems to have a gravitational field of its own — but the direction is clear. The direction is always toward flow. Toward the restoration of the current that connects the individual heart to the vast, unbroken field of love that the Vedic tradition calls Brahman — the ultimate reality, the ground of all being, the source from which every love originates and to which every love returns.

CHAPTER 3: THE HEART PRACTICE

Begin with the breath.

Sit in a position that feels sustainable — a chair, a cushion, the edge of a bed. The spine does not need to be perfectly erect. It needs to be supported. Place the hands on the knees, palms facing upward, in the gesture of receiving. Or place them on the heart, one on top of the other, in the gesture of devotion. Let the body choose.

Close the eyes. Turn the attention inward. Feel the breath as it enters the nostrils — the slight coolness of the inhale, the slight warmth of the exhale. Follow the breath down into the chest. Notice where it stops. In a grieving body, the breath often stops high — at the collarbones, at the upper ribs. It does not descend into the belly. It holds, as though the body has forgotten that the belly exists, or as though descending that deep means opening something that has been sealed.

Do not force the breath deeper. Simply notice where it goes. Notice where it pauses. Notice the quality of the transition between inhale and exhale — is there a gap, a held moment, a hitch. Every pause, every hitch, every held breath is a message. The body is saying: here. Pay attention here. Something is stuck here.

Now bring the attention to the heart center — Anahata, the fourth chakra, located at the center of the chest. In the Vedic tradition, Anahata means "unstruck" — the sound that arises without two things colliding. The heartbeat itself is an Anahata sound. It arose at the beginning of life and it continues without ceasing, without being struck from outside, self-generating, self-sustaining, a rhythm that began before the first memory and will continue until the final breath.

Place the awareness in the center of this rhythm. Feel the heartbeat. Not with the hands — with the inner attention. Sense the pulse as a vibration, a wave, a drumbeat in the center of the chest. Let the attention rest there. Let it settle like a bird landing on a branch — gently, with the possibility of flight, but for now, resting.

Now introduce the mantra. In the Vedic tradition, mantra is medicine in the form of sound. The specific mantra for heart healing is simple. It is the sound of the heart chakra itself: YAM.

Speak it internally. Not with the lips, not with the tongue. With the mind. With the inner voice that exists beneath thought. YAM. Let the sound vibrate in the chest. Let it fill the space around the heart. Let it ripple outward through the ribs, through the shoulders, through the arms, into the hands.

YAM. YAM. YAM.

With each repetition, the vibration deepens. The mantra does not ask the grief to leave. The mantra does not promise relief. The mantra enters the blockage the way water enters stone — through the smallest cracks, through the invisible fissures, through the spaces between molecules. The mantra is patient. It does not break the blockage. It softens it. It saturates it. It transforms it from the inside, the way a river transforms a canyon — not by force, but by persistence.

Continue the mantra for several minutes. Five minutes. Ten. Twenty. There is no required duration. The body will signal when it has had enough. A shift in the quality of the breath — suddenly deeper, suddenly easier — often marks the turning point. A warmth in the chest, a tingling in the hands, a spontaneous sigh that releases tension from the shoulders and the jaw.

If emotions arise, allow them. The mantra creates a container. It is not a dam — it does not hold the feelings back. It is a riverbank — it gives the feelings a channel, a direction, a shape. Tears flow within the sound. Anger moves within the vibration. Longing, that particular ache of grief that has no remedy except time, expresses itself through the steady pulse of YAM, and in that expression, it releases a fraction of its charge.

Now deepen the practice with visualization. See, in the mind's eye, a green light at the center of the chest. Emerald green — the color of Anahata, the color of growth, the color of the first leaf that pushes through the soil after a long winter. See this green light as a sphere, small at first, the size of a pearl, glowing softly in the darkness behind the breastbone.

With each repetition of YAM, the sphere grows. It expands like a sunrise. It fills the chest. It pushes gently against the walls of the blockage — not breaking them, but illuminating them from within. The blockage becomes visible. Its shape becomes clear. Its density becomes known. The light does not fight the darkness. The light simply reveals what the darkness has been holding.

The green light carries a quality. In the Vedic tradition, that quality is karuna — compassion. Not the thin compassion of pity. The fierce compassion of a mother lifting a car off her child. The compassion that says: this pain is real, this loss is real, this absence is permanent and devastating and unfair, and still — still — the heart continues. Still the pulse beats. Still the breath moves. Still the green light glows at the center of a chest that has been shattered and is, even now, in the process of remaking itself.

Hold the visualization. Hold the mantra. Hold the breath as it deepens and opens and descends into the belly for the first time in days or weeks or months. Feel the body soften. Feel the shoulders drop. Feel the jaw release its grip.

When the practice feels complete — and the body will know — let the green light fade slowly, like an ember cooling. It does not go out. It never goes out. It retreats to the size of a pearl and rests in the heart center, waiting for the next practice, the next session, the next moment when the hands return to the chest and the mantra returns to the inner ear and the light returns to the darkness.

Open the eyes slowly. Feel the room. Feel the surface beneath the body. Take three natural breaths without controlling them. Let the body set its own rhythm.

The practice is complete. The heart has been attended. The prana has been invited to flow. The blockage has been softened, not by force, but by the most powerful force the Vedic tradition knows: devotion. Devotion to the practice. Devotion to the breath. Devotion to the love that survives the body and continues — as the Gita teaches, as the rishis sang, as every grieving heart discovers when it is ready — continues without end.

CHAPTER 4: THE OPENING

Ananya is washing rice when the opening comes.

Four months have passed. She has practiced daily — the breath, the mantra, the green light. Some days the practice lasts twenty minutes. Some days it lasts three. Some days she sits on the cushion and the tears come before the mantra does, and the practice becomes simply crying, which is its own form of pranayama, its own discipline of breath and release and surrender.

The rice runs clear. She pours it into the pot. She adds water — the exact measure, two knuckles above the grain, the way her mother taught her, the way she taught her husband when he insisted on learning. He never got the water right. Always too much. The rice came out soft, almost porridge. She scolded him. He laughed. He ate it anyway.

The memory arrives without warning. It enters through the hands — the sensation of water and grain, the muscle memory of a thousand evenings in this kitchen — and it goes straight to the heart. And Ananya expects the pain. She braces for it. She waits for the familiar tightness, the constriction, the fist closing around the center of her chest.

But the fist does not close. Not fully. It starts to close — she feels the first contraction — and then something intercepts it. Something warm. Something green. Something that has been growing, quietly, in the center of her chest for four months of daily practice, of mantra and breath and devotion.

The warmth spreads. The contraction releases. And in its place — in the exact spot where the blockage has lived for four months — Ananya feels something she has not felt since the phone call.

She feels him. Not his body. Not his voice. Something more essential. Something that the Vedic tradition calls the atman — the individual soul, the spark of the divine that inhabits and then departs the physical form. She feels his presence in the kitchen. Not as ghost, not as hallucination, not as the desperate projection of a bereaved mind. As warmth. As familiarity. As the unmistakable quality of a specific love that she has known for nineteen years and that did not, despite every appearance, disappear when his body stopped.

The rice boils over. Ananya does not move. She stands at the counter with her hands wet and her heart open and her breath flowing deep into her belly, and she lets herself feel what she feels. The tears come — but they are different now. They are not the tears of the dam breaking. They are the tears of recognition. Of reunion. Of the stunned, grateful, impossible discovery that love is not stored in a body. Love is stored in the field. And the field does not die.

This is the opening. Not a grand enlightenment. Not a moment of transcendence that lifts the griever above human suffering into some pristine spiritual realm. The opening is ordinary. It happens at a kitchen counter, with wet hands and boiling rice. It happens in the body, not above the body. It happens because the heart has been tended — day after day, mantra after mantra, breath after breath — until the blockage has softened enough to allow the love back in.

Ananya still misses him. She will always miss him. The bed is still too wide. The evenings are still too quiet. The future she planned has been replaced by a future she did not choose and does not want.

But the flow has returned. Prana moves through her body again. She feels the water on her hands. She tastes the mango. She watches her daughter run across the playground and the warmth floods her chest — painful and beautiful and absolutely, unbearably alive.

This is what the Vedic tradition means by healing. Not the absence of pain. The presence of flow. Not the end of grief. The integration of grief into the larger current of a life that continues, that insists on continuing, that carries the love forward through every loss and every morning and every pot of rice cooked with too much water and eaten anyway, with laughter, with gratitude, with the full knowledge that the one who cooked it wrong is still here. Still here. Not in the chair. In the heart.

Give the heart permission. Permission to crack. Permission to flood. Permission to rebuild itself around the absence, the way a tree grows around a fence post — not by removing the obstacle, but by incorporating it, by making it part of the structure, by continuing to grow.

The heart is vast enough. The atman is infinite. The love does not end. Place the hands on the chest. Feel the heartbeat. Feel the green light. Feel the pulse of a love that outlasts the body and continues — continues — continues.

WHERE TO GO DEEPER

These teachings draw from the living stream of Vedic wisdom, a tradition that has accompanied human beings through loss and transformation for thousands of years.

For those who wish to continue the journey, the path leads in many directions. Study the Bhagavad Gita's teachings on the imperishable nature of the soul. Explore pranayama practices with a qualified yoga teacher who understands the relationship between breath and grief. Seek out kirtan and bhajan communities where devotional singing creates a collective field of healing sound.

Sai Maa's teachings on awakening, transformation, and the divine nature of the human heart offer a living bridge between ancient wisdom and contemporary experience. Explore recorded satsangs and darshan events for direct transmission of the healing frequency.

Find a community. Grief is a solitary experience, but healing is communal. The Vedic tradition has always understood that the individual heart heals faster in the presence of other hearts — in the warmth of sangha, of shared practice, of voices chanting together in a room where the sound rises and fills the space with a frequency older than language and stronger than loss.

The practice continues. The breath continues. The heart continues. The love continues. This is the teaching. This is the promise. This is the truth that every tradition of divine healing has carried through every century of human sorrow: the love continues.

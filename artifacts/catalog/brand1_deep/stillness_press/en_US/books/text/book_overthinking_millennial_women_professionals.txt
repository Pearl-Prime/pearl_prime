A NOTE ON THE TEACHINGS

Do not take this book on faith. The mind this book is written for would not accept it on faith anyway, and the tradition behind it would not want you to.

These teachings come from a contemplative lineage far older than the word overthinking, but the lineage asks for nothing you cannot verify yourself. In the Kalama Sutta, the Buddha set out the only standard that applies here: believe no teaching because it is old, because a teacher delivered it, because it is written, or because it sounds intelligent. Test it. Run it through your own mind. Keep whatever reduces the suffering. Discard the rest, including the words in this book, if they do not help.

So nothing here is a doctrine. Everything here is an experiment, and you are the laboratory.

The voice is Ahjan — a contemplative practitioner, not a therapist. He does not speak with clinical authority, and this book is not a substitute for clinical care. He speaks as someone who watched his own thinking go in circles long enough to learn how the circling is built — and what he learned is that overthinking is not a sign of a defective or weak mind. It is, very often, the misuse of an excellent one. The same engine that makes you good at your work is the engine that keeps you up rewriting a text message at midnight. The mind is not broken. It is simply running without an off switch, and this book is about where the switch is.

This is written for the woman whose mind is her instrument and her trap — who is praised for being thoughtful and thorough, and who pays for it in the hours spent rehearsing conversations that already happened and conversations that never will. It is addressed not to that brilliant, exhausting mind, but to the body underneath it, which has been bracing the whole time.

CHAPTER 1: THE DRAFT

It is past midnight and Priya is sitting up in bed with her phone, composing the fourth version of a message she could have sent two hours ago. The message says, in essence, can we move the meeting to Thursday. She has now written and deleted it three times — too curt, then too apologetic, then somehow both — and she is reading the current draft again, hunting for the tone that will not be misread, will not seem demanding, will not reveal that she is the kind of person who agonizes over a one-line text. Her thumb hovers. Her shoulders are pulled up toward her ears. Her breath is shallow and high. She has not noticed any of this, because all of her attention is inside the screen, inside the sentence, inside the imagined reaction of a person who is asleep and will read the message in nine hours and reply sure within thirty seconds without a single one of the thoughts Priya is currently having about it.

This is overthinking, and the first thing to see is what it actually is underneath the words. It looks like thinking. It feels like diligence. But watch the body: the raised shoulders, the held breath, the thumb that will not commit. This is not the calm, productive thought that solves a problem. This is a body in a low-grade threat state, and the looping is what the mind does when the body is braced and there is no actual danger to act on. The thinking is not solving anything. It is the sound the alarm makes when it cannot find the fire.

Here is the teaching, and it cuts against everything a high-achieving mind believes: more thinking is not the way out of overthinking. You cannot think your way to the bottom of a loop, because the loop is not a problem to be solved — it is a state to be exited. Priya believes that if she just finds the perfectly calibrated wording, the unease will resolve and she will be able to put the phone down. But the unease did not come from imperfect wording, and no perfect wording will dissolve it. The unease came first; the search for perfect wording is the form the unease took. She could find the ideal sentence and the body would simply move to the next thing to perfect, because the looping was never about the sentence. It was about a nervous system that does not feel safe to stop.

In the contemplative tradition this is described with great precision. The mind, left in a state of subtle threat, produces papañca — usually translated as proliferation, the mental spinning-out of a single contact into an endless web of thought, judgment, anticipation, and rehearsal. One small event — a meeting that needs moving — becomes a sprawling internal simulation: how it will land, what it implies about her, what she should have done sooner, what kind of person sends this kind of message. Papañca is the mind multiplying itself, generating ten thousand thoughts from one seed, and the more it proliferates the more real and urgent the whole construction feels, even though almost none of it corresponds to anything that is actually happening.

So this is the first move, and it has nothing to do with finding better words. Priya puts the phone down — face down — and places one hand flat on her chest. Not to fix the message. To feel what the message was distracting her from. Under the palm: a fast heartbeat. The breath, when she finally notices it, stuck high in the chest. The shoulders up near the ears. And she sees, maybe for the first time tonight, that she was never really wrestling with a sentence. She was bracing. The sentence was just where the bracing went.

Because the truth is plain once the body is in the picture: the message does not need a fourth draft. The first draft was fine. The first draft is almost always fine. What needs attention is not the wording but the state — the quiet, bodily sense of not-safe-to-stop that turned a one-line text into a midnight ordeal. And you do not exit that state by thinking harder. You exit it the way you exit any state the body is holding: by coming back into the body, slowing the breath, and letting the mind, deprived of the threat that was feeding it, finally run down. The loop does not end when you win the argument with it. It ends when you stop feeding it and turn, instead, to the one thing that is real — the hand, the breath, the body in the room.

CHAPTER 2: THE PATTERN

Her name is Hannah, she is thirty-three, and she replays conversations.

Not occasionally — constantly, as a kind of background process that runs underneath her days. A meeting ends and she is already reviewing it: the thing she said that landed wrong, the better thing she could have said, the expression on a colleague's face that might have meant boredom or might have meant nothing. A dinner with friends ends and on the walk home she is auditing it for the moment she talked too much, or too little, or revealed something she should have kept back. She wakes some mornings already mid-replay of a conversation from years ago, one that is finished, that no one else remembers, that cannot be changed by a single one of the revisions she keeps drafting.

Hannah would tell you she is just self-aware. Reflective. The kind of person who cares about how she affects others. And she is — but that is the surface. Underneath the self-awareness is a much older machine, and it has a shape, and the shape repeats.

Hannah grew up reading a room. In her family, approval was real but conditional and never quite predictable — warmth that could cool without warning, a parent whose regard had to be continually earned and could be lost by a wrong word, a wrong tone, a wrong choice that she often only identified after the fact, in the chill that followed. So Hannah learned to monitor. She learned to run the replay: to review every interaction for the misstep, to catch the error before it cost her, to rehearse the next encounter so thoroughly that she could not be caught off guard. By the time she was a teenager her mind had become a continuous editing suite, reviewing the footage of her own life for mistakes, and the reviewing was rewarded — with the relief of having spotted the problem, with the sense, however illusory, that enough analysis could keep approval from slipping away.

This is the part that high-achieving overthinkers most need to hear: the overthinking is not stupidity, and it is not weakness. It is intelligence conscripted into the service of safety. The same capacity that lets Hannah see three moves ahead in a negotiation, anticipate the objection before it is raised, and produce careful, considered work is the capacity that traps her in the replay at 2 a.m. The mind that overthinks is usually a very good mind. It was simply trained, early and well, that thinking is how you stay safe — that if you can just analyze thoroughly enough, you can prevent the bad outcome, secure the approval, control the uncontrollable reactions of other people. And so it never stops analyzing, because to the machine, stopping feels like dropping its guard.

The trouble is that the machine is solving a problem that no longer exists. The conditional approval of childhood is over. The colleagues are not the unpredictable parent. The friends are not auditing the dinner the way Hannah is. Most people are far too occupied with their own internal editing suites to be running the kind of forensic analysis of Hannah that she imagines and dreads. But the machine cannot tell the difference. To it, every social interaction is still a high-stakes evaluation that must be reviewed for errors, and so it runs the replay, faithfully, for events that no one else even remembers.

The Buddha mapped this loop and also its exit. The loop is the proliferation — contact, unease, and then the mind spinning the unease into a vast rehearsed simulation that feels like productive thought and is actually a treadmill. The exit is not a better simulation. The exit is seeing the proliferation as proliferation — catching the mind in the act of multiplying, often enough that the spell of this thinking is necessary begins to break.

When Hannah finally tries the contemplative approach, her instruction is deliberately small and deliberately physical, because the loop cannot be exited from inside the head. When she catches herself replaying — auditing a conversation, rehearsing a future one — she is to do three things: name it (replaying, or rehearsing), take one long breath out, and bring her attention to a single physical sensation, usually her feet on the floor or her hands in her lap. She is not to resolve the conversation. She is not to figure out whether the colleague was bored. She is simply to label the activity, exhale, and land in the body.

For a couple of weeks she says it barely works — she names the replay and it starts right back up thirty seconds later. But the instruction was never to stop the replay forever. It was to interrupt it, once, and then again, and then again, building the one muscle that overthinking has never let her develop: the muscle of returning. By the third week she notices that the gap between catching the loop and getting pulled back in has stretched — a few seconds, then a few breaths. She is not thinking less, exactly. She is identifying with the thinking less. She has started to experience her thoughts as events passing through awareness rather than as urgent instructions she has to obey.

And one evening, walking home from a dinner, she feels the replay start — the audit, the search for the moment she talked too much — and she names it, replaying, and breathes out, and feels her feet on the pavement, and the audit simply does not restart. There is just the walk. The cool air. The sound of her own steps. The conversation she was about to spend an hour reviewing recedes into what it actually was: a nice evening with friends, already over, requiring nothing further from her at all.

The overthinking does not vanish for Hannah. The editing suite is still installed; it still powers up. But the replays have stopped being a sentence she has to serve and become, more and more, just thoughts she can notice and decline — named, breathed through, set down — as she returns, again and again, to the only place the mind has ever actually been able to rest: here, in the body, in the present, where there is nothing to revise.

CHAPTER 3: THE PRACTICE

Everything here is designed to be done in the middle of a loop — at the desk, on the walk home, in bed with the phone in hand. The loop happens in the head, so every practice here deliberately moves attention out of the head and into the body, because that is the only door out.

PRACTICE ONE: NAME THE PROCESS, NOT THE CONTENT

This is the foundational tool, and it is the opposite of what overthinking wants you to do.

When you catch the mind spinning, do not engage with what it is spinning about. Do not evaluate whether the worry is valid, whether the conversation really went badly, whether the message really needs revising. Instead, name the process: planning. Rehearsing. Replaying. Worrying. Problem-solving-with-no-problem. Then let it be there without following it further.

The reason this works is mechanical, not mystical. Overthinking sustains itself by keeping you fused with the content — pulled inside each thought as though it were reality, as though resolving it were urgent and possible. Naming the process pops you out. It activates the observing part of the mind and reveals the thought as a thought — a mental event, weather passing through — rather than a command you must obey. You are not trying to stop the thinking. You are changing your relationship to it, from inside it to beside it. And from beside it, you can decline to follow, the way you can notice a song stuck in your head without having to sing along.

PRACTICE TWO: THE FEET AND THE FLOOR

This is the fastest way out of the head, and it can be done anywhere, invisibly, in the middle of anything.

Bring your full attention to the soles of your feet. Feel them inside your shoes, or against the floor. Feel the weight of your body pressing down through them. Feel the actual physical contact — pressure, temperature, texture — for three or four breaths.

That is the whole practice, and it is far more powerful than it sounds. Overthinking lives in the future and the past — in conversations not yet had and conversations already over. The feet live only in the present; they can report nothing but now. By moving attention deliberately to the most distant point from the head — the feet, the literal ground — you pull the center of gravity of your awareness down and out of the spinning. The mind cannot loop and feel the floor at the same time with full attention; there is not enough attention for both. Choose the floor. The loop, starved of attention, quiets. Gravity, it turns out, is the most reliable anti-anxiety device there is, and it is always available, and it never runs out.

PRACTICE THREE: THE GOOD-ENOUGH DECISION

This one is specifically for the overthinker's signature trap: the inability to stop deciding.

When you catch yourself on the fourth draft of anything — the message, the email, the choice between two reasonable options — set a deliberate limit and then honor it. One more pass, then it sends. Two minutes, then I choose. The decision does not have to be perfect. It has to be good enough, and then done.

This is harder than it sounds, because the overthinking mind has a deep, trained belief that the perfect choice exists and that enough analysis will find it — and that anything less than the perfect choice is a failure waiting to be discovered. But the perfect choice almost never exists; most decisions in a life are between several adequate options, and the cost of agonizing over them far exceeds the difference between them. The contemplative point is sharp here: the suffering is not in the decision. The suffering is in the grasping for a certainty that the situation does not contain. When you let a good-enough decision be enough — and tolerate the small discomfort of not having perfectly resolved it — you starve the loop of its favorite fuel. And almost always, the good-enough decision turns out to have been completely fine, which the body slowly learns, one sent message at a time.

PRACTICE FOUR: THE BODY KNOWS FIRST

This is for the deeper work — for learning to trust something other than analysis.

A few times a day, pause and ask not what do I think but what do I feel, in the body, right now. Then check: chest, stomach, shoulders, jaw. Is there tightness? Ease? A clench, a settling? You are not analyzing the feeling. You are just noticing that the body has a response, and that the response arrived before all the thinking did.

This matters because the overthinker has learned to override the body completely — to treat the mind's endless analysis as the only valid source of information and to distrust or ignore the body's faster, quieter signals. But the body registers a situation before the mind constructs its case about it. The tightness that arrives the instant you read a message, before you have thought a single thought, is real data. So is the small settling you feel when a decision is actually right. By practicing returning to the body and asking what it already knows, you begin to rebuild a channel of information that does not require ten thousand thoughts — a faster, truer, less exhausting way of knowing, the one you had before you learned that safety meant analyzing everything. The mind is a magnificent tool. It was never meant to be the only one.

CHAPTER 4: THE RETURN

Here is the whole teaching, small enough to carry through a sleepless, spinning night.

Overthinking is not a broken mind. It is an excellent mind doing the wrong job. Somewhere early, you learned that thinking is how you stay safe — that enough analysis could prevent the bad outcome, secure the approval, control how other people react. And so the mind never stops analyzing, because to the machine, stopping feels like lowering the guard. The loop looks like diligence and feels like care, but underneath it is a body in a low-grade threat state, and the spinning is simply what the mind does when it is braced and there is no real danger to act on. The thinking is not solving the problem. The thinking is the alarm, looking for a fire that is not there.

The work, then, is not to think your way out — you cannot, because the loop is a state, not a problem. The work is to exit the state: to name the process instead of chasing the content, to drop attention into the feet and the breath and the body, to let a good-enough decision be enough and tolerate the small discomfort that follows. Not by force, which is just more effort and more bracing, but by returning — again and again, ten thousand times if needed — to the present moment, where there is nothing to revise and nothing to rehearse, because the present is the one place the mind has never needed to defend.

And the work is to stop believing the loop's central lie: that this thinking is necessary, that you cannot stop until you have resolved it, that the analysis is keeping you safe. It is not keeping you safe. The danger it guards against — the lost approval, the social misstep, the imperfect choice — is almost always either imaginary or survivable, and the analysis itself has become the larger source of suffering. This is karma in its plain sense: the old action, compulsive thinking, produces the old consequence, exhaustion and a life half-lived inside one's own head. New action — the named loop, the breath, the return to the body — produces a new consequence, available now, this minute, every time you choose the floor over the spin.

There is a word in the old texts — nibbida, often mistranslated as disgust, but meaning something nearer to disenchantment. The spell breaks. The looping loses its hypnotic claim to be urgent and real. A rehearsed conversation becomes what it is: a thought, with a beginning and an end, that you can watch go by. Because every loop you have ever been caught in has eventually ended — every replay, every rehearsal, every fourth draft has, sooner or later, stopped, and the imagined catastrophe has, almost without exception, failed to arrive. The mind insists that this loop is the one that matters, that this one you must finish. The mind has always insisted that. The mind has almost always been wrong.

So receive this plainly, and let the body hear it, not just the brilliant, tired mind.

You have permission to send the first draft. You have permission to make a good-enough decision and walk away from it. You have permission to end a conversation and not review the footage. You have permission to not know exactly how you came across, to leave a small uncertainty unresolved, to let other people have their own unmonitored reactions. You have permission to stop analyzing and simply be in your life as it is happening, instead of three steps behind it, editing.

The overthinking taught you something real. It made you thoughtful, thorough, attuned to nuance, capable of work that careless minds cannot do. That is not a weakness; it is a genuine gift, forged early under pressure. The work now is not to destroy the mind that learned to think its way to safety. The work is to give it rest — to thank it for its tireless vigilance, and to show it, gently and repeatedly, that you are safe now even when you stop, that the floor is under your feet, that the present moment requires no analysis at all.

Every time you name the loop instead of following it — that is the practice.
Every time attention drops into the feet and the breath — that is the practice.
Every time a good-enough decision is allowed to be enough — that is the practice.
Every time the mind starts to spin and you return, instead, to the body — that is the path. Not the end of all thinking. The end of the suffering you were adding to it: the compulsion, the exhaustion, the slow conviction that you are simply someone whose mind never stops, and never can.

Let the identity go. Keep the breath.
The loop begins. Name it. Return to the floor.
The mind can rest. It has only been waiting for you to show it that stopping is safe.

CHAPTER 5: THE SPIRALS THAT WON'T STOP

Everything so far assumes a loop you can catch and interrupt. But some loops do not let you catch them. There is the 3 a.m. spiral that has you by the throat, where every named thought is replaced by three more before you can breathe. There is the spiral of a real decision with real stakes, where the looping feels not like a habit but like the only responsible thing to do. And there are the long stretches when the mind seems louder than ever and the practice seems useless. This chapter is for those.

First, the acute spiral — the one that will not be interrupted, usually at night, usually about something that cannot be acted on until morning. When naming the process and dropping into the feet does not work, when the mind simply overrides every attempt and races on, stop trying to win the fight directly and change the terrain instead. Get the spiral out of your head and onto paper. Keep a notebook by the bed and write the loop down — not to solve it, but to externalize it. The mind spins partly because it is terrified of forgetting, of losing the thread, of letting the unresolved thing slip away; writing it down tells the mind it is safe to stop holding it, that the worry is captured and will still be there in the morning. Often the act of writing a spiral down — seeing it in plain words, finite, on a page — drains half its power, because spirals depend on motion, on never quite landing, and the page makes them land. Then add one line: I will look at this tomorrow. And let the notebook hold it so your mind does not have to.

If even that fails and the spiral rages on, fall back to the body's bluntest tool: cold and movement. Cold water on the face, an ice cube in the hand, a few minutes of physical movement — these interrupt the racing mind at a level below thought, where the looping cannot follow. You are not trying to think your way calm. You are giving the nervous system a sensation so concrete and immediate that the spiral, starved of the attention it runs on, finally breaks. And then, in the small gap that opens, the long exhale and the feet on the floor — which could not get a foothold before — can finally do their work.

Second, the high-stakes decision, where the looping disguises itself as diligence. This is the overthinker's most convincing trap: a genuinely important choice, where it truly feels that more analysis is responsible and stopping is reckless. Here the contemplative point is precise. There is a difference between thinking that gathers what you need and thinking that spins past it. The first is finite: it collects the relevant information, weighs the real options, and arrives. The second is infinite: it circles the same ground, generates the same scenarios, seeks a certainty the situation does not contain. The test is simple — am I learning anything new, or am I just re-feeling the same anxiety in new words? If the analysis has stopped producing new information and is only producing more dread, it is no longer diligence. It is the loop wearing diligence as a costume. Gather what you genuinely need, set a real deadline, make the good-enough decision, and let the discomfort of uncertainty be there without trying to think it away. The discomfort is not a signal that you decided wrong. It is just the feeling of being a finite person making a choice without perfect information, which is the only kind of choice anyone has ever made.

Third, the long stretch. You practice for weeks; the loops quiet; and then a hard run comes and the mind is suddenly louder than ever, and the old verdict returns: it never worked, my mind will never stop, this is just who I am. This is the decisive moment, because how you meet the loud stretch determines whether it is a setback or a surrender. It is a setback. The mind does not quiet in a straight line; it quiets in a wavering one, with loud runs scattered through the calm, usually triggered by stress or poor sleep or a season of high stakes. A loud stretch does not erase the calm weeks or prove the practice failed. It is a trained mind, under load, reverting to its oldest habit — exactly as it was always going to at some point. The work is not to never spiral again. It is to meet the loud stretch with the same naming, the same return to the body, the same refusal to add I've failed to the noise.

Be gentle with the version of you who is spinning and cannot stop. She is not weak, and the loud mind is not a defect — it is an excellent instrument, briefly overwhelmed, doing the only thing it was ever trained to do under threat. Meet her the way you would meet a friend caught in a spiral: not with impatience, not with a verdict, but with steadiness. I know. Your mind is loud right now. It will quiet. And then the loop is named, and the breath goes long, and attention drops, once more, to the feet on the floor — where there is nothing to revise, nothing to rehearse, and nothing, in this exact moment, that the mind needs to solve at all.

WHERE TO GO DEEPER

This book is one doorway. If it quieted the loop even briefly, the tradition behind it has more, and all of it is freely given.

The foundational practice is mindfulness of the body and breath, set out most completely in the Satipatthana Sutta. For the overthinker, the body is the crucial anchor precisely because it is not the mind — it offers a place to stand that the looping cannot reach. Read the text slowly, and let breath-awareness become a habit by day, so that it is available to you when the spinning starts at night.

The contemplative teachers worth seeking — Ajahn Chah, Thich Nhat Hanh, Tara Brach, Joseph Goldstein — have left talks and writings, widely and freely available, that speak directly to working with a busy mind. Several distinguish, as this book does, between the thinking that solves and the thinking that merely spins. Let their voices be companions, not authorities. Test what they say in your own mind. Keep what helps.

And if the overthinking has tipped into something heavier — relentless rumination, intrusive thoughts, a mind you cannot get any relief from — please treat that as worth real care. Rumination is closely linked to anxiety and depression, and it responds well to professional approaches, including cognitive behavioral therapy. This book is not a substitute for that. Seeking help is not a failure of the very intelligence you are proud of; it is that intelligence, finally aimed at your own well-being instead of at managing everyone's perception of you.

The Kalama Sutta stands. Test everything. Keep what reduces suffering. Release the rest — including, if it does not help, this.

The loop will start again — that is the nature of a trained mind, and you are not failing when it does. But you are no longer trapped inside it, mistaking the spinning for the world. You are beside it now. You are the still awareness in which the thoughts arise and pass. And in that stillness — feet on the floor, breath moving, nothing to revise — the mind you were so afraid to stop discovers, at last, that it is allowed to rest.

The breath moves. The body settles. The thinking, unfed, grows quiet.

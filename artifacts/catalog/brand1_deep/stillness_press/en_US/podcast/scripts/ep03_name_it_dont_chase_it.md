## [COLD OPEN — 30-60s · HOOK · no music]

It's past midnight and you're writing the fourth version of a message you could have sent two hours ago. Too curt. Then too apologetic. Then somehow both. Your thumb is hovering. Your shoulders are up. [PAUSE]

The message says: *can we move the meeting to Thursday.* And the person you're agonizing over is asleep, and will read it in nine hours, and reply *sure* in thirty seconds — without a single one of the thoughts you're currently having about it.

## [THEME INTRO — 15-30s · brand intro music]

This is The Stillness Check-In, from Stillness Press. I'm Ahjan. Today, we're talking about the loop — the mind that won't stop rehearsing, replaying, redrafting — and why the way out is not, as it turns out, more thinking.

## [SCENE SETTING — 60-120s · ambient bed, low]

Look at what's actually happening in that midnight moment. [PAUSE]

It looks like thinking. It feels like diligence — like you're being careful, responsible, thorough. But look at the body: the raised shoulders, the held breath, the thumb that won't commit. That's not the calm, productive thought that solves a problem. That's a body in a low-grade threat state. And the looping is just what the mind does when the body is braced and there's no real danger to act on.

The thinking isn't solving anything. It's the sound the alarm makes when it can't find the fire.

## [TEACHING — 180-300s · no music]

Here's the part that's hard for a smart, capable mind to hear: *more thinking is not the way out of overthinking.* [PAUSE]

You can't think your way to the bottom of a loop, because the loop isn't a problem to be solved. It's a state to be exited. You believe that if you just find the perfectly calibrated wording — the perfect tone, the perfect choice — the unease will finally resolve and you'll be able to put the phone down. But the unease didn't come from imperfect wording. The unease came first. The search for the perfect words is the *form* the unease took. [PAUSE]

You could find the ideal sentence, and the mind would simply move to the next thing to perfect — because the loop was never about the sentence. It was about a nervous system that doesn't feel safe to stop.

The old contemplative texts have a precise word for this: *papañca* — proliferation. The mind, left in a state of subtle threat, takes one small thing — a meeting that needs moving — and multiplies it into a vast internal simulation. How it'll land. What it implies about you. What you should've done sooner. What kind of person sends this kind of message. Ten thousand thoughts from one seed. And the more it proliferates, the more real and urgent the whole construction feels — even though almost none of it corresponds to anything actually happening. [PAUSE]

And here's why your mind learned to do this. Somewhere early, you learned that thinking is how you stay safe — that if you just analyze thoroughly enough, you can prevent the bad outcome, secure the approval, control how other people react. So your mind never stops analyzing, because to the machine, stopping feels like dropping your guard. The overthinking isn't a defective mind. It's an excellent mind, doing the wrong job.

## [PIVOT — 30-60s]

But here's what most people miss. The danger your looping is guarding against — the lost approval, the social misstep, the imperfect choice — is almost always either imaginary or completely survivable. And the analysis itself has quietly become the larger source of suffering. The thing you're doing to feel safe is the thing making you exhausted.

## [PERMISSION — 30-60s]

So let me give you permission. You're allowed to send the first draft. You're allowed to make a good-enough decision and walk away from it. You're allowed to end a conversation and *not* review the footage afterward. You're allowed to leave a small uncertainty unresolved, and to let other people have their own reactions that you don't get to monitor or control.

## [PRACTICE — guided · 120-180s · ambient bed returns, very low]

Let's practice the core move. It's the opposite of what overthinking wants you to do. [PAUSE]

The next time you catch the mind spinning, don't engage with *what* it's spinning about. Don't evaluate whether the worry is valid. Instead, just name the *process.* Say to yourself, simply: *planning.* Or *rehearsing.* Or *replaying.* Or *worrying.* And then let it be there, without following it.

Let's try it now with whatever's in your head. Notice the thought. [PAUSE] Now name what kind of thought it is — not its content, just its category. *Planning.* *Worrying.* *Replaying.* [PAUSE] Good.

Now, one more step — drop out of your head and into your body. Bring your attention to the soles of your feet. Feel them against the floor, inside your shoes. Feel the actual weight of your body pressing down through them. [PAUSE — 3s] Stay there for three breaths. [PAUSE — 4s]

The mind can't loop and fully feel the floor at the same time — there isn't enough attention for both. So you choose the floor. And the loop, starved of attention, goes quiet. Gravity, it turns out, is the most reliable anti-anxiety device there is. It's always available, and it never runs out.

## [TAKEAWAY — 30-60s · no music]

So here's the whole thing in one sentence: *name the loop instead of chasing it — call it planning, or replaying, or worrying — then drop your attention into your feet, and let the spinning, unfed, grow quiet.*

You are not the thinking. You're the awareness the thinking passes through.

## [INTEGRATION / OUTRO — 60-90s · brand outro music, soft]

This week, see if you can catch one loop and name it. Just once. *Replaying.* *Rehearsing.* And then feel your feet on the floor, and decline to follow the thought any further — the way you'd notice a song stuck in your head without having to sing along. [PAUSE]

You won't think less, exactly. But you'll start to *identify* with the thinking less. And from beside the loop, instead of inside it, you can finally choose: I don't have to follow that. There's nothing to revise right now. There's just here. The floor. The breath. This moment, which requires no analysis at all. [PAUSE]

I'm Ahjan. This was The Stillness Check-In. The mind can rest — it's only been waiting for you to show it that stopping is safe. I'll see you next week. [PAUSE]

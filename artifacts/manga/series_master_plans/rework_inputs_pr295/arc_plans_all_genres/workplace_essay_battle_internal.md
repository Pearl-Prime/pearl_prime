## high_performer_workplace
Genre (this exercise): workplace | Primary topic: burnout
Note: reassigned from established genre for full-taxonomy coverage exercise (see genre_assignment_37brands.md)

Vessel: a real office-hierarchy drama. Protagonist Nozomi Arai is a "high performer" on a product team at a mid-size logistics-software company — the kind of employee managers point to in all-hands meetings. Cast follows `workplace_drama.md` §5 exactly: a boss (director-level, institutionally captured, not a villain), a peer (same level, externalizing coping style — takes long lunches, says the things Nozomi only thinks), and an outside friend (works in an unrelated field, provides the "why are you doing this to yourself" frame).

**Arc 1 (ep_001-012):** Junior-register open (per bible §7 Arc I, compressed): Nozomi is the newest high performer on the team, absorbing unspoken rules — who gets credit in meetings, whose calendar is untouchable. Project arc: a rushed product launch. Interpersonal arc: the peer needles Nozomi for taking on other people's overflow work "because it's easier than saying no." Arc closes on Nozomi training a more junior hire and hearing herself repeat a phrase her boss used on her — first flicker of self-recognition, no epiphany yet.
**Arc 2 (ep_013-024):** Competence is now assumed; costs become visible. Project arc: a headcount freeze forces Nozomi to absorb a second role without a second salary. Interpersonal arc: the boss shows a rare unguarded moment (mentions a project they gave up on, years ago) that Nozomi clocks but doesn't yet connect to her own trajectory. Escalation from Arc 1: burnout stops being an occasional late night and becomes a structural fact of her calendar — first appearance of `black_fill_intensity: high` spiral panels per bible §8. Arc closes on a missed personal event (the outside friend's birthday) that Nozomi only notices was missed two days later.
**Arc 3 (ep_025-036):** Nozomi is promoted into managing the junior hire from Arc 1, i.e. she now has some of the institutional power her boss has. Main tension (bible §7 Arc III): can she be inside the system without becoming it. Escalation: she catches herself about to hand the junior hire the same overflow work the peer once mocked her for accepting — and for the first time makes a different choice, though it costs her personally (she does the extra work herself rather than delegate it down). Interpersonal arc deepens: the peer, exhausted by their externalizing coping style, has a visible crack — the peer is not a stable foil, they are also being ground down, just louder about it. Reorg announcement mid-arc functions as the one legitimate deadline cliffhanger per bible §3.
**Arc 4 (ep_037-048):** Nozomi becomes something like a mentor figure, seeing her own institutional capture with distance for the first time (bible §7 Arc IV). No system reform, no boss removed, no "high performer" label ever renounced in dialogue — per bible §6 failure mode 7, resolution never comes through the institution changing. Escalation: final project arc is the largest yet (a company-wide reorg Nozomi partly has to implement), but the interpersonal arc resolves first and smaller — a genuine, unforced conversation with the outside friend where Nozomi describes her job accurately for the first time, out loud, and it sounds absurd, and she laughs. Final episode lands (not cliffhangers, per bible §3) on Nozomi's relationship to her own continuation at the company — undecided, but seen clearly. No named brand teacher, no stated cure; acceptance_layer stays authored_candidate.

## executive_calm_workplace
Genre (this exercise): essay | Primary topic: burnout
Note: reassigned from established genre for full-taxonomy coverage exercise (see genre_assignment_37brands.md)

Vessel: an artifact/confessional register distinct from high_performer_workplace's office-hierarchy drama — per `essay`'s `self_or_artifact_interaction` quality gate, this series is built around a single recurring physical/digital object the protagonist reviews like a diary, not around office scenes with a boss/peer/friend cast. Protagonist: Daniel Osei, a director-level executive whose calm, controlled public persona is the entire premise — and the artifact is his "zero-white-space calendar," a shared work calendar with no visible gaps, which he starts privately reviewing week by week the way someone reads old journal entries, per `modern_reader_story_doctrine.yaml`'s essay catalysts (`search_history_after_midnight`, `voice_memo_not_sent` — same register, different artifact). Secondary artifacts recur across arcs (draft emails never sent, a voice memo to his own past self, receipts from meals eaten alone at his desk) but the calendar is the spine artifact, per the doctrine's `avoid: [advice_blog_voice, no_visual_anchor, confession_without_revision, generic_memoir]` — every review of the calendar must produce a visual anchor (a specific week, a specific meeting block) and a revision of his own account of that week, not a static confession.

**Arc 1 (ep_001-012):** Daniel begins reviewing the past year of his calendar, ostensibly to prep a year-end report, and notices for the first time that weeks he remembers as "normal" show zero unscheduled blocks. Essay-register beat pattern: each episode opens on a calendar week as artifact, the caption layer narrates what he told himself that week was true, and closes on the gap between the two. No second character carries the plot — his assistant appears only as a voice on the phone, never fully in-panel, keeping the self_or_artifact_interaction gate central. Arc closes on Daniel drafting (not sending) an email to himself titled "what I actually did in March."
**Arc 2 (ep_013-024):** Escalation: Daniel starts annotating the calendar retroactively — small marks on weeks that "cost" something (a canceled call with his kid, a rescheduled doctor's appointment absorbed into a client dinner). The artifact register intensifies: he begins keeping the unsent voice memos as a second archive, listening back to ones from months prior and hearing a calmness in his own voice that he now recognizes as performance, not truth. No advice-blog voice — the revision is visual and specific (a particular memo, a particular date), never a stated lesson. Arc closes on him realizing an entire quarter of the calendar has no entry that isn't work — a first flicker of alarm rendered only through his handling of the physical artifact (calendar app open at 2am), never through narration stating "he was burned out."
**Arc 3 (ep_025-036):** Daniel begins editing the calendar going forward, not just reviewing it backward — testing whether he can leave a visible gap without a client noticing or a peer executive commenting. Escalation: the gate stays self_or_artifact_interaction even as the stakes rise — a major client relationship strains not because Daniel confronts anyone, but because he stops instantly answering messages within his usual response window, and the artifact (his message-response-time log, a second recurring object) becomes the site of the tension. Arc closes on the first week in over a year with two consecutive unscheduled evenings — and Daniel doesn't know what to do with them, rendered as a genuinely blank panel, not a triumphant one.
**Arc 4 (ep_037-048):** Escalation completes: Daniel's calendar review becomes an active practice rather than an accidental discovery — but it is never framed as a system, a method, or anything a reader could copy verbatim (no claimed cure). Final arc's artifact is the accumulated stack of unsent voice memos, which Daniel finally sends — one, to his kid, near the end, still imperfect, still unresolved about work. No brand teacher named in-panel; no calm-persona "fix." Final episode lands on a calendar week that has gaps in it and a caption that does not explain them. acceptance_layer stays authored_candidate.

## stabilizer_healing
Genre (this exercise): battle_internal | Primary topic: burnout
Note: reassigned from established genre for full-taxonomy coverage exercise (see genre_assignment_37brands.md)

Vessel: a purely interior, largely solitary battle-with-self register — distinct from both siblings above. Per `battle_internal`'s `self_or_memory_interaction` gate and `main_character_interaction_grammar.yaml` engine defaults (`conviction, philosophical_provocation, reflection`; visual grammars `sparse_realist, kinetic_internal`), this series has almost no external antagonist and minimal dialogue-driven cast — the "battle" is staged entirely through a recurring liminal vessel and externalized through object/body, never narrated directly. Protagonist: Iris Vance, a formerly high-functioning caregiver/first-responder-adjacent worker (a hospital-adjacent role — a triage coordinator, never named as a self-help archetype) who has stopped working shifts. The title's suggested vessel — "the bench between shifts" — becomes literal and recurring: a specific outdoor bench outside her workplace where she used to sit for the ten minutes between shift handoffs, which she now returns to alone, off-shift, as the site where memory-versions of herself confront her.

**Arc 1 (ep_001-012):** Iris sits on the bench for the first time in months, ostensibly just passing by. The "battle" is staged as a memory-self — an earlier, still-working version of Iris — who appears on the bench beside her in flashback-adjacent panels (sparse_realist, minimal backgrounds, per the visual grammar default) and needles her, not verbally in dialogue bubbles but through posture and mirrored gesture: memory-Iris sits upright, present-Iris slumps, and the panel geometry does the arguing. No mentor, no narrator states what this means. Arc closes with Iris leaving the bench before finishing whatever she came to do — a retreat, not a resolution.
**Arc 2 (ep_013-024):** Escalation: Iris starts returning to the bench deliberately, and the memory-self confrontations intensify — now multiple versions of her appear across different remembered shifts (a rookie-Iris, a burned-out-Iris from a specific bad week), rendered per `kinetic_internal` grammar as overlapping fractured panels rather than clean sequential ones. The body is the battlefield: hands that won't stop checking an absent pager, a uniform jacket she still owns but hasn't worn, kept folded in a way that mirrors how she used to fold others' discharge paperwork. Object externalization carries all the weight the doctrine forbids narrating directly. Arc closes on Iris finally staying on the bench until her old shift-change time passes, for the first time.
**Arc 3 (ep_025-036):** The internal battle sharpens into something closer to `conviction` and `philosophical_provocation` (per the engine defaults) — the memory-selves stop being adversarial and start asking Iris a genuine question she has no answer for yet: which version of herself she is trying to get back to, and whether that version was ever sustainable. Escalation: Iris brings an object to the bench for the first time — the folded jacket — and sits with it rather than avoiding it, rendered through hands and posture only, still no direct narration of "healing." A single wordless multi-panel sequence (bible-adjacent silent-panel convention, borrowed by analogy since battle_internal has no dedicated bible) marks the arc's emotional peak. Arc closes with Iris putting the jacket back, unworn, but placed somewhere different than before.
**Arc 4 (ep_037-048):** Escalation completes without resolution-as-cure: the bench stops being solely a site of confrontation and becomes, ambiguously, just a bench again — Iris sits there and nothing happens, no memory-self appears, which is itself unsettling to her in a new way (an absence she has to sit with). The internal battle's final register shift is toward `reflection` (engine default) rather than conviction — no vow, no named recovery, no brand teacher, no claimed cure. Final episode lands on Iris on the bench at the old shift-change time, present-tense, alone, doing nothing in particular — the "battle" ends not with a winner but with the bench no longer requiring a battle to sit on it. acceptance_layer stays authored_candidate.

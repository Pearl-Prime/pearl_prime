Demographic: shojo | Primary topic: anxiety | Genre: romance_josei_drama

**Arc 1 — Proximity (ep_001-012):** Mina Cho is drafted onto the AV/broadcast committee's two-person "bell desk" — the old sound booth above the gym that manually cues the school's non-digital period bell and PA chimes during the automated system's slow replacement — paired with Jun Osei, the composed transfer student nobody can rattle. Every shared shift puts her hand an inch from the same brass switch that, for her, has always meant *time is up, perform now*; his easy indifference to the switch is the first crack in that meaning. Small proximity gains happen entirely through bell-desk logistics — trading shifts, syncing watches, arguing over which chime tone to use for fire drills — never through declared feeling.

**Arc 2 — Rupture (ep_013-024):** During the all-school assembly, Mina is cued to press the ceremonial bell live in front of the auditorium and freezes on the switch for four full seconds of dead air — and when Jun reaches over and presses it for her without asking, she reads it not as help but as proof he already knew she'd fail, and stops showing up to shifts. The rupture sharpens when a botched afternoon fire-drill bell (rung a period early, on a schedule she'd signed off on) gets blamed on the bell desk publicly, and Jun, trying to shield her, tells the faculty advisor it was entirely his error — a lie she overhears and experiences as him deciding she's too fragile to own her own mistake.

**Arc 3 — Repair (ep_025-036):** Repair starts with an object, not a speech: Mina retrieves the sign-out sheet from the fire-drill day, corrects it in her own handwriting to name her actual scheduling error, and hands it to the faculty advisor herself before finding Jun to return the master bell-desk key he'd quietly taken back after the incident. He answers by telling her about the stutter that got him held back from student council at his old school — his own reason for going quiet under pressure — and hands the key back rather than keeping the desk running without her. They rebuild the desk's shift rotation together, and for the first time she is the one who presses the switch for a routine class-change bell while he stands back and lets her.

**Arc 4 — Commitment Ladder (ep_037-048):** The stakes personalize and scale: the district selects their school to pilot the new automated bell system, and Mina — the only student who has run both the old manual desk and understands its blind spots — is asked to co-present the changeover plan to the school board, with Jun as her co-presenter rather than her buffer. A late-arc setback (a live board-meeting technical failure that forces her to explain the system, unscripted, to adults staring at her) tests whether the growth holds under bigger pressure than any classroom bell ever was, and it does — imperfectly, voice shaking but sentences finished. The series closes the arc on Mina volunteering, unprompted, to ring the final dismissal bell of the school year by hand one last time before the old system is decommissioned, Jun beside her not steadying her hand but simply present — a chosen, incomplete confidence, not a cure. acceptance_layer: authored_candidate.

## confidence_core_romance

Demographic: shojo | Primary topic: imposter_syndrome | Genre: romance_josei_drama

**Arc 1 — Proximity (ep_001-012):** Celeste Rae is cast opposite Theo Marchetti in the drama program's spring production and inherits the shared dressing-room mirror rumored among students to "borrow" whoever's face you study hardest in it — a warped, silvering antique nobody will replace because half the department swears by it before auditions. She's taped a photo of the program's legendary former lead behind its frame and does her stage makeup to match it exactly, believing her own face never got her cast in anything; Theo, who reapplies his makeup badly and doesn't care, is the first person who keeps talking to her mid-transformation instead of waiting for the finished mask. Proximity builds through shared call times, prop runs, and the unglamorous logistics of two people getting ready in the same small room.

**Arc 2 — Rupture (ep_013-024):** During a technical rehearsal, the photo taped behind the mirror falls loose in front of the director and two castmates, and it becomes public that Celeste has been recreating another actress's exact look for months rather than developing her own — read by the cast as either strange or as an admission she doesn't believe her own face belongs on that stage. Theo, stung that even alone with him she never once dropped the borrowed look, says the line that breaks it open: "I don't think I've ever actually seen you." She pulls out of rehearsals for a week, and the rupture crests when the director nearly recasts her role to the actress in the photo.

**Arc 3 — Repair (ep_025-036):** Repair happens at the mirror, not in a hallway confession: Celeste returns to the empty dressing room, peels the taped photo off the glass, and — with Theo watching from the doorway, having followed her without being asked — does her stage makeup for the first time using only her own face as reference, badly at first, correcting it in real time while he says nothing and just holds the compact steady when her hands shake. The concrete repair gesture is her taping a new photo behind the mirror: not the old lead, but a photo Theo takes of her, undone makeup and all, that afternoon. She returns to rehearsal in the look she built herself.

**Arc 4 — Commitment Ladder (ep_037-048):** Stakes personalize and escalate: Celeste's mother, a former stage actress who never made it past regional theater, pushes her to audition for a citywide showcase using "a look that photographs like a lead" — a request to borrow a face again, now from family rather than legend. The arc's hardest beat is Celeste auditioning under her own unaltered look in front of her mother in the room, with Theo present not performing alongside her this time but simply in the audience, unable to fix the outcome for her. She doesn't win the showcase's top slot — she places, visibly her own work, and chooses to keep the mirror in the dressing room with no photo behind it at all, a running practice rather than a finished cure. acceptance_layer: authored_candidate.

## creative_unfold_social
Existing series: `creative_unfold_social__indie_cross__en_US__social_anxiety__the_studio_that_echoes_back` (REUSE_EXISTING — arc plan feeds authoring into this existing series identity, do not propose a new one)
Demographic: shojo | Primary topic: social_anxiety | Genre: romance_josei_drama

**Arc 1 — Proximity (ep_001-012):** Indie Cross takes a work-study shift at a converted water-tower community art studio whose cylindrical shape gives its top-floor recording nook a strange, generous echo — the one place in the building where a quiet voice comes back sounding fuller than it went in. She's assigned to share the shift with Sable Marchetti, the studio's easygoing sound-tech volunteer who runs the open-mic sign-up sheet Indie has never once put her name on. Proximity builds through studio-keeping tasks — patching cables, resetting the echo booth after each session, the two of them alone in the tower after closing — where Indie starts humming half-finished song fragments into the booth only because she believes no one but the room is listening.

**Arc 2 — Rupture (ep_013-024):** Sable, trying to get Indie onto the open-mic roster without pressuring her directly, plays a recording of one of those private echo-booth fragments for the studio's small regular crowd during a slow open-mic night — meant as a nudge, landing as a violation. Indie finds out mid-song, from someone else's compliment, and the rupture is immediate and specific: she stops using the booth entirely, hands back her key, and picks up a different shift on the opposite end of the building so their schedules never overlap. Sable's attempts to apologize keep happening in front of other studio regulars, which only confirms Indie's worst read — that anything she makes there is now public property.

**Arc 3 — Repair (ep_025-036):** Repair is a rebuilt object, not an apology speech: Sable spends a week re-soundproofing the echo booth's door on their own time so it can be locked from the inside during use — a physical, checkable guarantee rather than a promise — and leaves the new key with the front desk under Indie's name, no note attached. Indie tests it alone first, then invites Sable to sit outside the locked door, not inside it, while she records a song she chooses to let them hear afterward on her terms. The concrete turn is Indie handing over the recording herself, unprompted, instead of it being taken.

**Arc 4 — Commitment Ladder (ep_037-048):** Stakes personalize and scale: the studio's annual fundraising showcase — the thing that decides whether the water-tower space stays open at all — needs a closing performer, and the outgoing director asks Indie by name after hearing the booth recording Sable never should have shared but that turned out to be good. The arc's hardest beat is Indie performing live in the tower's ground-floor hall in front of a full paying crowd, Sable running sound from the booth above rather than standing beside her, so the choice to be heard is entirely hers to make or lose in the moment. She freezes for a beat, restarts the song herself without being rescued, and finishes it — imperfect, audibly nervous, unmistakably chosen — and the series closes the arc on the studio's lease being renewed, not on the fear being gone. acceptance_layer: authored_candidate.

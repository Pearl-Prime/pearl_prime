Chapter 1

You watched a colleague stay in the Slack thread until midnight and felt the pressure redistribute. If they can work that late, your refusal to becomes a choice, not a limit. Your shoulders feel the comparison immediately.

The Slack workspace is on a different phone now. Your WFH laptop stays on your actual desk and nowhere else. the avenue outside and morning sun cutting through the blinds are the only things you're noticing at 6:02pm. You turn and face the window.

The coworking space desk is cold under your wrists. Three slack channels are pinging in sequence. the avenue outside coffee shop below is closing soon. You gather your things and walk without checking any messages.

Your phone buzzes on the WFH desk. A Slack notification with your manager's name glows at 9pm. The cursor blinks over his message asking for the report. the avenue outside traffic hums distantly through your window. You press and hold the notification without opening it.

Before you keep reading, use your full lungs. Most breathing uses only the top third. This practice opens all three chambers.

This is three-part breath or Dirga Pranayama. You breathe into three zones in sequence: belly, ribs, upper chest. Then exhale in reverse. Most people use only the upper third of their lung capacity during normal breathing. This practice opens the full volume and teaches your body what complete breathing feels like.

Breathe into your belly first. Feel it expand. Then let the breath rise into your ribcage. Feel it expand sideways. Then let it fill your upper chest. Feel the collarbones lift slightly. Exhale in reverse: upper chest drops, ribs contract, belly falls. That is one three-part breath. Repeat for six cycles. You are using your full lung capacity for possibly the first time today.

Now, I want you to notice how much larger your breath feels compared to before. You did not gain lung capacity. You accessed capacity that was already there but unused. The parts of your lungs that were collapsed are now open. That is why you may feel a subtle shift in energy or clarity.

Now, as you return, one three-part breath before a stressful moment opens your lungs and changes the quality of every breath that follows for the next few minutes. The first full breath teaches the others.

Chapter 1

You have optimized everything you were told to optimize. The feeling you were optimizing toward has not arrived.

The inbox loads. Forty-two. You scroll without opening. Subject lines move past. morning sun cutting through the blinds shifts the light on your desk. Your desk lamp flickers once. You scroll to the bottom. Then back up. The number has not changed. the avenue outside is quiet at this hour. Your coffee is cold beside the keyboard. You scroll again.

You open the calendar. Tomorrow is full from nine. morning sun cutting through the blinds on your window. You scroll forward to Friday. Friday has three gaps. Each gap is thirty minutes. You hover over one. The tooltip shows it is blocked for focus time — you blocked it yourself, two weeks ago. the avenue outside is quiet for a moment. You close the calendar. The document is still open.

Someone stops at your desk. You take out one earbud. They ask a question. Your screen is behind you — open, visible. morning sun cutting through the blinds through the office window above your monitor. You answer. They nod and walk away. You put the earbud back. the avenue outside is there below. Your screen is still open behind you. You turn back to it.

Where in your life do you run from what is difficult? Can you name one place where
you use busy-ness, distraction, or sleep to not feel something? Not to change it,
but to see it. What would happen if you stopped running for just five breaths?

The body keeps the score, but it also keeps the remedy. Stillness is not passive. It is the most active form of listening.

What are you attached to that you pretend you are not? Look at it without judgment.
Why do you grip it? Is the thing itself the problem, or the story you built around
having it? Can you hold it lightly and still have it? What happens then?

Notice a thought that repeats itself in your mind. Follow it back. Who told you
this was true? How long have you carried it? If you set it down right now, what
would be different? Not forever. Just for today. What changes when the story stops?

Take one situation that usually triggers you. Not the biggest one. A medium one.
Next time it happens, pause for three breaths before responding. During those breaths,
notice what you feel. Do not try to change it. Just notice. This noticing is the
entire practice. Do this one time. See what happens.

Sit quietly for five minutes. Do nothing. Fix nothing. Change nothing. When your
mind pulls away, bring attention back. This is the entire practice. Five minutes.
One time this week. That is your integration. The rest of the week, continue as
you normally do. Notice if anything shifts.

Before you keep reading, use your full lungs. Most breathing uses only the top third. This practice opens all three chambers.

This is three-part breath or Dirga Pranayama. You breathe into three zones in sequence: belly, ribs, upper chest. Then exhale in reverse. Most people use only the upper third of their lung capacity during normal breathing. This practice opens the full volume and teaches your body what complete breathing feels like.

Breathe into your belly first. Feel it expand. Then let the breath rise into your ribcage. Feel it expand sideways. Then let it fill your upper chest. Feel the collarbones lift slightly. Exhale in reverse: upper chest drops, ribs contract, belly falls. That is one three-part breath. Repeat for six cycles. You are using your full lung capacity for possibly the first time today.

Now, I want you to notice how much larger your breath feels compared to before. You did not gain lung capacity. You accessed capacity that was already there but unused. The parts of your lungs that were collapsed are now open. That is why you may feel a subtle shift in energy or clarity.

Now, as you return, one three-part breath before a stressful moment opens your lungs and changes the quality of every breath that follows for the next few minutes. The first full breath teaches the others.

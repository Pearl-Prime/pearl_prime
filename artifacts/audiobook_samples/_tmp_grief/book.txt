Chapter 1

The all-hands announced the departures and there's a new gap in the org chart. Your hands know these gaps used to be people you worked with. Everyone moved on and the space they left is still here.

The coffee shop owner asked about your colleague. They came in together sometimes. Your mouth can't form the explanation. morning sun cutting through the blinds morning is when you usually saw them here. You're here alone now. You don't explain anything.

Your Slack DMs are full of people asking if you're okay. The person who usually asked if you're okay is gone. Your coffee shop table is too quiet. the avenue outside outside has less familiar faces now. You reply to everyone that you're fine.

Your coworker who sat across from you got cut in the last round. The open office desk is being cleared right now. the bus brings rush hour but not them. Your hands rest on your own desk and don't move toward theirs. It's not your desk to take.

Here's what's happening.

Every time you say that sentence—
*"Once I'm past this, then I'll be okay"*—

You're training yourself to treat the grief like a gate.

You're saying:
*"This feeling means I can't. This feeling means I'm not ready. This feeling means I should wait."*

And something inside you listens.

Because that's how it works. You learn.

So the next time you think about going somewhere, or saying something real, or letting yourself be seen—

Something inside says:
*"Wait. You're still grieving. That must mean it's not time yet."*

And you feel it again.

That heaviness. That tightness. That pull to wait.

Not because the thing itself is wrong.

But because you've been teaching yourself that *the grief* means stop.

And here's the hardest part—

The more you wait, the smaller your world gets.

The places you don't go. The conversations you don't have. The memories you don't speak out loud. The version of yourself you don't let exist.

All of it shrinks.

And something inside notices.

It says:
*"Okay, so we're not doing that anymore. Got it. That must be too hard."*

So the next time feels harder.

The next thing feels bigger.

The next moment of connection feels further away.

Not because you're getting worse.

Not because the grief is winning.

But because you've been living by a sentence that sounds like patience—

But works like a cage.

Here is the belief that has been running your life: grief first, then living.

It sounds reasonable. It sounds patient. But watch what it actually produces: a life on pause.

The correction: grief and living happen together. There is no "after grief." There is only "with grief."

The grief is not a sign that you should wait. It is not a warning that you're not ready. It is the natural consequence of having loved something that changed.

When you treat grief as a gate, you give it power it doesn't actually have. The gate is a fiction. The waiting is the real problem.

You don't need to be past the grief. You need to learn to move while it's still there.

Here's the thing no one tells you about grief:

The struggle to get past it is often worse than the grief itself.

The grief is heavy, yes. The grief is real.

But the constant battle—the *"I shouldn't feel this way still"* and the *"when will this end"* and the *"what's wrong with me that I'm still carrying this"*—

That's what makes it unbearable.

The grief is weight.

The struggle is fighting against gravity.

What if you didn't have to fight?

What if the grief could just be there—heavy, present, real—

Without the additional weight of trying to make it go away?

Here's what this book is about:

**Learning to live while the grief is still there.**

Not getting over it. Not getting through it. Not waiting for it to pass.

Just... living alongside it.

Because here's what I've learned:

The grief doesn't have to go away for you to have a life.

It can stay.

And you can still show up. Still connect. Still be present in the world that keeps existing.

Not by pretending you're fine.

Not by pushing through.

But by learning a different relationship with what you're carrying.

That's what we're going to do together.

Not fix the grief.

Not rush the grief.

Just learn to carry it—while also living.

This book won't try to fix your grief.

It won't give you stages to complete or timelines to follow.

It won't tell you what the loss should mean or how you should feel about it by now.

Instead, it will offer something simpler:

**A way to live while the grief is still there.**

Practical. Grounded. Real.

Not about becoming someone who doesn't grieve.

About becoming someone who grieves and lives anyway.

You don't have to be past it.

You just have to learn to carry it while you move.

So here's the invitation:

Stop waiting for the grief to pass.

Not because you should. Not because it's wrong to grieve.

But because the waiting is costing you the very thing you're waiting for: your life.

The grief can stay.

And you can still live.

That's what we're going to learn together.

Starting now.

Chapter 2

The position is being filled by someone external and your jaw tightens. The person who knew us and our work is being replaced. This is how organizations treat people who built them.

Your WFH desk work hasn't changed but there's more of it. The person who left was doing what now falls to you. rain streaking the glass evening comes and the task list doesn't shrink. Your manager hasn't mentioned extra pay. You don't ask.

Let's be clear about what that strategy did for you:

**It kept you safe.**

In those early days, weeks, months — the waiting protected something that needed protecting.

It gave you space to fall apart in private.
It prevented you from making decisions you weren't ready to make.
It let the worst of the acute grief move through without an audience.

The strategy worked.

Not perfectly. Not forever.

But in that season, it was exactly what you needed.

Here's what I want you to understand:

The waiting wasn't weakness. It was intelligence.

Your system recognized a threat — the threat of falling apart, of being seen in pieces, of making it all worse — and it found a solution.

Wait. Protect. Survive.

That's not failure. That's adaptation.

Here's what changed:

The conditions shifted. But the strategy didn't.

Early on, you needed protection. The grief was acute. The risk of falling apart was high. Waiting made sense.

But time passed.

The acute phase ended. The grief changed shape — still present, but different. More like weather than like drowning.

And yet the sentence kept running:

*"Once I'm past this, then I'll be okay."*

As if the threat was still the same.

As if you were still in those first weeks.

As if waiting was still the smart move.

The problem isn't that you built the shield.

The problem is that the shield became permanent.

What was once protection is now prevention.

Here's what protection became:

Prevention.

The same walls that kept you safe early on are now keeping you isolated.

The waiting that protected you from falling apart is now keeping you from showing up.

The sentence that helped you survive is now costing you your life.

Not all at once. Gradually.

Each time you didn't go, the not-going got easier.
Each time you didn't say the real thing, the silence got more natural.
Each time you waited, the waiting became default.

And now you're here.

Still waiting.

For a readiness that isn't coming.

For a version of yourself that might not exist.

For a day when the grief finally lifts — even though the grief may have already changed into something you could carry.

Here's the trade you've been making without realizing it:

Visibility for invisibility.

Connection for protection.

Living for waiting.

Every time you don't go, you stay safe — but also unseen.

Every time you don't say the real thing, you avoid vulnerability — but also connection.

Every time you wait, you protect yourself from the grief — but also from your life.

The trade made sense once.

Does it still?

Here's the distinction that changes everything:

**Danger vs. discomfort.**

Early in grief, acting while the loss was fresh could be genuinely dangerous. Bad decisions. Things said that couldn't be unsaid. Falling apart in situations that couldn't hold you.

That was real danger.

But now?

Now, most of the time, it's just discomfort.

The heaviness is uncomfortable, not dangerous.
The tears might come, which is uncomfortable — but not dangerous.
The grief might show, which feels vulnerable — but vulnerability isn't danger.

Your system learned to treat grief as a stop sign.

But grief isn't a stop sign. It's just weather.

You can move in weather.

Here's what you need to see:

The waiting has become autopilot.

You don't consciously decide to wait anymore. The sentence just runs — automatically, invisibly, beneath everything.

*"Once I'm past this, then I'll be okay."*

It feels like truth. It feels like the only option.

But it's not.

It's a strategy you learned. A habit you built. A pattern that runs because it's been running for so long.

And habits can change.

Patterns can shift.

The autopilot can be turned off.

You don't have to keep waiting.

Not because the grief should be gone by now.

But because waiting isn't the only way to carry it.

Here's the question you're not ready to answer yet:

**What if the waiting is costing you more than the grief?**

The grief is real. The grief is heavy. The grief deserves space.

But the waiting?

The waiting is shrinking your world.
The waiting is keeping you isolated.
The waiting is costing you the very life you're waiting to get back to.

You don't have to answer that question now.

Just let it sit.

And notice if something in you already knows the answer.

Chapter 3

The job that felt like home is now just a job because the person who made it home is gone. Your chest is hollow. You're in the same seat but you're somewhere different now.

The all-hands mentioned "ensuring continuity" after the restructuring. Your coworking space desk holds the projects they started. fluorescent light humming overhead afternoon is when you realize nobody will finish them quite the same way. the commuter rail below brings replacements who won't fill the space. You don't waste time training them on this person's history.

Here's what the sentence is really saying about you:

*"The grieving version of me isn't capable."*

*"The grieving version of me can't handle things."*

*"The grieving version of me shouldn't be seen."*

Every time you wait, you're reinforcing this belief.

You're saying: *"Who I am right now — carrying this weight — isn't enough."*

But what if you're wrong?

What if the grieving version of you is exactly as capable as any other version?

Just heavier. Just carrying more.

But still capable.

Here's the myth you've been believing:

*"Grief is something that passes. And once it passes, I'll be able to live again."*

That's the story. That's the assumption underneath the waiting.

But here's what actually happens:

Grief doesn't pass. It changes.

The acute phase ends. The constant ache becomes intermittent. The drowning becomes something more like weather.

But the absence is still there. The weight is still present.

Just... different.

So if you're waiting for the grief to be gone before you live —

You might be waiting for something that doesn't happen.

Not because you're doing it wrong.

But because grief doesn't work like that.

Here's the loop you're stuck in:

1. You feel the grief.
2. You tell yourself: "I can't do this while I'm feeling this way."
3. You wait.
4. While you wait, you feel more isolated.
5. The isolation makes the grief heavier.
6. The heavier grief makes you want to wait more.

And around. And around. And around.

The loop isn't obvious because each step feels logical.

*"Of course I can't show up when I'm this heavy."*

*"Of course the heaviness gets worse when I'm alone."*

*"Of course being alone makes me want to wait until I'm lighter."*

But the loop is the trap.

And waiting is the door that keeps it closed.

There's an invisible rule running underneath everything:

*"You can't live while you're grieving."*

You didn't write this rule. No one taught it to you explicitly.

But somewhere, you absorbed it.

Maybe from the way people talk about grief — as something to "get through" before returning to life.

Maybe from the expectation that grieving people should stay home, stay quiet, stay out of the way.

Maybe from your own fear of falling apart in front of others.

Whatever the source, the rule is running.

*"First grieve. Then live."*

But what if that rule is wrong?

What if grieving and living aren't sequential?

What if they happen at the same time, in the same person, on the same day?

Here's what the waiting is actually costing you:

**You're erasing yourself.**

Not on purpose. Not dramatically.

Just... slowly.

Every time you don't show up, you become a little more invisible.

Every time you wait, your world gets a little smaller.

Every time you tell yourself "not yet," you reinforce the belief that who you are right now — grieving — isn't allowed to exist.

The grief isn't erasing you.

The waiting is.

The grief is just weight. Heavy, but carryable.

The waiting is what's shrinking your life.

Here's the question you can't avoid:

**What if the person carrying the grief is also the person who gets to live?**

Not a future version. Not a "healed" version. Not a version that somehow got past it.

Just you. Right now. Carrying what you're carrying.

That question might feel impossible.

But sit with it.

Because if the answer is yes — if the grieving you is also the living you —

Then the waiting can stop.

And something different can begin.

The role that felt like identity was restructured away and someone became unemployed. Your shoulders carry the weight of knowing them. You're still here and they're not and the unfairness is visible every morning.

The company Slack has a goodbye message no one asked for. The laying off happened on a late afternoon light slanting across the desk morning. Your coffee shop table is where you're reading about it. traffic moving on the highway below has fewer people walking to the office. You didn't know it would feel this way.

Here's what the grief actually is:

**Information.**

Not a stop sign. Not a verdict. Not proof that you're not ready.

Just information.

It says: *"Something you loved is missing. Something changed. There's weight here."*

That's it.

It doesn't say: *"You can't function."*

It doesn't say: *"Stay home."*

It doesn't say: *"Wait until I'm gone."*

Those are interpretations you've added.

The grief itself is just weight. Just information. Just the body's way of holding what the mind hasn't figured out yet.

You can carry information while you move.

You can hold weight while you act.

The grief is not a gate. It's just what you're carrying.

Here's why trying to fix the grief makes it worse:

When you treat grief like a problem to solve, you add a second layer.

Layer 1: The grief itself. Heavy, but carryable.

Layer 2: The judgment about the grief. *"I shouldn't still feel this way. Something is wrong. I need to fix this."*

The first layer is natural.

The second layer is what makes it unbearable.

Think about what happens when you try to "process" the grief:

You check: *"Am I better yet?"*

You notice you're not better.

You feel worse about not being better.

The grief stays. Plus now there's frustration. Plus now there's self-doubt.

The fixing adds weight. It doesn't remove it.

Here's the shift:

**Acting while the grief is still there.**

Not waiting for it to lift.

Not pushing through in spite of it.

Not pretending it's not there.

Just... acting alongside it.

The grief is here. And you're going to do the thing anyway.

Not because you're strong. Not because you're brave.

Because waiting hasn't been working.

Because the grief isn't going anywhere soon.

Because your life is still happening, even while you're carrying this.

This isn't about ignoring the grief.

It's about refusing to let the grief decide when you get to live.

What season of life are you in? Spring with new beginnings? Summer with full bloom and activity? Autumn with release and letting go? Winter with rest and restoration? Each season has its purpose. You can't force spring in winter. Can you accept the season you're in and honor what it requires? The seasons always change. This one will too.

Now, I want you to notice something. Notice what you learned about yourself. That is useful information. Whatever happened — or did not happen — is exactly right.

Now, before you move on, there is more where that came from. Nothing has to change from this moment forward. Keep going.

If you just tried the breathing practice, here's what you proved:

**You can do something while the grief is still present.**

The grief didn't stop you from breathing.

The grief didn't stop you from reading this.

The grief doesn't actually stop you from most things.

The waiting is a habit. A belief. A pattern.

But it's not the truth.

The truth is: you can act while carrying this weight.

You just did.

Chapter 5

Your chest doesn't know how to grieve a coworker publicly. They're not family but they were your five days a week. The loss is real and you're not entitled to process it.

The email about severance packages is buried in the all-hands thread. You're calculating what they might get. Your coffee shop table is where this math happens secretly. the crosswalk at the corner outside shows people who still have jobs. You have a job and it feels precarious now. You don't celebrate it.

Here's what you're actually losing while you wait:

**Time.** Days and weeks and months that don't come back.

**Connection.** Relationships that fade when you're not present for them.

**Momentum.** The ease of doing things that comes from doing them regularly.

**Identity.** The sense of who you are that comes from participating in life.

The grief takes something from you. That's true.

But the waiting takes even more.

The grief is unavoidable.

The waiting is a choice.

There's a version of you that people don't get to see.

Not the "healed" version — the you who's carrying the grief and still showing up.

That version exists. You've been that person before.

But every time you wait, you hide that version away.

You decide: *"They can't see me like this."*

But "like this" is who you are right now.

And if you keep hiding, no one gets to know who you've become.

The world doesn't need the "past it" version of you.

The world can hold the grieving version too.

But only if you let yourself be seen.

Let's be honest about the trade you're making:

You're trading presence for protection.

Connection for comfort.

Life for safety.

And maybe that trade made sense early on.

When the grief was acute. When falling apart was a real risk. When the world felt too sharp.

But now?

Now the protection has become a prison.

Now the comfort is actually isolation.

Now the safety is costing you the very life you're trying to protect.

The trade doesn't make sense anymore.

But you're still making it.

Here's the hardest truth:

**Your life is happening whether you participate or not.**

Days are passing. Seasons are changing. People are moving through their lives.

And you're on the sidelines. Waiting.

For a readiness that might never come.

For a version of yourself that might not exist.

For permission that nobody else can give you.

The life doesn't stop because you're grieving.

It just happens without you.

And then one day you look up and realize: I missed it.

Not the grief — the grief was always there.

The life. I missed the life.

So here's the shift that has to happen:

**From "waiting until I'm ready" to "acting while I'm carrying this."**

Not because the grief should be gone by now.

Not because you're being weak or dramatic.

But because the waiting is costing more than the acting ever could.

The grief can stay.

And you can still show up.

That's not ignoring it. That's not pushing through.

That's just refusing to let the grief make all your decisions.

You're still the one in charge of your life.

Even while grieving.

Especially while grieving.

Chapter 6

The person who got you through your first job left yesterday. Your shoulders carry the loss of that specific connection. Someone who believed in you decided this place wasn't worth staying in.

Your coworker mentioned getting drinks to celebrate the smaller team. Your open office desk suddenly feels too exposed. the street below bar is where they'd want you to go. the train brings people toward normal and you're not ready. You decline and stay home.

Here's why you can't win the war against grief:

**Grief isn't an invader. It's a response.**

It's your body's way of holding the weight of loss.

It's love with nowhere to go.

You can't fight love. You can't defeat it. You can't make it leave by force.

You can only learn to carry it differently.

The war against grief is a war against part of yourself.

And you can't win a war against yourself.

You can only end it.

Here's what happens when you stop fighting:

**The grief doesn't get bigger.**

That's the fear, right? That if you stop fighting, the grief will take over.

But the opposite happens.

When you stop fighting, the grief becomes just grief. Heavy, but carryable.

The extra weight was always the fighting. The resistance. The judgment.

Grief + fighting = unbearable.

Grief alone = heavy, but manageable.

The war was always the problem.

Here's the distinction you need:

**Danger vs. discomfort.**

Carrying grief while you act is uncomfortable.

It's heavy. It's noticeable. It might mean tears come at unexpected moments.

But it's not dangerous.

You won't fall apart beyond repair.

You won't break in a way that can't be held.

You'll just be a person doing a thing while carrying something heavy.

The grief makes you think it's dangerous to act while carrying it.

But the grief is wrong.

It's just uncomfortable. And you can do uncomfortable things.

Here's the new relationship with grief:

**It's here. It stays. And I live anyway.**

Not "it needs to go." Not "I'll wait until it's smaller."

Just: it's here. I see it. I acknowledge it.

And I also get to live.

The grief isn't a visitor that needs to leave.

It's a part of you now. A heavy part. But a part.

And you can carry parts of yourself while still moving through the world.

This is the relationship: coexistence.

Not war. Not waiting. Just... living alongside.

Here's the permission you've been waiting for:

**You can stop fighting.**

You can let the grief be there.

You can carry it without trying to fix it.

You can live while it's still present.

This isn't giving up. This isn't letting the grief win.

This is ending a war that was never winnable.

This is choosing to live alongside what you're carrying.

This is finally letting yourself be a person who grieves and lives at the same time.

You don't need permission from anyone else.

You just needed to know it was possible.

It is.

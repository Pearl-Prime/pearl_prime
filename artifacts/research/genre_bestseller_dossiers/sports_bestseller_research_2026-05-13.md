## 3. Common arc structure

**3-act tournament spine + character-volume rotation.**

1. **Recruitment / formation** (vols 1–3): protagonist joins the club, ensemble cast introduced.
2. **First tournament defeat or qualifier** (vols 3–7): exposes a specific weakness; one rival foregrounded.
3. **Training arc** (vols 7–10): the named weakness is named, drilled, transformed; companion roster's individual arcs intercut.
4. **Reprise tournament** (vols 10+): named weakness is tested again, sometimes succeeds, sometimes the loss is the point.

Long-arc engine = **Companion Roster** (`manga_bestseller_magical_serial_framework_2026-05-12.md` §2.3). Each volume foregrounds a different teammate. The protagonist's through-line is the master arc.

## 4. Visual register

- **Kinetic linework**: speed lines on EVERY action panel; the manga visually *moves*. Slam Dunk + Blue Lock are the gold standard.
- **Anatomical specificity**: hands gripping ball, calf tension, sweat distribution. Sports manga readers are demanding about body language.
- **Panel composition**: tournament arcs use 2-page spreads for climactic shots, 6-panel grid for play-by-play.
- **Color tilt** (cover only): high saturation, single-sport-coded palette (Blue Lock = electric blue; Haikyu = orange).

## 5. Reader demographic

- **Age:** 12–24 primary; 25–40 nostalgia secondary
- **Gender:** ~65% male, ~35% female (Haikyu and figure-skating series shift toward 50/50)
- **Cultural anchor:** identity-through-mastery; "I am what I practice"
- **Therapeutic susceptibility:** HIGH — sports fans are pre-disposed to growth-framing, coach-as-mentor, and the named-weakness-named-strength loop (which is exactly the CBT shape).

## 7. Therapeutic-embed natural landing moments

Reference for the framework doc:

- **Post-defeat locker room** — coach or senior teammate lands the teaching as a debrief, never as a lecture. The reader was just emotionally hit; their guard is down.
- **Solo practice at dawn** — internal monologue where the protagonist names the weakness. Self-talk, not advice.
- **Watching from the bench** — protagonist watches a teammate fail at the same wall; recognition arrives as compassion.
- **The drive home after** — coach in the passenger seat, the radio playing, one line that fixes the whole arc.

## 8. Operator-example: the sports version of the elf-grief pattern

> "The volleyball team's libero blew the receive in the final point. Three days later he can't sleep, his hands shake when he eats. The coach finds him in the gym at 3am drilling the same receive 200 times. The coach doesn't say 'it's okay' — he stands next to him and says 'show me where you flinched.' They drill flinch-mapping for an hour. The libero starts to cry quietly. The coach says, 'good. Now you know where your fear lives. Tomorrow we work on the receive where your fear lives.' That is the entire scene. No therapy word is used. The reader gets: somatic mapping, exposure therapy, naming-the-fear — without the words."

## 3. Common arc structure

**Two engines:**

1. **Anthology / Case-of-the-week** (Junji Ito, Mieruko) — each chapter = one horror event; cumulative dread.
2. **Mystery Box** (Higurashi, Uzumaki) — one cosmic question runs the whole arc; the protagonist circles toward it.

The signature pacing move is the **slow-burn establishing scene**: 4–6 pages of normal life before the wrongness arrives. The reader has to be lulled.

## 4. Visual register

- **Linework**: hyper-detailed when the horror arrives (Ito's signature); deliberately plain in the buildup. Contrast IS the technique.
- **Negative space**: empty panels carry dread; the eye searches for the wrong thing.
- **Faces**: realism + 1-degree-off proportions ("uncanny valley face"). Junji Ito's eyes are 3% too large.
- **Color tilt** (cover): high contrast B&W usually; if color, a single sickly hue (greenish, jaundiced).

## 5. Reader demographic

- **Age:** 16–45
- **Gender:** ~55% female, ~45% male (counterintuitive; horror is one of the more female-balanced genres post-2018)
- **Cultural anchor:** controlled exposure to fear-as-rehearsal; somatic catharsis
- **Therapeutic susceptibility:** EXTREMELY HIGH — horror readers are pre-selected for somatic literacy; they already know fear lives in the body. This is the highest-leverage genre for somatic-resourcing self-help embedding.

## 7. Therapeutic-embed natural landing moments

- **The pre-confrontation breathing scene** — protagonist sits in their car / against the wall, counts breaths. Somatic resourcing.
- **The morning after** — kitchen scene, hands shaking on a coffee cup; co-regulation with a roommate or housemate.
- **The map-room / planning scene** — naming the threat, drawing it, externalizing it. Exposure therapy structure.
- **The aftermath therapy attempt** — protagonist tries to tell a therapist, the therapist disbelieves; the genre's metabolic pattern is the *invalidation* of clinical help, which is what makes the somatic self-help so welcome.
- **The walking-the-perimeter at dawn scene** — checking the house; OCD-adjacent + grounding ritual.

## 8. Operator-example: the horror version of the elf-grief pattern

> "After surviving a haunting in their apartment building, two roommates Mei and Aki move to a small coastal town. Mei is fine. Aki has panic attacks every time the floorboards creak. The town has an old woman, Baba, who runs a tea shop. Aki refuses to talk about what happened. One afternoon Baba notices Aki flinch at a passing seagull. Baba doesn't ask. Baba says: 'When you came in, you held your breath. Now you've been here twenty minutes. Have you noticed you're breathing?' Aki notices. Baba says: 'Your body remembers where it's safe before your mind agrees. Don't argue with your body. Come for tea Tuesdays.' Aki comes Tuesdays. Each Tuesday, Baba teaches a different somatic technique without naming it as therapy — orienting (look around the room, find five blue things), grounding (feet flat, name the floor), pendulation (notice ease, then notice tension, then return). The horror arc never resolves. The somatic recovery does. No therapy word. The reader gets: post-traumatic recovery via Polyvagal-coded interoception."

## 9. Phoenix series concepts

1. **Baba's Tea Shop** — exactly the above; horror-aftermath cozy spin-off.
2. **The Perimeter Walker** — OCD + supernatural; protagonist whose ritual checking is genuinely keeping a ghost at bay.
3. **The Wrong Apartment** — body-horror + chronic illness analog; the body is the haunted house.
4. **Five Blue Things** — anthology, each chapter = one survivor using one somatic technique to escape one supernatural event.
5. **The Therapist Who Believed** — meta-horror; a CBT therapist gets a client with a real ghost.

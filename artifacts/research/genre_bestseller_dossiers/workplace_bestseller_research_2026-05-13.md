## 3. Common arc structure

**Case-of-the-week (CoTW) + Life-stage rhythm.** Each chapter = one workplace incident (a customer, a project, a meeting) with one emotional resolution. The long arc is the protagonist's career rhythm — promotion, transfer, career pivot, retirement.

5-vol opening arc:
1. **Onboarding** (vol 1): new job; reader learns the workplace world through protagonist.
2. **First mistake** (vol 2): humiliation; the protagonist questions their fit.
3. **Mentor / senpai arc** (vol 2–3): senior coworker takes interest.
4. **First project ownership** (vol 3–4): protagonist drives one outcome; succeeds or fails meaningfully.
5. **Identity reframe** (vol 4–5): the protagonist decides whether to commit to this career, or transform it.

## 4. Visual register

- **Realist linework**: workplace manga tilts realist; offices and uniforms are recognizable to the reader.
- **Conversation-heavy paneling**: 6-panel grids for dialogue scenes; over-the-shoulder shots common.
- **Workplace prop fetish**: specific desks, specific bar equipment, specific bento boxes carry meaning.
- **Color tilt** (cover): muted office palette (grays, navy, beige) with one warm accent.

## 5. Reader demographic

- **Age:** 22–55 (oldest-skewing alongside historical)
- **Gender:** 50/50 historically; office-romance josei skews 70% female
- **Cultural anchor:** vocational-identity, salaryman-grind-as-modern-religion (especially in Japan)
- **Therapeutic susceptibility:** EXTREMELY HIGH — workplace burnout, perfectionism, imposter syndrome, autistic-coded special-interest at work, AuDHD masking. This is the under-served lane.

## 7. Therapeutic-embed natural landing moments

- **The smoking-section / vending-machine break scene** — coworker confides; informal therapy.
- **The izakaya after work** — alcohol-mediated honesty; co-regulation through shared exhaustion.
- **The senpai's mentorship one-liner** — the genre's most-loved beat; one sentence reframes the protagonist's whole week.
- **The toilet stall cry scene** — the genre's most-honest beat; private somatic release.
- **The "I quit" letter scene** — protagonist writes the resignation, then doesn't send it; rumination + commitment-acceptance.

## 8. Operator-example: the workplace version of the elf-grief pattern

> "Ayano, 28, is a marketing manager at a Tokyo cosmetics firm. She's been masking ASD-coded sensory overload at work for six years. Her senpai Mr. Takeda is a 45-year-old salaryman who's never been promoted; he runs an Excel macro empire from his desk in the back. Ayano has a meltdown in a meeting — overstimulated by fluorescent lights, three perfumes, and the new intern's voice frequency. She runs to the toilet. Takeda finds her there ten minutes later (he was in the next stall, he heard). He doesn't say it's okay. He says: 'I bring earplugs to every meeting. Foam ones, the soft kind. I keep two pairs in my desk. Take one tomorrow. The fluorescent lights are the same in every conference room — I can tell you which rooms are bearable. Room 4C has windows. Use 4C when you can.' Ayano cries. Takeda goes back to his macros. Ayano uses the earplugs. She books Room 4C. Six months later she's still there, sustainable. No therapy word. The reader gets: sensory accommodation, workplace self-advocacy, AuDHD masking-recovery, the senpai-mentor model of practical care."

This is the most-needed and under-served therapeutic-embed pattern. Workplace burnout content is huge but almost never landed inside compelling story.

## 9. Phoenix series concepts

1. **Room 4C** — exactly the above; AuDHD workplace masking-recovery, josei-coded.
2. **Mr. Takeda's Macros** — Mr. Takeda gets his own anthology spin-off; perennial salaryman as practical-therapy mentor.
3. **The Izakaya Counter** — a small bar near a corporate tower; nightly snippets of office-worker confession.
4. **The Quit Letter** — anthology, each chapter a different person who almost quit and what made them stay or go.
5. **Hospitality Hours** — a Bartender-style series for a hotel concierge; service-as-care, late-life dignity.

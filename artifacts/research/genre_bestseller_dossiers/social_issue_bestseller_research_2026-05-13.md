## 3. Common arc structure

**Single-arc serial (not long-arc).** Social-issue manga tend to be 5–10 volumes, structured as one extended trauma-recovery or witnessing arc. They are *not* multi-volume episodic. This is opposite to the dominant manga shape.

5-vol shape:
1. **Status quo of suffering** (vol 1): protagonist's pain is established slowly, with realism.
2. **Inciting witness** (vol 1–2): another character sees the pain.
3. **Catastrophic event** (vol 2–3): the issue breaks open.
4. **Slow climb / partial recovery** (vol 3–4): not a triumphant arc; recovery is partial and stuttering.
5. **Resolution-as-ambiguity** (vol 4–5): the arc closes without "happy ending"; the protagonist is *still* recovering when the manga ends.

## 4. Visual register

- **Hyper-realist linework**, often photorealist (Asano's Punpun is famously photorealist backgrounds + cartoony protagonist).
- **Negative space + silence panels**: 1-page silent reaction shots are genre-signature.
- **Faces hidden**: protagonists are often drawn faceless, abstracted, or with metaphor (Punpun is literally a bird-blob).
- **Color tilt** (cover): desaturated, gray-blue, sepia; deliberately joyless.

## 5. Reader demographic

- **Age:** 18–45 (skews older; this is seinen/josei territory)
- **Gender:** 50/50
- **Cultural anchor:** witnessing, the legitimacy-of-pain, the refusal of false resolution
- **Therapeutic susceptibility:** HIGH but unusual — readers come pre-validated; they don't want self-help, they want *to be seen*. Embedding must be HEAVIER on validation, LIGHTER on coping skills, than other genres.

## 7. Therapeutic-embed natural landing moments

The pattern here inverts. Instead of teaching, the embedding is **mirroring + witnessing**:

- **The "you don't have to be okay" scene** — another character sees the protagonist not-okay, doesn't try to fix it.
- **The "I noticed" scene** — a small specific noticing ("you stopped going to the convenience store") lands as care.
- **The "I tried therapy and it didn't work" scene** — genre's most-honest beat; validates readers who've had bad therapy.
- **The "small recovery" scene** — protagonist eats one meal, takes one shower; the magnitude is appropriate to the pain.
- **The "the world keeps being the world" scene** — recovery doesn't fix the world; protagonist re-enters an unchanged world with slightly more capacity.

## 8. Operator-example: the social-issue version of the elf-grief pattern

> "Yuki, 24, has been a hikikomori for three years after a workplace harassment incident. Her older brother Daichi has been bringing her dinner trays daily without asking her to come out. One night Daichi sits outside her door with two bowls. He says nothing for fifteen minutes. Then: 'I made the miso wrong tonight. Too salty. I won't be hurt if you don't eat it.' Yuki opens the door. They eat in the hallway, both facing forward, not looking at each other. Daichi says: 'I quit my job. I'm not sure what comes next. I thought I'd tell you because nothing has to happen with this information.' Yuki nods. The next night Daichi brings two bowls again. They eat in the hallway. They don't talk. This continues for forty-three nights. On night forty-four, Yuki sits on the porch instead of the hallway. No therapy word. The reader gets: low-demand connection, the cost of pushing recovery, the legitimacy of slow time, sibling co-regulation."

This is anti-self-help self-help. The genre's therapeutic move is to *refuse* the fast arc.

## 9. Phoenix series concepts

1. **Forty-Three Nights** — exactly the above; hikikomori recovery via patient sibling.
2. **The Therapist Who Was Wrong** — protagonist whose first therapist hurt them, second therapist apologizes for the field.
3. **The Sweat and Bench** — Real-coded; wheelchair-basketball + dignity in sport.
4. **The Convenience Store Counter** — anthology; the 24-hour clerk witnesses everyone's small recoveries.
5. **The Hospital Roommate** — psychiatric inpatient story; co-regulation between two reluctant strangers.

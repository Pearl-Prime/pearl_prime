## 3. Common arc structure

**Power Escalation Ladder + Mystery Box hybrid** (`manga_bestseller_magical_serial_framework_2026-05-12.md` §2.1, §2.2).

1. **Cockpit-as-character** intro (vols 1–2): pilot meets/inherits the mecha; the machine is a named entity.
2. **First sortie / first kill** (vol 2–3): the realness of war lands somatically.
3. **Squadron / faction politics** (vols 3–8): the meta-conflict layers in; political ambiguity grows.
4. **Pilot psychological collapse** (vols 8–12): the named trauma takes the cockpit hostage.
5. **Reconciliation or sacrifice** (vols 12–end): identity-as-pilot vs. identity-as-person.

The mecha is the trauma container. The pilot dissociates *into* the mecha; the recovery is dissociating *out*.

## 4. Visual register

- **Hardware splash panels**: the mecha reveal is the genre's signature shot, like the food reveal in gourmet manga.
- **Cockpit interior shots**: claustrophobic 4–6 panel grids when the pilot is destabilizing.
- **Wide-shot battle composition**: scale matters; mecha vs. cityscape, mecha vs. sky.
- **Color tilt** (cover): metallic / desaturated palette with one signal color (Gundam's red, Eva's purple).

## 5. Reader demographic

- **Age:** 14–45 (military hardware fans skew older)
- **Gender:** ~70% male historically; Witch from Mercury (female protagonist + queer romance) successfully shifted to ~50/50
- **Cultural anchor:** competence, hardware-fetish, war-as-meaning-vessel
- **Therapeutic susceptibility:** VERY HIGH — mecha is literally PTSD + dissociation + survivor-guilt rendered as plot mechanics. Eva is the canonical example.

## 7. Therapeutic-embed natural landing moments

- **The cockpit between sorties** — internal monologue; the pilot talks to the mecha (or to themself).
- **The hangar at 3am** — mechanic / engineer character delivers the teaching while doing maintenance.
- **The debrief scene** — superior officer asks "what happened?" and the pilot rehearses the answer multiple times.
- **The flashback to civilian life** — pre-war self lands as ghost; grief for the person they were before.
- **The shared mess hall scene** — squadron co-regulation; the meal repairs what the sortie tore.

## 8. Operator-example: the mecha version of the elf-grief pattern

> "Two pilot cadets, Yui and Ren, have been bunkmates for two years. In their first real sortie, Ren's mecha takes a fatal hit. Yui makes it back to base. The squadron CO orders mandatory grief counseling; Yui refuses, returns to flight duty next week. In the cockpit, mid-sortie, she hears Ren's voice in the comm channel — it's not real, it's her dissociation. Her hand freezes on the trigger. She returns to base shaking. The mechanic, an older woman named Hina who lost her own daughter in a previous war, finds Yui curled up next to her mecha at 3am. Hina doesn't sit with her. Hina starts polishing the mecha's gauntlet, slowly, methodically. After twenty minutes Hina says: 'When you come back from a sortie and the cockpit still smells like them, that's not haunting. That's your body saying — they were here, they mattered, you cared. Don't argue with your body.' Yui starts polishing the other gauntlet. Next sortie, Yui flies. No therapy word. The reader gets: complicated grief, dissociation, somatic resourcing, mentor-modeled co-regulation."

# Persona-Aware Location Grounding Research

**Purpose:** Replace urban-millennial default assumptions in 7,000+ therapeutic atoms with grounding details that actually match WHERE each persona consumes content.

**Research date:** 2026-04-05
**Affects:** All atom location variables, sensory cues, environmental descriptions

---

## 1. Where People Actually Read/Listen to Audiobooks

### Aggregate Data (All Demographics)

The most common activities during audiobook/podcast consumption, compiled from Audio Publishers Association surveys (2024), Audiolibrix Great Audiobook Survey (2024), YouGov (2023), and Edison Research (2025):

| Activity | Audiobooks | Podcasts |
|---|---|---|
| Commute / driving | 63% | 42% |
| Household chores | 54% | 49% (women 55%, men 43%) |
| Relaxing / falling asleep | 44% | -- |
| Walking / on foot | -- | 49% |
| At home (general) | -- | 90% |
| Exercising / gym | ~30% (est.) | 29% (men 32%, women 26%) |
| Before bed | 13% | -- |
| In car/truck | -- | 64% |
| Public transit | 10% | 37% |

**Critical finding:** The CAR is the dominant consumption environment, not public transit. 78% of US workers commute by car; 88% of those drive alone (US Census ACS 2024). Only 4% of workers use public transit. The "subway" or "train" default is wrong for the vast majority of listeners.

### By Persona

#### 25-45 Professionals
- **Primary:** Driving alone to work (69.2% of all US commuters drive alone)
- **Secondary:** Home office with headphones, gym/workout, walking the dog
- **Tertiary:** Before bed, weekend morning errands
- **Setting:** Car (suburban highway or arterial road), home office desk, gym floor, bed
- **Time windows:** 7-9 AM commute, lunch break walk, 5-7 PM commute, 9-11 PM wind-down

#### Gen Z (18-25)
- **Primary:** In bed at night (phone + AirPods), walking between classes or errands
- **Secondary:** Bus/rideshare, doing chores, getting ready in the morning
- **Tertiary:** At parents' house, in shared apartment, dorm room
- **Setting:** Bedroom (31% still live at parents' home; only 9% own a home), dorm, shared apartment
- **Time windows:** 7-9 AM commute/pre-class, late night in bed (this is the BIG one)
- **Device:** Smartphone is primary device for 56% of digital audiobook consumption
- **Key stat:** 58% of Gen Z considers audio the most immersive format (Spotify Culture Next 2024)

#### Gen Alpha (12-17)
- **Primary:** Bedroom (phone/tablet + earbuds), backseat of car, school bus
- **Secondary:** Walking to/from school, doing homework, at a friend's house
- **Tertiary:** Waiting rooms, before sleep
- **Setting:** Bedroom with LED strip lights and phone charging, school hallways, car backseat
- **Time windows:** After school 3-6 PM, bedtime 9-11 PM, morning getting ready
- **Device:** 58% have a smartphone by age 10; 51% of ages 0-8 have their own tablet or phone
- **Key stat:** 1 in 5 young Alphas use mobile devices for emotional regulation (Morning Consult 2024)

#### Parents (28-48)
- **Primary:** After kids' bedtime (the sacred window), morning before kids wake (~6 AM), car during school pickup/dropoff/errands
- **Secondary:** Kitchen while cooking, folding laundry, mowing lawn
- **Tertiary:** Waiting in the car at activities, walking the dog
- **Setting:** Kitchen counter at 6 AM, driver's seat in school pickup line, living room couch at 9:30 PM
- **Time windows:** 5:30-6:30 AM (before kids wake), school pickup 2:45-3:30 PM, after bedtime 8:30-10:30 PM
- **Key stat:** 50% of parents say they're too tired after work to read (PRH survey). Audiobooks solve this.

#### Healthcare Workers (Nurses/RNs)
- **Primary:** Drive home after 12-hour shift (the decompression commute), days off recovery
- **Secondary:** Break room during 30-min break, before bed on work nights
- **Tertiary:** Walking to parking lot, waiting for shift change
- **Setting:** Car (14-16 hour day including prep + commute), break room with fluorescent lights, bed after a night shift
- **Time windows:** Post-shift drive (7 AM or 7 PM depending on shift), 3 days off per week, rare 30-min breaks
- **Key stat:** 12-hour shifts are standard; a "work day" is actually 14-16 hours with prep + commute. 69% of EMS providers report not enough recovery time between traumatic incidents (SAMHSA).

#### First Responders (Fire/EMS/Police)
- **Primary:** Station downtime (evenings between calls), drive home after 24-hour shift
- **Secondary:** Personal time at station (calling home, studying), days off
- **Tertiary:** On apparatus during non-emergency transport
- **Setting:** Firehouse bunk/rec room, patrol car during quiet hours, home after multi-day shift cycle
- **Time windows:** Evening downtime at station 7-10 PM, post-shift drive home, 48-96 hours off between shift blocks
- **Key stat:** Firefighters work 24-on/48-off or 48-on/96-off schedules. Sleep at station is sporadic and interrupted. Decompression happens on the drive home and first hours off.

#### Executives (35-58)
- **Primary:** Business travel (plane, hotel room, Uber to meeting), morning routine, home office
- **Secondary:** Gym, walking meetings
- **Tertiary:** Weekend morning, airport lounge
- **Setting:** Business class seat, hotel room at 10 PM, home office at 5:30 AM, gym
- **Time windows:** 5-6:30 AM morning routine, travel days (entire flights), hotel evenings, weekend mornings

#### Retirees / 45+ Reinventors
- **Primary:** Morning walk, reading chair, garden
- **Secondary:** Before bed, while cooking, during exercise
- **Tertiary:** Car during errands, waiting rooms
- **Setting:** Living room chair, walking trail, garden bench, kitchen
- **Time windows:** Morning 7-10 AM (highest engagement window), afternoon 2-4 PM, before bed
- **Key stat:** Audiobooks offer accessibility for those with vision changes. Older adults listen while cooking, gardening, and before bed (DailyCaring 2024).

## 2. Suburban vs Urban Reality

### Where Americans Actually Live

| Category | Percentage | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Self-describe as SUBURBAN | 52% | American Housing Survey 2017 / HUD |
| Self-describe as URBAN | 27% | American Housing Survey 2017 / HUD |
| Self-describe as RURAL | 21% | American Housing Survey 2017 / HUD |
| Census "urban areas" | 80% | 2020 Census (includes suburbs in urban count) |

**The critical nuance:** The Census counts suburbs as "urban," which is why you see "80% urban." But when people self-report, the majority say SUBURBAN. This matters enormously for grounding language.

### Commute Reality

| Commute Method | Percentage | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Drive alone | 69.2% | ACS 2024 |
| Carpool | ~9% | ACS 2024 |
| Public transit | 4% | ACS 2024 |
| Walk | 2% | ACS 2024 |
| Bicycle | <1% | ACS 2024 |
| Work from home | ~15% | ACS 2024 (post-pandemic elevated) |

**Bottom line:** The typical self-help consumer pictures themselves in a CAR, not on a subway. "The train" as a default transit reference only works for ~4% of listeners. "The drive" or "the car" works for 78%.

### Millennial Homeownership (Our Core Demographic)

- 47-55% of millennials own a home as of 2024 (Apartment List / Redfin)
- 49% of millennials prefer SUBURBAN locations (vs 28% urban, 23% rural)
- Millennials are the largest homebuying group: 38% of all homebuyers in 2024
- Migration trend: continued movement from urban centers to suburban/exurban areas in 2024

**What this means for atoms:** The median millennial self-help reader lives in a suburban house or apartment, drives to work, has a kitchen table where the laptop lives, and worries about the mortgage. NOT a walkup apartment looking down at a city street.

## 3. Generational Grounding Cues

### Gen Alpha (born 2010+) -- Their Physical World

**Bedroom:**
- LED strip lights (usually color-changing, often purple/blue)
- Phone charging on nightstand or under pillow
- AirPods case on desk
- Multiple screens visible (phone, tablet, maybe laptop)
- Posters or tapestries, possibly from TikTok trends
- Hoodie draped over chair

**Landmarks/Hangouts:**
- Chick-fil-A (top restaurant for teens, 17% favorite)
- McDonald's (especially younger Alpha, 34-40%)
- Target (social hangout as much as store)
- Boba/bubble tea shops (94% of 20-29 year-olds bought boba in last 3 months per CLSA)
- The mall food court
- School cafeteria

**Beverage:**
- NOT coffee (only 31% of teens choose coffee; 39% prefer energy drinks)
- Boba tea, Starbucks pink drink, energy drinks (Red Bull 29%, Monster 26%, Alani Nu 17%)
- Water bottles (Stanley cups, Owala)

**Sensory Details Unique to Them:**
- Notification sounds (Discord ping, Snapchat chirp, TikTok scroll sound)
- AirPod case click
- Phone vibration on desk
- LED light color shift
- Hoodie drawstring fidgeting
- Stanley cup straw clicking
- The sound of a phone screen being tapped

**Snack anchors:** Goldfish, Lays, Doritos, Takis

### Gen Z (born 1997-2012) -- Their Physical World

**Living situation:**
- 31% still at parents' home (CNBC/Census 2024)
- ~10% doubled up with non-family roommates
- Only 9% own a home
- Dorm rooms for college-age
- Shared apartments with 1-3 roommates

**Beverage:**
- Iced oat milk latte (37% of 18-34 choose alt milk)
- Boba tea
- Matcha, chai (coffee is situational, only 47% drink it daily vs 65% of millennials)
- Energy drinks for studying/working

**Commute:**
- Walking, bus, rideshare (Uber/Lyft), biking
- Less likely to own a car than older generations
- Public transit use higher than average but still minority

**Sensory Details Unique to Them:**
- Laptop on bed (working from bedroom is normalized)
- Podcast playing through phone speaker while getting ready
- Iced drink sweating on a desk
- Roommate's noise through thin walls
- Ring light glow
- Venmo notification

**Stress anchors:**
- Rent payment app notification
- Job application portal refreshing
- Student loan balance
- Shared bathroom schedule
- "Quiet quitting" feeling at work

### Millennials (born 1981-1996) -- Their Physical World

**Living situation:**
- 47-55% homeowners, increasingly suburban
- Many have young children (peak parenting years)
- Home office is common (post-pandemic hybrid work)
- Kitchen table = workspace for many

**Beverage:**
- Coffee (65% daily), but upgraded: specialty pour-over, local roaster, Nespresso
- Wine in the evening (stress anchor)
- Sparkling water (La Croix, Topo Chico)

**Commute:**
- Suburban car commute is dominant
- Some hybrid: 2-3 days in office, rest WFH
- School dropoff adds to commute chain

**Sensory Details Unique to Them:**
- Laptop on kitchen table (the office-that-never-closes)
- School email notification
- Mortgage app push notification
- Baby monitor crackling
- Dishwasher running in background
- The specific silence after kids finally go to bed
- Zoom fatigue (camera-off muting)
- Grocery delivery app

**Stress anchors:**
- Childcare cost calculations
- "We should talk about the budget" text from partner
- School pickup time pressure
- Performance review email
- The mental load list that never ends

### Gen X (born 1965-1980) -- Their Physical World

**Living situation:**
- Peak homeownership years (suburban house, possibly paid off or close)
- Sandwich generation: 54% have a living parent 65+ AND a dependent child
- Some have adult children returning home (boomerang kids)

**Beverage:**
- Traditional coffee (drip, Keurig)
- Diet Coke
- Beer/wine on weekends

**Physical landmarks:**
- Suburban kitchen (command center of the house)
- Car at a red light (the thinking space)
- Office cubicle or corner office (still office-centric)
- Parent's doctor's office waiting room
- Kid's college campus during move-in

**Sensory Details Unique to Them:**
- Parent calling on the phone (not texting)
- College tuition payment portal
- Reading glasses they keep losing
- The weight of a paper planner alongside a digital calendar
- Doctor appointment reminder for their parent
- Empty nest bedroom door open
- Pharmacy drive-through for parent's prescriptions

**Stress anchors:**
- "Your father had another fall" phone call
- College acceptance/rejection letter alongside tuition bill
- Watching a parent's health decline while managing own career
- 77% report emotional stress from caregiving (family caregiver research)
- 43% have sleeping problems related to caregiving stress

## 4. Universal Sensory Anchors (Work Everywhere)

These body/environment cues are valid regardless of geography, generation, income level, or living situation. They should REPLACE location-specific cues as defaults in most atoms.

### Body Sensations (Truly Universal)

| Sensation | Associated State | Why It Works Everywhere |
|---|---|---|
| Jaw tightening/clenching | Stress, held words, "holding it together" | Sympathetic nervous system response, universal |
| Shoulders rising toward ears | Anxiety, hypervigilance | Universal muscle tension pattern |
| Stomach dropping | Fear, bad news | Vagus nerve response |
| Chest tightening | Panic, grief | Universal anxiety response |
| Hands going cold | Anxiety, adrenaline | Blood flow redirects to core |
| Breath getting shallow | Stress, overwhelm | Fight-or-flight breathing |
| Forehead pressure/tension | Overthinking, headache onset | Universal tension holding pattern |
| Throat closing/lump | Grief, unsaid words | Universal emotional response |
| Legs feeling heavy or restless | Depression, exhaustion, anxiety | Somatic depression/anxiety marker |
| Heat in the face/neck | Shame, embarrassment | Universal vasodilation response |

**These are the gold standard for therapeutic grounding.** They are evidence-based (somatic therapy literature), cross-cultural, cross-demographic, and impossible to get "wrong" for any listener.

### Light Quality (Universal Framings)

| Description | Why It Works | What to AVOID |
|---|---|---|
| "Screen glow in the dark" | Everyone has a phone/laptop | -- |
| "Overhead fluorescent hum" | Offices, hospitals, schools, stores | -- |
| "Morning light coming in" | Universal, no geography needed | "Fog burning off" (coastal only) |
| "The blue light of the phone at 2 AM" | Universal insomnia experience | -- |
| "The way everything looks different at 4 AM" | Universal for shift workers, insomniacs, anxious | "City lights below" (urban only) |
| "Light through closed eyelids" | Body-based, works anywhere | "Skylight" (architecture-specific) |

### Sound (Universal Framings)

| Description | Why It Works | What to AVOID |
|---|---|---|
| "Notification ding" | Universal smartphone experience | Specific app sounds |
| "Alarm going off" | Universal morning dread | -- |
| "The silence after everyone leaves" | Home, office, hospital, station | "Traffic noise fading" (urban) |
| "Your own breathing" | Ultimate universal anchor | -- |
| "The clock" | Works in any room | -- |
| "Door closing" | Someone arriving/leaving, universal | "Subway doors closing" (NYC) |
| "Water running" | Kitchen, bathroom, universal | "Rain on the window" (climate-specific) |

### Temperature (Universal Framings)

| Description | Why It Works | What to AVOID |
|---|---|---|
| "Cold hands" | Anxiety response, universal | -- |
| "AC blast" | Offices, cars, homes everywhere | -- |
| "Heat on the back of your neck" | Stress/shame response | -- |
| "The chill when you first get out of bed" | Universal morning sensation | "Frost on the window" (cold climates only) |
| "The warmth of a blanket" | Universal comfort | -- |
| "The car heating up in the sun" | Nearly universal (78% drive) | "Wind off the water" (coastal) |

### Time Cues (Universal Emotional Anchors)

| Time | Why It's Universal |
|---|---|
| "3 AM" | The anxiety hour. Everyone who's been awake at 3 AM knows |
| "The hour before the alarm" | Universal dread/anticipation |
| "Sunday night" | End-of-weekend anxiety, universal for workers/students |
| "The first five minutes after waking" | Before the day starts, before defenses go up |
| "Right before a meeting/appointment" | Anticipatory anxiety |
| "The moment the house goes quiet" | Parents, roommate-havers, shift workers returning |

## 5. Weather and Climate Grounding

### What FAILS Across Regions

| Description | Where It Fails | % of US Population It Misses |
|---|---|---|
| "Fog rolling in" | Desert Southwest, Deep South, Great Plains | ~40% |
| "Gray sky" / "overcast" | Southwest, Southern California, Florida (sunny) | ~30% |
| "Snow falling" | South, Southwest, Hawaii, most of California | ~45% |
| "Rain on the window" | Desert Southwest (Tucson gets 12" rain/year) | ~15-20% |
| "Autumn leaves falling" | Florida, Southern California, Hawaii, Deep South | ~25% |
| "Crisp winter morning" | South Florida, Hawaii, Southern Texas | ~15% |

### What WORKS Everywhere

| Description | Why It's Universal | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| "The sky" | Everyone has one | Unmodified by weather adjective |
| "Wind" | Exists everywhere | Not "cold wind" (fails in warm climates) |
| "The sun" | Universal | "Sunlight on your face" works anywhere |
| "The air" | Neutral | "The air on your skin when you step outside" |
| "Stars" | Nearly universal (light pollution varies) | "If you can see them" hedge works |
| "Darkness" | Universal | Evening, nighttime, pre-dawn |
| "The temperature change going inside/outside" | Works in all climates | Hot-to-cold or cold-to-warm, both universal |
| "The way the air smells different at night" | Universal sensory observation | -- |
| "Heat" | Universal (even cold places have summer, heaters) | "The way heat feels on skin" |

### Recommended Atmospheric Defaults

Instead of weather descriptions, use TRANSITION descriptions:
- "Stepping outside" (temperature change, air change)
- "Looking up" (sky, whatever's there)
- "The way light changes" (time-based, not weather-based)
- "The feeling of the air" (unspecified temperature/humidity)

## 7. Universal Fallback Defaults

When persona is unknown or for cross-persona atoms, use these geography-neutral, generation-neutral defaults:

```json
{
  "street_name": "outside",
  "transit_line": "the drive",
  "coffee_shop": "the kitchen",
  "weather_detail": "the light outside",
  "morning_sound": "the alarm",
  "workspace": "where you work",
  "home_space": "where you live",
  "stress_object": "the phone",
  "comfort_object": "the quiet",
  "beverage": "something warm",
  "waiting_place": "the waiting room",
  "social_place": "the people around you",
  "quiet_place": "the only room that's empty",
  "authority_figure": "the person you report to",
  "peer_space": "the people you work with"
}
```

**Design principle:** These defaults should feel like they were written FOR you, not AT a demographic. Vague-but-true beats specific-but-wrong.

## 8. Implementation Priority

### Phase 1: Fix the Biggest Misses
1. Replace ALL "the train" defaults with "the drive" (affects 78% of listeners)
2. Replace "the street below" with "outside the window" or persona-specific
3. Replace "gray sky" with "the light outside" (fails in 30% of US)
4. Replace "coffee shop" with persona-appropriate gathering place

### Phase 2: Add Body-First Grounding
5. Ensure every atom's grounding section starts with a BODY sensation (jaw, shoulders, breath) before any environmental detail
6. Body sensations have 0% demographic miss rate vs 20-40% for environmental details

### Phase 3: Persona-Specific Enrichment
7. Roll out full variable sets per persona
8. A/B test resonance with sample listeners from each demographic

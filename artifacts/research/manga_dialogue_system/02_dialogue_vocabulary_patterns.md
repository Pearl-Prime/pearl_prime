# Dialogue Vocabulary and Speech Patterns in Manga
## Design Reference Document — Phoenix Omega Manga Pipeline

**Version:** 1.0  
**Date:** 2026-04-17  
**Author:** Pearl_Research  
**Purpose:** Comprehensive reference for genre-accurate dialogue generation and pacing

---

## Overview

Manga dialogue is not simply text. It is a register, a rhythm, and a relationship signal. How a character speaks — their pronoun choices, sentence endings, honorific usage, and word length — tells the reader who they are, how they feel, and where they stand relative to every other character. This document catalogs speech registers, archetype patterns, emotional escalation vocabulary, silence conventions, and quantitative pacing norms across all eleven major manga genres.

---

## 1. Per-Genre Speech Register Analysis

### 1.1 Shōnen

**Formality level:** Informal to very informal. Casual slang, incomplete sentences, shouting fragments.

**Sentence length norms:** Short to medium. Battle dialogue: 1-8 words. Emotional declaration: 10-20 words maximum. Explanation: occasionally longer but still punchy.

**First-person pronouns:**
- Male protagonists: **ore** (俺) — assertive, masculine, informal. Naruto, Luffy, Tanjiro all use ore.
- Younger/gentler male: **boku** (僕) — softer, still young. Deku from MHA uses boku to signal his non-aggressive personality.
- Villains with pretension: **watashi** (私) or **ore-sama** — ironic or grandiose.
- Female leads in shōnen: **atashi** (あたし) — informal feminine.

**Sentence-ending conventions:**
- **zo/ze** (ぞ/ぜ) — assertive, masculine declaration ("I'll beat you, zo!")
- **da** (だ) — plain declaration
- **yo** (よ) — emphasis, informing
- **na/naa** (な/なあ) — musing, self-agreement
- **kke** (っけ) — recalling, uncertain
- Combined: **da ze**, **da yo**, **zo yo**

**Common exclamations:**
- うおおおお (UOOOO) — war cry
- くっ (ku) — pain, effort
- なんだと！(Nandato!) — "What did you say?!"
- まじか (Majika) — "Seriously?!"
- やばい (Yabai) — "This is bad!" / "Awesome!" (dual use)
- 行くぞ！(Iku zo!) — "Let's go!"
- うるさい (Urusai) — "Shut up!" / "Be quiet!"

**Honorific patterns:** Frequently dropped among friends and rivals. Sensei/master titles maintained. Rival may use surname only (Sasuke calls Naruto by surname only early on; dropping to given name signals relationship shift).

---

### 1.2 Shōjo

**Formality level:** Moderate. More polite than shōnen, but drops to informal in intimate moments.

**Sentence length norms:** Medium to long. Emotional processing often uses longer sentences. Internal monologue especially extends.

**First-person pronouns:**
- Female leads: **watashi** (私) — standard, can feel formal or neutral depending on context; or **atashi** for softness.
- Male love interests in shōjo: often **boku** (more sensitive, romantic quality) rather than ore.
- Formal/older male: **watashi** regardless of gender.

**Sentence-ending conventions:**
- **wa** (わ) — feminine assertion ("I'm going to tell him, wa")
- **no** (の) — feminine question inflection or statement softening
- **wa yo** (わよ) — strong feminine assertion
- **kashira** (かしら) — wondering aloud, feminine
- **mono** (もの) — justifying, slightly pouty

**Common exclamations:**
- きゃ！(Kya!) — shriek of surprise/embarrassment
- もう！(Mou!) — "Geez!" / exasperation
- え？(E?) — confused/surprised
- ほんとに？(Hontoni?) — "Really?"
- すごい (Sugoi) — "Amazing!"
- どうしよう (Dou shiyou) — "What should I do?" (the quintessential shōjo panicked murmur)

**Honorific patterns:** Formal honorifics maintained more carefully than shōnen. A boy calling the heroine by her given name without honorific is a significant intimacy signal. **-chan** between close friends, **-kun** for male acquaintances, **-san** for distance/respect. A character dropping from **-san** to given name is a plot beat.

---

### 1.3 Seinen

**Formality level:** Full range — from crude criminal slang (Berserk's soldiers) to ultra-formal academic speech (Monster's doctors).

**Sentence length norms:** Long. Seinen is the most text-dense genre. Characters explain, philosophize, and argue at length.

**First-person pronouns:** Full range. Ore for rough characters. Watashi for formal/professional. **Washi** (わし) for old men. **Jibun** (自分) for self-deprecating or military characters.

**Sentence endings:** Full range. Formal desu/masu in professional contexts. Da in narrative. Specific character voices matter more than genre defaults.

**Common exclamations:**
- ふん (Fun) — dismissive snort
- なるほど (Naruhodo) — "I see" (thoughtful)
- まあ (Maa) — "Well..." (hesitation, concession)
- 確かに (Tashikani) — "Certainly, indeed"
- くだらない (Kudaranai) — "Worthless/stupid"
- 面白い (Omoshiroi) — "Interesting..." (said by villain evaluating protagonist)

**Honorific patterns:** Varies by character arc. In Monster, Tenma addresses patients as **-san** throughout his arc — a signal of his humanism. Johan drops honorifics entirely, treating everyone with the same flat address — disturbing.

---

### 1.4 Josei

**Formality level:** Moderate. Adult professional register with emotional casualness.

**Sentence length:** Medium. Adult conversations are more measured than shōnen impulsiveness but less philosophical than seinen.

**Pronouns:** Watashi for women, often ore or boku for men (both present in josei male characters to signal personality type).

**Endings:** Standard polite register; wa/no for female characters. Desu/masu in workplace scenes.

**Exclamations:**
- はあ（Haa）— tired sigh
- え〜 (E~) — drawn-out surprise
- まじで？(Majide?) — "Seriously?"
- うわ (Uwa) — Surprise/dismay
- そうそう (Sou sou) — Agreeing/nodding along
- やだ (Yada) — "No way!" / "Gross!" / "Stop it!"

**Honorific patterns:** Workplace uses **-san** and **-sensei** carefully. Home/close friend scenes drop to given name or nickname. The moment a character begins calling someone by their given name in josei is as charged as a confession.

---

### 1.5 Kodomomuke

**Formality level:** Very informal, childlike registers with some formal speech for authority figures.

**Pronouns:** **Boku** for small boys, **atashi** for girls. Pokemon's Ash (Satoshi) uses boku. Adults use watashi.

**Sentence endings:** Simple da, yo, ne. Questions end clearly with ka. No complex endings.

**Exclamations:**
- わあ！(Waa!) — wonder
- うわあ！(Uwaa!) — bigger wonder
- やった！(Yatta!) — "I did it!" / "We won!"
- ちがう！(Chigau!) — "That's wrong!"
- むりむり！(Muri muri!) — "Impossible!"
- ずるい！(Zurui!) — "That's cheating!"

**Honorific patterns:** **-kun** and **-chan** heavily used. Older characters get **-san** or **-sensei**. Doraemon calls Nobita "Nobita-kun" formally despite being his personal robot — a gentle comedic formality.

---

### 1.6 Isekai

**Formality level:** Wide range within single work. Protagonist (modern-day Japanese) contrasts with archaic NPC speech. Villagers use simple language. Nobility uses formal register. Magic system explanations use technical vocabulary.

**Pronouns:** Protagonist often shifts from ore (casual modern) to watashi (trying to fit formal fantasy world). Ainz in Overlord uses formal watashi as undead king.

**Special endings:** Some isekai use archaic formal speech (**de gozaru**, **de aru**) for in-world characters to signal "this is a fantasy world." Re:Zero's Rem uses polite **de gozaimasu** as a maid.

**Exclamations:**
- チートだ！(Chiito da!) — "That's cheating/OP!"
- 転生した！(Tensei shita!) — "I've reincarnated!"
- ステータス！(Suteetasu!) — "Status!" (summoning status screen)
- 強すぎる (Tsuyosugiru) — "Too strong!"
- 俺TUEEEeee (Ore TUEEE) — "I'm too strong!" (internet-derived, genre self-awareness)

---

### 1.7 Horror

**Formality level:** Normal to slightly archaic. Horror derives power from ordinary speech discussing extraordinary things.

**Key convention:** Horror protagonists speak normally. Horror antagonists/entities may speak with slight wrongness — too formal, too calm, incorrect context.

**Pronouns:** Normal. What matters is context, not pronoun.

**Exclamations:**
- あ... (A...) — trailing shock
- 助けて (Tasukete) — "Help me"
- 嘘だ (Uso da) — "That's a lie"
- なぜ (Naze) — "Why"
- こんなはずじゃ (Konna hazu ja...) — "It wasn't supposed to be like this..."
- 逃げろ (Nigero) — "Run!"

**Special pattern:** Junji Ito characters often explain the horror in a detached, almost scientific way as they witness it — registering as clinical rather than panicked, which deepens the dread.

---

### 1.8 Sports

**Formality level:** Informal among teammates; more formal toward opponents and coaches.

**Technical vocabulary:** Sports manga introduces real sport-specific jargon. Haikyuu!! uses actual volleyball terminology (**toss**, **spike**, **receive**, **rotation**). Blue Lock uses football/soccer tactical vocabulary. Slam Dunk uses basketball plays.

**Pronouns:** Ore dominant among competitive male characters. Rivals using each other's surnames, then gradually shifting to given names as respect develops.

**Exclamations:**
- 行くぞ！(Iku zo!) — "Let's go!"
- 決めろ！(Kimero!) — "Decide it!" / "Score!"
- 受け取れ！(Ukeiro!) — "Receive it!" / "Take it!"
- 俺が決める (Ore ga kimeru) — "I'll decide this"
- うおお！(Uooo!) — exertion cry
- 信じろ (Shinjiro) — "Believe!" (to teammate)

---

### 1.9 Slice of Life / Iyashikei

**Formality level:** Gentle informal. No dramatic registers. Speech is warm, meandering, comfortable.

**Sentence length:** Variable. Short exchanges between friends; longer reflective inner voice passages.

**Pronouns:** Watashi or atashi for most adults. Children use boku/atashi. Yotsuba uses **jibun** sometimes, reflecting her verbal quirks.

**Exclamations:**
- あ〜（A~）— long thoughtful exhale
- そうだね (Sou da ne) — "Yeah, that's right" — agreement, warmth
- なんか (Nanka) — "Like, somehow..." (verbal filler)
- いいなあ (Ii naa) — "That sounds nice..."
- ほっこり (Hokkori) — warm comfortable feeling (sometimes spoken as exclamation)
- のんびり (Nonbiri) — relaxed, unhurried

**Honorific patterns:** Warm nicknames. Adults in iyashikei often have affectionate nicknames rather than formal address. In Barakamon, the children call Seishuu "Sensei" because that's literally what he is, but it becomes a term of affection.

---

### 1.10 BL/GL

**Formality level:** Ranges from formal (work settings) to intensely intimate (confession scenes with almost no speech at all).

**Pronoun dynamics:** BL relationships where one character uses boku and the other uses ore signal personality dynamic before anything explicit is said. In Given, Ritsuka uses ore; Mafuyu's pronoun choice is also deliberate.

**Endings:** Intimate scenes often trail off with... (ellipsis) rather than grammatical completion. Sentences left unfinished are a speech convention.

**Exclamations:**
- 好きだ (Suki da) — "I like/love you" — the confession
- 離さない (Hanasanai) — "I won't let go"
- ずっと (Zutto) — "Always" / "Forever"
- だめ (Dame) — "No" / "I can't" / "Stop" — layered meaning
- バカ (Baka) — "Idiot" — tsundere deflection

---

### 1.11 Mecha

**Formality level:** Military hierarchy enforces formal speech (pilots to commanders, crew to captain). Inside the cockpit during battle: raw, informal, fragmented.

**Pronouns:** Professional watashi in command contexts; ore during combat. Shinji Ikari uses boku — deliberately infantile for a child pilot.

**Technical vocabulary:** Callsigns, unit designations, tactical commands. EVA-01, Gundam RX-78-2, etc. Pilots referred to by callsign during mission.

**Exclamations:**
- 了解！(Ryoukai!) — "Roger!"
- 発進！(Hasshin!) — "Launch!"
- 撃て！(Ute!) — "Fire!"
- 逃げちゃだめだ (Nige-cha dame da) — "I mustn't run away" — Shinji's mantra
- 攻撃開始！(Kougeki kaishi!) — "Begin attack!"

## 2. Archetype Speech Patterns

### 2.1 Shōnen Protagonist (Hot-headed, idealistic, determination)

Register: ore, zo/ze endings, short punchy sentences, name-drops own goal, emotional

1. "I'm gonna be the strongest! Nobody can stop me, dattebayo!"
2. "I don't care if I can't win. I'll fight until I drop!"
3. "You think pain scares me? I've been through worse!"
4. "Quit underestimating me! I'll show you what I'm made of!"
5. "I promised... I promised him I'd never give up!"
6. "My fists are moving on their own... This is... this is the anger of my nakama!"
7. "It's not over! I can still fight!"
8. "Everyone waiting for me back home — I'm not gonna let them down!"
9. "That's not strength. Real strength means protecting the people you care about!"
10. "AAAAAAAAH!!" (pure exertion cry — often the only text on the page)

---

### 2.2 Shōnen Rival (Cool, dismissive, secretly respects protagonist)

Register: ore or watashi, short clipped sentences, sneer-quality, avoids showing vulnerability

1. "Don't misunderstand. I'm not interested in someone as weak as you."
2. "You call that a technique? I've seen better from children."
3. "... (silent evaluation) ...You've gotten stronger. Barely."
4. "Stay out of my way. I don't need your help."
5. "The next time we meet, I'll crush you completely. So train harder."
6. "Hmph. I suppose you didn't embarrass yourself too badly."
7. "Don't die before I get a chance to beat you properly."
8. "I have no interest in your sentimentality."
9. "What are you talking about? I've already forgotten about you."
10. "... You kept up with me. I'll acknowledge that much."

---

### 2.3 Wise Mentor/Sensei (Indirect, metaphorical, calm)

Register: watashi or washi (older), desu/masu formal, measured pace, questions back at student

1. "What do you see when you look at the river? Watch it for a while."
2. "I cannot teach you strength. I can only show you where to look for it."
3. "You're asking the wrong question. Try again."
4. "Power without restraint is just chaos. What distinguishes you from your enemy?"
5. "You've already figured it out. You simply haven't admitted it to yourself yet."
6. "Every master was once a student who refused to stop failing."
7. "The technique is not in the hands. It is in the space between heartbeats."
8. "When you fight to protect others, what does that feel like? Hold onto that feeling."
9. "I've taught you everything I can. The rest is yours to discover."
10. "... I see. You've finally understood." (said quietly after student's breakthrough)

---

### 2.4 Villain Monologue (Theatrical, justifying, dangerous calm)

Register: watashi or formal ore-sama, full sentences, philosophical framing, no shouting

1. "You call this justice? Let me show you what justice costs."
2. "I've already won. You simply haven't accepted it yet."
3. "Humans don't want freedom. They want the comfort of someone telling them what to think."
4. "Everything I've done, I've done for the world you claim to love. You should be thanking me."
5. "How disappointing. I expected more from someone who managed to reach me."
6. "Pain teaches. I am the most honest teacher this world has ever had."
7. "You can't defeat me with love and friendship. This isn't a story."
8. "I don't hate you. I simply find you... irrelevant."
9. "Go ahead. Strike me. Let everyone see what your 'justice' looks like up close."
10. "You and I are not so different. The only distinction is: I'm willing to finish what I started."

---

### 2.5 Tsundere (Harsh exterior → vulnerable core)

Register: informal, defensive, deflection phrases, often trails off mid-sentence

1. "I-it's not like I was worried about you or anything! I just happened to be nearby!"
2. "Don't get the wrong idea. I only helped you because it was convenient."
3. "You're an idiot. An absolute idiot... Don't ever do something like that again."
4. "I don't care what you do. At all. ...Just make sure you come back safely."
5. "Why are you looking at me like that?! Stop it!"
6. "H-hmph. If you're going to insist on it, then I suppose I could accompany you. This once."
7. "It's not like your smile does anything to me. Shut up."
8. "I wasn't crying! Something got in my eye — both eyes — repeatedly!"
9. "Fine! FINE! I'll admit it, okay?! Just this once!"
10. "...Thank you. I said thank you. Did you hear me? Good. I'm not saying it again."

---

### 2.6 Comic Relief (Mistiming, misunderstanding, self-deprecation)

Register: variable register used incorrectly (too formal or too casual at wrong moment), exaggerated reactions

1. "I have definitely never been more confident in my life. (immediately fails)"
2. "Ah yes, I totally understood that plan from the beginning." (has no idea)
3. "W-wait, when you said 'take care of it,' I thought you meant literally TAKE CARE OF IT—"
4. "I'll be the one to protect everyone! ...starting tomorrow. Today I'm resting."
5. "Is this the part where I'm supposed to be brave? Could someone give me a count-in?"
6. "My strategy is completely thought through. It involves running and screaming."
7. "I panicked, okay! My body moved on its own and then kept moving in the wrong direction!"
8. "Everyone was so moved by my speech. Unfortunately they were moved... away from me."
9. "Don't worry, I've got a plan. It's in three phases: Panic, More Panic, and Improvise."
10. "I'll have you know that was NOT me tripping. That was a tactical low-profile maneuver."

---

### 2.7 Kuudere (Flat affect, minimal words, rare warmth)

Register: short, declarative, minimal emotion signaling, occasional unexpected warmth

1. "...I see."
2. "It doesn't matter."
3. "You're in the way."
4. "I'll handle it."
5. "...That was well done." (highest praise available)
6. "No. Leave this to me."
7. "I'm not cold. I simply choose where my attention goes."
8. "...Don't die." (said quietly to someone they care about, maximum intimacy for this type)
9. "Your presence is... not unpleasant."
10. "Whatever. I wasn't concerned." (said while clearly having been concerned)

---

### 2.8 Yandere (Sweet surface → obsessive break)

Register: shifts from warm/normal to flat/dangerous; refers to target obsessively; sentences that sound wrong

1. "I've been watching you for so long. Don't you know how much I love you?"
2. "You smiled at her today. I counted. Three times." (delivered with a smile)
3. "It's okay. I took care of the problem. No one will bother us anymore."
4. "You don't need to go anywhere. Everything you need is right here. I've made sure of it."
5. "I love you so much it feels like I'm going to disappear. So I have to make sure you can't leave."
6. "You're mine. I know you don't understand that yet. But you will."
7. "I did everything for you. Everything. Why won't you see that?"
8. "I'm not going to hurt you. I just need you to stay a little longer. Just a little. Forever."
9. "Don't say her name again." (sudden flatness, the danger showing through)
10. "It's fine. You'll forget about everything else eventually. They always do."

---

### 2.9 Love Interest, Shōjo-Style (Flustered, sincere, emotionally processing)

Register: watashi/atashi, ellipses, incomplete sentences, sincere declaration after struggle

1. "I — I don't know when it happened but you're the first thing I think about when I wake up..."
2. "That's not... I mean, I wasn't... why does it matter to you?"
3. "When you said my name like that, I felt something I've never felt before and I don't know what to do with it."
4. "You're always there when I need you. I've started relying on that. Too much, maybe."
5. "It's stupid. It's so stupid. Why am I crying?"
6. "I want to tell you something but every time I try the words disappear."
7. "I think... I think I've liked you for a long time. Longer than I realized."
8. "I'm scared. I'm scared because if you don't feel the same way, everything changes."
9. "Then... then I'll say it. I like you. I like you so much it hurts sometimes."
10. "...Don't go. Please. I'll say whatever I need to say. Just don't go yet."

---

### 2.10 Sports Rival (Mutual respect, technical focus, rivalry as growth)

Register: ore or formal with opponent, technical vocabulary, respect growing through combat/competition

1. "Your form is different from last time. You've been training seriously."
2. "Don't hold back. If you're going to beat me, beat me at full strength."
3. "I've studied every game you've played this season. I know your patterns."
4. "That set was mine. But this match isn't over."
5. "You made me move. I didn't think anyone in this tournament could make me move."
6. "It's not about winning or losing anymore. You already know that, don't you?"
7. "The next time we play, try that new technique. I want to see what you've built."
8. "...Good match." (two words after an entire arc of rivalry — carries the whole arc)
9. "I don't need you to acknowledge me. Beating you was enough."
10. "You're the reason I've gotten this far. Don't you dare stop here."

---

### 2.11 Horror Antagonist (Surreal, unnervingly calm, body-horror adjacent)

Register: watashi, complete sentences, eerily reasonable tone, no affect, logical framework for monstrous things

1. "Are you in pain? That's interesting. Where does it hurt?"
2. "I find human screaming very informative. Keep going."
3. "You don't need to be afraid. This is simply what bodies do under the right conditions."
4. "I've always been curious about you. All of you. From the inside."
5. "You're looking at me as if I'm wrong. Isn't this what you all wanted? Connection?"
6. "No one will come. I made sure of it. We have all the time we need."
7. "You're very interesting. I hope you'll last long enough to show me more."
8. "It doesn't hurt. Or rather — it will stop hurting very soon."
9. "I'm not angry with you. I'm simply doing what I do."
10. "What's that expression? Fear? Disgust? ...Ah. Both at once. That's my favorite."

---

### 2.12 Isekai Exposition (World-rules explanation, overpowered protagonist discovery)

Register: ranges from tutorial NPC (formal, mechanical) to protagonist's stunned reaction (informal)

**NPC/System voice:**
1. "Congratulations. Your base stats have been measured. Would you like to view your Status?"
2. "The S-rank adventurer designation has not been awarded in three hundred years."
3. "Your magic affinity is... I'm sorry, this instrument must be broken. Let me recalibrate."
4. "In this world, skill acquisition requires either dedicated training or divine blessing. You have... hm."

**Protagonist reaction:**
5. "Wait. Wait wait wait. My attack stat is WHAT?"
6. "I thought isekai was supposed to have a tutorial. Where's the tutorial?"
7. "Is it normal for slime to have unlimited stomach capacity? Is that a thing here?"
8. "So to summarize: dragons are real, I can eat abilities by absorbing monsters, and no one finds this alarming."
9. "My job class is listed as 'Hero (Unemployed).' I have questions."
10. "I'm being way too calm about this. This is definitely a panic I'll have later."

## 3. Emotional Escalation Vocabulary

### The Emotional Dial: 1-10 Scale

**Levels 1-2: Baseline/Flat**
> Calm, undramatic, present. Low affect. Normal register.

- "Sure."
- "I guess."
- "What do you want to eat?"
- "The meeting is at three."
- "Ah. I see."
- "Fine by me."
- (Visual: neutral expression, normal bubble shape, no emphasis)

---

**Levels 3-4: Slightly Elevated**
> Interest, mild discomfort, gentle happiness, low-stakes surprise.

- "Oh — really? That's different from what I expected."
- "Hm. That's interesting, actually."
- "I'm not sure I like where this is going."
- "Wait, hold on. Say that again?"
- "That's... not what I was hoping to hear."
- "I'm kind of nervous, I guess? A little."
- (Visual: small raised eyebrow, slight bubble elongation)

---

**Levels 5-6: Engaged/Charged**
> Clear emotion, elevated stakes, marked change from baseline.

- "No — no, that can't be right. When did this happen?!"
- "You're serious. You're actually serious right now."
- "I've been waiting for this moment. Don't waste it."
- "Something's wrong. I can feel it."
- "I said stop! Why won't you listen to me?!"
- "This is — this is incredible. I can't believe this is real."
- (Visual: wider eyes, slightly spiky bubble, one word bolded)

---

**Levels 7-8: High Stakes**
> Intense emotion — fear, rage, grief, passionate love, desperate determination. Character changed by what's happening.

- "Don't you DARE say her name. Don't you DARE."
- "I can't — I can't breathe. Why won't it stop hurting?"
- "There's no going back from this. You understand that, right?!"
- "This is everything. Everything I've worked for. I won't lose it!"
- "Please. Please. I don't ask for much. Just this one thing."
- "I'll kill you! I SWEAR I'LL KILL YOU FOR THIS!"
- (Visual: spiky bubble, multiple words bolded, font size increase, tears or sweat)

---

**Levels 9-10: Peak/Explosion**
> Maximum output. Screaming declaration. Breaking point. The moment the chapter was building toward.

- "IIIIIIIIII WILL NOT LOSEEEEEEE!"
- "THIS IS MY DREAM! NOBODY GETS TO TAKE THAT AWAY FROM ME!"
- "FIGHT ME! COME ON! I'M RIGHT HERE!"
- (single word) "WHY!?"
- "I'LL DESTROY EVERYTHING! EVERYTHING!"
- (Often: just a wordless scream — AAAAAAH — that is the entire bubble)
- (Visual: ultra-jagged extreme bubble, text fills bubble, font maximum size, border thick, panel may break)

---

### The Cool-Down Arc

After a Level 9-10 peak, manga uses a structured decompression:

**Beat 1 (silence):** Single panel with no text. Character slumped, gasping, or staring.

**Beat 2 (quiet):** Small whisper bubble. "...Is it over?" / "...Did I...?" / "...Everyone okay?"

**Beat 3 (reassurance exchange):** Short, tender lines at level 2-3. "Yeah. We're okay." / "...Good." / "Don't scare me like that."

**Beat 4 (reflection):** Internal or spoken, level 3-4. "I didn't think I was going to make it." / "Something changed in that moment. I could feel it."

**Beat 5 (baseline restoration):** Level 1-2. Normal scene business. "Hey. You hungry?" / "We should probably get moving." / "...Yeah. Let's go."

This arc typically spans 2-5 pages in shōnen, 3-8 pages in shōjo/josei, and 1-2 pages in sports (then immediately pivot to next match drama).

## 4. Silence Conventions

### 4.1 Types of Silence in Manga

**Beat silence:** A single silent panel between two dialogue panels. Breathing room. Gives the previous line room to land. Used: after a confession, after a threat, after a death reveal. Duration: 1 panel.

**Emotional silence:** 2-4 panels without dialogue where a character processes something. Visual focus on face, hands, objects of significance. Used: grief, realization, falling in love, rage before action. Duration: 2-4 panels.

**Threatening silence:** A character does not respond. Their silence is the response. More powerful than any line. Used: villains ignoring a challenge; characters too angry to speak; someone so devastated they've gone past words. Duration: 1 panel, but with full facial expression.

**Contemplative silence:** Extended sequences without dialogue that convey mood, environment, or the passage of time. Iyashikei uses this structurally. Duration: potentially a full page.

### 4.2 "Ma" (間) — Meaningful Pause

**Ma** is the Japanese concept of meaningful negative space — the pause that is active rather than empty. In manga, ma is:
- The panel between a punch being thrown and it landing
- The frame where a character's mouth is open but no words are shown yet
- The single panel of just sky or trees before a difficult scene
- The beat where two characters look at each other and something passes between them

Ma is not the absence of content. It is the content. Western comics often struggle to replicate this because they conflate silence with "nothing happening." In manga, ma IS what's happening.

**Silence panel conventions by genre:**

| Genre | Silence density | Dominant type |
|-------|-----------------|---------------|
| Shōnen | Medium | Beat silence, threatening silence |
| Shōjo | High | Emotional silence, contemplative |
| Seinen | Very high | Contemplative, threatening |
| Josei | High | Emotional silence |
| Kodomomuke | Low | Beat silence only |
| Isekai | Low | Beat silence only |
| Horror | Very high | Threatening silence, contemplative |
| Sports | Medium | Beat silence (before climactic plays) |
| Iyashikei | Extremely high | Contemplative; defines the genre |
| BL/GL | Very high | Emotional silence, "ma" moments |
| Mecha | Medium | Beat silence before launch |

### 4.3 When Dialogue Would Ruin the Moment

**Scenarios where silence is correct (10 per major genre):**

**Shōnen:**
1. The protagonist first transforms/powers up — silent awe
2. The mentor's death — silence for at least 1 full page
3. Two rivals see each other again after training — mutual recognition, no words needed
4. The final blow landing — impact without dialogue
5. The protagonist's hometown in ruins — environmental devastation speaks
6. A child smiling after being rescued — pure visual
7. The villain unmasked — silent reveal
8. Two fighters catching their breath mid-battle — pure exhaustion
9. The last panel of a chapter cliffhanger — silent visual hook
10. A long-awaited reunion — tears but no words yet

**Iyashikei:**
1. Watching the sun set from a hillside
2. A cat settling into a warm patch of sunlight
3. Rain on a quiet street
4. A character eating their first meal in a new home
5. Two people sitting side by side reading
6. Snow starting to fall
7. A garden in the morning
8. Someone crying while watching the ocean
9. A character realizing they feel at home somewhere
10. The moment just before sleeping, window open, good air

### 4.4 Visual Techniques That Replace Dialogue

Rather than words, manga uses:

| Visual technique | Emotional content it carries |
|-----------------|------------------------------|
| Close-up on hands | Grief, tenderness, tension, resolve |
| Clenched fist | Suppressed rage, determination, fear |
| Sweat drop | Awkwardness, nervous humor |
| Open eyes wide | Shock, realization, terror |
| Eyes closed | Peace, exhaustion, emotional overwhelm |
| Looking away | Shame, evasion, suppressed feeling |
| Environmental detail | Mood, passage of time, memory |
| Falling tears | What words can't express |
| Forced smile | The gap between surface and interior |
| Characters at physical distance | Emotional distance, conflict |

## 5. Pacing Norms by Genre (Quantitative)

| Genre | Avg words/bubble | Bubbles/panel | Panels/page | Pages/chapter | Silence panel % | Monologue frequency |
|-------|-----------------|---------------|-------------|---------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| **Shōnen** | 5-15 (typical 8) | 2-4 | 5-9 | 18-25 | 15-25% | Low (peaks at arc ends) |
| **Shōjo** | 8-25 (typical 15) | 1-3 | 4-7 | 25-32 | 20-35% | High (inner voice) |
| **Seinen** | 10-40 (typical 20) | 1-4 | 4-8 | 20-50 | 20-40% | Very high |
| **Josei** | 8-30 (typical 18) | 1-3 | 4-7 | 25-40 | 20-30% | High |
| **Kodomomuke** | 3-12 (typical 6) | 2-5 | 6-10 | 16-20 | 10-15% | Very low |
| **Isekai** | 5-80+ for status screens | 2-6 | 5-9 | 20-35 | 10-20% | High (status/system) |
| **Horror** | 3-20 (typical 8) | 0-3 | 4-7 | 20-28 | 35-50% | Moderate |
| **Sports** | 4-15 (typical 7) | 2-5 | 6-10 | 18-28 | 20-30% | Low-moderate |
| **Iyashikei** | 5-20 (typical 10) | 0-2 | 3-6 | 20-30 | 40-60% | Moderate (gentle) |
| **BL/GL** | 5-30 (typical 15) | 1-3 | 4-7 | 20-35 | 30-45% | High |
| **Mecha** | 5-25 (typical 12) | 2-4 | 5-8 | 22-35 | 20-30% | Moderate |

**Notes on these metrics:**
- "Silence panel %" includes panels with no dialogue bubbles (SFX-only panels count as action, not silence)
- Isekai's wide bubble range is driven by status-screen caption boxes that can run 100+ words
- Horror's low bubble count per panel is deliberate — building dread through visual understatement
- Iyashikei's low panels/page reflects large establishing shots and single-image mood panels
- Sports shows high panels/page during active game sequences, dropping to shōjo-like pacing during emotional aftermath

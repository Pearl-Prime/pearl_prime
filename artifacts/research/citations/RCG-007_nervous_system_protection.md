# RCG-007 — Nervous system protection / polyvagal theory (MEDIUM)

**Audit ref:** `RESEARCH_AUDIT_2026_03_30.md` section A item 7
**Source file:** `docs/BESTSELLER_STRUCTURES.md` (~L155)
**Claim:** "It's your nervous system's attempt to protect you. It evolved to keep you alive."
**Research query:** polyvagal theory Porges critique trauma nervous system popular psychology

---

## Claim analysis

The claim frames trauma responses and stress reactions as the nervous system's protective evolutionary mechanism. This language is associated with polyvagal theory (Porges) and somatic trauma frameworks (van der Kolk, Levine) that are widely used in popular psychology and therapeutic contexts.

## Research findings

### Polyvagal theory — original framework

1. **Porges, Stephen W. (2011).** *The Polyvagal Theory: Neurophysiological Foundations of Emotions, Attachment, Communication, and Self-regulation.* New York: W. W. Norton. ISBN: 978-0-393-70700-7. Proposes that the autonomic nervous system has three hierarchical circuits (ventral vagal, sympathetic, dorsal vagal) that evolved to manage safety, danger, and life-threat responses.

2. **Porges, S. W. (2007).** "The polyvagal perspective." *Biological Psychology*, 74(2), 116-143. Technical exposition of the theory's claims about vagal circuits and their evolutionary origins.

### Critiques of polyvagal theory

3. **Grossman, P. & Taylor, E. W. (2007).** "Toward understanding respiratory sinus arrhythmia: Relations to cardiac vagal tone, evolution and biobehavioral functions." *Biological Psychology*, 74(2), 263-285. Challenges Porges' evolutionary phylogenetic claims, arguing the proposed evolutionary sequence of vagal circuits lacks comparative anatomical support.

4. **Taylor, E. W., Leite, C. A. C., & Skovgaard, N. (2010).** "Autonomic control of cardiorespiratory interactions in fish, amphibians and reptiles." *Brazilian Journal of Medical and Biological Research*, 43(7), 600-610. Provides comparative physiology evidence contradicting Porges' phylogenetic claims about vagal evolution.

5. **Porges has responded** to critiques by acknowledging the theory is a "working model" while maintaining its clinical utility. The scientific community remains divided: the theory is widely adopted in clinical trauma practice but questioned in comparative physiology.

### General evolutionary framing of stress responses

6. **Cannon, W. B. (1932).** *The Wisdom of the Body.* New York: W. W. Norton. The foundational "fight or flight" framework, which does describe stress responses as evolved protective mechanisms — well before polyvagal theory.

7. **Nesse, R. M. (2019).** *Good Reasons for Bad Feelings: Insights from the Frontier of Evolutionary Psychiatry.* New York: Dutton. Modern evolutionary psychiatry framework supporting the general claim that emotional/stress responses have protective evolutionary functions.

## Verdict

**PARTIALLY_CONFIRMED** — The general claim that stress and trauma responses evolved as protective mechanisms is well-supported in evolutionary biology and psychology (Cannon 1932; Nesse 2019). However, the specific framing — "your nervous system's attempt to protect you" — closely echoes polyvagal theory (Porges 2011), which has legitimate scientific critiques regarding its evolutionary phylogenetic claims (Grossman & Taylor 2007). The core idea is sound; the implied theoretical framework (polyvagal) is contested in its specifics.

## Recommendation

The claim is acceptable if attributed and hedged: "Stress responses are widely understood as evolved protective mechanisms (Cannon, 1932; Nesse, 2019). Polyvagal theory (Porges, 2011) offers one influential framework for understanding how the nervous system manages safety and threat, though aspects of its evolutionary claims remain debated in comparative physiology." Alternatively, keep the simpler framing but cite the general evolutionary basis rather than implying polyvagal theory specifically.

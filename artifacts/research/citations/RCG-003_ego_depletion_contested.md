# RCG-003 — Ego depletion / willpower as limited resource (HIGH)

**Audit ref:** `RESEARCH_AUDIT_2026_03_30.md` section A item 3
**Source file:** `docs/BESTSELLER_STRUCTURES.md` (~L265)
**Claim:** "willpower is a limited resource. The more you rely on it, the weaker it gets."
**Research query:** Baumeister ego depletion replication crisis 2023-2026 meta-analysis both sides

---

## Claim analysis

This restates the "ego depletion" theory originally proposed by Roy Baumeister and colleagues — that self-control draws on a finite mental resource that becomes depleted with use. The claim appears in a "Myth-Killer" structure example and presents it as established fact.

## Research findings

### Original theory

1. **Baumeister, R. F., Bratslavsky, E., Muraven, M., & Tice, D. M. (1998).** "Ego depletion: Is the active self a limited resource?" *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 74(5), 1252-1265. The foundational paper establishing the ego depletion paradigm via the "radish vs. chocolate" experiment.

2. **Baumeister, R. F., Vohs, K. D., & Tice, D. M. (2007).** "The Strength Model of Self-Control." *Current Directions in Psychological Science*, 16(6), 351-355. Review solidifying the "limited resource" model in mainstream psychology.

### Replication failures and challenges

3. **Hagger, M. S. et al. (2016).** "A Multilab Preregistered Replication of the Ego-Depletion Effect." *Perspectives on Psychological Science*, 11(4), 546-573. **23-lab registered replication** (N > 2,000) found **no significant ego depletion effect** (d = 0.04, 95% CI [-0.07, 0.15]). This is the most impactful failure to replicate.

4. **Carter, E. C., Kofler, L. M., Forster, D. E., & McCullough, M. E. (2015).** "A Series of Meta-Analytic Tests of the Depletion Effect: Self-Control Does Not Seem to Rely on a Limited Resource." *Journal of Experimental Psychology: General*, 144(4), 796-815. Meta-analysis finding the ego depletion effect shrinks to near zero after correcting for publication bias.

5. **Friese, M., Loschelder, D. D., Gieseler, K., Frankenbach, J., & Inzlicht, M. (2019).** "Is Ego Depletion Real? An Analysis of Arguments." *Personality and Social Psychology Review*, 23(2), 107-131. Comprehensive review concluding the evidence for ego depletion is "surprisingly fragile."

### Defenders and alternative models

6. **Baumeister, R. F. & Vohs, K. D. (2016).** "Misguided Effort with Elusive Implications." *Perspectives on Psychological Science*, 11(4), 574-575. Response to Hagger et al., arguing the replication used a weak paradigm.

7. **Dang, J. (2018).** "An updated meta-analysis of the ego depletion effect." *Psychological Research*, 82(4), 645-651. Found a small but significant effect (d = 0.21) but noted high heterogeneity and possible residual publication bias.

### Current consensus (2023-2026)

8. **Inzlicht, M. & Friese, M. (2019).** "The Past, Present, and Future of Ego Depletion." *Social Psychology*, 50(5-6), 370-378. Leading researchers in the field conclude that the original "limited resource" model is likely wrong, though self-control fluctuations are real and may have motivational rather than resource-based explanations.

## Verdict

**UNCONFIRMED** — The specific claim that "willpower is a limited resource" that "gets weaker" with use reflects the Baumeister ego depletion model, which has **failed to replicate** in the largest preregistered replication attempt (Hagger et al. 2016, d = 0.04) and shows evidence of **publication bias** in earlier supporting meta-analyses (Carter et al. 2015). The current scientific consensus treats ego depletion as **contested at best and likely wrong in its strong form** (Inzlicht & Friese 2019; Friese et al. 2019). Self-control fluctuations exist, but the "limited resource" metaphor is not supported.

## Recommendation

This claim appears in a "Myth-Killer" structure example, which is ironic since the claim itself is now considered a contested finding. Options:
1. **Replace** with a non-debunked example for the Myth-Killer structure
2. **Reframe** as: "The popular idea that willpower is a limited resource — once championed by psychologist Roy Baumeister (1998) — has faced serious challenges. The largest replication attempt found no significant effect (Hagger et al., 2016), and the current consensus treats the claim as contested."
3. **Use it as the myth being killed** rather than presenting it as fact

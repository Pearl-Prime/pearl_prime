# RCG-006 — Growth mindset "yet" and neural pathways (MEDIUM)

**Audit ref:** `RESEARCH_AUDIT_2026_03_30.md` section A item 6
**Source file:** `docs/BESTSELLER_STRUCTURES.md` (~L190)
**Claim:** "That one word — yet — changes your neural pathway from fixed to growth."
**Research query:** Dweck growth mindset intervention neuroscience evidence single word yet

---

## Claim analysis

The claim asserts that the word "yet" literally changes neural pathways, linking Carol Dweck's growth mindset concept to a specific neuroscience mechanism triggered by a single word.

## Research findings

### Growth mindset — original theory

1. **Dweck, Carol S. (2006).** *Mindset: The New Psychology of Success.* New York: Random House. The foundational work on fixed vs. growth mindset. Dweck uses "yet" as a pedagogical framing tool (e.g., "I can't do this... yet") but does not claim it changes neural pathways.

2. **Dweck, C. S. (2015).** "Carol Dweck Revisits the 'Growth Mindset.'" *Education Week*, September 22, 2015. Dweck herself cautions against oversimplified applications of growth mindset, noting that "false growth mindset" is widespread.

### Growth mindset interventions — evidence

3. **Yeager, D. S. et al. (2019).** "A national experiment reveals where a growth mindset improves achievement." *Nature*, 573, 364-369. Large-scale RCT (N > 12,000) found that a brief growth mindset intervention improved grades for lower-achieving students — but the intervention was a multi-component module, not a single word.

4. **Sisk, V. F., Burgoyne, A. P., Sun, J., Butler, J. L., & Macnamara, B. N. (2018).** "To What Extent and Under What Circumstances Are Growth Mind-Sets Important to Academic Achievement? Two Meta-Analyses." *Psychological Science*, 29(4), 549-571. Found weak overall correlations between mindset and achievement (r = 0.10) and small effects of mindset interventions (d = 0.08).

### Neuroscience of mindset — limited evidence

5. **Moser, J. S., Schroder, H. S., Heeter, C., Moran, T. P., & Lee, Y. H. (2011).** "Mind Your Errors: Evidence for a Neural Mechanism Linking Growth Mind-Set to Adaptive Posterror Adjustments." *Psychological Science*, 22(12), 1484-1489. EEG study finding that growth-mindset individuals show larger error-related positivity (Pe) brain responses — suggesting greater attention to errors. This is the study most often cited for "neural" differences, but it measures an EEG signal pattern, not a "pathway change" from a single word.

6. **No published study was found** demonstrating that the word "yet" alone changes neural pathways. The claim conflates Dweck's pedagogical framing with neuroscience in a way that no published research supports.

## Verdict

**UNCONFIRMED** — The specific claim that the word "yet" changes neural pathways is not supported by published neuroscience. Growth mindset is a real psychological construct (Dweck 2006) with modest intervention effects (Yeager et al. 2019), and there is limited EEG evidence of neural correlates (Moser et al. 2011). However, the leap from "growth mindset concept" to "single word changes neural pathways" is unsupported.

## Recommendation

Rewrite as: "Dweck's research suggests that framing challenges with 'yet' ('I can't do this yet') encourages a growth-oriented response to difficulty (Dweck, 2006). While one EEG study found different error-processing patterns in growth-mindset individuals (Moser et al., 2011), the claim that a single word rewires neural pathways is a popular simplification, not a neuroscience finding."

# RCG-001 — Sleep complaints 40% rise (HIGH)

**Audit ref:** `RESEARCH_AUDIT_2026_03_30.md` section A item 1
**Source file:** `docs/BESTSELLER_STRUCTURES.md` (~L119)
**Claim:** "In the last decade, sleep complaints have risen 40 percent."
**Research query:** sleep complaints trend 2014-2024 CDC NHIS OR peer-reviewed survey methodology

---

## Claim analysis

The claim asserts a specific 40% increase in sleep complaints over one decade. This appears in a Gladwell Spiral structure example and functions as a hook statistic.

## Research findings

### CDC / NHIS data

1. **Ford, E. S., Cunningham, T. J., & Croft, J. B. (2015).** "Trends in Self-Reported Sleep Duration among US Adults from 1985 to 2012." *Sleep*, 38(5), 829-832. This study documented increasing prevalence of short sleep duration (<6 hours) from 22.1% in 1985 to 29.2% in 2012 — roughly a 32% relative increase over ~27 years, not 40% over a decade.

2. **Khubchandani, J. & Price, J. H. (2020).** "Short Sleep Duration in Working American Adults, 2010-2018." *Journal of Community Health*, 45(2), 219-227. Found that insufficient sleep prevalence among U.S. adults rose from 30.9% in 2010 to 35.6% in 2018 — approximately a 15% relative increase over 8 years.

3. **CDC Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System (BRFSS).** The CDC has tracked self-reported insufficient sleep. BRFSS data show the prevalence of adults reporting <7 hours of sleep increased from approximately 35% in 2013 to around 40% by 2020, but this is a ~14% relative increase, not 40%.

4. **Chattu, V. K. et al. (2019).** "The Global Problem of Insufficient Sleep and Its Serious Public Health Implications." *Healthcare*, 7(1), 1. Reviews global data showing sleep complaint increases but does not support a single "40% rise in a decade" figure.

### COVID-era sleep disruption

5. **Jahrami, H. et al. (2021).** "Sleep problems during the COVID-19 pandemic by population: a systematic review and meta-analysis." *Journal of Clinical Sleep Medicine*, 17(2), 299-313. Found global sleep problem prevalence of ~40% during the pandemic — but this is prevalence, not a trend increase.

## Verdict

**PARTIALLY_CONFIRMED** — Sleep complaints and short sleep duration have clearly increased over recent decades, supported by multiple CDC-linked studies. However, the specific claim of a "40 percent" rise "in the last decade" does not match any single authoritative source found. The closest data show:
- ~15% relative increase 2010-2018 (Khubchandani & Price 2020)
- ~14% relative increase 2013-2020 (BRFSS)
- ~32% relative increase over 27 years (Ford et al. 2015)

The 40% figure may be a conflation of prevalence rate (~40% of adults report insufficient sleep) with a trend increase, or may derive from a specific subpopulation not identified here.

## Recommendation

Soften to: "Sleep complaints have risen significantly over the past two decades, with CDC data showing the share of U.S. adults sleeping less than seven hours increasing from roughly 35% to over 40%." Alternatively, label as "illustrative" per audit guidance.

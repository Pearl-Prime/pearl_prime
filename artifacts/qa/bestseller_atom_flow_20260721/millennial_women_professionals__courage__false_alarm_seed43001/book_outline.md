## Chapter 1 — recognition (The Thing You're Not Doing)

**Thesis:** You know what it is. You have known for a while. There is a specific thing — a project, a conversation, a risk, a version of your life — that you are not doing, and you know you are not doing it. This chapter names that specific structure: not the general category of avoidance, but the precise form it takes when you know exactly what you want and cannot make yourself go toward it.

## Chapter 2 — somatic_legitimacy (Your Body Is Treating Your Goal Like a Threat)

**Thesis:** The alarm that fires when you move toward the thing you want is real. It is not weakness, not cowardice, not evidence that you don't really want it. Your body has a mechanism for detecting novelty, visibility, and reversibility-if-wrong — and it treats those features as threat signals. Your goal has all three features. The alarm is not broken. It is working exactly as it was designed to work. The problem is that it cannot distinguish between a predator and a first draft.

## Chapter 3 — mechanism (The Alarm Was Wrong About the Cost)

**Thesis:** The false alarm on action does not just produce fear — it produces a chain of prediction. If I do this, then X happens, then Y happens from X, then the whole thing collapses. Each link in the chain feels logical. The chain itself is the mechanism, not the prediction. The spiral is the body's way of making the alarm feel rational. The disaster at the end of the chain is real in the body. It has not happened.

## Chapter 4 — destabilization (Waiting for the Fear to Pass Is the Wrong Strategy)

**Thesis:** The fear will not pass before you act. Not because you are particularly anxious or underprepared, but because the alarm on novel, visible action does not resolve through preparation or time. It resolves through experience. And the experience requires the action. The strategy of waiting for the fear to pass is structurally designed to never complete.

## Chapter 5 — practical_interruption (The Smallest Possible Version)

**Thesis:** You do not start with the full version of the thing. The alarm running while you act is not a sign you did it wrong. It is the definition of what you are doing.

## Chapter 6 — cost_exposure (The Accumulation of Not-Doing)

## Chapter 7 — hidden_belief (Readiness Is a Feeling I'll Have When I'm Ready)

**Thesis:** Underneath the preparation loop is a belief about the nature of readiness: that it is a feeling you will have once you have done enough preparation, and that the feeling, when it arrives, will make action natural and unscary. This belief is not malicious. It is also structurally false — because readiness in this context is not a feeling that precedes action but an experience that follows it.

## Chapter 9 — practice_under_pressure (The Most Personal Version)

**Thesis:** There is a version of the action that scares you most. Not the version that requires the most skill — the version that is most personally exposed. Where your specific anxiety is greatest, where visibility shame is loudest, where the spiral's catastrophe is most precisely targeted at something you actually care about. This chapter goes there — not because suffering is the goal, but because avoiding the most personal version is exactly how the preparation loop re-establishes itself after the initial action.

## Chapter 10 — reframe (The Alarm Was Wrong About the Cost)

**Thesis:** The alarm predicted a specific disaster. The disaster was the logic of the spiral — the chain of bad outcomes that felt inevitable from inside the alarm. Now you have done the thing. The disaster did not happen, or it happened differently than predicted, or the version of it that happened was survivable in a way the alarm did not communicate. The alarm was wrong about the cost. That is information about the alarm's reliability as a forecasting mechanism.

**Thesis:** The thing you were not doing is no longer the only version of the future available to you. You have acted before the all-clear arrived. You have survived the alarm. You have a record that includes both the alarm and the action. The practice of acting before certainty is not a single event — it is a capacity you have begun to develop, and it will be called on again. This chapter names what it makes possible.

Chapter 1
The Thing Nobody Talks About

I want to start by telling you something that took me a long time to say out loud.

For most of my adult life, I didn't know what was happening to me. I knew something was happening — I could feel it, every day, in my chest, behind my eyes, in the tightness of my shoulders that never went away. But I didn't have a name for it, and without a name, it was just me. It was just how I was. It was just the tax for being alive.

I'm a rugby player. I grew up in Ireland, in a culture that had very specific ideas about what men did with their feelings: they didn't. You kept it together. You performed. If something was wrong, you ran faster, trained harder, pushed through. The idea that you might sit down and say "I am struggling" — that wasn't in the vocabulary. That wasn't available.

So I performed. I got good at it. From the outside, my life looked like the kind of life you're supposed to want: sport, recognition, friends, purpose. Inside, I was running a private emergency. There was a constant hum of dread that I couldn't locate or name. There were nights when it crescendoed into something I can only describe as absolute terror — terror with no object, terror that seemed to come from inside the walls of my own mind.

What I didn't know then, and know now, is that what I was experiencing had a name. It's called anxiety. Specifically, the kind that doesn't announce itself as anxiety — the kind that disguises itself as personality, as temperament, as being "intense" or "high-performing" or "someone who cares a lot."

Here's what anxiety looks like in people who don't know they have it:

You are always slightly ahead of yourself, in the next moment, the next meeting, the next possible crisis, never fully present in the one you're in.

You replay conversations after they happen, looking for the thing you said wrong, the impression you made, the way they looked at you that might have meant something.

You feel an urgent, low-grade need to be useful, productive, moving — stillness is uncomfortable in a way you can't explain. You call it ambition. It might be something else.

You avoid certain situations not because they're dangerous but because the anticipation of them produces a physical sensation in your chest that you've learned not to push through.

You sleep badly, not because your life is bad, but because your mind won't stop running scenarios.

You feel, underneath everything, that you are running out of time. For what, you couldn't say.

None of that made me strange. None of it made me weak. It made me human, and specifically, it made me a human whose nervous system had been trying to protect me for years using the wrong tools for the situation.

That's the thing nobody talks about. Anxiety is not a flaw in the system. It is the system, responding to what it has learned. The problem isn't that it exists. The problem is that most of us were never taught to understand what it's doing, and so we've been spending our energy fighting ourselves instead of working with what we have.

I'm going to try to explain what I mean. Not in technical language — I'm not a doctor, and you can find that in a dozen other books. In plain language, the language of someone who has lived with this and who has, slowly and imperfectly, learned to live differently.

That's all I can offer you. But I think it might be enough.

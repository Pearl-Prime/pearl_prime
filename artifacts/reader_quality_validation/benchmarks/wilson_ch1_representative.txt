Chapter 1
A Letter to My Anxiety

Dear Anxiety,

I've been avoiding this. You know that. You know everything about me, which is the problem, isn't it? You've lived in my chest for as long as I can remember. You were there the morning of my first piano recital, when I was eight, standing in the wings of the church hall, certain I would forget every note and that everyone would see what I truly was: unworthy.

You were there when I was sixteen, in the back seat of the bus to school, rehearsing conversations I might need to have, preparing for attacks that never came, exhausted by the preparation. You sat beside me at every dinner table, every job interview, every first date. You came on my honeymoon.

I used to think you were just me. That you were the truest version of me — the one that saw things clearly, that understood how precarious everything was, that was doing the necessary work of keeping me safe. Everyone else seemed so easy in the world. I wasn't. I figured this was simply my nature.

But here's what I've come to understand, slowly, messily, over years of therapy and books and conversations at 3am and watching how you operate: you are not me. You are a thing that lives in me. And you are trying, in your limited and ultimately counterproductive way, to protect me. From what? From everything. From embarrassment and failure and loss and death and the terrifying possibility that none of it matters.

The strange thing is that once I understood this — that you are fear wearing the costume of reason — I stopped hating you quite so much. How could I hate something that was, at its root, trying to keep me alive? The method is wrong. The execution is disastrous. You have cost me sleep and relationships and opportunities and years. But the intention, in some primitive mammalian way, was not malicious.

Still. We need a new arrangement.

When I was thirty, I had what the doctors called a major depressive episode with features of generalized anxiety disorder. What I called it was: finally giving up on pretending to be fine. I couldn't get out of bed. I couldn't eat. The particular quality of that suffering is hard to describe — it wasn't dramatic, it wasn't tears and collapsing, it was more like being encased in wet concrete. Everything took tremendous effort. Nothing felt real.

In retrospect, I had been heading there for years, maybe decades. You had been doing your work so effectively, demanding so much vigilance, consuming so much of my mental bandwidth, that there was nothing left. The machine wore itself out.

Recovery was not a straight line. It never is. But this is what I want to tell you about recovery: it is not about becoming someone who no longer has anxiety. I will always have anxiety. The chemistry of my brain predisposes me to it; the events of my early life reinforced it; the culture I live in rewards it. What recovery taught me was how to have a different relationship with you. To see you when you arrive. To say: oh, there you are again. I know what this is. And then to decide, deliberately, what to do next.

That is a skill. It can be learned.

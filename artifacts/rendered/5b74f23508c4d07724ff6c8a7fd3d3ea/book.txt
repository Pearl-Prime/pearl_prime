What I had to learn to distinguish is the difference between a reasonable recalibration and an ongoing loss. The question I find myself coming back to, and that I think is worth sitting with, is: what would it mean to take your own signals seriously. Not to fix them immediately. Not to know what to do with them. Just to take them seriously as information rather than as weakness or inconvenience.

Most of the training — in school, in early work environments, in culture generally — is training in override. You feel tired; you continue. You feel uncertain; you project confidence. You feel like something is wrong; you push through and reassess later. The override can be useful, for a time and in a specific situation. As a general operating mode, applied indefinitely, it is a reliable path to depletion.

Taking your own signals seriously is a different orientation. It means treating the interior experience as data rather than as noise to be managed. It means asking, when something feels off, what that is actually telling you — not to be paralyzed by it, but to let it inform you. This is harder than override, because it does not have the same clear narrative of toughness. But it is more accurate. And accuracy, in the long run, is more sustainable.

I want to talk about the specific exhaustion of performing fine. Not performing poorly — that would be easier to locate, easier to name. I mean the exhaustion of continuing to produce the expected outputs, hit the expected marks, show up in the expected ways, while the interior experience of doing so has quietly become something else.

This is one of the more disorienting features of burnout for people who are good at their work. The competence does not leave first. The caring leaves first. You can continue to do the thing well after you have stopped feeling anything about whether it is going well. The skill is still there. The investment has gone somewhere else, or gone quiet, or simply run out of supply.

The disorientation comes from the gap between external signal and internal experience. The external signal says everything is fine. The internal experience says something is not. Because the external signal is legible and the internal one is harder to name, most people default to trusting the external one. They tell themselves the internal experience is temporary, or ungrateful, or disproportionate. It is usually none of those things. It is usually accurate.

Chapter 9

Something that took me time to understand is the difference between being busy and being depleted. They can look identical from the outside — full calendar, constant output, no apparent space. But they are not the same state, and they do not require the same response.

Busy is a condition of volume. There is a lot to do and you are doing it. Depleted is a condition of resource. The supply that the doing draws from has run low. You can be busy without being depleted, if the work is replenishing something as it draws. You can be depleted without being especially busy, if the draw has been steady for long enough and the replenishment has been absent.

The reason the distinction matters is that the solutions are different. More efficiency solves busy. More efficiency does not solve depleted — it often accelerates it, because it increases the draw on the same diminished supply. What solves depleted is replenishment, which usually requires not doing something, or doing something that returns rather than takes. That is a harder prescription to follow in an environment that rewards throughput. But it is the accurate one.

I have come to think that one of the most important skills — one that is almost never taught — is knowing the difference between a problem that requires more effort and a problem that requires less. Most of the environments that produce high achievers select, heavily, for the first. If something is not working, you try harder. If you are not where you want to be, you do more. Effort is the variable you adjust.

This works, until it does not. And where it stops working most reliably is in situations of depletion — where the problem is not insufficient effort, but a system that needs to be allowed to recover. In those situations, adding more effort does not solve the problem. It extends it. It applies the only tool you have been given to a situation that requires a different tool entirely.

The recognition that you might be in a situation that calls for less rather than more — for stopping rather than continuing — is genuinely difficult when effort is the identity. It can feel like giving up, or failure, or weakness. I want to say it is usually none of those things. It is usually the more accurate read of what the situation actually requires.

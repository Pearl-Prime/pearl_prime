Chapter 1

Your parents are having a serious conversation downstairs. Your stomach is tight and you're listening hard.

You're walking home on the street below after school with your backpack. The gray light through the window afternoon stretches long and empty ahead of you. Your phone stays silent—no texts from your friend group at all. Your sneakers tap against the pavement in steady rhythm. The walk usually takes twelve minutes but today feels much longer. You pass familiar houses but they look slightly different somehow. Your backpack straps dig into your shoulders with every step. The school building disappears behind you but doesn't feel far away yet.

Zoe texts a joke to the group chat and then watches the reactions. Not casually—intensely. Who laughed, who didn't respond, how long between the send and the first reaction. The monitoring converts a joke into a test. The joke was funny to her when she typed it. The moment she hit send, the joke became evidence of something: either she's funny or she's not. The watcher turns every social action into an evaluation. The evaluation runs on other people's responses. The joke was play. The watcher made it a performance review.

I have noticed that the anxiety of making a decision, any decision, can become quite intense. The decision might be small: what to wear, what to eat. The decision might be large: what to study, what kind of social group to be part of. But the mechanism that activates is the same. Once you decide something, you cannot decide something else. Once you choose one path, other paths are no longer available.

There is a mechanism that dwells on the roads not taken. It is terrified of regret. The anxiety is about choosing wrong and then having to live with that choice for a long time. The mechanism wants to avoid regret at all costs. So it tries to make the perfect choice, the choice that will have no regret attached.

But the problem is that perfect choices do not exist. Every choice involves losing something. Every path not taken is a road that is no longer available. The mechanism is trying to make a choice that involves no loss. And that choice does not exist. So the mechanism either stays frozen, unable to choose, or it chooses and then spends a long time dwelling on what was lost.

What I have come to understand is that the mechanism does not have access to the knowledge that choices are often reversible, changeable, adjustable. It treats every choice as if it is permanent. Once you choose, you are locked in. But most choices are not like that. You can change your mind. You can try something and then try something else. You can adjust your path as you go.

The mechanism is operating under the assumption that you must choose perfectly, right now, with full information about all possible futures. And that is an impossible standard. So the anxiety continues. The mechanism keeps looking for the perfect choice. The perfect choice never arrives. The anxiety stays.

What I have come to think about is the way that choosing with imperfect information, accepting the loss that comes with any choice, and staying flexible about adjustment, is actually the normal way that humans navigate. But the mechanism has not learned that yet.

I have noticed that nighttime anxiety is its own particular version. During the day, there are distractions. There are tasks. There are people. But at night, when the external world quiets down, the internal world becomes much louder. You lie in bed, and suddenly you are alone with all of your thoughts. And many of them are anxious thoughts.

There is a mechanism that activates more strongly at night: the mechanism that reviews the day, that looks ahead to the next day, that dwells on conversations and moments and things you said that were awkward. This is called nocturnal rumination, and it can be quite intense because there is nothing else to disrupt it. No teacher is lecturing. No friend is texting. No task is demanding your attention. The rumination has all the space it needs.

What I have come to understand is that this mechanism is not more active at night because you are worse at managing your thoughts at night. It is more active because the conditions are different. The nervous system is also preparing to sleep, which involves a shift in neurochemistry. The body is winding down. And the mind, as it winds down, often becomes more introspective, more reflective, more willing to dwell on things.

The anxiety at night is not a sign that you are failing at something. It is a sign that the conditions at night—the quiet, the lack of distraction, the shift in nervous system state—are conditions in which the anxiety mechanism activates more readily.

This does not mean the anxiety is inescapable. But it does mean that understanding why it is worse at night, rather than just believing that you are failing at sleep because you cannot stop your anxious thoughts, changes the relationship to the experience. The mechanism itself is very normal. It is just louder when everything else is quiet.

I have noticed that the anxiety that comes from the awareness of your own anxiety can become quite recursive. You are anxious, and you notice that you are anxious, and you become anxious about the anxiety, and the system spirals. The mechanism that is supposed to help you manage your internal states has turned into a source of more internal turmoil.

What I have come to understand is that this mechanism is related to self-awareness. The capacity to observe your own mental states is a useful capacity. It helps you understand yourself. It helps you manage. But when the mechanism of self-observation turns critical, when it turns into evaluation and judgment, then the observation becomes another layer of activation rather than a way to help.

You are anxious, and the mechanism says: this is bad. You should not be anxious. Good people are not anxious. What is wrong with you. The evaluation takes the original anxiety and adds shame and self-criticism on top. The original anxiety is now partnered with the anxiety about the anxiety and the shame about being anxious.

The mechanism would actually be more helpful if it could simply observe without judgment. Anxiety is present. That is what is happening. But the mechanism cannot seem to do that. It must evaluate. It must judge. It must try to fix. And the trying to fix becomes another loop.

What I have come to think about is the difference between observing what is present and evaluating whether it should be present. The first is useful. The second creates more disturbance. But the mechanism is built to do the second. It is built to evaluate. That is what self-awareness does. It compares what is present to what should be present. And if there is a mismatch, it treats the mismatch as a problem.

But what if the anxiety is just what is present right now. What if it does not need to be evaluated. What if it just needs to be noticed, without the judgment that it is bad or wrong or a sign that something is broken.

The mechanism does not know how to do that yet.

I have noticed that there is often a gap between how anxious you appear and how anxious you actually are. You are in the room, apparently functioning, apparently okay, and inside your nervous system is running at full activation. No one can see that. And because no one can see it, you assume that everyone else is not running at full activation, that everyone else is actually calm.

This assumption is almost certainly wrong. The mechanism that reads other people's external presentation and concludes something about their internal state is not very accurate. People are skilled at presenting as more calm than they are. You are skilled at it too, even when you are convinced you look anxious.

What I have come to understand is that the gap between presentation and interior is very common, especially in settings where anxiety is stigmatized, where being anxious means being weak or broken. People hide. You hide. And then everyone looks calm and you conclude that you are the only one who is not.

The mechanism that compares your interior to other people's exterior and concludes you are different is running on incomplete data. If you could see other people's interiors, the data would look very different. The gap between how it looks and how it feels is not unique to you.

I have noticed that perfectionism and anxiety work together in a particular way. The perfectionism sets a standard that is very high and often impossible to meet consistently. The anxiety then fires when that standard is not met, or when it seems like it might not be met, or when there is any possibility of falling short.

What makes this particularly powerful is that the perfectionism feels like a virtue. It feels like caring. It feels like having standards. It feels like the thing that will keep you safe, because if you can just be perfect enough, nothing bad will happen. You will not fail. You will not be embarrassed. You will not disappoint anyone.

But perfectionism is actually a kind of anxiety in disguise. It is the nervous system's attempt to control the future by making yourself invulnerable. If you are perfect, you cannot be harmed. The logic is appealing, but it is built on a false premise. No one is invulnerable. No one can be perfect. The standard itself is a guarantee that you will fail to meet it.

What I have come to understand is the way this mechanism creates a kind of constant low-level activation. You are always falling short. You are always aware of the gap between the ideal and the actual. The anxiety is built into the structure itself, not into any particular failure.

The perfectionism protects you from the anxiety by promising that if you just try hard enough, you can achieve safety. But the trying hard enough is the anxiety. It is the same mechanism wearing a different mask. And the mask is so convincing that you believe the solution is to try even harder, to be even more perfect, to close the gap further.

The mechanism is built on the belief that safety is available through control. The anxiety is the system's way of managing that control.

I have noticed that the way anxiety speaks about the future is very different from the way reality typically unfolds. The anxiety voice is specific, certain, and catastrophic. It tells you exactly what will happen, who will see, and how bad it will be. But the actual future is almost always less specific, less certain, and less catastrophic than the version the anxiety described.

This is not because you are bad at predicting the future. It is because the anxiety mechanism is not trying to predict the future accurately. It is trying to activate your threat response. It needs the prediction to be specific enough and bad enough to get you to act. So it manufactures specificity and certainty and catastrophe.

What I have come to understand is that you can check the anxiety's predictions against what actually happens. Not to prove that the anxiety is wrong. The mechanism itself is trying to protect you. But its method involves consistently overestimating the threat. Once you have enough data to see that pattern, the next prediction feels slightly different. Not safe, but less certain.

I have noticed that comparison is one of the most powerful amplifiers of anxiety. You are anxious about your performance, and then you look at what someone else is doing, and you perceive that they are doing better, and the anxiety intensifies. They got an A, you got an A-minus. They answered the question confidently, you stumbled. They seem calm, you are not calm.

This comparison mechanism is nearly involuntary. The brain is constantly evaluating how you are doing relative to others. This was useful at some point in human history. It helped you know where you stood in the social hierarchy. It helped you identify threats. It helped you navigate. But in the context of school, where the comparison is constant and visible and quantified, the mechanism becomes a source of intense anxiety.

What I have come to understand is that the comparison is almost always incomplete and biased. You are comparing your interior experience to someone else's exterior. You are comparing your anxiety to their apparent calm. You are comparing your self-doubt to their confidence. But you are not seeing their interior. You are not seeing their doubt. You are not seeing the cost of what they appear to be accomplishing.

The mechanism that compares you to others does not have access to information about others' interior states. It only has access to the visible performance. So the comparison is inevitably skewed. And the skew is almost always in the direction of making you look worse by comparison.

What I have come to think about is the way that social media intensifies this mechanism. You are now able to see the curated exterior of dozens of people simultaneously. You are able to compare yourself to versions of people that are carefully constructed to look better than the actual person is. The comparison mechanism is running on overdrive.

The mechanism itself is not new. But the amount of information it has to compare against has expanded exponentially. And the expansion of information is an expansion of anxiety.

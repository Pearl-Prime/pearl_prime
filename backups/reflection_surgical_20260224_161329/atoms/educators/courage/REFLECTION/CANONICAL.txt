## REFLECTION v01
---
What I notice is a particular quality of choice that exists every time I close my classroom door. What I am about to teach is what I believe students should learn. What I am about to teach is not necessarily what the administration told me to teach. What I am about to teach is not necessarily aligned with what the test is measuring. The mechanism that activates is what I think of as pedagogical-fidelity-tension.

The mechanism recognizes that I have a choice. I can teach what I am told to teach, or I can teach what I believe students should learn. These are not always the same thing. The mechanism is the awareness that I have agency, even though the agency comes with risk.

What I have come to understand is that the choice is never simple. If I teach what I believe, I am risking questions from administration, parent complaints, concerns about test performance. But if I teach only what I am told, I am betraying my own understanding of what students actually need.

I have noticed that the mechanism activates differently depending on the day. Sometimes the pressure to conform is strong. Sometimes the pressure to be true to my own understanding is strong. The mechanism is constantly navigating between these pressures.

What the mechanism does is make teaching a conscious act of choice rather than a neutral implementation of curriculum. Every lesson is a decision. Every decision carries consequences. The mechanism knows this with full clarity.

## REFLECTION v02
---
I notice the particular courage that is required to look directly at what is not working in my classroom and say it out loud. A lesson strategy that I thought would work is failing. A student is in crisis and my responses have been inadequate. A system I created is breaking down. The mechanism that activates is what I think of as honest-assessment-courage.

The mechanism is the willingness to face what is actually true rather than what I hoped would be true. The mechanism requires me to admit failure, inadequacy, or the need for change. The mechanism is resisting the pull toward denial.

What I have come to understand is that this courage is not recognized by systems that value consistency and performance. The courage to admit something is not working is often treated as weakness. But the mechanism knows that the honest assessment is the only path toward real change.

I have noticed that the mechanism activates in moments of private recognition before it activates in moments of public acknowledgment. I notice to myself that something is not working. Then I begin the process of acknowledging it to others.

What the mechanism does is create a foundation for growth that begins with honest reality rather than hoped-for outcomes. The mechanism is saying: I will look at what is actually true, even if the truth is uncomfortable.

What I have come to understand is that this courage is often costly. Advocating for a student against the system can mean difficult conversations with administrators. It can mean being positioned as difficult. It can mean being isolated from colleagues.

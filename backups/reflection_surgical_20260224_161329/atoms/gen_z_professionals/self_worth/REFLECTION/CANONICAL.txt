## REFLECTION v01
---
The mechanism of the self-worth that is assembled from external metrics is the mechanism that your generation knows intimately. Your worth was measured from childhood. First, it was grades. Then it was test scores. Then it was GPA. Then it was the job title. Then it was the salary. Then it was the follower count.

Each metric replaced the previous one. Each metric became the way you knew if you were okay. This is the mechanism I want to name: the mechanism that outsources the assessment of your own value. You do not know if you are good unless someone else tells you.

And the someone else who tells you is the metric.
---

## REFLECTION v02
---
I have watched the way that the self-worth that is built on metrics is fragile. Because metrics can change. The grade you received last semester is not the grade you need to receive this semester. The job title you have is not the job title you need to have next year. The follower count you have is not the follower count you need to have next month.

So the mechanism is the constant chasing. The mechanism is that stability is impossible because the metric keeps moving. The mechanism is that you are perpetually behind. Perpetually not quite good enough. The mechanism is the treadmill that accelerates.

And the mechanism is that you learn to run faster and faster.
---

## REFLECTION v03
---
The mechanism of the self-worth that evaporates when the metrics disappear is the mechanism that is particularly dangerous. You graduate. The GPA no longer matters. You are not in school. So the metric that told you if you were good is gone. And you realize you do not have an internal sense of whether you are good.

So the mechanism is the transition. You are trying to build a sense of self-worth from scratch. But you do not know how because you have never built it from scratch. You have only ever known metrics.

So the mechanism is that you are searching for the new metric that will tell you if you are okay.

And the mechanism is that you can never rest on accomplishment because the accomplishment is immediately past.

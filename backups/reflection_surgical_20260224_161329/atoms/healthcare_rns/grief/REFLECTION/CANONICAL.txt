## REFLECTION v01
---
I want to name something about the grief that happens in nursing and is never processed because it is not named as grief.

A patient dies. The death is recorded. The next patient arrives. The shift continues. And the grief is not named. There is no moment of acknowledgment. There is no space for processing. The death is just the next event.

The mechanism is the normalization of loss. When loss happens repeatedly and is treated as part of the workflow, the loss stops being named as loss. It becomes just what happens. The patient died. Document it. Move on.

But the grief is still there. It is just unnamed. It is just incorporated into the rest of the emotional work of the shift. And because it is unnamed, it is not processed. It accumulates.

What I have noticed is that the accumulated unnamed grief becomes part of the baseline emotional state. You are functioning but underneath is the weight of all the losses that were never named.

The mechanism is the way repeated loss without ceremony becomes incorporated trauma.

## REFLECTION v02
---
I want to talk about the specific grief of losing someone you have cared for, knowing that they depended on you.

You have cared for a patient. You have been their advocate. You have held space for their fear. And they have died. And the grief is complicated by the knowledge that they depended on you. That you were supposed to protect them.

The mechanism is the collision between your responsibility and the death. You were supposed to keep them safe. And you did not. The death feels like a failure of your responsibility.

But the death was not a failure. The death is just what happens. But the mechanism of responsibility makes the grief feel like failure.

What I have noticed is that this grief is particularly acute in nurses who feel deeply their responsibility for patients. The sense that the death is somehow your responsibility, your failure, your inadequacy.

The mechanism is the way responsibility for care gets confused with responsibility for life and death.

## REFLECTION v30
---
I want to end by naming something about what grief actually is in nursing, underneath all of these specific manifestations.

Grief is the evidence that you have cared. Grief is the proof that the person mattered. Grief is the appropriate response to loss.

The mechanism is that grief, no matter what form it takes, is the response to the absence of someone who was present. The absence has meaning because the presence had meaning.

And in nursing, the grief is there. It is unnamed. It is unprocessed. It is accumulated. It is transformed into numbness or anger or depression. But it is there.

The grief is evidence that you are a person who cares. The grief is evidence that the person who died mattered. The grief is the appropriate response to their absence.

What I want to say clearly is: the grief is not something to be overcome. The grief is not something to be fixed. The grief is the mark that the person left on you. The grief is the evidence that you loved someone, even in the context of professional care.

And the grief matters. The accumulation matters. The processing matters. The acknowledgment matters.

Because without the acknowledgment of grief, the losses are just the next thing, and the person becomes less than human, and you become less than human in your own eyes.

The grief is what keeps you human in a profession designed to numb you.

## REFLECTION v01
---
I want to name something about the way self-worth in nursing becomes completely entangled with the capacity to help others.

Before nursing, you had a self that was separate from helping. You had worth because you were a person. But nursing teaches you that your worth is specifically located in the helping. Your worth is how much you care. Your worth is how much you sacrifice. Your worth is how much you are needed.

The mechanism is the replacement of intrinsic worth with functional worth. Your worth becomes dependent on your utility. On what you do for others.

What I have noticed is that this starts early. In nursing school, you learn that good nurses care deeply. Good nurses sacrifice. Good nurses put others first. The implication is that not putting others first is not being a good nurse. Which means not putting others first is not being worthy.

So you organize your life around being needed. You become the person who shows up. The person who helps. The person who sacrifices. And your worth becomes completely dependent on the continuation of that role.

The mechanism is the replacement of worth as being with worth as doing.

## REFLECTION v02
---
I want to talk about the specific way self-worth is constructed around being indispensable.

You learn that you are valuable because you are needed. The unit needs you. The patients need you. The system depends on you. So you are important.

But the mechanism is fragile. If you are not needed, are you not important. If the unit runs without you, what is your value. The worth is completely dependent on being essential.

What I have noticed is that this creates a particular form of entrapment. You are valuable because you are needed. So you cannot afford to be less needed. You cannot reduce your hours. You cannot set limits. You have to maintain the level of need.

The mechanism is the way worth constructed around necessity prevents protective boundaries.

## REFLECTION v03
---
I want to name something about the way self-worth becomes lower as the distance from direct care grows.

Bedside nursing is the truest nursing. Administrative nursing is less nursing. Nursing education is less nursing. Other roles are less nursing. The further you get from direct patient care, the less of a "real nurse" you are.

The mechanism is the hierarchy of worth within the profession. The person in direct care is the most worthy. Everyone else is further down.

What I have noticed is that this can trap people in roles that are harming them. A nurse wants to move into a role that would be less physically demanding or emotionally demanding, but she believes she would be less worthy in that role. So she stays.

The mechanism is the way the profession's value hierarchy prevents nurses from protecting themselves.

## REFLECTION v04
---
I want to talk about the way self-worth is damaged by the encounter with systemic limitations.

You want to provide good care. You want to care deeply. You want to help. And the system prevents you. You do not have enough time. You do not have adequate staffing. You do not have the resources to do what you believe is right.

The mechanism is the collision between the worth-giving identity (the helper) and the system's limitations. You cannot help in the way you want to help. So you are failing at the thing that defines your worth.

What I have noticed is that this creates a particular form of shame. You are not able to be the nurse you want to be because the system will not permit it. So you are failing at being you.

The mechanism is the way systemic limitation damages self-worth constructed around helping.

## REFLECTION v05
---
I want to name something about the way self-worth becomes dependent on patient outcomes.

A patient recovers and you feel worthy. The patient dies and you feel unworthy. A patient improves and you are a good nurse. A patient declines and you are a bad nurse.

The mechanism is the complete externalization of worth. Your worth is determined by things outside your control. You have made your worth dependent on outcomes you cannot control.

What I have noticed is that this creates vulnerability to outcomes that are not actually your responsibility. A patient dies for reasons beyond your control and you feel like you are a failure.

The mechanism is the way outcome-dependent worth creates false responsibility.

You look at other nurses and you see them doing things you cannot do. Coping better. Managing better. Caring more. And you feel less worthy.

The mechanism is the way comparison to myth undermines actual worth.

## REFLECTION v30
---
I want to end by naming something about what self-worth in nursing actually is, underneath all of these specific manifestations.

Self-worth in nursing has been systematically tied to the capacity to help others. It has been positioned as dependent on utility, on necessity, on sacrifice, on external validation. The self has been slowly erased in service to the role.

The mechanism is the way the profession has taught nurses that they have worth only insofar as they disappear. The more of yourself you give away, the more worthy you are. The more invisible you become, the more essential you are.

And the mechanism creates a particular kind of damage: the person who has completely disappeared into the role has no self to return to when the role can no longer be the only thing.

What I want to say clearly is: your worth is not dependent on your usefulness. Your worth is not dependent on helping. Your worth is not dependent on the continuation of any role. Your worth is intrinsic. It is because you exist. It is not because of what you do.

But the nursing culture has made this impossible to know. The nursing culture has systematically erased intrinsic worth in favor of functional worth. And the person has been left without access to the ground beneath the role.

The recovery of self-worth would require the acknowledgment that the current structure is a structure of harm. That the way worth has been constructed is not sustainable. That a different relationship to worth is possible.

But that is not something the profession will teach you. That is something you have to discover on your own.
